Abstract

Hot stuff when it gets chilly: Redox-flow batteries (RFBs) for energy storage at subzero temperatures would facilitate the use of renewable energy in cold regions. Such non-aqueous RFBs with high volumetric capacity, high voltage, and excellent cycling stability between 20 and −40 °C have been developed with the porphyrin H2TPP as a bipolar redox-active material and a Y-zeolite–poly(vinylidene fluoride) ion-selective membrane.

Abstract

Electrochemical energy storage with redox-flow batteries (RFBs) under subzero temperature is of great significance for the use of renewable energy in cold regions. However, RFBs are generally used above 10 °C. Herein we present non-aqueous organic RFBs based on 5,10,15,20-tetraphenylporphyrin (H2TPP) as a bipolar redox-active material (anode: [H2TPP]2−/H2TPP, cathode: H2TPP/[H2TPP]2+) and a Y-zeolite–poly(vinylidene fluoride) (Y-PVDF) ion-selective membrane with high ionic conductivity as a separator. The constructed RFBs exhibit a high volumetric capacity of 8.72 Ah L−1 with a high voltage of 2.83 V and excellent cycling stability (capacity retention exceeding 99.98 % per cycle) in the temperature range between 20 and −40 °C. Our study highlights principles for the design of RFBs that operate at low temperatures, thus offering a promising approach to electrochemical energy storage under cold-climate conditions.

Redox-flow batteries (RFBs) are promising large-scale electrochemical energy-storage technologies for wind and solar power generation.1 However, the application of both aqueous and non-aqueous RFBs is restricted by the environmental temperature, especially in areas with subzero climate conditions, where the renewable energy sources, especially wind, are generally abundant.2 Aqueous RFBs, such as traditional vanadium RFBs, are only suitable for operation at temperatures above 10 °C, owing to the instability of V2+, V3+, and V4+ at low temperature. Strategies involving mixed-acid electrolytes or additives have been proposed to reduce the operational temperature to −5 °C,3 but this temperature is still not low enough for the operation of RFBs in cold regions. As compared to aqueous RFBs, non-aqueous RFBs can provide electrolytes with a lower freezing point and higher cell voltage, thus making them suitable for use in cold regions.4, 5 However, non-aqueous organic RFBs still face challenges, such as the low solubility of organic active materials, limited electrolyte stability, and capacity decay during cycling.6 Recent research indicates that the solubility of organic redox-active materials in electrolytes can be improved by introducing functional groups,7 adding additives,5d or using suspension electrolyte systems,8 whereas instability of the electrolyte and capacity degradation is normally caused by the instability of organic active materials and poor ion selectivity of the separator.9 Ion-exchange membranes usually lead to high cell resistance in non-aqueous electrolytes. Porous membranes, such as Celgard (pore diameters of 14–21 nm) and Daramic separators (median pore size of 0.15 mm), have generally been used in non-aqueous RFBs owing to their high ionic conductivities and low area resistance.10 However, these porous membranes show poor ion selectivity, which gradually leads to cross-contamination of the electrolyte from both the anode and the cathode side and further leads to efficiency decline and cycling-capacity decay.11 Therefore, the design of non-aqueous RFBs with stable organic active materials and an efficient separator is of vital importance.

Porphyrins, a kind of natural pigment from many plant and animal species, have been proposed for use in photoconducting and molecular electronic devices.12 Meanwhile, zeolites with a uniform pore-size distribution have been applied widely in industrial catalysis.13 However, porphyrins have not been used as organic redox-active materials and Y-zeolites have not been used as separators with controlled pore size for RFBs. We report herein the combination of a porphyrin-based redox-active material and a Y-zeolite–poly(vinylidene fluoride) (Y-PVDF) ion-selective membrane with an ion-conducting microporous (pore size<2 nm) separator for RFBs that operate in a wide temperature range from 20 to −40 °C. As active materials for RFBs, porphyrins contain an aromatic π-conjugated macrocycle with good conductivity and stability for reversible oxidization/reduction. What is more, the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane, which is like an ionic sieve with both pore-size control and high ionic conductivity, retains H2TPP as well as redox products and permits the transport of ClO4− but not TBA+ ions owing to the pore size exclusion effect. The constructed RFBs demonstrated high electrochemical performance in temperature range between 20 and −40 °C.

Typically, 5,10,15,20-tetraphenylporphyrin (H2TPP) was employed as the bipolar redox-active material. The cyclic voltammogram (CV) of H2TPP shows four reversible one-electron-transfer reactions at potentials of −1.88, −1.55, 0.69, and 0.95 V versus Ag/Ag+ (Figure 1 a). The average anodic and cathodic redox potentials [(Epa+Epc)/2] within the electrochemical window are demonstrated in the supporting electrolyte tetrabutylammonium perchlorate/dichloromethane (TBAP/CH2Cl2; see Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). These four reactions include two one-electron oxidation and reduction processes at the conjugated macrocycle to give a porphyrin π-anion radical and dianion or π-cation radical and dication. This reactivity was also examined by EPR and NMR spectroscopy (Figure 1 b; see also Figure S2). The redox couples [H2TPP]2−/H2TPP and H2TPP/[H2TPP]2+ can serve as the anode and cathode, respectively, for the H2TPP symmetric RFB. The ΔE0 value between the 1/1′ and 4/4′ peaks is 2.83 V, which is the cell voltage for the H2TPP symmetric RFB with two-electron redox reactions. The cell voltage was further confirmed by calculating the ΔE0 values between [H2TPP]2− and [H2TPP]2+ by density functional theory (DFT; see the Supporting Information for the calculation details).

The stability and conductivity of H2TPP were also studied by DFT. The highest occupied molecular orbital/singly occupied molecular orbital (HOMO/SOMO) plots illustrate the location of outer electrons and offer qualitative information about their electronic characteristics.14 The stability of H2TPP and its different redox states were verified by HOMO/SOMO diagrams (Figure 2). Because of the aromatic π-conjugated macrocycle of H2TPP, all the HOMOs/SOMOs of H2TPP in different redox states are well-localized and retained within the structure, thus implying that the backbone of the H2TPP molecule is able to support one or two positive or negative charges. As a result, H2TPP is stable in all the redox states. Furthermore, the HOMO–SOMO–LUMO energy-level difference of the redox species is very small, thus suggesting that the redox reaction of H2TPP can occur very easily (see Table S2 in the Supporting Information). The band gap generally refers to the energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band, and is a major factor in determining the electrical conductivity of a substance.15 The fundamental gap of H2TPP as calculated by DFT is 2.29 eV and is approximately equal to the band gap,16 which implies semiconductor character of H2TPP (see the Supporting Information for calculation details).

The electrochemical kinetics of H2TPP was investigated by recording the CVs of H2TPP at scan rates ranging from 10 to 1500 mV s−1 (see Figure S3). A Luggin capillary probe in close proximity to the working electrode serves to compensate for a majority of the ohmic potential loss (see Figure S4). At higher scan rates, the potential difference between all of the oxidation and reduction peaks increased (see Table S3). Furthermore, the redox peak currents correlated linearly with the square root of the scan rate, thus indicating that the redox reaction is diffusion-controlled. The diffusion coefficients (D) for all the redox states of H2TPP are between 1.011×10−6 and 1.282×10−5 cm2 s−1. The kinetic rate constants (ko) for [H2TPP]2−/[H2TPP].−, [H2TPP].−/H2TPP, H2TPP/[H2TPP].+, and [H2TPP].+/[H2TPP]2+ are 4.874×10−3, 3.480×10−3, 5.231×10−3, and 1.423×10−3 cm s−1, respectively (see Table S4). The diffusion coefficients and rate constants of H2TPP are as high as or several orders of magnitude higher than those of previously reported organic molecules, V3+/V2+, and other species commonly used in redox-flow batteries.17 The fast electrode kinetics of H2TPP, which is probably a benefit of the π-conjugated macrocycle, would be expected to result in a low activation polarization loss when used in RFBs.

Subsequently, an ion-selective membrane with high ionic conductivity was designed for the H2TPP RFBs. This membrane utilizes a commercial poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) porous membrane as the substrate, and Y-zeolite was deliberately selected as the ionic sieve material owing to its appropriate pore size (Figure 3 a,b). The Y-zeolite was obtained by high-temperature calcination and examined by X-ray diffraction (XRD; see Figure S5) to ensure the crystal phase. The pore structures were verified by high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) accompanied by selected-area electron diffraction (SAED) (Figure 3 c).

The prepared Y-zeolite–poly(vinylidene fluoride) (Y-PVDF) ion-selective membrane has a dense surface covered by Y-zeolite particles and a clear sandwich cross-section structure with Y-zeolite layers (the optimized thickness is 7–8 μm) attached on each side (Figure 3 d; see also Figures S6 and S7). The pore size of the Y-zeolite (0.74 nm for 12-ring apertures) is between the calculated diameters of H2TPP (1.141 nm) and one of the supporting-electrolyte ions (ClO4−, 0.547 nm; see the Supporting Information for the calculation details). Thus, the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane retains H2TPP and its redox products and permits ClO4− but not TBA+ ions to transport owing to the pore size exclusion effect. The H2TPP retention ability of the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane as compared to the PVDF porous membrane was examined by ion-diffusion measurements (see Figures S8 and S9). It was clear that the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane retained H2TPP much more efficiently than the PVDF porous membrane.

The ion selectivity of the Y-PVDF membranes was further demonstrated by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS; see Figure S10). The results showed that ClO4− is the charge-transport species and the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane prevents the crossover of H2TPP and its redox products. The electrochemical performance of Y-PVDF, PVDF, and standard cation- and anion-exchange membranes was tested by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements (see Figure S11). The Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane had the lowest area resistance (1.12 Ω cm2) and the highest ionic conductivity (3.82 mS cm; see Table S5). The high selectivity and conductivity of the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane should make it ideal for use in the H2TPP RFBs.

Normally, the concentration of redox-active materials determines the theoretical volumetric capacity of a battery.5a H2TPP is only sparingly soluble (ca. 10 mm) in pure CH2Cl2 and even less in other common solvents (see Figure S12), which results in a low theoretical volumetric capacity of 0.536 Ah L−1. Thus, a suspension of H2TPP was used to increase the concentration beyond the solubility limit. Ketjen Black (KB) with different weight ratios was mixed with H2TPP to form H2TPP@KB composites with elevated conductivity (see the Supporting Information for preparation details). EIS tests determined that the optimal composition was 5 wt % KB (H2TPP@KB-5; see Figure S13). SEM and TEM of H2TPP@KB composites showed that the H2TPP molecule and the conductive carbon percolating network were uniformly integrated (see Figure S14).

To optimize the concentration of H2TPP, we performed EIS and rheological measurements on H2TPP and H2TPP@KB-5 suspensions to analyze their conductivity (see Figure S15) and viscosity (see Figure S16). In the Nyquist plots, the high-frequency intercept on the real axis provides the electrolyte ohmic resistance. At higher solid fractions, the resistance of H2TPP suspensions increased as the H2TPP concentration increased. For comparison, the resistance of H2TPP@KB suspensions was smaller than that of H2TPP suspensions with the same H2TPP concentration, thus revealing that the electrical conductivity of the electrolyte was improved by the addition of conductive KB. This improvement was observed because electron transport can occur when the agglomerated particles in the suspension form conductive carbon-percolation networks, and the suspensions allow for continuous charge storage or ionic migration. However, the viscosity of suspensions increased greatly when KB was introduced as a conductive additive. Considering both the conductivity and flowability of the electrolyte, we used a 200-H2TPP@KB-5 (200 mg mL−1 H2TPP with 5 wt % KB) suspension as the anolyte and catholyte for the batteries.

The symmetric H2TPP RFBs were constructed by employing the H2TPP-based suspension electrolyte (200-H2TPP@KB-5) as the catholyte and anolyte and the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane as the separator (see Figure S17). The RFBs can be safely charged and discharged within the voltage window of −3.2 to 3.2 V at temperatures between 20 and −40 °C. Representative charge/discharge curves for constant current cycling at 1 mA cm−2 at different temperatures (20, 0, −20, −40 °C) are displayed in Figure 4 a–d. As the temperature decreased, the shapes of charge/discharge curves changed a little with enhanced polarization. EIS measurements were performed on the H2TPP-based suspension electrolyte (200-H2TPP@KB-5) at different temperatures (see Figure S18). The resistance of the suspension increased as the temperature decreased (see Table S6) owing to increased ionic resistance and retarded ion diffusion, which leads to enhanced cell polarization. However, the resistance of the suspension was only twice as high at −40 °C as at 20 °C, and remained at a low level of about 50 Ω. This value is generally acceptable for availability at low temperature.

The RFBs were also tested at different temperatures at other current densities (see Figures S19–S22). At 20 and 0 °C, pulse current densities of up to 5 mA cm−2 were achieved. Even at lower temperatures (−20 and −40 °C), the operational current densities still reached 2 mA cm−2. These low-temperature operational current densities are comparable to those reported for other organic RFBs at room temperature. This excellent electrochemical performance is ascribed to the high ionic conductivity of the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane, good electrical conductivity of the suspension electrolyte, and fast electrode kinetics of H2TPP.

The symmetric H2TPP RFBs had stable cycling performance at all temperatures between 20 and −40 °C (Figure 4 e; see also Figures S23–S26). At a theoretical volumetric capacity of 8.72 Ah L−1, the materials utilization was around 92, 83, 69, and 63 %, at 20, 0, −20, and −40 °C, respectively. The capacity retention exceeded 99.98 % per cycle. Coulombic efficiencies higher than 99 % were attained in almost all cases at the tested temperatures, which indicate good stability of redox-active H2TPP, good chemical reversibility of the redox reactions, and very low crossover of the redox-active species. We attribute this stable cycling and high coulombic efficiency to three aspects: the good stability and reversibility of H2TPP, the high ion selectivity of the Y-PVDF ion-selective membrane, and the symmetric structure of the H2TPP batteries. However, the voltage efficiency was less than 50 % in the RFBs as tested, which resulted in energy efficiency below 50 % (see Tables S7–S10). This result contrasts with the high voltage efficiencies expected from redox couples with electron-transfer rate constants as high as those observed for H2TPP. We attribute this low voltage efficiency to the high viscosity and ohmic resistance of the suspension electrolyte.

In summary, our study highlights the construction of symmetric RFBs that can be applied at low temperature for realizing electrochemical energy storage under cold-climate conditions. H2TPP with high stability, good conductivity, and fast electrochemical kinetics was employed as a bipolar redox-active charge-storage molecule in the RFBs. The redox couples for the anolyte and catholyte, [H2TPP]2−/H2TPP and H2TPP/[H2TPP]2+, offer a voltage of 2.83 V. The H2TPP-based suspension electrolyte, which is stable at low temperature, was prepared as the catholyte and anolyte by a simple and rapid method, and the theoretical energy density at cycling concentration is 24.68 Wh L−1. The Y-PVDF ion-selective separator with high ionic conductivity and a specific pore size (0.74 nm) endowed the H2TPP RFB with excellent cycling capability (capacity retention exceeding 99.98 % per cycle) at temperatures between 20 and −40 °C. Furthermore, the symmetric RFBs simplified the cell design and can be stored in the discharged state for a long time without electrolyte degradation. This study lays the foundation for the development of RFBs to function as large-scale electrochemical energy-storage devices in cold-climate areas.
