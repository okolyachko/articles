Differential pH as a method for increasing cell potential in organic aqueous flow batteries†

Amirreza Khataee , Kristina Wedege , Emil Dražević and Anders Bentien *
Department of Engineering, Aarhus University, Hangoevej 2, 8200 Aarhus, Denmark. E-mail: bentien@eng.au.dk

Received 7th June 2017 , Accepted 26th September 2017

First published on 9th October 2017

Abstract

In aqueous flow batteries, high battery cell potentials close to the electrochemical window of water are paramount for high cycle efficiency, power and energy density. The standard potential of many organic redox species has a strong dependence of pH and opens the possibility for increasing the cell potential by pH tuning. In the present work, a differential pH aqueous flow battery is demonstrated. The positive side is based on bromine operated at pH ∼ 2, while the negative side is based on anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonate disodium operated at pH ∼ 8. This results in a cell potential of 1.3 V which is a significant increase from 0.86 V when operated under acidic conditions on both sides. Furthermore, the differential pH of the flow battery is remarkably stable and shows no significant change over a period of at least 14 days (∼100 cycles). The high stability is attributed to electrochemical buffer effects of the redox solutions on each side.

Introduction

Further deployment of renewable electricity in the power grid largely depends on the availability of low cost electrical energy storage. Storage cost can be quantified by the levelized cost of electricity storage given by LCES[$ kW per h per cycle] = CC[$ kW h−1]/(N × η) where LCES expresses the cost of storing and discharging one kW h in a cycle, CC are the capital costs per kW h, N the number of cycles during lifetime and η is the cycle energy efficiency.1,2 Here, aqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs) are considered a promising technology with potential for significant reductions of the LCES because of the generally large number of lifetime cycles and relatively low capital costs.3–6 As a rule of thumb, the total capital costs of RFBs consist of 1/3 from redox active species, 1/3 from the stack and 1/3 from the balance-of-plant.7 Cost optimization is therefore a complex task that involves not only optimization of the redox pair solutions, but also the stack and the interplay between the redox pairs and the stack. An important point here is that, for a specific stack area resistance, the cycle efficiency of the stack and the energy density of the redox solution per se scales with the cell potential (Ecell), while the power density of the stack scales with Ecell2. For this reason, it is advantageous to identify redox pairs and solvents that optimise Ecell to the limits of e.g. the electrochemical window of water. Until recently only inorganic redox pairs had been investigated for RFBs. This includes iron–chromium,3,8 zinc–bromine,3 bromine–polysulfide3,9 and all vanadium.10,11 Briefly all these RFB technologies are challenged by certain issues, for some of them this is a long-term chemical stability while for the others this is energy efficiency. In vanadium RFBs, the main issue is relatively high cost of the redox pairs. To overcome some of the challenges, use of redox active organic compounds in aqueous flow batteries has been proposed. Some of the advantages of organic compounds are their low-cost potential and tuneability of redox potential and solubility.12–18 A wide range of organic molecules have been demonstrated in aqueous solvent RFBs, including quinones, tetramethylpiperidin (TEMPO) and viologen radicals, and phenazine/alloxazine based chemistries.6,14,15,17,19–22 Many of the organic redox species, e.g. phenazine/alloxazine and quinones, include protons in the redox reaction whereby the solvent pH has a large influence on the standard potential (E0) and thus Ecell.17,23 On the other hand, organic radicals, organometallic complexes, organometallic species and many inorganics (halogens) have pH-independent E0. This opens for the possibility of increasing Ecell of organic–organic or organic–metal/halogen RFBs by tuning the pH. Alternatively, the catholyte and anolyte can be operated at different pH, provided that the pH difference remains stable. Earlier a RFB with 9,10-anthraquinone-2,7-disulphonic acid (AQDS) on the negative side and Br−/Br2 on the positive side was operated at pH = 0 with a practical Ecell = 0.86 V.15,24 In the present study, we tested the feasibility of differential anolyte/catholyte pH in a RFB based on the same redox species with NH4+ as the main supporting electrolyte and a single Nafion membrane separator. Recently, another pH differential flow battery system has been demonstrated where iron and zinc redox solutions are operated at pH 0 and 14, respectively. In order to maintain the large pH difference, a cell design with two membranes and an intermediate electrolyte layer is used. Still, large concentrations of protons and hydroxyl ions are in contact with both membranes and will inevitably lead to neutralization in intermediate electrolyte layer with time.25 In contrast to this, we have constructed a simpler one-membrane, one-supporting electrolyte system by taking advantage of the steeply changing redox potential of an anthraquinone with modest concentrations differences of both protons and hydroxyl ions. In terms of voltage enhancement, this should be a more promising approach when considering long-term differential pH stabilization. In the present we demonstrate a differential pH RFB with a Ecell that is increased to approximately 1.3 V. The concept is outlined in Fig. 1, where it can be seen from the Pourbaix diagram of AQDS and Br−/Br2 that remains pH-independent while E0AQDS (unbuffered) decreases slightly with pH and with an abrupt change around pH = 2.4.23 The cell potential is surprisingly stable and remains constant over a testing period of at least 30 days.

Experimental section

Materials

All chemicals were commercially available and used without further purification. Anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid disodium salt (Na2AQDS) was supplied from Combi-Blocks (95% pure, used for battery tests) and TCI (97% pure, used for cyclic voltammetry and rotating disk electrode measurements). Ammonium bromide (NH4Br, ≥99.0% pure, Sigma-Aldrich) was used as both catholyte and supporting electrolyte. All electrolytes were prepared using Milli-Q water. Bromine (Br2, 99.99% pure, Sigma-Aldrich). Nafion-117 cs and carbon papers (electrodes) were from FuelCellStore and Fuel Cells etc., respectively. Hydrochloric acid 37% AnalaR NORMAPUR (VWR chemicals), and sodium hydroxide (≥98%, Sigma-Aldrich) were used for pH titration.

Solubility and conductivity

A GENESYS 10S UV-vis spectrophotometer and VISIONlite software was used for evaluating aqueous solubility of Na2AQDS in MilliQ water (18.2 MΩ cm−1). Nine 25 mL solutions of Na2AQDS with nominal concentrations of 0.1 M to 1.3 M were prepared in MilliQ water. All solutions were stirred either by hand for a few minutes in the case of low concentrations (<0.5 M) or with magnetic stirrer in the case of high concentrations (≥0.5 M). 10 μL of these solutions were then transferred to 2 mL MilliQ water to reach 200 times dilution and the absorbance of each sample was measured at a wavelength of 480 nm through a 1 cm polystyrol/polystyrene cuvette. UV-vis measurements were complemented by conductivity measurements of the non-diluted solutions using a conductivity probe (MU6100, VWR).

Cyclic voltammetry

Cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of Na2AQDS and Br2 solutions in NH4Br were recorded at room temperature in a three-electrode electrochemical cell consisting of a 3 mm diameter polished glassy carbon disk working electrode (CHI), a platinum-wire counter electrode and Ag/AgCl reference electrode with a CHI Instruments 660E potentiostat. The glassy carbon disk was polished with CHI instruments polishing pads and aluminum oxide powders with different particle sizes (1, 0.3 and 0.05 micron) each for 5 minutes on individual polishing pads. The solutions were purged with nitrogen for at least 10 minutes before each CV.

Rotating disk electrode voltammetry (RDE)

Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) studies of Na2AQDS solution in NH4Br and H2SO4 was carried out at room temperature using a rotating disk electrode system (RDE710, GAMRY instruments). LSV measurements were performed at constant potential sweep rate of 10 mV s−1 using a freshly polished 5 mm diameter glassy carbon disk electrode as a working (rotating) electrode, a platinum-wire electrode and Ag/AgCl reference electrode.

Polarisation curves and battery cycling tests

RFB tests were performed in a cell comprised of two graphite plates with a 25 cm2 interdigitated flow field. The channel width, depth and the width of landing is 2 mm, 1.18 mm and 1.6 mm, respectively. Six Toray carbon papers (thickness of 0.19 mm) were placed on each graphite plate (Fig. S1a in the ESI†), sealed using 0.65 mm Viton and 0.45 mm Teflon gaskets which faced the Nafion-117 cs membrane. Carbon papers were pre-treated by four hours immersion at 50 °C in a mixture of concentrated sulfuric and concentrated nitric acid in a volume ratio of 3:1. Two gold-plated copper plates were used as current collectors. The flow cell was assembled using two 0.7 cm thick stainless steel endplates screwed by six bolts. Solutions were circulated through the cell by two DDA (Grundfos) pumps. All experiments were carried out using a BTS-5V3A battery tester with a four-wire setup. During charge/discharge cycles, the anolyte and catholyte pH were measured at different state-of-charges using a pHenomenal MU 6100 L (VWR) pH electrode after appropriate calibration using pH 4, 7 and 10 buffers (Metrohm). The starting pH of the bromine/ammonium bromide solution was adjusted to 1.5 by addition of HBr, while the AQDS/ammonium bromide solution was used at its natural pH.

Results and discussion

Solubility of Na2AQDS

The solubility limit of Na2AQDS in MilliQ water was estimated from UV-vis and conductivity measurements. Fig. 2 shows the absorbance and conductivity versus the concentration. The solubility limit is estimated to be 1 M as both the absorbance and conductivity appears to saturate above this value. The maximum observable solubility of Na2AQDS in 2 M NH4Br was around 0.8 M in its uncharged state (quinone form) at room temperature. However, such a solution shows thixotropic behaviour and the practically usable concentration of Na2AQDS was found to be 0.5 M in 2 M NH4Br. In addition to the data shown here, the conductivity of 2 M NH4Br were measured to be 206 mS cm−1, compared to 506 mS cm−1 for 1 M H2SO4 (Section S2 in the ESI†).
Fig. 2 Conductivity and UV-vis absorbance (at 480 nm wavelength) of Na2AQDS in water versus concentration.

Reversibility and stability of bromine and Na2AQDS at selected pHs

Bromide ions are present in the form of dissolved NH4Br salt on the positive side of the battery and are oxidized to bromine according to reaction (1):
 
Br2 + 2e− ⇌ 2Br−, E0 = 1.08 VSHE(1)

CVs of 0.03 M bromine in 2 M NH4Br at pH = 2 was carried out and is displayed in Fig. 3 (solid orange curve), where the formal electrochemical potential (E0) of 1.11 VSHE is reproduced.26,27


Fig. 3 Cyclic voltammograms at 80 mV s−1 on a glassy carbon electrode in 2 M NH4Br containing 0.03 M of Na2AQDS at pH 8 (pink curve) and pH 0 (dashed pink curve) and cyclic voltammogram at 50 mV s−1 of 0.03 M bromine in 2 M NH4Br at pH 2 (orange curve).

Bromine in aqueous solution have different disproportionation pathways, such as the one in eqn (2), to form Br−, HBrO/BrO−, Br3− and BrO3− which generally results in aqueous solutions of acidic pH.27–29

 
Br2 + H2O ⇌ HBrO + H+ + Br−, K = 7.2 × 10−9(2)

In an alkaline environment, the disproportionations are much more evident:

 
Br2 + 2OH− ⇌ Br− + BrO− + H2O, K = 2 × 108(3)

However, as seen from eqn (2) and (3), Br2 is stabilised in acidic while being unstable in alkaline solutions. As bromine in this work is operated on the positive side at pH ∼ 2, we can estimate that the disproportionation products exist at a maximum concentration of 0.008 M in concentration (Section S3 in the ESI†). The effect of this on the battery cycling capacity and pH behaviour will be discussed later. Fig. 3 also shows the CVs of 0.03 M Na2AQDS in 2 M NH4Br at pH ∼ 8 and pH = 0. pH ∼ 8 is the naturally resulting from adding Na2AQDS and NH4Br to the solvent because of the basicity of the aqueous solution of Na2AQDS, the source of which will be explained in details later. From Fig. 3 it is seen that E0pH=0 = 0.21 VSHE and E0pH=8 = −0.19 VSHE which is consistent with previous results.15,17 Together with this indicates that Ecell can be increased from a theoretical 0.86 V for a RFB operated in acidic solvent to 1.3 V if operated with differential pH. The CVs of 0.05 M Na2AQDS in 0.2 M NH4Br solutions were also measured at a range of different pHs and the formal potentials from these experiments are shown in the Pourbaix diagram in Fig. 1. In the range up to approximately pH = 2.4, the potential decreases with 34 mV per pH unit, followed by an abrupt decrease down to ∼−0.17 VSHE after which only a slow decrease is observed. The Pourbaix diagram is markedly different from the one earlier reported for the acidic AQDS/bromine RFB,15 where it was measured in buffered solutions. At pH < 7, two protons are involved in the reaction while at 7 < pH < 11 and at pH > 11 only one or no protons are involved in the reactions, respectively.15,30 In the present investigation, the supporting electrolyte is NH4Br where the NH4+ deprotonation reaction has pKa = 9.25. This difference between the buffered and non-buffered Pourbaix diagrams has earlier been explained by local pH environment changes close to the electrode surface as the electrochemical reduction/oxidation of AQDS depletes/enriches the local environment with H+.30,31

Na2AQDS electrochemical reaction kinetics

The diffusion coefficient (D) and standard heterogeneous electron transfer rate constant (k0) of Na2AQDS were measured using rotating-disk electrode LSV. Briefly, the diffusion limited current produced by the reduction of electroactive species at the electrode surface, can be related to the rotation rate of the electrode using the Levich equation:32
 
iL = nFAD2/3ω1/2ν−1/6C(4)
where iL is the mass transport-limited Levich current, n is the number of electrons transferred (here 2 or 1.5 as discussed below), F is Faraday's constant equal to 96485C mol−1, A is the electrode area which is 1.96 × 10−5 m2, ω is the rotation rate, ν is the kinematic viscosity equal to 8.9 × 10−7 m2 s−1 and C is the bulk concentration of 1 mM. Fig. S4-III in the ESI† shows RDE test results for Na2AQDS at pH 8. The limiting currents were determined in the diffusion limited region for Na2AQDS at pH 8, and plotted versus ω1/2 in the Levich plot in Fig. S4-IIIb in the ESI.† The limiting current increases linearly with ω1/2, which suggests that there is no kinetic limitations in the electron transfer reaction. D of Na2AQDS was calculated from the slope of the Levich plot and is 7.5 × 10−6 cm2 s−1 for Na2AQDS (pH 8). This is higher than the value of 3.9 × 10−6 cm2 s−1 (Section S4 in the ESI†) at pH 0 and higher than other reported values for organic redox compounds used in aqueous flow batteries.6,14,19 We measured the number of electrons transferred during reduction and oxidation with ferricyanide as a reference (Fig. S4-IV in the ESI†) and it can be seen that only 1.44 electrons are being transferred per AQDS molecule. In a very recent study it has been measured that AQDS in acidic media at concentrations >10 mM receives and accepts 1.5 electrons per molecule due to carbonate-induced dimerization of the molecules. As such, CV tests are in agreement with the result that not two electrons are involved in the electrochemical reaction, and this is even observed at as low a concentration as 1 mM in the neutral NH4Br electrolyte.33 If these values for n are used in eqn (4), this results in values of D of 5.3 × 10−6 cm2 s−1 and 1.1 × 10−5 cm2 s−1 in acidic and mild alkaline solution, respectively. Here a significantly higher D is seen in mild alkaline which is also reflected in the higher limiting currents in 2 M NH4Br compared to 1 M H2SO4 (Fig. S4-V in the ESI†). The reason for the higher D is hypothesized to be that AQDS molecules are coupled in dimers and maybe also higher order species in acidic media, though decreased electron transfer is observed in the pH 8 tests.33 Koutecky–Levich analysis was applied to extract k0. Inverse currents i−1 were plotted versus ω−1/2 at six different Na2AQDS reduction overpotentials. Fig. S4-III(c) in the ESI† gives straight lines which is described by the Koutecky–Levich equation:32
 
(5)

Fig. S4-III(d) in the ESI† plots the overpotential versus log(ik) and from the slope (28.5 mV) of a linear fit, intercept (91.2) with the y-axis and the Butler–Volmer equation we find a transfer coefficient (α) of 0.426 and k0 = 7.1 × 10−2 cm s−1 for Na2AQDS in 2 M NH4Br and at room temperature which is around 10 times higher value than k0 = 7.2 × 10−3 cm s−1 in 1 M H2SO4.15 Peak separation analysis was performed additionally to confirm the RDE results regarding higher k0 at pH 8 than pH 0 (Fig. S4-VI in the ESI†). The kinetic constant is notably higher than for other organic and metallic redox couples.9,15,19 Thus, both D and k0 are improved at pH = 8 in 2 M NH4Br compared to that at low pH.

Buffer capacity of redox solutions

The naturally resulting pH of a 0.3 M aqueous bromine solution is 2.66 theoretically29 (measured to 2.69 experimentally in our case). The resulting pH of our 2 M NH4Br/0.3 M Br2 catholyte is approximately 2.8 i.e. slightly higher due to re-equilibration of the disproportionation reactions described in (Section S3 in the ESI†). This is also a consequence of NH4Br being a weak acid (pKa ∼ 9.3) as a pure 2 M aqueous NH4Br solution has a pH of 4.5. Before battery tests, the pH of the bromine electrolyte was decreased to 1.5 by addition of HBr, which from our experience provides a stable battery cycling pH of approximately 2.

With respect to Na2AQDS, it has recently been reported that CO2 binds to phenol groups in Na2AQDS, forming a CO2 adduct so a freshly prepared solution of Na2AQDS contains significant amounts of carbonate species causing the pH of such a solution to be slightly alkaline.33 Aqueous 0.05 M Na2AQDS without supporting electrolyte, has a pH of around 10.5 and when titrated with a 0.5 M HCl two neutralization steps are detected as shown in Fig. S5-I in the ESI† and corresponds to a total of 0.4 equivalents per Na2AQDS or 20% CO32− of the molar fraction because of the divalent charge of CO32−. When the solution is titrated back with 0.5 M NaOH, less than 0.1 equivalents could be titrated (<5% molar fraction) and is most likely because of CO2 evaporation when CO32− is acidified.33

When Na2AQDS is proton exchanged to H2AQDS followed by NaOH titration, about 1.7 equivalents per AQDS can be titrated (Fig. S5-II in the ESI†). The shape of the curve is a characteristic of a strong acid and corresponds to about 86% of the AQDS if there are two strong acid groups per AQDS. When titrated back with HCl, 0.33 equivalents are titrated (17% molar fraction) and is somewhat unexpected since it would be expected that most CO32− is evaporated of as CO2 in the acidic starting solution. It is however, possible that not all CO32− has been removed. Nonetheless, the main point here is that the solutions used for battery tests (0.5 M Na2AQDS in 2 M NH4Br) have a pH of around 8 that is determined (a) by the equilibrium between the basicity of CO32− bound in Na2AQDS and (b) the buffering ability of NH3/NH4+. Furthermore, such a solution has a relatively large base buffer capacity as can be seen from Fig. 4. In the case of the base titration the capacity is almost four equivalents per AQDS because of the 4:1 ratio between NH4Br and AQDS where the titration reaction is NH4+ + OH− → NH3 + H2O. In case of acid titration, there is only 0.4 AQDS equivalent of carbonate (CO32− + 2H+ → HCO3− + H+ → H2CO3) obtained in NH4Br solution.


Fig. 4 Titration of oxidised 0.5 M Na2AQDS in 2 M NH4Br with 1 M NaOH (left side) and 1 M HCl (right side).

Battery tests

Battery polarisation curves and battery cycling were performed with 100 mL of 0.5 M Na2AQDS in 2 M NH4Br on the negative side, while 500 mL 2 M NH4Br/0.3 M Br2 (pH adjusted to 1.5 in the beginning) was used on the positive side, whereby the battery capacity is limited by Na2AQDS. In order to minimize thixotropic effects, a flow rate of 180 mL min−1 was used on the negative side, while the flow rate on the positive side was maintained at 50 mL min−1.

Fig. 5a displays polarization curves measured at different states-of-charge (SOC). At high SOC, the curves are linear, while at 10% SOC linearity is observed only above −40 mA cm−2. From regressions of the data in the linear region a cell resistance of 0.14 Ω was found and corresponds to an area specific resistance of 3.5 Ω cm2. This is higher than the 0.323 Ω cm2 measured at 20 °C of the acidic AQDS/bromine RFB using an optimized cell.22,24 In the current work, there have been no attempts to optimize cell geometry, however it is an open question whether area resistance as low as 0.323 Ω cm2 can be reached. On one hand the higher D and k0 of AQDS at higher pH will improve the performance, on the other hand the lower conductivity of the NH4+ supporting electrolyte will decrease the performance. Here it is noted that conductivity of NH4Br at higher concentrations (>0.1 M) is about half that of e.g. HCl and H2SO4 (conductivity measurements are found in Section S2 in the ESI†).34 NH4+ is the main charge carrier through the Nafion membrane. Still, non-negligible amounts of Na+ ions (1 M) are also transported through the membrane during cycling. The osmotic pressure between the two sides is relatively small because of the similar ionic strengths on both sides. This is corroborated by the fact that during the course of the long-term experiments no significant change of volumes between the sides was observed. Any small osmotic pressure difference was probably levelled out by coupled diffusion of NH4+/Br− and Na+/Br− through the membrane.


Fig. 5 (a) Polarisation curves at three different states of charge (SOC). Inset shows Ecellversus SOC. (b) Charge/discharge capacity measurement and current efficiency versus cycle numbers. Inset shows charge/discharge cycles 1 to 5. (c) Voltage–capacity for selected cycles. (d) pH measured at random SOC (red and green data points) and Ecell at 50% SOC (blue data points) as function of cycle number. All measurements are with 0.5 M Na2AQDS in 2 M NH4Br (negative side) and 0.3 M Br2 in 2 M NH4Br (positive side) at 40 mA cm−2 using flow rate of 180 mL min−1 on the negative and 50 mL min−1 on the positive side.

The inset of Fig. 5a shows Ecell as a function of SOC and shows that a practical battery potential of about 1.3 V is obtained and is significantly higher than the 0.86 V for the acidic AQDS/bromine battery.15,22,24

The inset of Fig. 5b shows the voltage during charging–discharging of the first five cycles where a Nernstian-like behavior is seen, while Fig. 5c shows the voltage–capacity plots for some selected cycles. The charge–discharge capacities as function of the cycle number are shown in the main panel of Fig. 5b along with the current efficiency. The practical charging capacity is around 1.81 A h as average of all cycles and corresponds to 93% of the theoretical capacity which was calculated based on 1.44 electrons per Na2AQDS. For lower current densities the utilisation approaches 100% (Section S6 in the ESI†). With increasing cycle numbers, it is seen from Fig. 5b that the charge capacity decreases with a total of 10% (average 0.05% per cycle) over the course of 200 cycles. At low concentration tests, a capacity loss of 0.02% per cycle was observed (Section S7 in the ESI†). In these tests the C-rates are two times higher and the lower capacity loss could indicate that the loss is time dependent. In previously reported studies on the acidic AQDS/bromine RFB, capacity losses of 0.03% per cycle were observed at higher C-rates.15,24

Some of the capacity loss is related to AQDS leakage through the sealing gaskets. Coloration of the graphite block and gaskets was observed after the cell was disassembled and indicates electrolyte leakage routes on both the negative and positive side. Since the capacity is limited by Na2AQDS (0.05 mol) the total capacity loss of 10% corresponds to 2.1 g of Na2AQDS over 30 days and such large quantities have not been observed in the gaskets. At the same time using the previously reported permeability of 9 × 10−16 m2 s−1 of Na2AQDS in Nafion a negligibly small crossover of 7 × 10−8 moles/cycle is calculated.24 This is supported by no detection of Na2AQDS on bromine side using cyclic voltammetry measurements.

Together these observations question the chemical stability of Na2AQDS. Previously oxidized Na2AQDS in aqueous bromine/bromide solutions showed no decomposition after 48 h at 100 °C.15 However, it can be speculated that reduced Na2AQDS is less stable in the present case at pH = 8.

With respect to the current efficiency (Fig. 5b) it is about 95% with a slight tendency to decrease with cycling. The relatively low current efficiency is most likely a consequence of self-discharge through Br2 crossover, where it reacts with reduced Na2AQDS to form the discharged species. From bromine permeability values in Nafion an estimated Br2 crossover from 1.3% to 13% per cycle is found and agrees with the order of magnitude of the current efficiency of 95%.35–37

In addition to the battery performance tests, the pH values of both sides and Ecell at 50% SOC (Section S6 in the ESI†) were measured as function of the cycle number and are shown in Fig. 5d. The pH was measured at random SOC for the first 94 cycles (14 days) and it is seen that the pH remains remarkably stable at approximately 2 and 8.2 for the positive and negative side, respectively. Ecell is about 1.3 V and remains very stable with cycle numbers up to 200 (30 days).

To investigate the reactions during charge and discharge in more detail, another battery cycling test was made while measuring the pH on both sides every 30 min during the first two cycles. The result is shown in Fig. 6 and it is seen that the pH increases slightly during charging on the Na2AQDS side while it decreases upon discharge. The relatively small pH change during charging and discharging can be explained by the presence of the buffers NH4+/NH3 and H2CO3/HCO3−/CO32− that have pKa ∼ pH ∼ 6–10 and thereby accessible protons for the electrochemical AQDS reduction/oxidation without significant change of the pH value. If no buffers were present, the pH increase would be more dramatic during charging and vice versa during discharging. Hydrogen (E0H2O/H2·pH=8 = −0.47 V) and oxygen (E0H2O/O2·pH=2 = 1.11 V) evolution on negative and positive side, respectively, could be the underlying mechanism for the pH change during a cycle. However, considering that E0H2O/H2·pH=8 < E0AQDS/AQDSH−·pH=8, E0H2O/O2·pH=2 ∼ E0Br2/Br−·pH=2, that the pH change is reversible and that hydrogen/oxygen evolution have significant overpotentials on carbon electrodes38–40 it is unlikely that this side reaction contributes to the pH change.


Fig. 6 pH change (coloured circles) of the negative and positive side of the battery during charging and discharging versus time. pH values were measured every 30 min (30 min corresponds to 25% state of charge change at the given current density).

An interesting effect was observed in a continuation of the battery cycling in Fig. 6. After approximately 24 hours a significant drop in the pH to about 4 in the discharged state was observed on the Na2AQDS side together with the formation of a shoulder/decrease in the voltage curve (Fig. S9-I in the ESI†). This effect was not seen in main battery cycling experiments shown in Fig. 5 and was attributed to carbonates reacting into CO2 and release into the atmosphere due to the many openings of the system during the pH measurements. This was confirmed by bubbling CO2 into the negative side at fully charged state whereby the shoulder and the pH drop disappeared in the subsequent cycles (Fig. S9-II in the ESI†).

Furthermore, a reference experiment similar to the battery cycling in Fig. 5 was made, however, the difference being that no charging or discharging was done. Hereby the solutions were circulated in the discharged state all time during the experiment. The data are shown in Fig. S9-III (ESI†) where it is seen that after 24 hours the pH on the Na2AQDS side has been acidified to a value almost the same as that of the Br2/Br− side. The acidification is most likely due to crossover of Br2 to the Na2AQDS side where it disproportionates and consumes OH− or forms H+ (eqn (2) and (3)). However, coupled H+Br− diffusion across the membrane could also contribute. Nonetheless, the fact that the pH in the reference experiment rapidly equilibrates clearly shows that the stable differential pH during cycling is a dynamic process related to the battery cycling. It is likely that this is a combined effect of the buffers on the Na2AQDS side which ensures a relatively stable pH during cycling on a short time scale (hours) while there is a net transport of H+ to the Br2/Br− side during cycling that balances the crossover of Br2 (and/or H+Br−) on a long time scale (days). The origin of the net transport of H+ is not clear, however, net (or asymmetric) transport of solvents and ions through ion exchange membranes is not uncommon. i.e. in vanadium flow batteries there is a net flow of both water and vanadium from one side to the other during cycling.41 These transport processes are not easily described because it involves both osmotic and electro osmotic effects.42 This is complicated further by a high dependence of the transport coefficients in the membranes on the supporting electrolyte, that change during charge and discharges. Nonetheless, it is hypothesised that it could be related to the significantly higher mobility of H+ relative to Na+ or NH4+ in both solution and in the Nafion membrane.

Conclusions

High battery cell voltages that are close to the limits of the electrochemical window of water are essential for increasing the cycle efficiency, power and energy density of aqueous flow batteries. In this study, it is demonstrated the AQDS/bromine flow battery can be operated at differential pH, which increases Ecell to 1.3 V. This is significantly higher than the value of 0.86 V when operated under acidic conditions, while the utilization of the electroactive material is equally good. The differential pH is remarkably stable for at least 14 days (∼100 cycles) and Ecell, remains stable for at least 30 days (200 cycles). In the current case, the standard heterogeneous electron transfer rate constant and diffusion coefficient of Na2AQDS at neutral pH are improved compared to the ones in acidic, however, at the cost of lower NH4+ supporting electrolyte conductivity.
