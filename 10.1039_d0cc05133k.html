A stable organic dye catholyte for long-life aqueous flow batteries†

Hongbin Li , Hao Fan *, Mahalingam Ravivarma , Bo Hu , Yangyang Feng and Jiangxuan Song *
State Key Laboratory for Mechanical Behavior of Materials, Shaanxi International Research Center for Soft Matter, Xi’an Jiaotong University, Xi’an 710049, China. E-mail: songjx@xjtu.edu.cn; Fanh2018@xitu.edu.cn

Received 28th July 2020 , Accepted 22nd September 2020

First published on 22nd September 2020

Abstract

An organic dye, Basic blue 3 (BB3), was reported for the first time as a two-electron catholyte for aqueous redox flow batteries. The exceptional stability of BB3 enabled the full battery to achieve a high capacity retention of >99.991% per cycle during 1500 cycles.

Aqueous redox flow batteries (ARFBs) have attracted much attention as one of the most promising large-scale energy storage installations1–6 due to their independent energy/power deployments, low cost, long-term life, and high safety.7,8 Compared to inorganic metal active species such as vanadium9 and zinc10 in traditional ARFBs, organic redox-active materials, composed of C, H, O, N and some other Earth-abundant elements, are lower in price.11 Other attractive advantages of organic redox-active materials are the diversity and tailorability of their structure, which result in various benefits such as proper redox potential, fast kinetics, high stability and solubility obtained by researchers via molecule design engineering.12–15

To date, the organic materials utilized as catholytes for ARFBs are mainly focused on TEMPO,16–19 ferrocene20–22 and benzoquinone23,24 derivatives. However, they cannot meet the actual needs of ARFBs for different reasons. TEMPO and ferrocene derivatives have been exploited as catholytes in a neutral environment. Nevertheless, the low ionic conductivity of the anion exchange membrane in neutral electrolyte restricts the rate performance. Moreover, TEMPO derivatives easily cause irreversible side reactions of disproportionation and ring-opening, resulting in severe structural damages.25 Although benzoquinone has a high redox potential, its poor electrochemical stability hinders its application in long-life batteries.23,24 Therefore, there is an urgent need for organic catholyte materials with a high redox potential, attractive solubility and exceptional stability.

Benefitting from the π-conjugated structure, aza-substituted basic dyes such as phenazine-based organic molecules exhibit stable electrochemical reversibility.26–29 However, phenazine-based materials were just used in negative pole due to their low redox potentials. Some researchers have reported a stable phenothiazine-based catholyte with relatively high potential by introducing a sulfur atom to the core structure.29,30 These studies provide great enlightenment to the research of catholyte material. It inspires us that introducing heteroatom atoms such as oxygen family atoms to a stabilized π-conjugated structure is an effective way to obtain a new class of redox active materials with increased redox potential and high stability.

Herein, we reported a highly stable organic dye, Basic blue 3 (BB3), with an oxygen atom doped π-conjugated structure, as the catholyte for long-life ARFBs. In this work, BB3 was synthesised conveniently via an asymmetric cyclization reaction (Scheme S1 and Fig. S1, ESI†). Nitrogen-doped graphene (NG) was introduced to modify the electrode and efficiently reduce the peak separation (ΔE) from 308 to 63 mV in cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves. With acetic acid (HoAc) as the cosolvent, BB3 in H2SO4–HoAc aqueous solution reached a maximum solubility of 2.5 M, corresponding to the charge capacity of 134 Ah L−1. Paired with V2+/V3+ (vanadium) as the anolyte, the full battery of BB3/V with NG-modified electrode exhibited a high battery capacity retention of >99.991% per cycle or >99.94% per hour during 1500 cycles.

The working principle of the BB3/V ARFB is illustrated in Fig. 1. BB3 is one of the typical phenoxazinum derivatives, in which three heterocyclic frameworks provide a stable π-conjugated system, and the p-π electron donating conjugated effect of two nitrogen atoms makes a steady resonance structure.31 The heteroatom oxygen increases the electron affinity of the molecules, and thus improves the redox potentials to meet the catholyte demand.30 The redox reactions of BB3 take place with two electrons and two protons in one step to balance the charge with the extra positive charge located at the nitrogen atom of the side ammonium group.32 Compared to single electron transfer molecules, BB3 with two-electron transfers can efficiently improve the battery capacity.


Fig. 1 (a) Redox mechanism of BB3. (b) Schematic representation of the full battery of BB3 as the cathode paired with V2+/V3+as the anolyte.

High solubility of the redox active materials is desired to achieve high capacity of the full battery. As calculated using the capacity formula, provided in the ESI,† ARFBs with organic electrolyte require an equivalent electron concentration of at least 1.5 M to reach a competitive capacity of all-vanadium ARFBs (∼40 Ah L−1). In this work, BB3 receives a maximum solubility of 2.5 M in mixed solvent (H2O + HoAc volume ratio of 1:1, containing 3.5 M H2SO4) as measured using UV-vis analysis (Fig. S2, ESI†). Considering its ability to store two electrons per molecule, this solubility corresponds to a theoretical equivalent electron concentration of 5.0 M and a charge capacity of 134 Ah L−1. The high solubility can be attributed to the hydrophilic tertiary amines group at both side chains of BB3. In addition, HoAc has been demonstrated as an useful cosolvent,30 which can significantly increase the solubility of BB3. Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) analysis further proves that there is only some negligible change in the structure of BB3 in the mixed supporting solution (Fig. S3, ESI†).

CV curve was performed with a bare glass carbon (bare GC) electrode in an electrochemical workstation to investigate the electrochemical reversibility of BB3. As shown in Fig. S4 (ESI†), the redox process of BB3 is acidity dependent due to the protonation/deprotonation of BB3 involved in the redox reaction. The result indicates an apparent CV curve with a redox potential of 0.54 V vs. SHE in 3.5 M H2SO4 as the supporting solution. ΔE from the CV curve is a useful parameter to estimate the redox dynamics of active species.33 The CV curve of BB3 with a large ΔE reveals sluggish redox kinetics, which might lead to a decrease in energy efficiency by creating large over potentials in the full battery.33,34

In order to overcome the drawback of the sluggish kinetics, various nanomaterials have been used as catalysts in the redox process of the active species.35–37 In this work, we introduced NG as an electrocatalyst to modify the bare GC electrode, named GC@NG. The three-dimensional morphology of NG can supply more active sites for redox reaction (Fig. 2a).38 The high concentration of nitrogen atoms (6.5 at%, Fig. 2b and c) is in favour of superior catalytic activity for the BB3 redox reaction. As shown in Fig. 2d, the ΔE significantly reduces to 63 mV from 308 mV, indicating a remarkable catalytic effect. Previous works have shown that nanocarbon materials could effectively catalyze the redox process.39–41 However, in this work, no obvious improvement is obtained in the BB3 redox process on a pure graphene modified bare GC electrode (Fig. S5, ESI†). Therefore, it is inferred that the heteroatom nitrogen plays an important role in the redox process for BB3 on GC@NG.38


Fig. 2 (a) SEM image of the morphology of pure NG. (b) XPS spectrum and (c) N1s high resolution spectra of pure NG. (d) CV curves of 0.05 M BB3 in 3.5 M H2SO4 under 0.1 V s−1 on bare GC and GC@NG, respectively.

The dynamic parameters of BB3 during the redox process were measured using linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) on a rotating disk electrode device with the rotations from 400 to 2500 rpm. The LSV curves were obtained by sweeping from the positive to negative potentials on bare GC (Fig. S6, ESI†) and GC@NG (Fig. S7, ESI†) electrodes, respectively. For the bare GC electrode, typical sigmoidal LSV curves show one wave at ∼0.45 V (Fig. S6a, ESI†), indicating a one-step reaction in the reduction process of BB3. In contrast, the GC@NG electrode shows two waves at ∼0.40 and ∼0.52 V in its LSV curves. The low potential wave is rotation-dependent, representing the reduction process of BB3. The higher one is rotation-independent (Fig. S7a and b, ESI†), suggesting the adsorption process of BB3 on GC@NG, which is further confirmed using the LSV measurements from the reversed sweeping direction (Fig. S8, ESI†). The disappearance of the high potential wave at ∼0.52 V in the positive sweeping (Fig. S8, S7c and d, ESI†) could be attributed to the consumption of absorbed BB3. Clearly, the LSV measurement performed under the no adsorption condition is a better way to determine the diffusion coefficient (D0) and redox constant rate (k0) of redox materials. According to the Levich equation,33 the D0 of BB3 in the bulk solution is 1.1 × 10−7 cm2 s−1. The redox constant rate k0 of BB3 on GC@NG calculated using the Koutecky–Levich equation is 2.87 × 10−3 cm s−1, which is about 1.5 times greater than that of the bare GC electrode (1.95 × 10−3 cm s−1), revealing that NG effectively facilitates the BB3 redox reaction.

By pairing with V2+/V3+ as the anolyte, two BB3/V ARFBs were constructed with 0.05 M BB3 in 3.5 M H2SO4 as the catholyte using bare carbon paper (bare CP) and NG modified CP (CP@NG) as the working electrodes, respectively. Compared with the bare CP based battery, the CP@NG based battery displays lower overpotentials and higher battery capacity, even at high current densities (Fig. 3a and b). Since the resistances of the two batteries are comparable (Fig. S9, ESI†), the performance difference can be attributed to the NG catalyst. The effect of NG on performance is also apparent in the rate capability test (Fig. 3c). The coulombic efficiencies (CE) of the two batteries are over 99%. The battery with CP@NG exhibits a much better energy efficiency (EE) than that using bare CP (Fig. 3d). For example, the CP@NG based battery delivers ∼70% EE under 60 mA cm−2 charge and discharge current density, much higher than that (∼40%) of the bare CP based battery. The long-time cycling stability of the 0.05 M BB3/V ARFBs were conducted at 40 mA cm−2. The CP@NG based battery exhibits a weak declination in capacity during 1500 cycle testing (Fig. 3e). The variation tendency is similar to the battery using bare CP in limited 100 cycles (Fig. S10, ESI†). Therefore, the introduced NG is independent of the battery lifetime. The CP@NG based battery displays a high capacity retention of >99.991% per cycle or >99.94% per hour during 1500 cycles, which is outstanding among the reported catholytes for ARFBs (Table S1, ESI†).

(e) Charge and discharge capacity and CE of the BB3/V ARFB with 0.05 M BB3 using CP@NG as the cathode electrode at 40 mA cm−2 for 1500 cycles.

The average CE is over 99%, suggesting that the side reaction was negligible and the membrane crossover of the redox species during cycling was at a low rate.42 CV curve of the post-cycled catholyte shows no obvious peak current loss and redox potential shift compared to the initial one (Fig. S11, ESI†). In addition, the post-mortem 1H NMR spectrum exhibits a negligible slight chemical shift of the specific proton, compared to the initial structure (Fig. S12, ESI†). All the experiment results and analyses prove that the highly soluble BB3 possesses an excellent electrochemical stability for ARFB application. The high stability is mainly attributed to the π-conjugated system provided by the three heterocyclic frameworks of the BB3 core structure. Corresponding proportion capacities are exhibited when the catholyte concentration increases from 0.05 M to 0.5 M and 1.6 M in the full battery test, respectively (Fig. S13 and S14, ESI†). It indicates that the electrochemical process is independent of the solution concentration.

In summary, we have reported, for the first time, phenoxazinium derivative BB3 as a promising catholyte for ARFBs. The BB3 exhibits a two-electron, two-proton redox reaction in one step with a redox potential of 0.54 V vs. SHE in 3.5 M H2SO4. The BB3 has a solubility as high as 2.5 M in mixed acid solution, corresponding to the theoretical charge capacity of 134 Ah L−1. The exceptional stability of BB3 enables the full BB3/V battery with CP@NG electrode to achieve a high capacity retention of >99.991% per cycle or >99.94% per hour during 1500 cycles, as well as higher energy efficiency and higher battery capacity than those of the cell using bare CP. This work, in which phenoxazinium derivative is utilized as a redox-active material, will widen the road for the development of organic catholyte materials for high performance ARFBs.
