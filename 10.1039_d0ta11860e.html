New highly soluble triarylamine-based materials as promising catholytes for redox flow batteries†

Elena I. Romadina *ab, Ivan A. Volodin a, Keith J. Stevenson a and Pavel A. Troshin b

Abstract

A series of arylamines bearing oligoethylene glycol ether solubilizing moieties were designed and comprehensively evaluated as promising catholyte materials for non-aqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs). The triphenylamine core maintains the chemical stability of the radical cation, the ethylene glycol chains enhance the solubility up to complete miscibility with organic solvents, and the electron-withdrawing bromine substituents increase the redox potential of the compounds up to 0.61 V vs. Ag/AgNO3. The best material showed 99% coulombic efficiency in combination with good stability in over 50 charge–discharge cycles in laboratory RFB cells. The designed triarylamine-based catholyte materials appear promising for the development of next-generation high-voltage and high-capacity RFBs.

The constantly growing demand for sustainable and renewable energy sources requires the development of next-generation technologies for energy storage.1 Redox flow batteries (RFBs) deserve special attention due to their excellent scalability, flexible design capabilities, long lifetime, and safety.2 However, they still exhibit the lowest specific capacity among the electrochemical energy storage technologies,3 which hinders their wide-scale commercialization. The specific capacity of RFBs primarily depends on the concentration of active substances. Although certain simple inorganic salts and metal complexes demonstrate high solubilities in water, some novel organic materials which demonstrate comparable or even higher solubilities in both aqueous and non-aqueous systems were recently reported.4–8 Thus, the application of organic solvents instead of aqueous solutions enables a wide electrochemical stability window of the electrolyte (e.g., up to 5 V for MeCN9), which makes possible the fabrication of RFBs with high voltages and hence substantially increased energy densities.

Currently, several families of organic compounds have been explored as promising redox-active species for non-aqueous RFBs. Among the anode materials for RFBs, the anolytes, there are molecules that meet all the requirements for application in RFBs: high solubility, completely reversible redox process at low potentials (ca. −2 V vs. Ag/AgNO3), and high stability under charge–discharge cycling conditions.10 In contrast, the diversity of reported catholytes is poorer. The vast majority of known catholyte species exhibited low potentials of 0–0.5 V vs. Ag/AgNO3, which are not suitable for the fabrication of high-voltage RFBs,11–13 whereas others possess higher potentials (up to 1.1 V vs. Ag/AgNO3), but low solubilities in organic solvents.14–17

Herein, we present rationally designed triarylamine derivatives as promising catholyte materials for RFBs. It should be emphasized that triarylamine-based polymers were widely explored as cathode materials for Li-ion batteries,18,19 while their soluble analogs with low molecular weights remain practically uninvestigated in RFBs. Most likely, this family of compounds was overlooked since triphenylamine is not appropriate for application in RFBs due to its low solubility in polar organic solvents (77 mM in MeCN, Table S1, ESI†) and strong tendency to undergo oxidative polymerization on the electrode surface.20 Substitution of protons at the para-positions in phenyl rings of PTA increases reversibility of the redox process and stability of the intermediate radical cation species. Hence, tris(4-bromophenyl)amine showed sufficient electrochemical stability to be evaluated as the catholyte material,21 whereas an extremely low solubility of ∼13 mM (Table S1, ESI†) strongly limits its practical application potential.

However, it is known that the solubility of organic molecules in polar solvents such as MeCN can be increased via the introduction of oligomeric ethylene glycol ether substituents into their structure.12,22 Therefore, we have focused on the design of new triarylamine-based compounds incorporating ethylene glycol ether moieties and bromine substituents as promising catholyte materials for RFB applications (Scheme 1).


Scheme 1 Synthesis of the ethylene glycol-substituted triarylamines M1–M7.

All the designed compounds were from cheap commercially available precursors (see Scheme 1 and the ESI† for details). 1-Bromo-4-(2-(2-methoxyethoxy)ethoxy)benzene was introduced into the Buchwald–Hartwig reaction with different amines to give compounds M1, M2, M5, and M7 in 77–85% yields. After that, M3, M4, and M6 were prepared via bromination by N-bromosuccinimide (NBS) of compounds M1, M2, and M5, respectively (Scheme 1). All the obtained triphenylamine-based products (M1–M4) were viscous liquids at ambient temperature (25 °C) and exhibit complete miscibility with MeCN, whereas the tetraphenylbenzene-1,4-diamine-based adducts (M5–M7) are solids with high (>2.2 M) but still limited solubility in MeCN (Table 1).

The electrochemical properties of the synthesized compounds were estimated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) vs. Ag/AgNO3 (reference electrode was calibrated by Fc/Fc+, Fig. S3, ESI†). Compounds have demonstrated one (M1–M4) or two (M5–M7) quasi-reversible oxidation waves with peak splitting being in the range of 0.14–0.23 V (Table 1 and Fig. S4, ESI†). Continuous CV testing for 100 cycles revealed the poor electrochemical stability of compounds M1 (Fig. 1a) and M5 (Fig. S4e, ESI†). The decrease in redox peak currents and increase in the peak potential splitting suggest oxidative polymerization of these compounds on the working electrode (WE) due to the presence of reactive non-substituted para-positions of phenyl rings in these compounds. The introduction of bromine substituents resulted in the largely improved electrochemical stability of the compounds and an increase in their redox potentials. Fig. 1 illustrates the effect of H to Br substitution for the M1 and M3 pair of compounds.


Fig. 1 Continuous voltammetric cycling of 3 mM (a) M1 and (b) M3 in 0.1 M TBABF4/MeCN solution.

Compounds M2–M4, M6, and M7 were found to be stable upon their continuous electrochemical cycling. Nevertheless, the presence of the non-substituted para-position at the phenyl ring of M2 makes possible the dimerization of the molecule with longer galvanostatic cycling, resulting in soluble dimer products.

The highest redox potentials were observed for compounds M3 and M7 (0.57 and 0.61 V, respectively), which could be attributed to the presence of multiple bromine substituents in their structures.

To estimate the stability of triarylamine-based compounds at low potentials, cyclic voltammograms were recorded for M3, M4, M6, and M7 within the range of −2.0 V to +1.0 V vs. Ag/AgNO3. In the CV curves, no signs of decomposition or side electrochemical processes (Fig. S5, ESI†) were observed, enabling the possibility of the fabrication of the high-voltage (E ∼ 2.6 V) RFB cells.

We also explored the effect of moisture in the solvent on the electrochemical stability of the designed materials. Anhydrous organic solvents are costly to produce and maintain, so the design of catholyte and anolyte compounds, whose redox process is tolerant to the presence of water in the solvent, can be one of the key approaches to reduce the price of the whole RFB device. The addition of amounts of water of up to 8 vol% did not affect the reversibility of electrochemical oxidation of M3 (Fig. S6, ESI†), whereas higher concentrations of water decreased the solubility and induced precipitation of this compound. Thus, the presence of moisture in the solvent causes no significant effects on the electrochemical behavior, which consequently eliminates requirements for using costly anhydrous solvents.

Diffusion coefficients were estimated for the electrochemically stable substances M2–M4, M6, and M7 using the rotating disk electrode (RDE) measurements.

The resulting linear sweep voltammetry curves have exhibited two types of behaviors: simple one-electron reduction for compounds M2 (Fig. S5a and b, ESI†), M3 (Fig. S5c and d, ESI†) and M4 (Fig. S5e and f, ESI†), and simple two-electron reduction for M6 (Fig. S5g and h, ESI†) and M7 (Fig. S5i and j, ESI†). For the purpose of comparison, we measured under the same conditions, the diffusion coefficient of TEMPOL+ (D = 5.93 × 10−6 cm2 s−1) as a reference compound (Fig. S5k and l, ESI†). It was found that the diffusion of M2, M3, and M4 is faster as compared to that of the bulkier M6 and M7, whereas all studied arylamines showed comparable or higher diffusion coefficients than TEMPOL+ (Table 1).

During the next stage of this study, we investigated triarylamine-based catholytes in the assembled laboratory RFB cells (Fig. 2a, see the ESI† for specification of the cell parameters). Butylviologen diperchlorate (V1) was selected as the redox-active anolyte component (Fig. S2, ESI†). The reduction potential of V1 was found to be −0.75 V vs. Ag/AgNO3 (Fig. S4h, ESI†), leading to a potential difference of 0.89–1.3 V vs. triarylamines (Fig. 2b).


Fig. 2 (a) Schematic diagram of the assembled RFB device; (b) CV curves for a mixture containing V1 (10 mM) as the anolyte and M3 (10 mM) as the catholyte in 0.1 M TBABF4/MeCN at a scan rate of 200 mV s−1; (c) dependence of the discharge capacity of the cycle number for 10 mM redox-flow cells containing M3 and V1 during 50 cycles for various background electrolytes.

In order to reveal the optimal electrolyte composition, we explored systematically, the series of RFB devices using the M3/V1 redox couple and various supporting salts: tetrabutylammonium tetrafluoroborate (TBABF4), tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate (TBAPF6), NaClO4, LiClO4, and LiPF6. Fig. 2c summarizes the observed charge–discharge cycling behaviour of all the studied systems, while all the cycling profiles are presented in Fig. S8 (ESI†). It is clear that the background electrolyte salt has a strong impact on the operational stability of RFBs. Using LiPF6 leads to the fastest capacity decay, probably due to the chemical activity of this salt.23–25 To evaluate the processes responsible for capacity decay, we measured the pH values of all electrolyte solutions (Table S2, ESI†). A decrease of the pH value of the LiPF6-based electrolyte was observed, which is apparently associated with the hydrolysis of Li+ or PF6− ions induced by the presence of residual amounts of water. The generated acids could interact with triarylamines, yielding quaternary ammonium bases, leading to the fast capacity decay of the RFBs. In contrast, the TBABF4 and NaClO4 based electrolytes are more sustainable towards hydrolysis and their utilization in RFBs leads to the stable cycling behavior. Therefore, these two salts were selected for the further systematic study of different arylamine-based catholytes.

To assess the charge–discharge cycling stability of RFBs using compounds M3, M4, M6, and M7 as catholytes, another series of the experiments was carried out (Fig. S8–S10, ESI†). Fig. 3 shows the maximal obtained discharge capacities, discharge capacities after 50 cycles, and maximal coulombic efficiencies (CEs) observed in these experiments. Higher capacities were delivered by the compounds based on triphenylamines M3 and M4 as compared to their tetraphenylbenzene-1,4-diamine analogs M6 and M7. The relatively low charge and discharge capacities of RFBs based on M6/V1 and M7/V1 redox couples (Fig. S10, ESI†) might be explained by the hindered transfer of the second electron, resulting in the limitation of the practical specific capacity to ∼50% of its theoretical value. Thus, the compounds based on triphenylamine (M3, M4) can be considered as more promising since they exhibit higher capacities due to the simplicity of single-electron transfer and good stability of the generated radical cation provided by the appropriate functionalization of the TPA core.

Comparing the effects of the supporting electrolyte salts on the RFB performance, it can be concluded that the application of TBABF4 results in higher capacities but lower CE as compared to NaClO4. Indeed, using NaClO4 provided an average CE of ∼97%, whereas TBABF4 led to significantly lower efficiencies ranging from 81 to 95%. To the best of our knowledge, a systematic study on the effect of electrolyte composition on the CE of organic RFBs has not been reported in the literature so far. We strongly believe that this effect could be related to the ability of the anions of the supporting salt to cross the membrane; however, we found out that BF4− and ClO4− have similar values of ionic radius (2.25 Å for ClO4− and 2.05 Å for BF4),26 so the ion size could not be the key parameter responsible for the CE value. The second parameter, which could impact the cell CE, is the dissociation of the supporting electrolyte in the MeCN. To estimate this parameter, we measured both solutions' resistance (Fig. S13, ESI†). The resistance of the NaClO4 solution was found to be 6 Ω lower (∼71 Ω for 0.1 M NaClO4 in MeCN; ∼76 Ω for 0.1 M TBABF4 in MeCN), indicating the better dissociation of NaClO4 in the solution. Nevertheless, replacing the polymeric Neosepta AHA membrane with a composite or a fully ceramic membrane will increase the efficiency of RFBs regardless of the supporting electrolyte.

Fig. 3c shows the electrochemical performance of the symmetric RFB device using the M3/V1 redox couple. The RFB cell showed a coulombic efficiency of ∼65% in the first cycle, which was improved to >80% in the 50th cycle. The initial charge capacity was equal to the theoretical value. At the same time, the discharge capacity was ∼170 mA h L−1 which corresponds to 63% consumption of the active materials. The flow cell demonstrated a discharge capacity increase to ∼192 mA h L−1 during the first 12 cycles with further decrease to 172 mA h L−1 in the 50th cycle.

The application of different charge–discharge currents to the M3/V1 battery demonstrated the ion-conductivity limit of the Neosepta AHA membrane (Fig. 3d and S12, ESI†). Therefore, the shift from the charge–discharge currents to 3 mA does not lead to a decrease in the battery capacity; a further current increase resulted in significant capacity decay and high crossover value (what could be seen from the increase of the coulombic efficiency to more than 100% for 5 mA and 6 mA currents).

For the same electrochemical system, the self-discharge experiment was performed, confirming strong impact of the crossover (Fig. S11, ESI†). In the absence of the external factors, a 2 mV h−1 potential recession was observed.

However, the performed study featured compound M3 as the most promising candidate for further studies due to the optimal combination of properties such as miscibility with MeCN, the highest oxidation potential among all the evaluated compounds, its ability to deliver high practical capacities, and good operational stability upon charge–discharge cycling in RFB tests.

To summarize, we introduced here for the first time, the highly soluble redox-active triarylamine-based compounds as promising catholytes for all-organic redox flow batteries. Rational molecular design allowed us to enable reversible and stable electrochemical behaviour for the target compounds, tune their redox potentials within the range of 0.03–0.6 V vs. Ag/AgNO3 and provide high diffusion coefficients exceeding that of the TEMPO+ benchmark molecule. We also found important molecular structure–electrochemical performance relationships, which can be considered as important guidelines for future research in this direction. The designed compounds demonstrated high solubility in acetonitrile ranging from >2.2 M to complete miscibility, a wide electrochemical stability window and good operational stability upon the charge–discharge process. Such a result provides an opportunity to create high capacity and high-performance RFBs, by focusing on the improvement of the battery design (optimum electrode materials, flow flame construction, electrolyte flow rate, etc.), along with separator, solvent, and anolyte selection.
