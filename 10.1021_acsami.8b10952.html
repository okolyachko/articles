    Abstract

    Alloxazine and ferrocyanide are suggested as the redox couple for an aqueous organic redox flow battery (AORFB). Alloxazine is further modified by carboxylic acid (COOH) groups (alloxazine-COOH) to increase the aqueous solubility and to pursue a desirable shift in the redox potential. For obtaining a better AORFB performance, the overall redox reactivity of AORFB should be improved by the enhancement of the rate-determining reaction of the redox couple. A carboxylic acid-doped carbon nanotube (CA-CNT) catalyst is considered for increasing the reactivity. The utilization of CA-CNT allows for the induction of a better redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH because of the role of COOH within alloxazine-COOH as a proton donor, the fortified hydrophilic attribute of alloxazine-COOH, and the increased number of active sites. With the assistance of these attributes, the mass transfer of aqueous alloxazine-COOH molecules can be promoted. However, CA-CNT does not have an effect on the increase of the redox reactivity of ferrocyanide because the redox reaction is not affected by the same influence of protons that the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH is affected by. Such a behavior is proven by measuring the electron transfer rate constant and diffusivity. With regard to AORFB full cell testing, when CA-CNT is used as a catalyst for the negative electrode, the performance of the AORFB increases. Specifically, the charge–discharge overpotential and infrared drop potential are improved. As a result, the voltage efficiency affected by the potentials increases to 64%. Furthermore, the discharging capacity reaches 26.7 A h·L–1, and the state of charge attains 83% even after 30 cycles.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Because of the growing concern about the climate change from the excessive use of fossil fuels and its effects on the environment, development toward renewable energy sources—such as wind and solar energies—has been in urgent demand. (1,2) However, renewable energy sources, characteristically, thus far have had limitations because of the unpredictable amount of energy harvested. (3,4) It is important to mitigate these sorts of problems for the progression of renewable energy. One way to address the uncertainty of harvest is to develop large-scale energy storage systems (ESS) which can store surplus energy. An example of a potential ESS is a redox flow battery (RFB), which uses metal-based active species, such as vanadium, iron, and chromium, and has received strong attention. It is known that the metal-based active species can offer outstanding benefits such as superior electron conductivity, good stability under acidic electrolytes, and high battery performance. Additionally, vanadium redox flow batteries (VRFBs) can reduce the crossover of active species and show a long life span because of the use of the same active material in anolyte and catholyte. (5−9)
    A VRFB, however, has limitations with regard to cost and operating temperatures. In terms of cost, vanadium is very expensive because it is a rare metal. For example, its cost in 2017 was $ 140 per kW h, which was more expensive than the other precious metals, such as Pd, Ag, and and so forth. (10) In addition, its available temperature range is very narrow (10–40 °C). If the operating temperature goes beyond this range, the V5+ ion is precipitated, resulting in a rapid degradation in the performance of VRFB. (11) These factors negatively affect the balanc–of-plant (BOP) cost, which is required to be finely controlled. For example, precision designing for heat exchangers may be needed, and an expensive BOP cost may become a big burden for easy market entry.
    Thus, there have been many attempts to alleviate the problems of a VRFB or replace it with a new type of flow battery, altogether. In this prospect, RFBs using organic materials dissolved in aqueous state as the active species are proposed as an alternative. For this purpose, different kinds of organic active species—such as quinones, aza aromatics (like alloxazine and quinoxaline), (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl, violigen, and polythiophene have been considered. (12−19) RFBs consisting of organic active species can provide various benefits over conventional VRFBs. Organic active species are relatively cheaper than their metal counterparts and can easily control the standard redox potential. They are usually considered less toxic and are also soluble under strong acidic/basic electrolyte conditions. (20) Furthermore, the operating temperature range is not as crucial as it is for VRFBs; so, unlike vanadium ions, some of the organic active species will not precipitate even at high temperatures. This advantage of acceptable temperature range enables the reduction in cost for BOP construction and the utilization of waste heat emitted from geothermal and combined heat and power. (21)
    Although nonaqueous organic RFBs are desirable for their high energy density because of high solubility in organic solvents and their wide potential window for operation, nonaqueous electrolytes have disadvantages such as lower ionic conductivity, limited current density, and higher cost compared with the aqueous electrolytes. (22−26) Instead, when the organic species are used in an aqueous electrolyte, the problems of nonaqueous organic RFBs can be significantly alleviated, and benefits such as high ionic conductivity, low cost, and high stability can be incorporated. Because of such merits, some research groups have studied RFBs using aqueous organic active species. For instance, Narayanan et al. introduced quinone-based materials as the active materials for RFBs because quinone could lead to a fast redox reaction because of its two-electron redox reaction mechanism, producing a high Coulombic efficiency (>95%). However, the open-circuit voltage (OCV) (0.7 V) was lower than that of VRFB (1.26 V). In addition, its solubility in aqueous solution was low. (27) In another case, Liu et al. used ferrocene modified by N-alkylation as the active species of the positive electrode. Although, initially, ferrocene has low solubility in aqueous solutions, the solubility of the modified ferrocene significantly increased up to 4.0 M because of N-alkylation. When the modified ferrocene combined with methyl viologen was used as the redox couple, its cell potential reached 1.06 V. However, because of the relatively complex N-alkylation process and the use of the toxic methyl viologen, this process needs improvement. (28) In addition, Lin et al. reported an alkaline quinone flow battery using 2,6-dihydroxyanthraquinone and ferrocyanide/ferricyanide as the active species of the negative and positive electrodes. Because ferrocyanide/ferricyanide has beneficial properties such as low toxicity and nonvolatility, cost efficiency, nontoxicity, nonflammability, and safety, fabrication of aqueous organic RFB (AORFB) using this is promising. According to the results, the current efficiency (CE) was above 99%, and the energy efficiency (EE) was 84% at 100 mA·cm–2. (29)
    Aziz et al. (15) first introduced alloxazine and ferrocyanide as the negative and positive active species for AORFB. Because alloxazine does not solubilize well in water, it was modified by adding functional groups like COOH and OH to increase the solubility and shift the standard redox potential to a more desirable direction. Among them, in terms of aqueous solubility, alloxazine modified by COOH (alloxazine-COOH) was the best as the active species for the negative electrode. Additionally, its synthetic procedure was simple. In this prospect, we conclude that the redox couple—alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide—is one of the most advanced redox couples for AORFB.
    In this study, alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide dissolved in KOH solution were also used as the active materials for RFB research. The redox reactions of the redox couple are shown in Scheme 1.
    In addition, we newly suggest the utilization of a catalyst to enhance the redox reactivity of the rate-determining reaction (RDR) of the couple because of the lack of previously reported efforts. For this, we initially identify the RDR out of the alloxazine-COOH- and ferrocyanide-related redox reactions and then increase the reaction rate of the RDR by adopting a proper catalyst and evaluate how the catalyst progresses the reaction rate of RDR and the performance of AORFB. Carboxylic acid-doped carbon nanotube (CA-CNT) is selected as the catalyst. The CA-CNT consists of a carbon-based material (CNT) and an oxygen functional group (carboxylic acid), which has good chemical stability against an alkaline electrolyte and can be simply and easily applied to the electrode. In addition, the oxygen functional group of COOH can enhance the hydrophilicity and catalytic activity of the electrode. (30,31)

    2.1. Synthesis of Alloxazine and Alloxazine-COOH

    CNT (multiwalled, >98% carbon basis) and CA-CNT (multiwalled, carboxylic acid-functionalized, >8% carboxylic acid-functionalized) powders that were utilized as catalysts were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich.
    For synthesizing alloxazine, o-phenlyenediamine and alloxan monohydrate were mixed with boric acid and acetic acid that were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. o-Phenylenediamine (20 mmol) was initially dissolved into 170 mL of acetic acid with stirring, and 22 mmol of boric acid and 21 mmol of alloxan monohydrate were further added. (32) The entire solution was then stirred. After this process, the precipitated product was filtered, removed, and washed with deionized water. For synthesizing alloxazine-COOH, 3,4-diaminobenzoic acid (purchased from Alfa Aesar) that was carboxylic acid-functionalized o-phenylenediamine interacted with alloxan monohydrate. The synthetic scheme of alloxazine and alloxazine-COOH is described in Figure S1. The yield obtained during the synthesis of alloxazine and alloxazine-COOH was 90%.
    The synthesizing steps and conditions of alloxazine-COOH were the same as those of alloxazine. In addition, to determine the optimal mixing time for synthesizing the two materials (alloxazine and alloxazine-COOH), the stirring time was controlled. As for the stirring time, various timescales from 1 min to 6 h were designed and used.
    Furthermore, the following two different synthetic methods—identified as one-step and two-step methods—were used. In the one-step method, all the materials (alloxan monohydrate, 3,4-diaminobenzoic acid, boric acid, and acetic acid) were mixed simultaneously. In contrast, in the two-step method, 3,4-diaminobenzoic acid and acetic acid were initially mixed, and then boric acid and alloxan monohydrate were added to the mixed solution with stirring for 3 h. The mixing time effect of the catalysts was also evaluated. Here, mixing indicates how long alloxan monohydrate was held and reacted with 3,4-diaminobenzoic acid. Durations of 2 and 24 h were chosen as the mixing times.

    2.2. Materials Characterizations

    To identify whether the carboxylic acid functional groups were well attached to alloxazine, the alloxazine sample consisting of carboxylic acid functional groups was analyzed by proton nuclear magnetic resonance (1H NMR) spectroscopy without further purification. The 1H NMR spectra were recorded using a Varian Mercury 700 (700 MHz) NMR spectrometer at 25 °C. For the measurement, dimethyl sulfoxide was used as a solvent to dissolve the sample.
    To measure the solubility of all of the active species that were dissolved into 1 M KOH solution, UV–vis spectrophotometry was used. Initially, excessive quantities of the active species (4 g each of alloxazine and alloxazine-COOH) were dissolved in 4 mL of 1 M KOH. After this, the top of the unprecipitated solution was collected and the UV–vis spectrum was measured by UV–vis spectrophotometry. A correlation of concentration versus absorbance peak was obtained by measuring the absorbance peak from the known concentration of each active species, and five absorbance peaks that were extracted from different concentrations were used to form a linear calibration curve. The maximum solubility of each solution dissolved in 1 M KOH was then estimated by the linear calibration curve.

    2.3. Electrochemical Measurements

    Cyclic voltammogram (CV) measurements were performed in the potential ranges of −1.2 to −0.4 V versus Ag/AgCl for the redox reaction of alloxazine-COOH and 0.2–0.8 V versus Ag/AgCl for that of ferrocyanide which was obtained from potassium ferrocyanide decahydrate purchased from Sigma-Aldrich using CHI 720D (CH Instruments, USA). For half-cell tests, Pt wire and Ag/AgCl were considered as the counter and reference electrodes, respectively, and a glass carbon disk (GCE) was used as the working electrode. As electrolytes, 0.1 M of both alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide was dissolved in 1.0 M KOH. In detailed explanation, 10 mL of each catalyst ink, which was prepared by mixing 10 mg of catalyst powder with isopropanol, distilled water, and 5% Nafion 117 solution, was loaded onto the GCE. To compare the redox reactivity of alloxazine and alloxazine-COOH, the CV curves were measured at a scan rate of 10 and 100 mV·s–1. These were also used to determine the (i) optimal condition of alloxazine-COOH, (ii) cell voltage obtained by the combination of alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide, (iii) reaction kinetics like diffusivity and electron transfer rate constant of alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide, and (iv) effect of CA-CNT catalyst on the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH.

    2.4. AORFB Single Cell Measurements

    A scheme and an actual cell kit of the AORFB single cell are depicted in Figure 1. For AORFB single cell tests, the solutions of alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide that were all dissolved in KOH were applied to the negative and positive electrolytes. The catholyte contains 0.6 M alloxazine-COOH dissolved in 2.5 M KOH within a volume of 15 mL, whereas the anolyte contains 0.4 M potassium ferrocyanide and 0.04 M potassium ferricyanide dissolved in 1 M KOH within a volume of 56.3 mL. The active area of the AORFB single cell was 4 cm2, and a Nafion 117 membrane was used as the separator to reduce the crossover of the active species and promote the proton conductivity. (33) During the charge and discharge steps, the current density was fixed at 60 mA·cm–2. Catalyst-coated carbon felts were used to evaluate the effects of the catalysts on the AORFB performance. For catalyst coating, 40 mg of each catalyst was combined with a mixture consisting of 10.5 mL of ethyl alcohol and 0.5 mL of Nafion 117 solution. Then, the catalyst-coated carbon felts were dried at 80 °C. In addition, for investigating the correlation of OCV and the state of charge (SOC), the processing time was controlled for calculating the actual SOC of the AORFB single cell. Here, the actual SOC can be defined as the ratio of actual capacity to theoretical capacity, and this is calculated as a product of the applied chargingtime and current density. (34) For this, the charging time for obtaining the target SOC was applied to the corresponding AORFB full cell with an increase in voltage, and when the charging time reached the predetermined time range, it was stopped for 30 s to measure the OCV of the charging step.

    3.1. Evaluation of Redox Reactivity of the Organic Redox Couple Species

    With regard to the redox couple, ferrocyanide was used as received, whereas alloxazine-COOH was modified from pure alloxazine as explained in the Experimental Section. For deciding the optimal alloxazine-COOH from pure alloxazine, the COOH mixing, which can be considered as a dominant parameter to affect the modification of pure alloxazine, was performed at 5, 10 min, and 1, 2, 3, and 6 h. The redox reactivity of such synthesized alloxazine-COOHs was measured using the CV curves that were scanned at a potential range of −1.2 to −0.4 V versus Ag/AgCl (Figure 2), whereas the chemical structure—which is measured to check whether the COOH groups are well-attached to alloxazine or not—was inspected by NMR (Figure S2).
    According to Figure 2, when the COOH mixing time was 2 h, the peak current density (Ipa and Ipc), the ratio of the peak current density (Ipa/Ipc), and the peak potential difference (ΔEp)—which are the main evidences of redox reactivity—were best in alloxazine-COOH and, thus, alloxazine-COOH was selected as optimal and used for the subsequent processes. Even in the spectra obtained by NMR, the COOH groups were well attached to alloxazine, forming proper alloxazine-COOH (Figure S2a,b). According to the NMR analysis, three peaks of alloxazine-COOH were observed near 8.2–8.8 ppm, which means the COOH groups were well-formed. (35) Conversely, there were no peaks observed of pure alloxazine in this region. In addition, the alloxazine-COOH samples prepared by both 2 h one-step and 2 h two-step processes (Figure S2b) exhibited the same peak positions (Figure S2c). On the other hand, the sample prepared by the 24 h one-step process showed small peaks near 7.2–7.6 ppm. It is possible that these are from impurities or other by-products, as previously reported (Figure S2d). (36) Ultimately, when the stirring time is too long, the impurities or other by-products are probably produced during the formation of alloxazine-COOH.
    Another important parameter is the aqueous solubility of the active species dissolved in the KOH electrolyte (pH 14) because a high solubility induces the increases in energy density, power density, and AORFB performance. For the negative electrode, the attachment of COOH to pure alloxazine is supposed to increase its aqueous solubility in the KOH electrolyte. To calibrate the aqueous solubility in pure alloxazine and alloxazine-COOH samples, 2 g of each sample was dissolved in 2 mL of the KOH electrolyte, and the pure alloxazine-dissolved KOH and alloxazine-COOH-dissolved KOH were regularly extracted for solubility measurements and the results are represented in Figure 3. For the measurements, the absorbance peak detected at 432 nm was mainly recorded. (37) According to the measurements, ∼0.15 M of pure alloxazine was dissolved in 1 M KOH, while 2 M of alloxazine-COOH was dissolved in 1 M KOH . This shows that, with the addition of COOH, the solubility of alloxazine-COOH in 1 M KOH increases 13 times more than that of pure alloxazine. This is attributed to an increase in the hydrogen bonding between KOH and COOH by the addition of COOH, and the improved hydrogen bonding subsequently increases the aqueous solubility of alloxazine-COOH dissolved in KOH. (38) Furthermore, the OCV of alloxazine-COOH (anolyte) and ferrocyanide (catholyte) was shown to be 1.13 V, which is comparable with that of VRFB (1.26 V) (Figure S3).
    Next, it is important to determine the RDR out of the alloxazine-COOH- and ferrocyanide-related redox reactions because enhancing the redox reactivity of the RDR is a priority for increasing the performance of AORFB. (39) To investigate the RDR, the electron transfer rate constant (ks) and diffusion coefficient (D) are the major reaction kinetic parameters, which are measured using the Laviron and Randles–Sevcik equations, respectively. (40,41)
    For obtaining ks, multiple CV curves showing the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide are estimated under different scan rates, and the correlations between the scan rates and redox peak currents (Ipa and Ipc vs ν) and those between the logarithm of the scan rates and the difference in the redox peak potential and average peak potential (Epa – Eavg and Epc – Eavg vs log ν) are represented in Figures 4 and S4. Laviron’s equation is used to calculate ks from the data. According to the calculation, the ks values of alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide are 0.002 and 0.044 s–1, respectively, indicating that alloxazine-COOH is the RDR. Besides ks, D of the two active species is determined using the Randles–Sevcik equationwhere jp is the peak current density, c is the concentration of active materials in the electrolyte, z is the number of electrons involved in the electrode process, v is the potential sweep rate, and D is the diffusion coefficient of the active materials. To attain this value, a correlation between the redox peak currents and the logarithm of scan rate (Ipa and Ipc vs ν1/2) is obtained. From the plot, D of alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide is determined to be 5.9 × 10–6 and 5.6 × 10–6 cm2 s–1, respectively. This means that the D values of the two active species are similar. Taken together, the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH was considered the RDR, and for increasing the performance of AORFB, the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH should be improved.

    3.2. Effect of Catalyst on Redox Reactivity of the Organic Redox Couple Species

    To enhance the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH, new catalysts like carboxylic acid-functionalized CNT (CA-CNT) and pure CNT are applied. The pure CNT is considered as a catalyst because the nanostructure of CNT may have a positive influence on the enhancement of the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH. In addition, with the utilization of CA-CNT—the purpose of which is to increase the amount of hydrophilic groups, such as COOH and OH—the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH will be promoted further.
    To evaluate the effects of the catalysts on the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH, CV curves are measured (Figure 5). According to Figure 5, when CA-CNT is used as the catalyst, the reaction rate of alloxazine-COOH (demonstrated as the peak current density) increases more than that of alloxazine-COOH when tested without the catalyst under similar experimental conditions.
    The theoretical mechanism explaining the effect of the CA-CNT catalyst on the redox reaction of alloxazine-COOH is presented in Figure 6. Two main reasons can be hypothesized. First, when the COOHs of the CA-CNT catalyst make contact with a strong base, such as KOH, they can act as proton donors, and as a result, metal carboxylate salts (COO– and K+) are produced, and the released proton easily interacts with the nitrogen of −C═N–C– in alloxazine-COOH, increasing the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH. (42) Second, when it comes to hydrophilicity, the COOHs of CA-CNT make the catalyst more hydrophilic, and this facilitates contact with the molecules of alloxazine-COOH because of improved mass transfer. (43) In turn, this can lead to (i) an increase in the number of active sites on the catalyst surface that can contact the aqueous anolyte and (ii) an increase in the reaction rate of alloxazine-COOH.
    It is worth noting that unlike CA-CNT, the effect of CNT catalyst on the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH was not significant because the CNT catalyst does not contain COOH groups promoting the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH. Despite this, because of its typical nanostructure that can produce a larger active surface area, the catalyst-sprayed carbon felt can lead to better electron transfer and redox reactivity than that without a catalyst. (44)
    To more accurately quantify the effects of the corresponding catalysts on the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH, its kinetic parameters, such as ks and D, are again measured (Figures 7 and 8). The ks value of alloxazine-COOH measured with the CA-CNT catalyst is 0.36 s–1, whereas its D value is 9.4 × 10–4 cm2·s–1. In contrast, the ks and D values of alloxazine-COOH measured with the CNT catalyst and without the catalyst are 0.22 s–1 and 2.5 × 10–5 cm2·s–1 and 0.002 s–1 and 5.9 × 10–6 cm2·s–1, respectively. Indeed, with the adoption of the CA-CNT catalyst, ks and D are 180 and 159 times more improved than those measured without the catalyst. On the basis of this, it is proven that the CA-CNT catalyst has a strong positive impact on enhancements in the kinetic parameters of alloxazine-COOH.
    It should be noted that the CA-CNT catalyst was even applied to ferrocyanide for the positive electrode. However, this did not affect the increase in the redox reactivity of ferrocyanide. This is because the redox reaction of ferrocyanide is not influenced by the protons that mainly affect the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH. Such a behavior was verified by measuring the CV curve (Figure S5). When the CV curves exhibiting the redox reactivity of three samples—ferrocyanide without catalyst, ferrocyanide with CNT, and ferrocyanide with CA—were measured, there were no changes in the redox reaction peak.

    3.3. Performance Evaluations of AORFB Single Cells Using Alloxazine-COOH and Ferrocyanide Redox Couple

    According to the optimized catalytic activity and kinetic property data, CA-CNT and CNT are useful as catalysts in the negative electrode of the AORFB single cell, and it is necessary to investigate the effects of these catalysts on the performance of the AORFB single cell. For this, AORFB single cells using CA-CNT and CNT catalysts were prepared, and, as a control, a cell without catalyst was also fabricated. For the convenience of recognition, these were denoted as AORFB-CA-CNT, AORFB-CNT, and AORFB-No-Cat, respectively.
    Charge and discharge cycle tests of the AORFB single cells were initially performed, and the results are shown in Figure 9. According to Figure 9, AORFB-CA-CNT experiences the longest charge–discharge time during the entire cycles. For example, the total time experienced by AORFB-CA-CNT for the initial four cycles was 14.1 h, whereas those of AORFB-CNT and AORFB-No-Cat were 13.4 and 12.2 h, respectively. In addition, in terms of the starting charge and discharge voltages, those of AORFB-CA-CNT, AORFB-CNT, and AORFB-No-Cat were 1.29 and 1.04 V (AORFB-CA-CNT), 1.30 and 1.03 V (AORFB-CNT), and 1.32 and 1.01 V (AORFB-No-Cat). There are several noticeable findings about the AORFB single cell test results. First, both the charge–discharge overpotential and infrared (IR) drop potential were improved with the use of catalysts, proving that the redox reaction of alloxazine-COOH—which was the RDR—was promoted by the catalysts. Second, in a comparison of CA-CNT and CNT catalysts, AORFB-CA-CNT resulted in a better performance than AORFB-CNT in terms of charge–discharge time and overpotential. As mentioned earlier, this is due to the proton donor role and the excellent hydrophilicity of CA-CNT.
    From Figure 9, it is expected that the VE and EE of AORFB-CA-CNT will be higher than those of AORFB-CNT and AORFB-No-Cat. Thus, the efficiencies of the AORFB single cells were measured, and the results are represented in Figures S6 and S7. Although CE was similar among them, the VE and EE of AORFB-CA-CNT were better than those of the others (the VE and EE of AORFB-CA-CNT, AORFB-CNT, and AORFB-No-Cat are 64 and 64, 62 and 62, and 60 and 60%, respectively). Similar CE indicates that the two active species (alloxazine-COOH and ferrocyanide) rarely cause crossover, and the crossover rate is even lower than that of VRFB using the same Nafion 117 membrane. This trend in efficiencies is well compatible with the trend in the charge–discharge time and overpotential observed in Figure 9.
    Another parameter to support the effects of the catalysts on the performance of the AORFB single cell, discharging capacity, and SOC was considered (Figure 10). According to Figure 10, during the cycle test, those of AORFB-CA-CNT were the best.     In this study, alloxazine and ferrocyanide were used as the active species for AORFB, with an additional modification of COOH to alloxazine (alloxazine-COOH). With the formation of alloxazine-COOH, both the aqueous solubility and cell potential increased. For further improvement toward the overall redox reactivity and performance of AORFB, the RDR needed to be identified and an increase in its redox reactivity needed to be accomplished. To find out the RDR, kinetic parameters, such as electron transfer rate constant (ks) and diffusivity (D), were measured and, as a result, the redox reaction of alloxazine-COOH was determined to be the RDR. For increasing its reactivity, a CA-CNT catalyst was utilized. With the use of CA-CNT, a better redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH was achieved. This was due to the proton donor role of COOH within alloxazine-COOH and the fortified hydrophilic attribute of alloxazine-COOH, as well as the increased number of active sites by the adoption of CNT. In addition, the mass transfer of aqueous alloxazine-COOH molecules was also facilitated. The increase of the redox reactivity of alloxazine-COOH by the use of CA-CNT was verified by measuring the kinetic parameters (ks and D of alloxazine-COOH using the CA-CNT catalyst were 0.36 s–1 and 9.4 × 10–4 cm2·s–1, respectively, whereas the values were 0.22 s–1 and 2.5 × 10–5 cm2·s–1 for alloxazine-COOH using the CNT catalyst and 0.002 s–1 and 5.9 × 10–6 cm2·s–1 for that without the catalyst. With regard to the AORFB full cell tests, when the CA-CNT catalyst was adopted for the negative electrode, the performance of AORFB-CA-CNT increased. The charge–discharge overpotential and IR drop potential were improved; CE (99%) and EE (64%) also improved; and the discharging capacity and SOC of 26.7 A h·L–1 and 83%, respectively, were also observed even after the operation of 30 cycles. To summarize, an active species of AORFB, alloxazine-COOH, was found to be the RDR, and when a CA-CNT catalyst was newly adopted, both the redox reactivity and mass transfer of alloxazine-COOH were improved, and these enhancements led to a further improvement in the performance of the AORFB full cell.
