Nitrogen-doped porous graphene as a highly efficient cathodic electrocatalyst for aqueous organic redox flow battery application†

Jianyu Cao a, Zhentao Zhu a, Juan Xu *a, Meng Tao a and Zhidong Chen *b

First published on 28th March 2017

Abstract

The redox flow battery (RFB) is considered one of the most attractive energy storage technologies because of its high efficiency, long service life and great safety. In this work, nitrogen-doped porous graphene (NPG) was achieved by embedding nitrogen atoms into the graphitic matrix via a sacrificial-template-assisted pyrolysis approach, and then was employed to catalyze electrochemically the redox reaction of 1,2-dihydrobenzoquinone-3,5-disulphonic acid (BQDSH2) for aqueous organic RFB application. The electrocatalytic performances of this catalyst toward the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox couple were greatly enhanced, including a 6.7 times higher rate constant and much lower peak potential separation value compared to those of a glassy carbon electrode. Furthermore, a 9,10-anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid (AQDS)/BQDS RFB with NPGs as the cathodic BQDS/BQDSH2 catalyst exhibited a maximum power density of ca. 62.4 mW cm−2, about 3.3 times higher than that of the pristine RFB without NPGs, and a minimal discharge capacity fade during the cycling test. The abundant nitrogen defects in NPGs are beneficial to the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox reaction by providing active sites and enhancing the transfer rate of protons and reactants, contributing to the improvement of AQDS/BQDS RFB performance.

Introduction

The excessive use of conventional fossil fuels has not only produced air pollution and green house effects, but has also led to the depletion of resources.1 Although the use of abundant, clean and safe renewable energies, including solar, wind, tide, biomass power, etc., could reduce the risk of environmental pollution and promote the harmonious development of man and nature, these energy sources have some inherent limitations, such as unpredictable and intermittent nature and slow response.2–4 For example, solar power generation depends on weather conditions, further leading to the need to store and transform the gained energy. Even for thermal or nuclear power generation, large-scale energy storage devices are required to alleviate the grid's peak–valley difference and to reduce energy wastage due to the huge variations in electricity demand between day and night. Therefore, the development of reliable, affordable and scalable means of storing energy is imperatively required for the increasing prosperity and sustainable future of our society.

Among the various types of energy storage technologies, the redox flow battery (RFB) has been regarded as an efficient and reliable solution to store energy generated by renewable sources, owing to its electroactive species wholly existing in the liquid form in the basic design.5 Furthermore, the power and energy capacity are independent of each other, which contribute to achieve maximum performance and minimum cost for the RFBs and consequently meet the large scale energy storage requirements.6 Furthermore, the aqueous RFB has been preferably adopted because of its distinctive advantages such as higher ionic conductivity, easier purification, lower cost and higher safety as compared to its non-aqueous counterpart, despite having a relatively low energy density due to the working voltage limitation.7

Nowadays, the most reported electroactive species in RFBs are inorganic redox couples such as Fe3+/Fe2+, V3+/V2+, VO2+/VO2+, Br2/Br−, etc.,5 while the utilization of organic species is rare. For organic species, there are plenty of opportunities because their numbers are so enormous. Moreover, the large flexibility of modern organic synthesis alleviates the design of new molecular structures. Thus, a tremendous amount of research effort in aqueous RFBs needs to be devoted to the search of appropriate organic species. Electroactive organic compounds involving reversible redox reactions are promising alternatives to inorganic species as electrolytes of flow batteries due to their rapid reaction kinetics, higher theoretical capacity, good safety, environmental friendliness and potential low cost.8 The redox potential and solubility for organic compounds can be conveniently modified by introducing functional groups.9,10 Among the numerous types of organic compounds, quinone and its derivatives possess excellent electrochemical reversibility.11 Their redox mechanism is generalized as an enolization reaction and a reverse reaction of the carbonyl group, promoted by a conjugated structure.8,12,13 Recently, a quinone derivative, 9,10-anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid (AQDS), reported by Huskinson et al.,14 exhibited a rapid and reversible redox reaction on a glassy carbon electrode in sulfuric acid. Then, an aqueous RFB utilizing the AQDS/AQDSH2 redox couple as the anolyte and the Br2/Br− couple as the catholyte was further assembled, which yielded a peak galvanic power density of more than 600 mW cm−2. Recently, Narayanan et al.15,16 reported an organic redox species, 1,2-dihydroxybenzene-3,5-disulfonic acid (BQDSH2). When employed as cathodic redox species, BQDSH2 shows a high standard potential and good solubility (∼2 M at room temperature) in water because of two sulfonic acid groups in its molecular structure. However, the catalytic kinetics of BQDSH2 and performance optimization have not been studied yet.

Traditionally, graphitic carbon materials can serve as electrodes to collect electrons and catalyze the redox reactions.17,18 However, pure carbon materials have often shown low kinetic reversibility, especially when the redox couple reaction involves several elementary steps.19 Precious metals have shown high catalytic activity, but they are not cost-effective.20 Therefore, the exploration of low-cost and durable catalysts that possess superior catalytic activity toward electrochemical reactions between redox species is warranted for applications of RFBs. In this work, we have prepared nitrogen-doped porous graphene (NPG) with abundant nitrogen defects which can effectively catalyze the redox reaction of BQDSH2 for aqueous organic redox flow battery application.

Experimental

Materials

1,2-Dihydrobenzoquinone-3,5-disulphonic acid disodium salt (the sodium salt of BQDSH2, 97%), 2,4-dihydroxybenzoic acid, oleic acid, and dicyandiamide were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. 9,10-Anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid disodium salt (the sodium salt of AQDS, 95%) was obtained from Tokyo Chemical Industry (TCI). Formaldehyde (37 wt%) and ammonium hydroxide (25 wt%) solution were obtained from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd. Amberlyst 15H ion-exchange resin was purchased from Aladdin Industrial Corporation. All chemicals were used as received without further purification.

Preparation of NPGs

NPGs were synthesized via a sacrificial-template-assisted pyrolysis method that was previously described by our group and others.21,22 A brief preparation procedure was described as follows: firstly, 2,4-dihydroxybenzoic acid (2.5 mmol) and formaldehyde (7.5 mmol) were dissolved in 95 mL of ultrapure water by ultrasonic stirring to form a uniform solution. Then, a 5 mL solution that contains oleic acid (56 μL) and ammonium hydroxide (180 μL, 25 wt%) was added dropwise to the above solution under slow stirring at 30 °C. A stable emulsion was formed after being stirred for about 30 min. Dicyandiamide (4.0 g) was added into 50 mL of the above-mentioned emulsion and stirred for 3 h. Then, a yellow powder was obtained by rotary evaporation to remove the solvent at 60 °C, which was employed as the precursor after drying in an oven at 60 °C for 12 h. Finally, NPGs were achieved by heating the precursor to 1000 °C for 1 h under a nitrogen atmosphere. During the pyrolysis process, the heating rate before and after 600 °C is 2.0 and 3.3 °C min−1, respectively.

Characterization

The specific surface area, pore volume, and pore size of NPGs were evaluated using a Micromeritics ASAP 2460 analyzer at 77 K. Before measurements, the sample was degassed at 180 °C under vacuum for 6 h. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method and the single point method were utilized to calculate the specific surface area and pore volume, respectively, while the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) method was used to determine the pore size distribution curve. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images were obtained on a JEM-2100 TEM (JEOL, Japan) operating at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. For TEM measurements, NPGs were dispersed in ethanol to obtain a uniform dispersion. Then, a drop of dispersion was mounted onto copper grids covered with a holey carbon film. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) experiments were carried out on an Escalab 250 X-ray photoelectron spectrometer (Thermo Scientific) with Al Kα radiation as an X-ray source for radiation. The binding energy was referenced to the C1s spectrum at 284.5 eV.

Electrochemical analysis

Electrochemical experiments were conducted on an Interface 1000 potentiostat (Gamry Instruments Inc.) using a conventional three-electrode electrochemical cell at room temperature. Before measurements, a glassy carbon (GC, 0.1963 cm2) disk electrode was polished with 0.05 μm Al2O3 powders, ultrasonicated, and cleaned with deionized water. NPGs (8 mg) were dispersed into a mixture composed of 5 wt% Nafion ionomer solution (0.15 mL), isopropanol (1 mL), and ultrapure water (1 mL) by at least 30 min of sonication to form a homogeneous ink. Then, 8 μL of this ink was transferred onto the GC disk electrode, and left to dry overnight to serve as the working electrode. A saturated calomel electrode (SCE) was used as the reference electrode and a Pt wire electrode served as the counter electrode. BQDSH2 (1 mM) was pre-dissolved in a 1 M H2SO4 solution that served as the electrolyte. Prior to measurements, the electrolyte was deaerated by high-purity N2 for 20 min. Rotating disk electrode (RDE) measurements were conducted using the same GC disk electrode mounted onto a rotator (Pine Research Instrumentation). The electrode was then rotated at 200, 300, 400, 500, 700, 900, 1200, and 1600 rpm. All electrode potentials were converted to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) scale.

Flow cell measurements

A commercial flow cell (Fuel Cell Technologies Inc.) with an active area of 5 cm2 was used for redox flow battery measurements. A proportional integral derivative (PID)-controlled heating element and two hot plates were integrated into this flow cell for temperature control. Two pieces of Toray carbon papers (TGP-H-060, non-wet-proofed) pre-treated according to a previously reported method14 were utilized as the anode. Another two pieces of pre-treated Toray carbon papers (TGP-H-060, non-wet-proofed), on which NPGs (2 mg cm−2) and Nafion ionomer (20 wt%) were coated, were utilized as the cathode. Homemade silicone rubber gaskets were used in the flow cell to avoid fluid leakage. Sodium salts of BQDSH2 and AQDS were flushed twice through a column containing Amberlyst 15H ion-exchange resin to remove the sodium ions. Then, a BQDSH2 (1 M, 20 mL) solution was used as the catholyte and an AQDS solution (1 M, 20 mL) as the anolyte. MPF2002 semi-preparative infusion pumps (Shanghai Sanotac Scientific Instrument Inc.) were used to circulate the catholyte and anolyte at a rate of 80 mL min−1. A Nafion112 membrane (50 μm, DuPont) was used as a proton-exchange membrane to separate the catholyte and anolyte. The picture of the laboratory scale flow cell of the aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFB is shown in Fig. 1. In order to avoid the reaction of the reduced form of AQDS (AQDSH2) with oxygen, electrolyte tanks were placed in a sealed container and a nitrogen flow was maintained at all times above these electrolytes. Flow cell measurements were conducted on an Interface 5000 electrochemical workstation (Gamry Instruments Inc.). The flow cell was charged at 1.2 V until a fixed amount of charge ran through the cell and during this process, the color of the electrolyte changed from red to dark green (AQDS to AQDSH2) and from colorless to red (BQDSH2 to BQDS). Polarization curves were measured at an SOC of 100%. For each discharging current point along a polarization curve, a duration time of 90 s was used to obtain a stable voltage. Immediately, a charging step of the same current and duration time is used to recover the initial SOC. During charge–discharge cycling, a constant current density of ±100 mA cm−2 was used with cut-off voltages of 0 and 1.2 V for the aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFB with NPGs and cut-off voltages of 0 and 1.4 V for the aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFB without NPGs, respectively. The ohmic resistances (Rs) of the AQDS/BQDS RFB were determined by using electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) which were recorded with sweeping frequencies from 100 kHz to 0.1 Hz on an Interface 5000 electrochemical workstation (Gamry Instruments Inc.).

Results and discussion

Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms for NPGs are presented in Fig. 2a. A large H2 hysteresis loop occurs at high relative P/P0 pressures, indicating the existence of abundant mesopores within the sample. These mesopores are mainly created by the in situ polymerization and subsequent decomposition of dicyandiamide.22 Textural properties of NPGs are summarized in Table 1. It can be observed that the total specific surface area of NPGs is ca. 468.1 m2 g−1, most of which (ca. 409.3 m2 g−1) is provided by mesopores, demonstrating that NPGs are typical mesoporous materials. The total pore volume of NPGs was estimated to be ca. 1.01 cm3 g−1, which is much larger than that of previously reported reduced graphene oxides (RGOs)23,24 due to the existence of abundant mesopores. The BJH pore size distribution plot for NPGs (the inset of Fig. 2a) reveals that two separate peaks appear at 3.7 nm and 14.4 nm, respectively. The template molecules probably play an important role in the formation of both types of mesopores.22 The average pore diameter is about 10.3 nm which was estimated from the desorption isotherm. The TEM image of NPGs is shown in Fig. 2b. The lateral dimension of abundant wrinkles ranges from 400 to 700 nm, which can be presumed to be multiple graphene layers formed in the pyrolysis process. A detailed inspection of TEM images of NPGs (the inset of Fig. 2b) reveals that the thickness does not exceed 5 nm.

XPS characterization was widely utilized to determine the doping content of heteroatoms and chemical state of elements in electro-catalytic materials. The distinct C1s, N1s and O1s peaks are observed in the XPS survey spectrum, as shown in Fig. 3a. The elemental contents in NPGs are 88.49 at% carbon, 5.59 at% nitrogen, and 5.92 at% oxygen, which were calculated from the corresponding peak areas using relative sensitivity factors. The high concentrations of nitrogen atoms of NPGs contribute to their superior catalytic activity for the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox reaction as will be discussed later. The C1s spectrum (Fig. 3b) of NPGs shows two strong peaks located at 284.6 eV and at 285.4 eV, respectively, corresponding to sp2-hybridized graphitic carbon atoms and C–O/C–N species. The weaker broad peak around 287.7 eV can be assigned to CO groups on the surface of NPGs. The O1s signals around 532.1 and 533.2 eV are assigned to CO and C–O species (Fig. 3c), respectively. The N1s spectrum of NPGs can be deconvoluted into three peaks (Fig. 3d), assignable to pyridinic nitrogen (N1, 398.3 eV), graphitic nitrogen (N2, 401.0 eV), and oxidized species of nitrogen (N3, 403.1 eV).25 In addition, it can be seen from Fig. 3d that the graphitic nitrogen is the most abundant form (66.6 at%), followed by the pyridinic type (26.8 at%) among the three nitrogen species.

Fig. 4 shows the CV curves of 1 mM BQDSH2 (sodium salt form) in a 1 M H2SO4 solution on GC and NPG electrodes. It can be seen that both CV curves exhibit a pair of well-defined redox peaks. The corresponding redox reaction is presented in the inset of Fig. 4. The formal potential (E0, shown in Table 2) of the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox couple, defined as the average value of the anodic peak potential (Epa) and the cathodic peak potential (Epc), is found to be 0.897 V on the NPG electrode and 0.927 V on the GC electrode. The separation between Epa and Epc (ΔE = Epa − Epc) on the NPG electrode is ca. 52 mV, which is much lower than that on the GC electrode with a value of ∼186 mV, suggesting that NPGs significantly accelerate the kinetics of the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox reaction. To further investigate the effect of the catalytic electrode on the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox couple, RDE tests were performed under a nitrogen atmosphere. The RDE voltammetry curves at different rotation rates for the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox couple on NPG and GC electrodes, respectively, are shown in Fig. S1a and c,† from which Koutecky'–Levich curves (shown in Fig. S1c and d†) were obtained by plotting the reciprocal of the current at different overpotentials versus the reciprocal of the square root of the rotation rate. It can be observed that the data at each overpotential can be perfectly fitted with a straight line. The intercept value of the fitted straight lines gives the reciprocal of iK, the current density in the absence of mass transport limitations. Fig. 5 shows a plot of overpotential versus log(iK) for the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox couple on the NPG electrode, which can be linearly fitted with a slope of 67.3 mV per decade defined by the Butler–Volmer equation as 2.303RT/(βnF), where R is the universal gas constant, T is the temperature in kelvin, β is the charge transfer coefficient, n is the electron transfer number, and F is Faraday's constant. This gives a β value of ca. 0.44 (shown in Table 2). The x-intercept gives the log of the exchange current (i0), equal to FACRk0, where A is the electrode area and CR is the concentration of BQDSH2, and also gives a rate constant (k0) of 9.61 × 10−4 cm s−1. However, the plot of overpotential versus log(iK) obtained on the GC electrode (the inset of Fig. 5) gives a slope of 86.6 mV per decade and a corresponding k0 value of 1.44 × 10−4 cm s−1. The rate constant of the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox couple obtained on the NPG electrode is about 6.7 times greater than that obtained on the GC electrode, again revealing that NPGs effectively accelerate the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox reaction.

Extensive studies revealed that nitrogen-doped carbon nanomaterials might effectively catalyze a lot of important electrochemical reactions, such as oxygen reduction,26–30 carbon dioxide reduction,31 production of hydrogen peroxide,32 and redox reaction of vanadium species.19 For example, graphitic nitrogen and pyridinic nitrogen were confirmed to promote the activity of the oxygen reduction reaction due to their charge delocalization that is beneficial to breaking the O–O bond.26,27 Jin and Zhang19 analysed the relationship between the peak current density and concentration of graphitic nitrogen species and then revealed that graphitic nitrogen doped into the graphene lattice that acted as the active centre catalyzes the VO2+/VO2+ redox reaction by weakening the VO bond. Therefore, similarly, it is believed that graphitic nitrogen and pyridinic nitrogen behave mainly as catalytically active sites for the BQDSH2/BQDS redox reaction by affecting the breaking and formation of phenolic H–O bonds of BQDSH2, since the two types of nitrogen forms are the dominant components (up to 93.4 at%) in the as-prepared NPGs. In addition, a high density of nitrogen defects in NPGs not only creates abundant active sites,33,34 but also facilitates the adsorption and coordination of reactants and protons on the catalytically active site,35 which can be another reason responsible for the enhanced catalytic activity of NPGs.

As widely reported, modification of carbon materials by introducing hydrophilic groups onto the surface can enhance wettability towards the electrolyte, thus improving their electrochemical performance during redox reactions (e.g. VO2+/VO2+, etc.) of RFBs.36,37 In order to investigate the effect of wettability, the contact angles of water distributed on three different electrodes were measured and are shown in Fig. S2.† It can be observed from Fig. S2† that the contact angle of water distributed on the NPG electrode is ca. 136.2°, which is slightly larger than that of bare Toray carbon paper (133.0°), but significantly lower than that of the Vulcan XC72R carbon electrode (146.1°). In comparison to the commercial XC72R carbon, NPGs exhibit enhanced surface wettability due to their high nitrogen content, which is beneficial for promoting the transfer of reactants.

Fig. 6 shows the galvanic polarization curves of aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFBs with and without NPGs as the cathodic BQDS/BQDSH2 catalyst and their corresponding power density curves. It can be observed that there is almost no difference in the open circuit voltage (OCP) for two organic RFBs. However, with an increasing current density, the polarization curve of the RFB with NPGs shows a much flatter voltage drop compared with that without NPGs, demonstrating that NPGs as the cathodic catalyst play a significant role in promoting the electrochemical redox reaction of the BQDS/BQDSH2 couple. Furthermore, the maximum power density for the RFB with NPGs reached ca. 62.4 mW cm−2, nearly 3.3 times higher than that without NPGs (only 19.3 mW cm−2).

In comparison to other aqueous organic RFBs utilizing BQDS/BQDSH2 redox couples that were presented in the literature (Table 3), electrochemical measurements and flow cell studies revealed a much greater rate constant (k0) and superior power density of aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFBs with NPGs. It should be noted that, besides electrocatalysts, other structural parameters, such as flow-field design, wettability of the electrode, flow rate and concentration of redox species, also have a significant influence on the performance of RFBs. For example, Narayanan group16 recently improved the peak power density of BQDS-based aqueous RFBs from ca. 30 up to 100 mW cm−2 by adopting a flow-field design with a “flow-through” feature and increasing the wettability of the graphite felt electrode to promote the transfer of electrolytes.

Ohmic resistance is one of the key factors that affects the performance of RFBs. Ohmic losses in RFBs generally include electrical resistances of the electrodes, membranes and electrolyte solutions. The ohmic resistances (Rs) of the AQDS/BQDS RFB with and without NPGs were determined by EIS analysis to be 0.545 and 0.755 ohm cm2, respectively. In comparison to its counterpart, the AQDS/BQDS RFB with NPGs shows a much lower Rs value, which mainly comes from the improved electrical contact after the addition of highly conductive NPGs. Furthermore, the effect of the supporting electrolyte (e.g. sulfuric acid) was also investigated. A plot of measured ohmic resistance versus the concentration (C, mole per liter) of sulfuric acid added in the catholyte for the AQDS/BQDS RFB with and without NPGs is shown in Fig. S3.† It can be seen that with the sulfuric acid concentration increasing from 0 to 1 M, the ohmic resistances of the AQDS/BQDS RFB with and without NPGs decreased slowly from 0.545 and 0.755 ohm cm2 to 0.490 and 0.710 ohm cm2, respectively, indicating that the addition of sulfuric acid to the electrolyte has a minimal effect on the internal resistance.

Fig. 7a compares the voltage profiles at the same charge–discharge current density of 100 mA cm−2 for aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFBs with and without NPGs. A significantly reduced overpotential marked in the figure in both charge and discharge processes again suggests the positive effect of the addition of NPGs into the cathode catalyst layer, leading to a lower charge voltage and higher discharge voltage. Fig. 7b shows constant current cycling data collected at ±100 mA cm−2 for the aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFB with NPGs, using voltage cut-offs of 0 V and 1.2 V. The charge and discharge times are nearly equal for each cycle and no obvious decay is observed, suggesting that excellent stability of the catalytic effect was rendered by the NPG electrode. Although the aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFB with NPGs showed a much lower overpotential compared to that without NPGs, a large polarization was still observed from its charge–discharge curves shown in Fig. 7b, which might be due to the existence of large mass transfer limitations under the current test conditions (e.g. relatively low flow rate compared to the RFBs reported previously in the literature16,39). The optimization of structures and operating parameters in future is expected to significantly reduce this polarization. A long cycling test (up to 100 cycles) for the aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFB with NPGs in the cathode catalyst layer was conducted under a current density of 100 mA cm−2 to obtain further insight into the effects of NPGs on long-term stability, which is shown in Fig. 7c. It can be seen that the average current efficiency and discharge capacity retention for each cycle are 99.0% and 98.8%, respectively. Almost no current efficiency and discharge capacity retention fading were observed after 100 cycles, suggesting good stability of both the NPGs and their electro-catalytic effect on the cathodic BQDS/BQDSH2 redox reaction over repetitive cycling. A large fluctuation of discharge capacity retention on cycling was observed from Fig. 7c, which may be due to slight irregular fluctuation of the flow rate caused by the plunger pump or accidently pumped gas bubbles39 that makes the BQDSH2/BQDS redox process on the surface of NPGs inhomogeneous in different cycles.

Conclusions

In summary, nitrogen-doped porous graphene with a high nitrogen concentration was successfully prepared by a sacrificial-template-assisted pyrolysis method and its electro-catalytic activity toward the BQDS/BQDSH2 redox reaction was investigated. Such a NPG electrode shows superior catalytic activity with a rate constant (k0) of 9.61 × 10−4 cm s−1, which is about 6.7 times higher than that of a GC electrode. Utilized as the cathodic BQDS/BQDSH2 couple catalyst within the aqueous AQDS/BQDS RFB, the NPGs exhibit excellent cell performances with a maximum power density of ca. 62.4 mW cm−2, which is ca. 3.3 times higher than that of the RFB without NPGs, and a minimal discharge capacity fade during the cycling test. We propose that graphitic nitrogen and pyridinic nitrogen in NPGs are mainly catalytically active sites, and abundant nitrogen-containing groups facilitate the adsorption and coordination of the reactant and electrons on the active site, which both contribute to the high catalytic activity of NPGs.
