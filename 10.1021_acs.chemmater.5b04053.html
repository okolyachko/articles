    Abstract

    We describe an electrochemically mediated interaction between Li+ and a promising active material for nonaqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs), 1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-6,7-dimethoxy-1,1,4,4-tetramethylnaphthalene (TDT), and the impact of this structural interaction on material stability during voltammetric cycling. TDT could be an advantageous organic positive electrolyte material for nonaqueous RFBs due to its high oxidation potential, 4.21 V vs Li/Li+, and solubility of at least 1.0 M in select electrolytes. Although results from voltammetry suggest TDT displays Nernstian reversibility in many nonaqueous electrolyte solutions, bulk electrolysis reveals significant degradation in all electrolytes studied, the extent of which depends on the electrolyte solution composition. Results of subtractively normalized in situ Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (SNIFTIRS) confirm that TDT undergoes reversible structural changes during cyclic voltammetry in propylene carbonate and 1,2-dimethoxyethane solutions containing Li+ electrolytes, but irreversible degradation occurs when tetrabutylammonium (TBA+) replaces Li+ as the electrolyte cation in these solutions. By combining the results from SNIFTIRS experiments with calculations from density functional theory, solution-phase active species structure and potential-dependent interactions can be determined. We find that Li+ coordinates to the Lewis basic methoxy groups of neutral TDT and, upon electrochemical oxidation, this complex dissociates into the radical cation TDT•+ and Li+. The improved cycling stability in the presence of Li+ relative to TBA+ suggests that the structural interaction reported herein may be advantageous to the design of energy storage materials based on organic molecules.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Redox flow batteries (RFBs) are electrochemical systems well-suited for multihour energy storage at the low system costs needed for economically viable grid storage. (1, 2) Whereas the majority of RFBs presently rely on water-soluble species as the charge-storage material, transitioning to nonaqueous chemistries offers the opportunity to increase cell voltage via wider electrochemical stability windows of electrolyte solutions and to leverage new electrochemical couples which are incompatible with aqueous electrolyte solutions due to either low solubility, chemical instability, or redox potentials outside of the stability window. (3-6) If realized together, these advantages lead to higher energy density, smaller system footprint, and lower costs of energy. Recently, efforts deploying electroactive organic molecules as storage materials in RFBs have gained traction because properties such as redox potential, solubility, diffusivity, and cycling stability can be tuned by modifying the molecular structure, which, in turn, can lead to enhanced performance. (4-12) For example, Wang and co-workers demonstrated that adding an anionic substituent group to ferrocene enhances solubility from 0.04 to 0.85 M in carbonate-based electrolyte solutions, which leads to marked improvements in cell energy densities when coupled with a Li-graphite anode. (4)
    Identifying and employing active materials with higher solubility and redox potentials will enable further improvements in energy density. For emerging materials, how modifying molecular structure to enhance one property subsequently impacts other properties is often unclear. Of particular concern are interactions between the active material, electrolyte, and solvent, as these have proven effects on both electrochemical and solution properties, as well as stability. (7, 13-15) In some cases these interactions can be advantageous, as demonstrated by Hernandez-Burgos and co-workers, who reported that coordination of carbonyl-based organic molecules to Mg2+ resulted in increased energy density due to increasing of the redox potential. (7)
    Several promising classes of active materials for the positive electrolyte in RFBs are based on redox shuttles for Li+-ion batteries, an electroactive organic species added to batteries to provide overcharge protection, as these materials have established cycling stability and reasonable gravimetric capacity. (5, 16, 17) Of particular note are derivatives of di-tert-butyl-di-n-oxybenzene (DDB), a redox shuttle displaying a reversible redox potential ca. 3.90 V vs Li/Li+, which have recently been developed and explored as candidate positive electrolytes. (5, 6, 14, 16, 18) Lessons learned from the DDB platform led to the design, synthesis, and evaluation of 1,2,3,4-tetrahydro-6,7-dimethoxy-1,1,4,4-tetramethylnaphthalene (TDT), a redox shuttle which undergoes reversible electrochemical oxidation to a radical cation TDT•+ at potentials above 4.15 V vs Li/Li+ (see our comment on reported reference electrode potentials in the Experimental Methods section). (19, 20)
    Unlike ferrocene, a molecule with well-established properties and stability in many nonaqueous solutions, the behavior of emerging redox-active organic molecules, such as TDT, in a given environment is less clear. Redox-active organic molecules can undergo a plethora of parasitic reactions, (21) and elucidating these often complex reaction pathways is a time- and material-intensive endeavor. Moreover, newer materials, especially those which are not yet commercially available, may not be readily synthesized in large enough quantities to enable comprehensive testing, and therefore improved scientific methodologies for analyzing structural changes pursuant to electrochemical reactions are desirable. A handful of ex situ and in situ methods have been applied to analyze the stability of families of redox-active organic molecules and estimate trends in cycling performance. (13, 17, 22) However, these approaches only partially describe complex interactions occurring in a real electrochemical cell at different stages of polarization. Some in situ methods, such as UV–vis spectroelectrochemistry, are useful under dilute conditions but may not be a suitable probe for the high-concentration environment of RFBs. Electron paramagnetic resonance spectroscopy provides insights into how radicals interact with their environment but can only interrogate solution components displaying paramagnetism. (13) Subtractively normalized in situ Fourier transform spectroscopy (SNIFTIRS) is an operando spectroscopic method suitable to characterizing organic materials within complex reaction mixtures employed in RFBs, such as those described herein. (23-26) Because SNIFTIRS spectra reveal changes in composition at the electrode surface and within the first few micrometers of solution during electrochemical cycling and these changes can be correlated to both potential and time, this method is a powerful analytical tool for elucidating electrochemical reaction mechanisms. Calculations from first-principles, such as density functional theory (DFT), is another tool for accelerating the discovery and development of new materials. (27) DFT is often applied in combination with electrochemical studies to predict the effects of molecular structure and electrolyte interactions on redox potential, (7, 10, 15) but the fidelity of these calculations presumes the modeled structure is a good approximation of the active material within the reaction mixture. Further, uncertainty in calculations of redox potential, as high as 0.2 V, leads to difficulty discriminating between models of similar energies. When combined with both spectroscopic and electrochemical data, however, DFT calculations augment insights about structure that are difficult to unambiguously determine from electrochemistry data alone. (23)
    The purpose of this study is to determine how electrochemical cycling modulates the structure of TDT and its interactions with electrolyte, with the aim of understanding how these types of interactions impact cycling stability. TDT can be reversibly cycled in a wide variety of electrolytes in propylene carbonate (PC) and 1,2-dimethoxyethane (DME) using cyclic voltammetry (CV). However, evidence of irreversible decomposition is apparent when large volumes of TDT are electrochemically cycled using bulk electrolysis, and the extent of capacity loss differs with electrolyte. Interestingly, the presence of Li+ as the electrolyte cation resulted in higher Coulombic efficiencies, denoting improved stability in these solutions. Comparing the results of SNIFTIRS with DFT simulations reveals that neutral TDT molecules sequester Li+ in a methoxyether complex, and these coordinated Li+ ions are subsequently released upon electrochemically oxidizing TDT to the radical cation, TDT•+ (Scheme 1). Coordination of Li+ to neutral TDT enhances the stability of the solution during cycling by deterring parasitic attack by TDT•+ on the Lewis basic veratrole group.

    Chemicals and Materials

    Propylene carbonate (PC, 99.7% anhydrous), 1,2-dimethoxyethane (DME, 99.5% anhydrous), dichloromethane (DCM, >99.8%), lithium perchlorate (LiClO4, battery grade, 99.99% trace-metal basis), tetrabutylammonium perchlorate (TBAClO4, electrochemical grade, >99.99%), lithium bis(trifluoromethane)sulfonimide (LiTFSI, 99.95%), and ferrocene (98%) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO). Electrolyte salts were dried overnight at 60 °C under vacuum and immediately transferred to the glovebox prior to use. Solvents were dried using activated aluminum oxide powder (basic, Sigma-Aldrich). Lithium foil (Li, 99.9% metals basis, Alfa Aesar) was packaged under argon and opened inside of the glovebox. TDT (99.9%, battery grade) was synthesized (19) and generously provided by the Materials Engineering Research Facility at Argonne National Laboratory. All materials were stored and prepared for analysis in a positive-pressure, Ar-filled glovebox (MBraun, Exeter, NH) with oxygen and moisture content below 1 ppm. Following drying, the water content of electrolyte solutions used for experiments was <25 ppm as measured by Karl Fischer coulometry (Mettler-Toledo C20 coulometric titrator).

    Electrochemical Characterization

    Electrochemical characterization was performed in the glovebox using a CH760E potentiostat (CH Instruments, Austin, TX). The glovebox temperature was about 29 °C. Where noted, a correction to the uncompensated resistance was applied during the measurement. A glassy carbon working electrode (GC, 3 mm diameter) and a gold working electrode (Au, 2 mm diameter) were purchased from CH Instruments. A carbon fiber ultramicroelectrode (UME, 11 μm) was purchased from BASi (West Lafayette, IN). We used a Vycor-fritted reference electrode consisting of a Li metal foil immersed in a solution of 1.0 M LiClO4 in PC to ensure the accurate comparison of potentials measured within the different solutions. A Au coil served as the counter electrode in CV experiments. The CV experiments were carried out in glass vials in which the electrodes were inserted through a Teflon cap. Constant potential bulk electrolysis experiments were conducted in an commercially available bulk electrolysis cell (BASi) consisting of a reticulated vitreous carbon (RVC) working electrode, a fritted counter electrode, and a Vycor-fritted reference electrode consisting of a Li metal foil immersed in a solution of 1.0 M LiClO4 in PC. A Li metal foil served as the counter electrode for all bulk electrolysis experiments in which Li+-containing electrolyte solutions were used. A Au coil served as the counter electrode for the experiments in TBA+-containing electrolyte solutions. A consistent solution volume of 25 mL and a stir rate of 1200 rpm were used for all bulk electrolysis experiments.
    The reference potential scale for all measurements reported herein was calibrated to a ferrocene standard (standard reduction potential, Eo = 0.40 vs SHE) (Supporting Information, Figure S1), and all potentials are reported versus Eo for Li/Li+ (−3.04 vs SHE). We note that, commonly, potential measurements made using Li metal foil in Li+-containing electrolyte solution are reported versus the measured potential of Li/Li+, but these values are not always corrected to the standard potential (Eo) of the Li/Li+ redox couple, as done here. Correcting the reference potential scale was necessary for these studies to ensure that experimental results could be compared to the calculations from DFT theory. Li metal foil electrodes used without calibrating the potential scale to a known standard reference couple are quasi-reference electrodes whose potential may deviate from the Eo of Li/Li+ by more than 0.1 V (Supporting Information, Figure S1). This difference should be kept in mind when comparing these results to other reports from the literature.

    SNIFTIRS

    A Thermo Nicolet 8700 FTIR spectrometer with a liquid nitrogen cooled MCT high D* detector was used in the SNIFTIRS measurements. The experiments were carried out in specialized sealed cell to keep moisture out. (25) Reflectance conditions were set up using a Au working electrode (5 mm diameter, Pine instruments, Grove City, PA) pressed against a 60° CaF2 prism to form a thin electrolyte layer of ca. 10 μm between the working electrode and prism. P-polarized light was used in the measurements by employing a polarization filter to enhance signal from the interface. A Pike optical box (Pike Technologies, Madison, WI) was used to control the incidence beam angle close to 63°. Electrochemical measurements made using a Au working electrode showed that the electrochemical properties of TDT were not sensitive to the choice of working electrodes used in the studies reported herein. The potential of the cell was controlled against a Ag/Ag+ quasi-reference electrode (BASi), and a Au wire served as the counter electrode. The potential of the quasi-reference electrode was calibrated to ferrocene prior to use in the SNIFTIRS experiments. A correction to the uncompensated resistance was applied during the measurement to ensure accurate correlation of spectra with electrochemical potential. Prior to sweeping the potential and recording in situ spectra, a reference spectrum was recorded at 4.10 V vs Li/Li+, a potential below the measured onset of TDT electrooxidation. Next, spectra were recorded while cycling the potential between 4.10 and 4.40 V at a sweep rate of 0.002 V/s. Eight interferograms were collected and averaged to form one single spectrum every 7 s. These spectra were then subtractively normalized with respect to the reference spectrum to produce a series of difference spectra.

    Computational Methods

    All calculations presented in the paper were performed using the Gaussian 09 software. (28) The B3LYP/6-31+G(d) level of theory was used to compute the structure and energies of all species in the gas phase. The same level of theory was used to calculate zero point energies, free energy corrections (298 K, 1 atm pressure), and solvation energies. The SMD2 model was used to compute the solvation free energy by a single point energy calculation on the gas phase optimized geometry using water as the dielectric medium for selected molecules. We find that this is an effective approximation for computing free energies of redox active species in solution, and this level of theory is reasonably accurate for the computation of reduction potentials.     Electrochemical Properties of TDT in Different Electrolyte Solutions

    The electrochemical properties of redox-active organic molecules can be influenced by explicit interactions with electrolyte and solvent. (7, 15, 21) Of particular concern for organic RFB applications, in which the active material must undergo hundreds of cycles or more, is the stability of the active species, which are susceptible to parasitic reactions such as nucleophilic or electrophilic attack, or scavenging by trace water, during charging and discharging. (21) These processes are also sensitive toward solution composition; (21) thus optimizing the stability of emerging RFB active materials requires characterizing them in a range of different electrolyte solutions. We analyzed the electrochemical properties of TDT in solutions containing LiTFSI, LiClO4, and TBAClO4 dissolved in PC and DME using CV with UME and bulk electrolysis. These salts were selected because the anions exhibit superior stability against degradation via thermolysis or hydrolysis, are stable against electrochemical oxidation up to at least 5 V in many aprotic solvents, and can be considered ideal for electroanalytical characterization. (42) The solubility of TDT in PC with 1.0 M electrolyte is only around 0.4 M, however, we found that TDT concentrations as high as 1.0 M within 1.0 M electrolyte can be achieved by using DME as the solvent.
    Figure 1 displays the CVs recorded using carbon fiber UMEs immersed in solutions containing 0.01 M TDT along with 0.10 M electrolyte (LiTFSI, LiClO4, and TBAClO4) dissolved in either PC or DME. The different diffusion-limited current plateaus can be attributed to the different viscosities of the electrolyte solutions, in accordance with the Stokes–Einstein relation. These data were analyzed using the Tomeš criterion (43, 44) (Table 1), which states that for reversible systems, |E3/4 – E1/4| ≈ 60/n mV, where E3/4 and E1/4 are the potentials at 3/4 and 1/4 the current value of the diffusion-limited current plateau, respectively, and n is the number of electrons transferred in the redox reaction. Analysis of the CV data in Figure 1 suggests that electrochemically oxidizing 0.01 M TDT to TDT•+ is reversible in the presence of 0.10 M LiTFSI, LiClO4, and TBAClO4 dissolved in PC, as well as 0.10 M LiTFSI in DME, indicated by a deviation of only a few mV from the criteria of reversibility, 60 mV. Electrochemically oxidizing TDT appears quasireversible when LiClO4 or TBAClO4 served as the electrolyte in DME, indicated by |E3/4 – E1/4| of 69 and 74 mV, respectively (Table 1). Considering the solubility of TDT is at least 1.0 M within 1.0 M LiTFSI in DME, we tested the reversibility of electrochemically oxidizing TDT at concentrations up to 1.0 within this electrolyte and found that increasing the concentration of TDT only produced a small change in |E3/4 – E1/4|, which varied from 59 to 64 mV (Table 1). The UME data were further analyzed using the standard current-overpotential formalism, (43) in which a plot of log(current) vs potential approaches a singularity where the overpotential, η = 0 = Eox, the redox potential. Eox was found to be 4.21 V vs Li/Li+ and did not vary with the electrolyte. Additional voltammetry and analysis can be found in the Supporting Information (Figure S2).

    Bulk electrolysis can reveal side reactions, which may be difficult to detect due to the short time and length scale of CV experiments. Additionally, electrolyzing large volumes of solution via bulk electrolysis is a better approximation of RFB working conditions, compared to only interrogating the microscale volume of solution nearby a UME or disk working electrodes during conventional CV measurements. Bulk electrolysis of 0.005 M TDT dissolved within solutions containing 1.0 M of various electrolytes revealed significant cycling instability, which was not as apparent from the CV studies. Specifically, electrochemically oxidizing the entire solution at 4.35 V (charging), and subsequently reducing the solution at 3.95 V (discharging), resulted in Coulombic efficiencies (CE) of <99% (Table 1). CE values were calculated as the coulombs passed during discharging (Qdischarging) divided by the coulombs passed during charging (Qcharging). Bulk electrolysis cycling of 0.005 M ferrocene, a model compound with demonstrated stability, in 1.0 M LiClO4 and PC, was used as a benchmark for cycling performance. The CE of ferrocene was 99%, and therefore values of CE significantly lower than this indicate instability due to decay of TDT•+ through parasitic processes.
    Bulk electrolysis cycling of 0.005 M TDT in the presence of 1.0 M LiTFSI and PC resulted in 88% CE. Electrolyzing 0.005 M TDT in the presence of both perchlorate salts in PC gave lower CE values: 68% and 49% for LiClO4 and TBAClO4, respectively. The CE value obtained from bulk electrolysis of TDT in the presence of LiTFSI and DME, 85%, was comparable to those obtained within the solution of LiTFSI in PC solvent (Table 1). In the case of perchlorates, however, the results of TDT bulk electrolysis did not follow a similar pattern when the solvent was changed to DME: 84% CE when the electrolyte was LiClO4 in DME and <1% CE when the electrolyte was TBAClO4. Interestingly, bulk electrolysis cycling of TDT in the presence of both Li salts and DME produced comparable results, 84% CE and 85% CE for LiClO4 and LiTFSI, respectively.
    Due to having a relatively smaller ionic radius, Li+ experiences a higher surface charge density, compared to electrolyte anions. As a result, Li+ exhibits stronger association with polar solvent, whereas interactions between the anions and solvent are weaker. (45) The dielectric constants of PC and DME are 65 and 7, respectively. (45) In PC, the relatively high solvent dielectric promotes full solvation of the LiClO4 and LiTFSI salts, up to electrolyte concentrations of at least 1.0 M. (45) However, within the relatively lower dielectric media of the DME, intermolecular interactions between Li+ and anions, as well as other basic, electron rich moieties in the organic media, are more prevalent than in PC. (45) For example, an associative interaction is expected between Li+ and the alkoxy groups of DDB-type organic redox shuttles, (6) and similar interactions could occur with the methoxyether groups of TDT. Due to the high charge density on Li+ and poor screening by the low dielectric of the DME, these intermolecular interactions are expected to be more prevalent in the Li+-containing electrolyte, compared to TBA+.
    Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) studies show that chemical oxidation of TDT results in a radical cation, TDT•+ (Supporting Information, Figure S3). Intermolecular interactions between TDT•+, electrolyte, solvent, and even the neutral parent molecule could conceivably accelerate parasitic side reactions which cause radical decay or deter them. For example, the varying CE values for bulk electrolysis of TDT in different electrolytes are conceivably due to parasitic side reactions between the cation radicals and solution constituents, leading to radical decay and a subsequent reduction in Qdischarging and thereby CE. These parasitic reactions, such as the reaction with solvent, can be expected to influence intermolecular interactions: coordination of electrolyte cations to solvent or the neutral TDT parent molecule could deter a reaction with the TDT cation radical through either electrostatic repulsion or steric hindrance. Another way electrolyte may impact parasitic reactions is by directly modulating the stability of a solution component. Indeed, perchlorate anions are found to decrease the oxidative stability of PC (45, 46) and could hence promote decomposition pathways involving PC, which is a possible reason why the stability was found to decrease in the presence of perchlorates in PC (Table 1). Therefore, we are encouraged to further study how such intermolecular interactions may relate to the stability of TDT during electrochemical cycling.

    Monitoring Dynamic Structural Interactions Using in Situ Spectroscopy

    Determining how TDT interacts with solution constituents during cycling is an important step to understanding stability within the reaction mixture, especially given the dependence of stability on electrolyte seen in perfunctory bulk electrolysis tests (Table 1). We used SNIFTIRS to investigate the relationship between electrolyte and cycling stability because this technique reveals changes in vibrational energy modes of organic and inorganic species composing the electrochemical interface and therefore enables correlating structural evolutions with electrochemical reactions. (23, 24) Resolving these structural changes using traditional FTIR absorbance measurements is challenging because of the complexity of the reaction solution. The numerous organic functionalities present in the different solution components give rise to many overlapping peaks in the IR spectrum (Supporting Information, Figure S4), making interpretation of the spectral fingerprint difficult. Unlike conventional FTIR, the vibrational signatures obtained from SNIFTIRS experiments reflect only the vibrational modes that are changing during the course of the experiment, thereby simplifying the spectroscopic fingerprint. We selected LiClO4 in PC and DME and TBAClO4 in DME as the electrolytes for SNIFTIRS studies because these salts are more pure than LiTFSI, and the common anion enables controllably studying the impact of Li+ on the different stabilities observed in bulk electrolysis studies of these solutions. Because SNIFTIRS is compatible with the highly concentrated conditions required of grid-scale RFB materials, (3) we also chose to increase the concentrations of the active species above the millimolar concentrations used in CV and bulk electrolysis. The concentrations of electrolyte and TDT for the SNIFTIRS studies were 0.3 and 0.1 M, respectively. Concentrations higher than these were found to cause difficulties compensating for cell resistance in the in situ cell.
    Figure 2a–c shows the SNIFTIRS data correlating to the cyclic oxidation and reduction of TDT in LiClO4 and PC (Figure 2a), LiClO4 and DME (Figure 2b), and TBAClO4 and DME (Figure 2c). In these difference spectra, positively increasing absorbance peaks (pointing upward) are termed appearance peaks and denote the emergence of a new species or new vibrational modes at the electrochemical interface following an electrochemical reaction. Peaks displaying a negative absorbance intensity (pointing downward) are termed disappearance peaks and indicate consumption of a species or the eradication of vibrational modes present in the starting material following an electrochemical reaction. Considering the spectra in Figure 2a, corresponding to a solution of 0.1 M TDT in 0.3 M LiClO4 in PC, the rise of three prominent appearance peaks at 1420 cm–1, 1490 cm–1, and 1530 cm–1, as well as a less intense peak at 1270 cm–1, coincides with the onset of electrochemical oxidation of TDT around 4.20 V. The evolution of disappearance peaks at 1260 and 1510 cm–1 occurred simultaneously with the rising absorbance of the appearance peaks. As the potential sweep continues to higher potentials the intensity of these changes increase, and some additional appearance peaks having lower intensity become visible around 1370 and 1407 cm–1. Upon reversing the direction of the potential sweep and returning the working electrode potential to 4.10 V, these peaks diminished and a featureless baseline was observed again, indicating that the structural changes related to these spectral signals are reversible. The spectra measured in a solution of 0.1 M TDT in 0.3 M LiClO4 in DME (Figure 2b) display similarities to results obtained in PC (Figure 2a). Specifically, we again detected appearance peaks at 1420, 1490, and 1530 cm–1 and disappearance peaks at 1260 and 1530 cm–1, relating to the onset of electrochemical oxidation of TDT at 4.20 V. Unlike the spectra recorded in PC, the spectra for the DME solution displays an additional disappearance peak at 1220 cm–1. Some weakly absorbing peaks appear between 1300 and 1400 cm–1 in both solutions; however, these features are difficult to resolve and compare with good fidelity due to low signal intensity.
    The spectra measured in a solution containing 0.3 M TBAClO4 in DME with 0.1 M TDT (Figure 2c) differ slightly from those measured in solutions containing LiClO4 (Figure 2a,b). First and foremost, oxidizing TDT in the TBAClO4 solution did not produce a prominent disappearance peak at 1530 cm–1, in contrast to the results obtained in both LiClO4 solutions (Figure 2a,b). Second, a featureless spectrum was not obtained upon returning the potential to 4.10 at the end of the cycle, also in contrast to the results obtained in LiClO4. The persistence of these spectral features after returning the electrode potential to its initial value, 4.10 V, indicates irreversible reactions pursuant to the oxidation of TDT in the TBAClO4 and DME solution. These results, in particular, the lower signal intensity and evidence of irreversibility, are consistent with our earlier observations that electrochemical oxidation of TDT in the presence of DME and TBAClO4 is not reversible (Figure 1b and Table 1). Additional representations of the SNIFTIRS data can be found in the Supporting Information (Figure S5).
    Control experiments performed in solutions containing only the supporting electrolyte show only a featureless capacitance, indicating that the electrolyte is stable within the potential range used for SNIFTIRS studies (Supporting Information, Figure S6). This indicates that these spectral features can be assigned to structural changes related to electrochemically oxidizing the TDT to TDT•+ and that these changes are reversed upon the subsequent electrochemical reduction of TDT•+ to the starting material. Overall, the SNIFTIRS experiments show that electrochemically oxidizing TDT in the presence of Li+ is reversible in the time frame of this experiment; however, irreversibility and degradation of some solution components is evident in the presence of TBAClO4. These results agree with the earlier findings from electrochemical characterization (Table 1).

    Computational Simulations of Structural Changes Related to Cycling

    We used DFT calculations to predict the interactions between TDT, DME, and electrolyte ions that could take place during electrochemical cycling, when TDT is converted to and from TDT•+. We then compared simulations of vibrational energy with the SNIFTIRS data to guide our interpretation of the spectral signatures. We selected the possible reaction of TDT with Li+ in DME as the basis of these calculations because the higher solubility of TDT in DME is advantageous for applications of TDT as an active material in RFBs, and there is good reason (vide supra) to believe that intermolecular interactions with Li+ may be related to the higher CE observed for Li+-containing electrolytes in DME (Table 1).
    The ΔGsolv for forming the TDT–anion complexes was positive (Supporting Information, Table S1) and therefore a TDT–anion complex is unrealistic. The ΔGsolv for forming contact ion pairs between the TDT•+ and the anions following charging was also positive, indicating that TDT•+ and the electrolyte anions also prefer to be solvated in solution rather than forming close contact pairs. However, due to the large size of the TDT•+ cation, exactly calculating the structure of complexes of TDT•+ with other solution constituents is imprecise, and therefore, the energies of these complexes are superficial estimates (Supporting Information, Table S1). The size problem is encountered for predicting the structure of complexes involving TBA+, and therefore, we did not consider these interactions further. Optimized structural models (Supporting Information, Figure S7), ΔG of selected reactions in the gas (ΔGgas) and solution phase (ΔGsolv) (Supporting Information, Table S2), Eox of TDT–electrolyte complexes (Supporting Information, Table S1), and simulated vibrational energy spectra (Supporting Information, Figure S8) are provided in the associated content.
    Figure 3 illustrates the structure and corresponding ΔGgas and ΔGsolv for three possible complexes between TDT and Li+ in DME: DME–Li+–DME, DME–Li+–TDT, and TDT–Li+–TDT. The DME–Li+–DME complex is considered the starting point of the reaction (0.00 eV). Forming the TDT–Li+–DME complex via displacement of one DME from the DME–Li+–DME complex by TDT is thermodynamically favorable in solution (−0.02 eV). Subsequently replacing the remaining DME molecule by TDT and forming the TDT–Li+–TDT complex is slightly uphill in the solution phase (0.01 eV). Note that this reaction is thermodynamically downhill when using gas phase approximation (ΔGgas = −0.12 eV); thus, these complexes could conceivably exist in some equilibrium in solution. The calculated equilibrium constants (ΔG = −RT ln k) favor forming the TDT–Li+–DME complex in DME. Given the error of the calculation and the favorable ΔGgas for forming the TDT–Li+–TDT complex, consideration of this structural model can be justified with discretion. Note that 1,2-dimethoxybenzene (veratrole) displays a strong association with Li+, similar to 12-crown-4 ether, a compound with proven affinity for extracting Li+ ions from solvents including water. (47-50) As TDT contains a veratrole group, it is rational that it could display a similar affinity for Li+.
    The Eox values of bare TDT, TDT–Li+, TDT–Li+–DME, and TDT–Li+–TDT are 3.92, 4.29, 4.22, and 4.33 V, respectively. Interestingly, the calculated redox potential of bare TDT (3.92 V) was significantly lower than experimentally observed redox potentials (see Figure 1), and the TDT–Li+–TDT, TDT–Li+–DME, and TDT–anion models (Supporting Information, Table S1) when the water dielectric was used to approximate the solvation effects of PC. When the lower dielectric constant of DME was used to approximate the effects of solvation, the calculated redox potential of bare TDT is 4.23 V, which is not significantly different from the electrolyte-containing models, and agrees with experiments (see Figure 1). However, using the dielectric constant of DME to approximate solvation effects for the other TDT models gave unrealistic results, namely, Eox values that were more positive of 5.00 V, a potential that lies beyond that of the electrolyte stability limit. Experimentally, changing the solvent from PC to DME shifted Eox by only ca. 15–30 mV (Figure 1a,b and Supporting Information, Figure S2). One possible cause of the anomalous effect of solvent dielectric on the predicted potentials could be explicit solvent and electrolyte interactions beyond those explored here.
    Considering the results of the electrochemistry and SNIFTIRS experiments, which indicate enhanced stability of TDT in the presence of Li+, we explored the effects of potential cycling on the structural interaction between Li+ and TDT in more detail. Due to the size of these calculations, we did not compute analogous reaction sequences using PC as the solvent. The calculated Eox of the lithium-coordinated TDT models, TDT–Li+, TDT–Li+–DME, and TDT–Li+–TDT, are all in agreement with the experimentally observed Eox for TDT, 4.21 V, given the uncertainty of the DFT values (±0.2 V). Thus, differentiating the exact structure of the active species is not possible based on electrochemical and DFT data alone but can be achieved by further exploring the reaction mechanism and comparing this with SNIFTIRS data. From the computed ΔGsolv (Supporting Information, Table S2) and Eox (Supporting Information, Table S1), we propose a detailed sequence of reactions to explain the structural evolution of two models, TDT–Li+–DME (Figure 4) and TDT–Li+–TDT (Figure 5), during electrochemical cycling. In short, these reaction cycles consist of complexation, dissociation, and electrochemical reactions. Figure 4 shows the predicted process for electrochemically oxidizing the TDT–Li+–DME model. Step 1 represents formation of TDT–Li+–DME from the precursors, DME–Li+–DME and neutral TDT (ΔG = −0.02 eV). TDT–Li+–DME undergoes one electron electrochemical oxidation at Eox = 4.22 V and forms a cation radical, TDT•+–Li+–DME (step 2). Coordination between TDT•+ and Li+ within the TDT•+–Li+–DME structure is not favorable, and thus TDT•+ dissociates from the TDT•+–Li+–DME complex (ΔG = −0.28 eV for the dissociation step), after which the Li+–DME complex likely coordinates to a nearby DME molecule (step 3). Finally, electrochemical reduction of TDT•+ gives way to neutral TDT (step 4), completing the cycle.
    Figure 5 shows the predicted reaction sequence for the electrochemical oxidation of the TDT–Li+–TDT model in DME. Following the one electron electrochemical oxidation of the TDT–Li+–TDT complex at Eox = 4.33 V (step 1), dissociation of the TDT•+–Li+–TDT complex is favorable. In step 2a, the TDT•+–Li+–TDT complex partially dissociates into TDT•+ and a TDT–Li+ complex which forms a TDT–Li+–DME complex in the presence of a DME. The ΔG for the dissociation of the TDT•+ is −0.44 eV. Alternatively, the TDT•+–Li+–TDT complex can completely dissociate into TDT•+, a neutral TDT molecule, and Li+, which is then solvated by two free DME molecules. The ΔG for this step is −0.73 eV. The TDT•+–Li+–TDT dissociation products, TDT–Li+–DME and uncoordinated TDT, are predicted to undergo electrochemical oxidation at Eox = 4.22 V (step 2c) and 3.92 V (step 2d), respectively. These potentials are less positive than the calculated Eox for electrochemical oxidation of TDT–Li+–TDT, and along with the large negative ΔG accompanying the subsequent dissociation events, we predict that steps 2a through 2d will occur spontaneously following the electrochemical oxidation of TDT–Li+–TDT in step 1. Therefore, electrochemically oxidizing TDT–Li+–TDT results in two TDT•+, which are subsequently electrochemically reduced to neutral TDT molecules, and again form the complexes with Li+.

    Computation-Guided Interpretation of SNIFTIRS Data

    We compared simulated difference spectra, generated from the DFT reactions (Figures 4 and 5), to the data from SNIFTIRS experiments to guide the interpretation of the spectroscopic signature. The simulated spectra were created by using the computed vibrational spectra of the neutral models TDT, TDT–Li+–DME, and TDT–Li+–TDT as the baseline for the spectrum of the oxidized model, TDT•+ (Supporting Information, Figure S8). This is analogous to the subtractive normalization applied in the SNIFTIRS experiments which generates the difference spectra. The resulting difference spectrum can be considered a spectroscopic fingerprint of the electrochemical reaction, having a unique pattern of appearance and disappearance peaks relating to the structural evolution occurring with cycling. Simulated difference spectra corresponding to three possible electrochemical reactions are compared to the measured spectra at 4.40 V (Figure 6). Note that these plots have all been rescaled on the y-axis to allow for easier qualitative comparison between the simulated and measured spectra. This was done by normalizing each spectrum to the most intense peak between 1400 and 1450 cm–1. In each simulation, the starting structure is one of three neutral models of TDT, and the predicted product of electrochemical oxidation is TDT•+. The simulated difference spectrum in Figure 6a is representative of the reaction TDT → TDT•+, in which Li+ does not chelate with the neutral TDT prior to electrochemical oxidation. Likewise, the simulated difference spectra in Figure 6b–c correspond to the reactions TDT–Li+–DME → TDT•+ and TDT–Li+–TDT → 2TDT•+, respectively. The spectroscopic fingerprints of the simulated difference spectra obtained from the Li+-coordinated models (Figure 6b,c) were well-matched to the experimental SNIFTIRS data (Figure 6d) within the error of the calculation (±60 cm–1), whereas the simulated spectrum for the uncoordinated TDT (Figure 6a) did not have as many features in common with the experimental data and most importantly lacks the strong disappearance peak between 1500 and 1550 cm–1 which is present in both the Li+-coordinated models and the experimental data. A more detailed discussion of the structural changes associated with the vibrational modes reflected in Figure 6 is included with the associated content (Supporting Information, Figure S8). Overall, these simulations clearly indicate that coordination of Li+ to TDT (Supporting Information, Figure 7a) can lead to the pronounced differences in IR absorption modes between 1200 and 1700 cm–1 for reactions of coordinated and bare TDT. In particular these results can explain the intense disappearance peak between 1500 and 1550 cm–1, which was observed experimentally in SNIFTIRS data at 4.40 V when LiClO4 was used as the supporting electrolyte (Figure 2a,b and Figure 6d).
    We also predicted the vibrational spectra corresponding to DME–Li+–DME (Supporting Information, Figure S8c) but did not include this species in our comparison of the SNIFTIRS spectra to the simulated difference spectra for several reasons. First, the measured difference spectra correlate with the electrochemical oxidation of TDT, so there is good reason to use the electrochemical reaction for the simulating the spectral fingerprint. Second, the predicted vibrational energies for DME–Li+–DME do not differ significantly from TDT–Li+–DME and TDT–Li+–TDT, and additionally, the predicted intensities of the vibrational modes for for DME–Li+–DME are significantly lower than those of TDT–Li+–DME and TDT–Li+–TDT. Therefore, including the DME–Li+–DME complex within the simulation does not introduce significant changes to the spectral fingerprint.
    To further validate the affinity of TDT for coordinating to Li+, we demonstrated Li+ uptake by TDT as follows: 0.001 mol of LiTFSI remained as an insoluble salt after mixing with 10 mL of DCM, a poor solvent for Li salts. We found that the entirety of the salt mass readily dissolved upon subsequently adding 0.002 mol of TDT to the same solution (Supporting Information, Figure S9).

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    If paired with a hybrid Li-graphite anode such as those used in previously reported studies, a flow battery employing TDT dissolved in DME could, in theory, achieve energy densities as high as 111 Wh/L, based on a 1.0 M concentration of TDT and the observed redox potential, 4.21 V vs Li/Li+. Despite results from CV studies indicating TDT displays ideal reversibility, bulk electrolysis cycling revealed significant degradation. More specifically, electrolyzing TDT in Li+-containing electrolyte solutions resulted in improved cycling stability, compared to cycling studies conducted in TBA+-containing electrolyte solutions. Accordingly, the structural changes accompanying electrochemical oxidation of TDT required closer examination in an effort to uncover interactions between TDT, electrolyte, and solvent, which impact stability. Using a combined DFT and in situ spectroscopic approach, we determined that neutral TDT forms a coordination complex with Li+, which dissociates upon electrochemical oxidation. Taken together, these results demonstrate that the interaction between the active material and Li+ may be an advantageous design strategy for stabilizing TDT, as this does not involve adding bulky functional modifications. Further investigations are underway to examine the role of the electrolyte and the solvent in the pathways of decomposition and the strength of electrolyte interactions in different solvents, as well as possible interactions between TDT and other cations, including multivalent cations, of relevance to organic energy storage systems.
