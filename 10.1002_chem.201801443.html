Abstract

Better together: The C−C coupling of redox-active phenoxyl radicals and electron-deficient perylene imides leads to two remarkably stable bifunctional radicals with cooperatively enhanced performances for non-aqueous organic redox-flow batteries (see figure).

Abstract

The search for high-performance organic redox-active materials for non-aqueous redox-flow batteries remains a key challenge. Organic radicals and aromatic imides are two promising classes of redox-active materials with complementary advantages, such as the specific capacity, operating voltage, and stability, etc. Herein, this work reports two stable bifunctional radicals synthesized by the C−C coupling of redox-active phenoxyl radicals and perylene diimides (PDIs, 1.) or benzo[ghi]perylene triimides (BPTIs, 2.). The incorporation of electron-deficient PDIs or BPTIs into phenoxyl radicals is desired, to not only increase the number of redox-active groups per molecule and, thus, improve their specific capacities, but also to increase the redox potential and the stability of the phenoxyl radicals and, thus, enhances their battery voltages and cycle lives. When serving as the redox-active species in the catholyte of a non-aqueous static redox-flow battery, both radicals 1. and 2. exhibited a cooperatively enhanced performance with an unprecedented initial discharge voltage up to 3.12 V versus Li+/Li, which is the hitherto most presentable potential for imide- and radical-based energy storage materials in redox-flow batteries.

Introduction

One of the hurdles to achieving an increased share of intermittent renewables at the grid scale is the need to provide a stable energy output to the end-users. This will require robust energy storage devices to deliver stable and flexible electricity that adheres to accepted standards for voltage and frequency, whether grid-connected or off-grid.1 Over the past decades, a number of energy storage technologies has been introduced and successfully demonstrated, including thermal (e.g., latent or sensible heat), mechanical (e.g., pump hydro, compressed air), and chemical/electrochemical (e.g., fuel cells and rechargeable batteries) ones. Amongst these technologies, rechargeable batteries composed of an electrode, a separator, and an electrolyte are attractive because they can be installed anywhere, free from geological/geographical restrictions.2 However, one of the serious issues for common stationary rechargeable batteries is that most of the electrode materials are highly soluble in electrolyte.

To solving this issue, a new energy storage technology named redox-flow batteries (RFBs) has emerged, which can store energy in soluble active materials dissolved in liquid-phase electrolytes.3 At present, most RFBs are based on aqueous active materials, but they suffer from low chemical reactivity and narrow electrolyte-stable redox potential window during practical charge/discharge processes.4 Therefore, researchers turned their eyes to non-aqueous organic systems because of the abundant availability and tunable structures. Until now, many redox-active organic molecules (ROMs) have been employed as active materials for non-aqueous RFBs, such as quinones, imides, alkoxybenzenes, and stable radicals.5 Particularly, radical ROMs possess a high redox potential up to 3.0 V, which is beyond all comparisons.6 Owing to the protection by two bulky tert-butyl groups at the ortho positions, 2,6-di-tert-butyl phenoxyl radicals are recognized as one of the most stable radical moieties, which have already been introduced into insoluble polymers to serve as ROMs for conventional lithium-ion radical batteries, exhibiting a presentable operating voltage of approximately 2.6 V.7 To find applications in RFBs, and simultaneously increase the operating potentials and stabilities, the 2,6-di-tert-butyl phenoxyl radical group is advised to be incorporated into electron-deficient π scaffolds because the incorporation of electron-withdrawing functional groups is demonstrated to increase the operating voltages of ROMs, and the extension of π systems is anticipated to disperse their free electrons and thus, enhance their stabilities.

Aromatic imides are an attractive class of electron-deficient π scaffolds bearing a certain number of redox-active carbonyl groups, which have already been demonstrated as promising ROMs for organic lithium-ion batteries.8 The incorporation of the 2,6-di-tert-butyl phenoxyl radical group into these unique π scaffolds affords bifunctional species with two classes of redox-active functional groups: carbonyls and phenoxyl radicals, which is anticipated to not only achieve larger specific capacities, but also increase their redox potentials, electronic conductivities, stabilities, and cycle lives in charge/discharge processes. Herein, we report that it was indeed viable. For the realization of this concept, the highly soluble 4-hydroxy-2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl-substituted perylene diimide (1, Scheme 1) and its lateral-extended benzo[ghi]perylene triimide (2) were chosen. In order to enhance the solubility, both compounds were terminated by bulky 2,6-di-tert-butyl phenyl groups at the imide positions. According to previous reports,9 1 and 2 are anticipated to easily produce the target bifunctional radicals 1. and 2. through mild chemical/electrochemical reactions. Due to the cooperative effect, when 1 and 2 were used as redox-active materials in non-aqueous static organic-lithium redox-flow batteries, both of them exhibited an unprecedented initial discharge voltage up to 3.12 V versus Li+/Li. In addition, because of the extension of the π systems and the incorporation of another two electron-withdrawing and redox-active carbonyl groups, the radical 2. exhibits a high initial discharge specific capacity retention of 76.7 % after 100 cycles at 0.2 mA cm−2 as well as an energy efficiency of approximately 88.9 %, which is much better than those of 1..

Experimental Section

Materials and measurements

Perylene 3,4,9,10-teteracarboxylicacid dianhydride (PTCDA, 98 %), 2,6-diisopropylaniline (97 %), ethanol (analytical reagent grade), propionic acid (analytical reagent grade), dimethyl sulfoxide (analytical reagent grade), iodoethane (C2H5I, analytical reagent grade), 2,6-di-tert-butylphenol (98 %), maleic anhydride, p-chloranil, hydrochloric acid, silver trifluoromethanesulfonate (AgOTf, 98 %), and graphite felt were obtained from commercial suppliers. All chemicals and reagents were used as received unless otherwise stated. Potassium carbonate (K2CO3) and graphite felt was dried under vacuum at 100 °C for 24 h. The intermediates N,N′-bis(2,6-diisopropylphenyl)-1,6,7,12-tetrachloro-3,4:9,10-perylenetracarboxdiimide (3) and benzo[ghi]perylene 1,2,4,5,10,11-hexacarboxylic trianhydride (4) were prepared according to the literature.10

Nuclear magnetic resonance spectra were recorded on a Bruker Avance 400 spectrometer, with working frequencies of 400 MHz. Chemical shifts are given in parts per million (ppm) and referred to TMS as internal standard. 1H coupling constants J are given in Hertz (Hz). High-resolution mass spectra (HRMS) were acquired on a Thermo Scientific Exactive Plus Mass spectrometer equipped with an electrospray ionization (ESI) source. The UV/Vis/NIR spectra were recorded on a Perkin–Elmer Lambda 900 spectrometer equipped with a PTP-1 Peltier temperature controller and steady-state emission spectra were acquired by using an Edinburgh Instruments FLS920 spectrometer. Electron spin resonance (ESR) measurements were performed on a Bruker model E-500 electron paramagnetic resonance spectrometer at room temperature.

X-ray diffraction analyses

Single crystals of compound 1 suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were obtained by dissolving each of them in a solvent mixture (dichloromethane/ethanol 90:10) followed by slow evaporation of the solvents within several days. However, for the lateral-extended compound 2, the single crystals obtained were too small to be measured in spite of several attempts by changing the solvents and methods (evaporation and diffusion methods).

Suitable single crystals of 1 were mounted on a glass fiber for the X-ray measurement. Diffraction data were collected on a Rigaku-AFC7 equipped with a Rigaku Saturn CCD area detector system. The measurement was made by using graphic monochromatic MoKα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å) at 153 K under a cold nitrogen stream. The frame data were integrated and an absorption correction by using the Rigaku CrystalClear program package was done. All calculations were performed with the SHELXTL-97 program package,11 and structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares against F2. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically and hydrogen atoms of the organic ligands were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms. Crystals of 1 had voids with badly disordered solvents and the crystallographic refinement for them was completed with the solvent contribution subtracted from the data by using SQUEEZE in the PLATON package of the crystallographic software.12 CCDC 1830417 (1) contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre.

Fabrication of organic–lithium flow batteries and electrochemical measurements

The non-aqueous electrolyte preparation and the organic-lithium redox flow cell assembly were completed inside an argon-filled glovebox (H2O content: 0.35 ppm, O2 content: 0 ppm) under an Ar atmosphere into half lithium batteries by using CR2032 coin cell hardware. The RFBs electrolyte was prepared by dissolving 1 or 2 and lithium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiTFSI) (battery grade) in a mixture of 1,3-dioxacyclopentane/1,2-dimethoxyethane (DOL/DME, 1:1 by volume) at room temperature, with concentrations of 0.03 m for 1 or 2 and 1.0 m for LiTFSI. The battery was assembled with a 0.25 cm thick graphite felt disk (SGL Carbon Group, Germany) soaked with above electrolyte (0.05 mL) as working electrode and a piece of lithium disk as counter electrode, with a porous polypropylene film as the separator (Celgard 2400, USA) in between.13 The whole assembly was subsequently sealed in the battery compartment. The current rates for the galvanostatic measurements were calculated based on the theoretical specific capacity of each compound.

The solution-state cyclic voltammetry (CV) experiments were carried out at room temperature in argon-purged solutions of the electrolyte and recorded on an electrochemical workstation (CHI 660D, CH Instruments, China). All solution-state CV experiments were performed by using a glassy carbon disk as the working electrode, a Pt wire as the counter electrode, and a Ag/AgCl or Ag/AgOTf (10 mm in DME/DOL 1:1 by volume) electrode as the reference electrode. The concentration of the sample and the supporting electrolyte (LiTFSI) were 0.5 mm and 1.0 m, respectively. The cyclic voltammograms of the batteries composed of 1 or 2 were conducted on two-electrode cells with a lithium disk as the counter electrode and cycled within a voltage window of 1.65–3.5 V (vs. Li/Li+) under a scan rate of 0.1 mV s−1 on an electrochemical workstation (IVIUM, Netherlands). The galvanostatic charging/discharging experiments were measured on a Neware battery testing system (BTS7.5.6, Shenzhen, China) in the potential range 1.65–3.5 V vs. Li/Li+ for the batteries containing 1 or 2 at varies current densities. The reversibility of the redox reactions were validated by UV/Vis/NIR spectroscopy. Samples were prepared in a glovebox under the protection of Ar. Electrolyte was used as solvent to dissolve both the discharged and recharged forms of the active materials for UV/Vis/NIR measurements. All electrochemical testing was conducted at room temperature.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis and characterization

Following the reported synthetic process14a as deposited in Scheme 2, a facile nucleophilic substitution of the 1,6,7,12-tetrachlorinated perylene diimide (PDI) 3 with excess amounts of carbon nucleophilic 2,6-di-tert-butylphenolate ions in dimethyl sulfoxide gave mono(4-hydroxy-2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl)-substituted PDI 1 in 36.3 % yield. It is not surprising that the monosubstituted PDI 1 but no multi-substituted PDIs were obtained as the major product because of a SRN1 reaction mechanism and steric hindrance. In such radical sequence, besides the expected carbon nucleophilic substitution reaction, the starting material 3 and possible intermediates could also undergo a partial or complete hydrodebromination reduction reaction in the presence of hydrogen donors, such as trace amounts of water in solution. Indeed, the deprotonation of 2,6-di-tert-butylphenol under the present conditions in situ yielded half equivalent of water, which implies that the hydrodebromination is inevitable. At the same time, the steric hindrance exacerbates the hydrogen abstractions but hampers the carbon nucleophilic substitution. Similarly, the carbon nucleophilic substitution of the 2,7-dichlorinated benzo[ghi]perylene triimide (BPTI) 6 with excess amounts of 2,6-di-tert-butylphenolate ions afforded the mono(4-hydroxy-2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl)-substituted BPTI 2 as the only substituted product in 24.5 % yield.

The biaryl structures of 1 and 2 with a C−C coupling reaction to one 4-hydroxyl-3,5-di-tert-butylphenyl unit at the bay position is initially confirmed by their 1H NMR spectra (see Figures S3 and S7 in the Supporting Information), exhibiting a sharp singlet close to δ=5.48 and 5.60 ppm for 1 and 2, respectively, which is unequivocally assigned to the free proton of the hydroxyl group in the substituents. This structural feature was further corroborated by their 2D 1H,1H COSY (correlation NMR spectroscopy) NMR, 13C NMR, and mass spectra (see Figures S4, S8, S9, S10, S12, and S14 in the Supporting Information) as well as single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses (Figure 1 a). Due to the perpendicular bulky 2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl groups at the imide positions and the 4-hydroxy-2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl moiety at the bay position, almost no π–π interactions have been observed in the crystal of 1 (Figure 1 b). As a result, both 1 and 2 are highly soluble in various organic solvents, including organic electrolytes, which is particularly vital for their applications in non-aqueous organic–lithium redox-flow batteries.

Solution properties

The optical properties of the neutral compounds 1 and 2 in dichloromethane have been explored by UV/Vis/NIR absorption and fluorescence spectroscopy (see Figure 2 a). Due to the limited influence of the substituents at the imide positions on the optical properties of the PDIs, almost the same absorption spectrum (λmax=534 nm) was observed for 1 compared to the reported PDIs bearing the same substituents at the bay positions.14a Similarly, a broad and intensive absorption with an absorption maxima at λ=472 nm ( =21 186 cm−1) was observed for 2, which is shifted bathochromically by 5 nm (227 cm−1) in comparison to that of the parent BPTI without bay substituents (absorption maximum at λ≈467 nm).14 However, from the single-crystal structure of 1 shown in Figure 1 a, the PDI skeleton is almost not distorted. Accordingly, the broad band of 1 and 2 with less vibronic fine structure throughout the whole visible range is not governed by the distortion of the aromatic system evoked by sterical congestion in the bay area, but more likely by a weak intramolecular photoinduced charge-transfer (PICT) transition from the phenyl moieties to the electron-deficient PDI or BPTI core. Similar conclusions can be drawn from the fluorescence spectra of 1 and 2 that exhibit broad emission bands with large Stokes shifts at λ=693 and 670 nm, and fluorescence quantum yields of only 11.5 and 9.1 %, respectively.

The electrochemical properties of 4-hydroxy-2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl-substituted 1 and 2 in dichloromethane have been investigated by cyclic voltammetry technique (see Figures 2 b and S19 in the Supporting Information). Although the results obtained in dilute solution are not representative of their electrochemical behaviors in an organic–lithium redox-flow battery, they provide valuable information on the number of redox events and their reversibility. As expected from the design principle, owing to the introduction of the 4-hydroxy-2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl substituent at the bay position, three reversible one-electron waves with half-wave potentials of −0.55, −0.73, and −0.85 V versus Ag/AgCl were observed in the CV pattern of 1, which are supposed to result from the formation of a substituted phenoxyl radical 1., a biradical 1..− with a phenoxyl radical and a charge-delocalized radical anion as well as a dianion radical 1.2− with a phenoxyl radical and a charge-delocalized dianion. Although the reported first reduction potentials for common bay-unsubstituted PDIs to their charge-delocalized radical anion PDIs are around −0.52 V,15 which is slightly larger than those of phenols to phenoxyl radicals (−0.60 V),16 in this case, the C−C coupling between the electron-donating 4-hydroxy-2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl substituent and the electron-withdrawing PDIs leads to reversed reduction potentials between the phenol substituent and the PDI skeleton. Accordingly, the phenol substituent in 1 is first oxidized into its phenoxyl radical during the electrochemical reaction. To our surprise, the oxidized phenoxyl radical substituent has a serious influence on the second reduction potential corresponding to 1./1..−, which is remarkably decreased to −0.73 V in comparison to those of the bay-unsubstituted PDIs (−0.52 V), but slight on the third reduction potential for 1..−/1.2− (−0.85 V). Similar CV changes could also be observed in the CV pattern of 2, which exhibits four reversible one-electron waves with half-wave potentials of −0.54, −0.78, −0.87, and −1.44 V versus Ag/AgCl. Except for the first three reduction potentials assigned to 2., 2..−, and 2.2−, the last is attributed to the formation of a trianion radical 2.3− with a phenoxyl radical and a charge-delocalized trianion. Due to the low reduction potentials related to the 4-hydroxy-2,6-di-tert-butylphenyl substituents (−0.55 and −0.54 V for 1 and 2, respectively), both of them could also be easily reduced by a mild oxidizing agent (e.g., PbO2) to produce their radicals.

To examine the stability of 1. and 2., both of them were produced from the neutral 1 and 2 heterogeneously under an inert atmosphere at room temperature by adding PbO2 to the corresponding dichloromethane solution followed by filtration of the excess amount of oxidant, and the generated radicals have been characterized by UV/Vis/NIR absorption and electron spin resonance spectroscopy (Figures 2 c and d). In contrast to the absorption spectrum of 1. with typical vibronic progressions and absorption maxima at λ=482, 530, 590, and 633 nm, the absorption spectrum of the lateral-extended 2. exhibits sharper and more intense bands with absorption maxima at λ=380, 442, and 472 nm. Obviously, such hypsochromic shifts and different absorption band shapes are related to the fusion of the electron-withdrawing maleimide units to the perylene core, which is consistent with the higher redox potentials of 2 in the previous electrochemical experiments. Similarly, the radical 2. shows a well-resolved EPR signal with a doublet-fine structure centered at giso=2.0047, which is much different from that of 1. with a broad signal with giso=2.0045. Also because of the incorporation of a more electron-deficient imide into the phenoxyl radical, the time-dependent UV/Vis/NIR absorption spectral changes reveal that 2. is more stable than 1. when exposed to air (Figure S16 in the Supporting Information). It is worth pointing out that such stable radicals are of great importance for the achievement of high-potential and long-cycle-life redox-flow batteries.

Redox-flow battery properties

Motivated by the high solubility and stability of 1. and 2. in organic solvents, both of them should be ideal active materials for redox-flow battery applications. The RFB electrolytes were prepared by dissolving the neutral 1 or 2 and LiTFSI in 1,3-dioxolane and 1,2-dimethoxyethane (1:1) at room temperature, with concentrations of 0.03 m for 1 or 2 and 1.0 m for LiTFSI. The available redox reactions and their reversibility of them have been first investigated by cyclic voltammetry technique in a static lithium redox-flow battery, which was assembled with a 0.3 cm thick graphite felt disk soaked with 0.2 mL of the above electrolyte as working electrode and a piece of a lithium disk as counter electrode, with a polypropylene separator (Celgard 2400, USA) in between. However, it should be noted that Li metal is defective as an anode owing to the dendrite formation issue. The development of alternative anode materials is of great significance for battery commercialization. Recent research indicates that Li metal can be replaced by graphite5a or some organic redox-active species (e.g., 5,10,15,20-tetraphenylporphyrin)3e to make a full battery without safety concerns. As shown in Figures 3 a and b, one set of sharp peaks at approximately 2.50 V versus Li/Li+ accompanied with an unresolved wave at approximately 3.10 V for 1, as well as another shapeless wave at around 1.82 V for 2 has been observed from their CV patterns in static cells. In the light of their redox processes in dichloromethane and those of similar PDIs,15 the intense peaks centered at about 2.50 V for both compounds are assigned to the reduction waves of two longitudinal imide units, whereas the diffuse peak at low potential field (1.82 V) for 2 is assigned to the reduction of its lateral imide unit. Accordingly, the highest potentials at approximately 3.10 V correspond to the formation of their phenoxyl radicals. Notably, both CV shapes are largely different from those in the dilute solutions, but more like those in the solid states,15 implying that both ROMs should be presented as the aggregates during the CV measurements.

In agreement with the CVs in static lithium RFB cells, the galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of 1 and 2 exhibit unprecedented operating voltages around 3.10 V in the first cycle at a current density of 0.2 mA cm−2 (see Figures 3 c and d). Except for this short plateau at approximately 3.11 V assigned to the formation of the phenoxyl radical 1., the discharge process of 1 shows another long plateau at around 2.52 V, which results from the formation of the dianion radical 1.2− through consecutive one-electron reductions in one step (Figure 3 c). Reversibly, the oxidation of 1.2− resulted in two close plateau at about 3.15 and 2.60 V, respectively. The specific capacities of 1 during the first charge and discharge are 1.01 and 0.99 Ah L−1, which are 41.9 and 41.8 % of its theoretical capacity (2.41 Ah L−1), respectively. The low capacity ratio herein might be attributed to the large pore size (≈64 nm) of the polypropylene separator used, which is easily passed through for the small ROMs, such as 1 and its oligomeric aggregates. Similarly, for the lateral-extended 2, a voltage profile consisting of three distinct and smooth plateaus at 3.12, 2.42, and 1.79 V (Figure 3 d) has been observed in its discharge process, which corresponds to the formation of 2., 2.2−,and 2.3− species, respectively. Due to the π extension and the fusion of redox maleimide units at the bay position, the charge and discharge capacities of 2 in the first cycle (1.66 and 1.63 Ah L−1) as well as the carbonyl utilization rate (around 51.7 and 50.8 % corresponding to the theoretical capacity, 3.21 Ah L−1) are significantly increased in comparison with those of 1.

The performance superiority of the lateral-extended 2 in comparison with 1 is also expressed in their rate capability as well as cycling tests. As can be seen from the top row in Figure 4, although both ROMs maintain a prefect two-step galvanostatic charge/discharge profile at current densities of 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, and 1.0 mA cm−2, their capacities loose as the current density increased. 2 yields specific capacities of approximately 1.69, 1.46, 1.40, 1.25, and 0.94 Ah L−1 at current densities of 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, and 1.0 mA cm−2, respectively, which correspond to 52.6, 45.5, 43.6, 38.9, and 29.3 % of its theoretical capacity (3.21 Ah L−1), which is much higher than those of 1 (≈1.19, 0.72, 0.67, 0.63, and 0.58 Ah L−1 under the above-described current rates, corresponding to 49.3, 29.8, 27.8, 26.1, and 24.0 % of its theoretical capacity, respectively). More importantly, the different current cycle tests indicate that when cycled under these current rates for ten times, most of them remain rather stable specific capacities except for those under the lowest current (Figure 4 c). Due to the high stability of 2 in electrolytes, the capacity of 2 could restore up to 1.56 Ah L−1 (≈92.3 % of the initial value) as the current recovered to 0.05 mA cm−2, having obvious advantages compared with 1, which is further reflected in their long-time galvanostatic cycling experiments. As shown in Figure 4 d, when cycled at a current density of 0.2 mA cm−2, the battery, which uses 2 displays a rather stable charge/discharge curve with a retain capacity of 1.33 Ah L−1 after 50 cycles, and 1.25 Ah L−1 after 100 cycles, around 76.7 % of its initial discharge specific capacity, whereas that of 1 continues to give a more rapid decay with a left specific capacity of 0.57 Ah L−1 after 50 cycles, remaining 23.6 % of its initial discharge specific capacity.

The Columbic efficiency (CE) and the energy efficiency (EE) are two critical parameters for organic RFBs that guarantee the complete transformation of the system energy during battery discharge/charge processes. Figure 4 e depicts the CEs and EEs of RFBs, which use 1 (0.03 m) and 2 (0.03 m) under a current density of 0.2 mA cm−2. For both ROMs, the CEs are almost quantitative and have not faded out even after 100 cycles (99.5 and 99.3 % for 1 and 2, respectively). However, the EEs have a relatively serious decay with 90.2 and 88.9 % retentions after 100 cycles for 1 and 2, respectively. The energy transformation with such high CEs and EEs is rather rare in the available imide-based organic redox-flow batteries. In addition, the high rechargeability of 2 is further confirmed by the recovery of the UV/Vis/NIR spectra, which are identical to that of the pristine active material upon discharging and recharging of the batteries (Figure 4 f).

As shown in Figure 4 f, the UV/Vis/NIR spectrum for the redox-active component obtained from the battery containing 2 after the full discharging and recharging exhibits a vibronic-progressed band with three absorption maxima at λ=392, 431, and 487 nm, which is close to the absorptions for the phenoxyl radical 2., indicating the pristine and charge states of the redox-active component exist as 2.. However, these bands disappeared after discharging, accompanied by the appearance of new broad absorption bands at the long-wavelength region with multiple absorption maxima at λ=479, 521, 562, 608, 642, and 698 nm, as well as a weak NIR absorption at λ=885 nm, which may arise from the absorption of PDI dianions.17 Accordingly, together with the above CVs in solutions and in static RFBs, a plausible electrochemical redox mechanism for 1 and 2 can be proposed. As depicted in Scheme 3 a, the reduction process of 1 is proposed to proceed in two steps: the pristine phenoxyl radical 1. firstly obtains one electron and one lithium ion to form its phenolate lithium salt, which is further reduced at the two carbonyl groups of the PDI skeleton by two consecutive one-electron transfers to generate the final discharged state. Due to the incorporation of maleimide units at the bay position, except for the similar first two reductions at 3.12 and 2.42 V, a third one-electron reduction reaction takes place for 2 (Scheme 3 b), which corresponds to a reduction potential of 1.79 V and thus, forms the final discharged state of 2 bearing four lithium cations.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we have demonstrated a high-performance non-aqueous organic redox-flow battery based on two stable bifunctional radicals by conjugated C−C couplings between redox-active phenoxyl radical units and electron-deficient PDIs (1.) or BPTIs (2.). Due to the synergistic effect, both radicals exhibited an unprecedented initial discharge voltage up to 3.12 V versus Li+/Li when served as the redox-active species of the catholytes. Owing to the extension of the π systems and the incorporation of another two redox-active carbonyl groups at the bay positions, the radical 2. exhibits a high initial discharge specific capacity retention of 76.7 % after 100 cycles at 0.2 mA cm−2 as well as a high energy efficiency of approximately 88.9 %, which is the hitherto most presentable performance for imide- and radical-based energy storage materials in redox-flow batteries.
