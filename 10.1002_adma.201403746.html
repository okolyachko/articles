Abstract

A TEMPO-based non-aqueous electrolyte with the TEMPO concentration as high as 2.0 m is demonstrated as a high-energy-density catholyte for redox flow battery applications. With a hybrid anode, Li|TEMPO flow cells using this electrolyte deliver an energy efficiency of ca. 70% and an impressively high energy density of 126 W h L-1.

A redox flow battery (RFB) stores its energy in the redox-active materials dissolved in liquid electrolytes circulating between the external reservoirs and the cell compartments; this gives rise to its main advantages of decoupled energy and power and excellent scalability compared to traditional batteries, in which redox-active materials are confined to the solid state in electrodes.1 The cell voltage and the concentration of the active redox species in the electrolytes dictate the energy density of a RFB. Figure 1 shows the cell voltage and the effective molarity (the concentration multiplied by the number of electrons involved in the redox reactions) of most reported true aqueous and nonaqueous flow batteries, where the isograms represent the different energy density levels of 50, 100, and 200 Wh L−1, respectively. As shown in Figure 1, conventional aqueous flow batteries are generally low energy density systems (∼25 Wh L−1) due to the limited cell voltage (<1.8 V) and/or active material concentration.2, 3 On the other hand, although cell voltages of >2.0 V have been demonstrated in various nonaqueous flow systems, such as those based on all-organic active materials4 and redox non-innocent ligands,5 they have thus far failed to demonstrate a high energy density. This is mostly due to the low concentration of the active redox species, which usually is around 0.1 M or less, resulting in an energy density less than 10 Wh L−1.2 Even for the recently developed Li-S flow batteries, the energy density is compromised by the limited maximum achievable concentration of the soluble redox species, leading to an energy density of ∼30 Wh L−1.6 Although semi-solid Li-ion7 and hybrid Li metal redox flow batteries8, 9 offer inspiring cell designs, they are facing daunting challenges such as low utilization ratio, high system complexity, and difficulty to scale-up. Therefore, in order to achieve high-energy-density flow batteries, electrolytes with electroactive materials that have high concentrations and high redox potentials need to be developed.

Here we report a new nonaqueous electrolyte containing high concentrations of a stable, heterocyclic nitroxide radical compound, 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine-1-oxyl (TEMPO) as the electroactive material, dissolved with an excess amount of lithium hexafluorophosphate (LiPF6) as the supporting charge carrier in a solvent mixture of ethylene carbonate (EC)/propylene carbonate (PC)/ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC) at a weight ratio of 4:1:5. TEMPO is a red-orange solid with a melting point of 36–38 °C and can be sublimed under vacuum. The high stability of the nitroxide radical originates primarily from the following two structural features of TEMPO: (1) delocalization of the unpaired electron by conjugation over the N-O bond; and (2) steric restriction from the four methyl groups on adjacent carbons. The branch-type polymers of TEMPO, especially poly(2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidinyloxy-4-yl methacrylate) (PTMA) in which the methacrylate main chain bears the TEMPO unit in the side chains as the redox active sites, have been studied extensively in lithium batteries and organic radical batteries.10 The TEMPO-based polymeric cathode materials show desirable redox potential, high rate capability and good long-term cycling stability because of the fast, stable and reversible electrochemical redox reactions in nonaqueous electrolytes.11 In this work, TEMPO demonstrates a high solubility of 5.2 M in the EC/PC/EMC solvent, possibly related to the similarity in polarity with the solvent. Even with the presence of LiPF6, an electrolyte composition of 2.0 M TEMPO in the 2.3 M LiPF6/EC-PC-EMC supporting electrolyte can be successfully prepared. The Li-organic RFB flow cell system12 using the organic TEMPO catholyte paired with the Li-metal anode (denoted as Li|TEMPO) thus exceeds other RFBs in both concentration (2.0 M) and voltage (3.5 V), leading to a high theoretical volumetric energy density of 188 Wh L−1, as shown in Figure 1. At a TEMPO concentration of 2.0 M, we demonstrate that the Li|TEMPO flow cell delivers an energy density of 126 Wh L−1, about five times that of the aqueous all-vanadium flow battery. Typical properties of the TEMPO-based electrolytes with different compositions, including the density, viscosity, and ionic conductivity, are summarized in Supporting Information Table S1.

To identify the redox couples and evaluate the reversibility of the redox reactions, cyclic voltammograms (CV) were performed in an electrolyte of 0.005 M TEMPO and 1.0 M LiPF6 dissolved in EC/PC/EMC (4:1:5 by weight). TEMPO displays well-defined redox peaks in the potential range of 2.5–4.0 V at scan rates ranging from 10 to 500 mV s−1 (Figure 2a). A peak separation of ∼61 mV at 10 mV s−1 suggests one electron transfer between the nitroxide radical and the oxoammonium cation (Scheme 1). Shown in Figure 2b, the ratio of the cathodic and anodic peak currents is very close to 1 at all scan rates, indicating the excellent reversibility of the redox reaction. The CV curves of the 5th, 50th, 100th and 200th cycles at the scan rate of 50 mV s−1 almost completely overlap with each other (Figure 2c), demonstrating the high stability of the TEMPO redox couple. The high reversibility and stability of the redox reaction make TEMPO a promising candidate for flow batteries.

A lithium-graphite hybrid anode (described in detail in the Supporting Information, Note S2) and a fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) additive were adopted in the Li|TEMPO flow cell to synergistically protect the Li-metal electrode.13 The hybrid anode consists of electrically connected Li and graphite felt and provides good protection to the Li anode by introduction of Li+ ion intercalation/de-intercalation chemistry reducing involvement of Li metal in cell reactions.14 The Li|TEMPO flow cell using a catholyte of 0.1 M TEMPO in 1.0 M LiPF6 with 15 wt% FEC was able to operate at current densities up to 10 mA cm−2 with an energy efficiency (EE) above 75% (Figure 3a). Such an operational current density is significantly higher than those used in most nonaqueous flow systems, usually below 0.5 mA cm−2.15 The Coulombic efficiency (CE) of above 99%, indicating low self-discharge, is primarily ascribed to the highly stabilized solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI). Stable cycling performance of 100 cycles was obtained with a CE of 99%, a voltaic efficiency (VE) of 87% and an EE of 86% at 5 mA cm−2 (Figure 3b). In addition, the flow cell exhibited constant cyclic capacity throughout the 100 cycles with an average capacity retention of 99.8% per cycle. The cell efficiencies and the cycling stability of the Li|TEMPO flow battery greatly exceed those of recently reported nonaqueous flow chemistries delivering low EEs (∼60%) or plagued with serious capacity decay.4, 16

To fully demonstrate its potential, the TEMPO-based nonaqueous electrolytes at higher TEMPO concentrations were electrochemically tested in the Li|TEMPO flow cells, including 0.8 M TEMPO in 1.2 M LiPF6 (for details see Supporting Information Note S3 and Figure S1), 1.5 M TEMPO in 1.8 M LiPF6, and 2.0 M TEMPO in 2.3 M LiPF6, all with EC/PC/EMC (4:1:5 by weight) solvent mixture. The resulting voltage profiles as a function of the energy density are plotted in Figure 3c and the test results are summarized in Table 1. Because of the high viscosities of these electrolytes, the flow cells were cycled at relatively low current densities, e.g., 2.5 mA cm−2 at 1.5 M and 1.0 mA cm−2 at 2.0 M. Despite cycle durations at least eight times as long, the Li|TEMPO flow cells still demonstrated CEs above 84% and EEs close to 70% at both TEMPO concentrations. The volumetric charge and discharge energy densities are 170 and 119 Wh L−1 for the 1.5 M flow cell, and 183 and 126 Wh L−1 for the 2.0 M flow cell, respectively. The redox material concentration and the system energy density of the current Li|TEMPO flow battery are at least one order of magnitude higher than those of most reported nonaqueous flow batteries, which typically have 0.1 M concentration and less than 10 Wh L−1 energy density.4, 5, 9, 17 Although long-term cyclability is a challenge at high TEMPO concentrations due to the limitation of the Li-metal anode, the TEMPO-based nonaqueous electrolyte has demonstrated great potential for delivering high energy density in the Li|TEMPO flow battery.

The state-of-charge (SOC) of a flow battery is defined as the ratio between the oxidized/reduced species and the total redox-active species in the positive/negative electrolytes, e.g., the percentage of the oxoammonium species in the total redox species (TEMPO + oxoammonium) for the Li|TEMPO flow battery. Close monitoring of the SOC is of critical importance for redox flow batteries, to detect detrimental issues such as side reactions, overcharging, and electrolyte imbalance. Spectrophotometric or potentiometric methods have been used to measure the SOC for aqueous all-vanadium flow batteries.18 So far, no such techniques have been developed for nonaqueous flow batteries. Here we demonstrate for the first time a SOC-estimating method based on electron spin resonance (ESR) spectroscopy to detect the free radicals present in the TEMPO species.

During the charging process of a Li|TEMPO flow cell using 0.5 M TEMPO in 1.0 M LiPF6 with 15 wt% FEC, seven catholyte sample aliquots (∼50 μL) were extracted at the charge times of 0 (before charge), 6, 15, 28, 40, 45, and 64 (end of charge) minutes, respectively (Figure 4a) and were subjected to ESR measurements. Figure 4b shows the ESR spectra for the seven samples. At lower SOCs and thus higher radical concentrations, the ESR spectra are dominated by spin-spin interactions and substantially restricted dynamics due to the rigid lattice, which lead to broad peaks. The split, sharp peaks at lower radical concentrations (higher SOCs) represent a pronounced dynamic effect of sparsely populated radicals in the electrolyte solutions.19 The TEMPO concentrations of these samples were derived from comparing the peak area of the electrolyte samples with that of an externally prepared 0.5 M TEMPO solution in 1.0 M LiPF6 in EC/PC/EMC. In summary, a high-performance TEMPO-based nonaqueous electrolyte has been developed and evaluated in a hybrid Li|TEMPO RFB. The TEMPO redox material offers a high redox potential (3.5 V versus Li/Li+) and a high solubility (2.0 M in the supporting LiPF6 electrolyte), which are key parameters for an electrolyte to achieve high energy density in flow battery applications. The hybrid Li-graphite anode and the FEC SEI-stabilizing additive used to protect the Li anode led to good cyclability for the Li|TEMPO flow cells at 0.1 M and 0.8 M TEMPO concentrations. At 2.0 M TEMPO, the Li|TEMPO flow cell delivers an EE of ∼70% and a discharge volumetric energy density of 126 Wh L−1 that far exceeds most state-of-the-art aqueous and nonaqueous flow batteries. The flow battery tests of the TEMPO-based electrolyte have demonstrated great potential for offering high energy density; it is also highly adaptable to other nonaqueous redox flow chemistries—for example, as the catholyte in an all-organic flow battery. In addition, we demonstrated that ESR can be used to estimate the SOC for nonaqueous flow batteries when redox species with unpaired electrons are present.

Experimental Section

Materials: All chemicals were used as received without further purification. Lithium foil (99.9%, 0.75 mm thick) was purchased from Alfa Aesar. TEMPO (98%) was obtained from Sigma-Aldrich. EC, PC, EMC, FEC and LiPF6 in battery grade were procured from BASF.

Viscosity and Conductivity: The viscosity measurements for the TEMPO-based nonaqueous electrolytes were carried out on a Brookfield DV-II+ Pro viscometer (Brookfield Engineering Laboratories, Middleboro, MA). The ionic conductivity of the TEMPO electrolytes was measured on an Oakton CON 6+ conductivity meter (Oakton Instruments, Vernon Hills, IL). Both measurements were performed at 25 °C.

Cyclic Voltammetry: All the electrolyte preparations, CV tests, and flow cell assemblies were done in an argon-filled MBRAUN glove box (Stratham, NH) with H2O and O2 levels less than 1 ppm. The CV test was performed with a CHI760D Potentiostat (CH Instruments, Austin, TX) at scan rates of 500, 200, 100, 50, 20, and 10 mV s−1. A three-electrode configuration was used with a CHI102 platinum working electrode (CH Instruments), a Li-metal counter electrode and a Li-metal reference electrode.

Flow Cell Tests: The flow cells were assembled with a hybrid anode, a polyethylene-based porous separator, and a graphite felt (SGL Group, Germany) at the cathode side. The hybrid anode consisted of a Li foil and a graphite felt that were directly stacked. Both graphite felts had a compression ratio of ∼15% to ensure optimal electrical conductivity. The active size of the flow cell was 2 cm wide × 20 cm long. A static TEMPO electrolyte (11 mL) was added to the anode compartment and was not flowed, so it was not included in calculating the energy density. A flowing TEMPO electrolyte (13 mL) was circulated through the graphite felt cathode by using a MasterflexL/S peristaltic pump (Cole-Parmer, Vernon Hills, IL) at a flow rate of 50 mL min−1. Galvanostatic charge/discharge cycling was performed at room temperature on an Arbin BT-2000 battery tester (Arbin Instruments, College Station, TX).

ESR Measurements: The ESR measurements were performed using a Bruker Elexsys 580 spectrometer fitted with an SHQE resonator with microwave frequency ∼9.3 GHz (X band) at 20 mW power. All spectra were collected at room temperature using conventional 4 mm × 3 mm quartz ESR tubes. The radical concentration was calculated using a spin counting method in which the peak areas of electrolyte samples were compared with an externally prepared standard, 0.5 M TEMPO solution in 1.0 M LiPF6 in EC/PC/EMC.
