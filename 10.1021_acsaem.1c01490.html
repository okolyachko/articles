    Abstract

    Recent developments toward high-energy-density all-organic redox flow batteries suggest the advantageous use of molecules exhibiting multielectron redox events. Following this approach, organic anolytes are developed that feature multiple consecutive one-electron reductions. These anolytes are based on N-methylphthalimide, which exhibits a single reversible reduction at a low potential with good cycling stability. Derivatives with two or three imide groups were synthesized to enable multielectron reduction events. By incorporating suitably designed side chains, a volumetric capacity of 65 Ah/L is achieved in electrolyte solutions. Bulk-electrolysis experiments and UV–vis–NIR absorption spectroscopy revealed good cycling stability for the first and second reduction of monoamides and diimides, respectively, but a loss of stability for the third reduction of triimides. We identify N-2-pentyl-N′-2-(2-(2-methoxyethoxy)ethoxy)ethylaminepyromellitic diimide as a very promising multielectron anolyte with an excellent volumetric capacity and superior cycling and shelf-life stability compared to monoimides and triimides. The outstanding performance of this anolyte was demonstrated in proof-of-principle redox flow batteries that reach an energy density of 24.1 Wh/L.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    For a sustainable future, it is necessary to produce environment-friendly energy from sources such as wind and solar. (1−3) However, both are intermittent and dependent on seasons as well as on day- and nighttime, whereas energy demand does not necessarily follow these patterns. (2,4) To provide energy whenever it is needed and prevent the power grid from overloading, it will be essential to effectively store surplus energy. (2,5) Redox flow batteries (RFBs) are currently emerging for large-scale energy storage with operating installations up to 15 MW and a 200 MW battery system under construction. (6,7) Mostly, inorganic transition metals such as vanadium and halogens in aqueous solutions are used, which face several challenges. (3,8−10) A major issue for further development is the low energy density due to the limited cell voltage and limited solubility of the redox compounds. Additionally, active materials such as vanadium are relatively scarce and pose environmental concerns. A suitable alternative presents itself with all-organic nonaqueous RFBs. There is a wide range of suitable solvents, which can provide a 3–4 times wider electrochemical potential window than water. (3,11) Furthermore, in all-organic nonaqueous RFBs, active materials are carbon based and utilize abundant elements. However, one of the main challenges of organic redox-active materials is still their relative poor solubility and thus low volumetric capacity, especially in the presence of a supporting electrolyte salt. (10) Various techniques have been used to improve the molarity of redox-active materials in nonaqueous solvents, including chemical modification, (3) solid dispersions, (12) the use of additives, (13) or redox shuttles. (14) Simply increasing the concentration of active species is a two-edged sword. On the one hand, it enhances the volumetric capacity, but it often comes at the price of an unfavorable increase of electrolyte viscosity and increased cell resistance. Furthermore, it can result in the unwanted precipitation of salts. (15−19) Another approach is the use of multiredox materials and transfer of multiple electrons per molecule to improve capacity and circumvent some of the aforementioned drawbacks. To date, however, multiredox materials still face limitations in solubility and long-term stability. (20−22)
    Newly developed molecules are commonly evaluated for their suitability in RFBs by considering their volumetric capacity, which represents the number of storable charges per unit volume. The volumetric capacity is commonly expressed in [Ah/L] and is determined as C = nF[c], where n is the number of charges that can be stored per molecule, F the Faraday constant, and [c] the molar concentration. (5) Another important parameter for a battery is the energy density, expressed in [Wh/L], and given by E = CVbat, where Vbat is the voltage of the battery. Thus, it is essential to design molecules with high molar solubility in the solvent/electrolyte mixture. Techno-economical models have presented guidelines of around 100 Ah/L for the volumetric capacity of redox-active materials to present a viable alternative to aqueous technologies. (23,24) Recent work focuses on small molecules with single redox events. (8,9,15,16,18,19,25−28) However, molecules with multiple redox events would increase the volumetric capacity. (21,29−35) Importantly, this should not go at the cost of other parameters such as solubility.
    N-methylphthalimide was first proposed as an anolyte for use in aqueous RFBs by Zhen Li et al. and later in nonaqueous RFBs by Wei et al. (15,36) It shows good cycling performance with fast kinetics and a cell voltage of ∼2.3 V in combination with 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1-methoxy-4-[2′-methoxyethoxy]benzene as catholyte. (15) These results were achieved in a high viscosity and low conductive solution of lithium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiTFSI) in dimethoxyethane as a supporting electrolyte. The volumetric capacity was limited by the solubility of N-methylphthalimide. (25) Subsequently, various studies were published using phthalimides as the redox electroactive units in battery applications. (9,37−42) Recently, Zhao and Yu et al. published an eutectic mixture of N-butylphthalimide and 1,1-dimethylferrocene, to raise the solubility and achieve a record battery with a theoretical energy density of 24.1 Wh/L at 1 M concentration. (19)
    Here, we investigate a series of imide derivatives that exhibit multiple one-electron reduction events per molecule aimed at improving the volumetric capacity and performance in RFB applications. These molecules comprise a benzene core and contain one, two, or three imides and different solubilizing side chains. We use acetonitrile (MeCN) as a solvent because of its large electrochemical potential window, high dielectric constant, good solubilization of organic compounds, and low viscosity. (9,11,43) Tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate (TBAPF6) and N-ethyl-N,N-dimethyl-N-(2-methoxyethyl)ammonium bis(fluorosulfonyl)imide (N112.1O2FSI) are used as supporting electrolytes for their inertness, high ionic conductivity, and solubility. (11,25,44) Optimizing the molecular structures led to derivatives with a theoretical volumetric capacity of 65 Ah/L in acetonitrile containing N112.1O2FSI. Reduction potentials, electrochemical reversibility, and electron-transfer kinetics were determined using cyclic voltammetry (CV, Figure 1a). Cycling stability and shelf-life stability were evaluated at the half-cell level in bulk-electrolysis and UV–vis–NIR spectroscopy experiments. The best imides show excellent capacity retention in symmetric H-cell and flow cycling (Figure 1b). The optimized pyromellitic diimide-based anolyte achieves an energy density of 24.1 Wh/L in a RFB (Figure 1c) at 0.5 M concentration (equivalent to 1 M of transferable electrons) with a ferrocene derivative as catholyte. This represents the highest reported energy density for a prototype nonaqueous RFB and is similar to currently operating aqueous RFBs. (6,19,34,45)

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Molecules with a benzene core containing one (1a–g), two (2b–i), or three (3b–g) imide groups were synthesized from phthalic anhydride, pyromellitic dianhydride, or mellitic acid, respectively (Scheme 1). To achieve high solubility of the active compounds in electrolyte–solvent mixtures, various branched alkyl (b–d) and linear oligo(ethylene glycol) (e–h) side chains were considered. Branched alkyl chains create asymmetry and reduce π–π stacking interactions and thereby increase solubility. Nevertheless, the polarity of the electrolyte–solvent mixture ultimately limits increasing the solubility using aliphatic groups. Oligo(ethylene glycol) side chains increase the polarity and offer higher solubility in polar electrolyte solutions. (46−51) The different types of side chains were combined in asymmetric diimide 2i. The imide derivatives were synthesized in a straightforward, one-step condensation reaction with primary amines in an autoclave microwave reactor (Scheme 1). For the synthesis of compounds 3b–g, it is important to use strictly anhydrous conditions to obtain good yields. The synthetic procedures and characterization with NMR and mass spectroscopy are detailed in the Supporting Information.
    The solubility of the synthesized imide derivatives in acetonitrile was measured (Figure S1, Supporting Information) and used to determine the volumetric capacity (Figure S2, Supporting Information). While alkyl chains provide reasonably good solubility (>0.5 M) for the monoimide derivatives 1a–d, they are less effective for the di- and triimides. Oligo(ethylene glycol) side chains provide good solubility to these di- and triimides in acetonitrile. Interestingly, the diimides require longer oligo(ethylene glycol) side chains to maximize their solubility than the triimides. In general, the solubility in acetonitrile increases with an increasing number of ethylene glycol units in the side chain, up to the point where the molecule becomes a fully miscible liquid with acetonitrile at room temperature. Past this point, longer side chains cause a reduction of the maximum concentration due to an increased molar volume, which reduces the concentration of electroactive units. It is remarkable that for the most soluble mono-, di-, and triimide derivatives, the volumetric capacity is not too different. While this begs the question if multielectron anolytes present a real benefit over compounds with single redox events, highest volumetric capacities were obtained for 2i and 3g, which exhibit two and three reduction events, respectively.
    To obtain more realistic values for the achievable volumetric capacity, the electrolyte salt that provides counter ions in the redox reactions must be considered. Hence, the solubility of the most promising imide derivatives was determined in acetonitrile containing 1 equiv of TBAPF6 for every single-electron reduction event the molecule can sustain (Figure S3, Supporting Information). For all derivatives, the solubility dropped by a factor 2. This reduction in solubility matches values found in literature for other compounds. (16,25,52) With TBAPF6 in acetonitrile, the highest volumetric capacity is 50 Ah/L for compound 1f (Figure 2). The relatively low solubility of TBAPF6 in acetonitrile (1.8 M), compared to that of the imides, limits the volumetric capacity in several cases. (53) To circumvent this problem, the solubilities of 1f, 2g, 2i, and 3g for which TBAPF6 is clearly limiting the volumetric capacity, were determined in acetonitrile containing an ionic liquid electrolyte (N112.1O2FSI) (Figure S3, Supporting Information). (11,54) This increased the solubility and led to theoretical volumetric capacities of 56, 58, and 64 Ah/L for 1f, 2g, and 3g, respectively. By breaking the molecular symmetry and using asymmetric diimide (2i), the solubility increased to 65 Ah/L in N112.1O2FSI/MeCN (Figure 2). Another crucial reason for replacing TBAPF6 by N112.1O2FSI was the higher conductivity for the latter salt. Potentiostatic electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (PEIS) measurements in a flow cell using solutions containing either TBAPF6 or N112.1O2FSI, resulted in a more than 2 times lower solution resistance for the N112.1O2FSI system (Figure S4, Supporting Information).
    The electrochemical reduction potentials of the imide derivatives shown in Scheme 1 were determined using CV. Figure 3 shows cyclic voltammograms of compounds 1f, 2f, and 3f, representative for the mono-, di-, and triimide derivatives at different scan rates. Table S1 (Supporting Information) lists the reduction potentials for all imide derivatives. Compound 1f exhibits a quasi-reversible single-electron reduction at −1.88 V (versus ferrocene/ferrocenium (Fc/Fc+)) (Table 1). 2f exhibits two consecutive single-electron reduction waves at slightly higher potentials (−1.23 V, −1.84 V). 3f displays the two single-electron reductions at even higher potentials (−1.03 V, −1.64 V) and a third reduction event at a very low potential of −2.36 V. The average reduction potential per electron is E̅1/2 = −1.88, −1.56, and −1.68 V for 1f, 2f, and 3f respectively. The increase of the average reduction potential for multielectron derivatives lowers the energy density of batteries based on these multielectron anolytes. This energy loss is, however, negated if multielectron derivatives have a higher volumetric capacity. Slightly lower reduction potentials were found for the di- and triimide derivatives with glycol-based compared to aliphatic side chains (Table S1, Supporting Information), which is attributed to a weak inductive effect of oxygen atoms. No consistent trend was seen for the monoimide derivatives.

    CV also provides information about the electrokinetics and chemical reversibility in dilute solutions. The ratio of cathodic (ipc) and anodic (ipa) peak currents allows for an initial assessment of the reversibility of a redox process. Examination of the first and second reduction events reveals excellent chemical reversibility (Table 1) with ipc/ipa close to unity. The value drops for the third reduction wave of the triimide (Table 1), indicating low chemical reversibility. A reason for this instability could be the very deep reduction potential, which makes it prone to side reactions. Indications for the formation of side products can be seen in the CV after storing a charged solution of the triimide for 10 days as new peaks appear (Figure S5, Supporting Information). Mass transfer processes and electrokinetics also play an important role in RFB applications. (55) Fast diffusional and electron-transfer processes are vital for achieving high current densities and minimizing overpotentials. To analyze the kinetic behavior of the three molecules, CV at different scan rates (Figure 3) enables determining the diffusion coefficients (D) via the Randles–Ševčik equation (see the Supporting Information and Figure S6) and heterogeneous electron-transfer rates (k0) following the Nicholson method (see the Supporting Information and Figure S7). (56,57)D decreases with increasing molecular size (Table 1), in agreement with the Stokes–Einstein relation. The same trend is observed for k0. The second reduction event of the triimide reveals the second-highest value for k0. The values for D and k0 are comparable with values reported for similar molecules and are considered appropriate for RFB applications. (15,19)
    To evaluate the electrochemical cycling stability, bulk-electrolysis experiments were conducted (Figure S8, Supporting Information). Monoimide 1f and diimide 2f display comparably good cyclability, but triimide 3f degrades rapidly when fully reduced. To investigate the reduction events in more detail, UV–vis–NIR absorption spectra were recorded at various state-of-charge (SOC) during the reduction process. Figure 4 shows the UV–vis–NIR spectra of 5 mM solutions of 1f, 2g, and 3f in acetonitrile with 200 mM of TBAPF6 at various SOCs. Clear optical transitions with isosbestic points can be seen for the neutral and reduced states of the mono- and diimide compounds. For monoimide 1f, the radical anion shows a series of absorptions at 238, 264, 313, and 413 nm in the UV–vis region and a weak and broad transition at ∼825 nm in the NIR (Figure 4a). Similarly, the radical anion of 2g shows UV–vis bands at 221, 255, 282, 339, and 413 nm and a NIR band at 714 accompanied by a vibronic band at 648 nm (Figure 4b). The spectrum of the dianion of 2g is much simpler with two strong transitions at 281 and 518 nm, with vibronic peaks at 227, 514, and 523 nm, respectively. These transitions correspond well to literature data for related imides. (58−60) For triimide 3f, clear transitions from neutral to singly and doubly reduced species can be distinguished. The main transitions for the radical anion of 3f occur at 232, 269, 398, 794, and 892 nm, while those of its dianion are at 269, 661, and 705 nm (Figure 4c). However, upon accessing the third reduction at higher SOCs, there is no clear appearance of a new signal except for a minor peak at 548 nm.
    UV–vis–NIR absorption spectroscopy was used to monitor the shelf-life stability of fully reduced anolyte solutions. Figure 4d shows the decay of the normalized absorption maxima of the reduced compounds 1f, 2g, and 2i versus time. Doubly reduced diimides 2g and 2i show excellent charge retention at low concentration (5 mM) with ∼5% capacity loss after 20 days. The capacity retention of the diimides increases at higher concentration (50 mM) to a loss of only 1.7% within that same period (Figure S9, Supporting Information). Importantly, the material does not degrade but reoxidizes as inferred from the proportional formation of singly reduced diimide for doubly reduced diimide lost. No signs of deprotonation of the solvent were observed. Combined with the increased stability observed at higher concentration, the loss of charge is therefore likely extrinsic in nature. For the singly reduced monoimide 1f, a significant lower shelf-life stability was determined; more than 30% of its charge was lost over the 20-day period at a concentration of 5 mM. For the triply reduced triimide 3f, it was not possible to determine the capacity retention because side reactions occurred rapidly. This is apparent with the formation of a peak at 548 nm when the SOC is above 60% (Figure 4c). The peak continued to grow over 3 days while keeping the reduced solution in a nitrogen-filled glovebox (Figure S10, purple line, Supporting Information) until it disappeared again after 10 days (Figure S10, gray line, Supporting Information). A comparison of cyclic voltammograms of the initial solution and the solution after 10 days of 3f clearly shows the loss of redox-active material as well as the formation of new species with redox events at different potentials (Figure S5, Supporting Information). These observations, in combination with the rapid capacity loss observed in the bulk-electrolysis experiment, indicate a high lability of the triply reduced species of 3f, making it unsuitable for RFB application.
    Among all synthesized molecules, diimide 2i is the most promising candidate for RFB applications because it is electrochemically reversible and exhibits the highest volumetric capacity density with a superior charge retention compared to mono- and triimides. The stability of 2i was further examined in a symmetric cycling experiment in an H-cell, which effectively combines an electrochemical cycling experiment with a continuous monitoring of the capacity retention of a charged solution. A 10 mM solution of doubly reduced 2i in TBAPF6/acetonitrile was prepared via bulk electrolysis. The fully reduced solution was then mixed with an equal amount of neutral 2i solution of the same concentration, yielding a starting solution at 50% SOC. This solution was equally divided over the compartments of a symmetric H-cell. By applying a positive current over the electrodes of the H-cell (positive polarization), the solution in one compartment is reoxidized toward 0% SOC while the other one is further reduced toward 100% SOC. Applying a negative current (negative polarization) results in the reversal of the SOCs between the compartments. This represents one cycle in the symmetric H-cell cycling. After a short equilibration of the system within the first four cycles, only a negligible loss of capacity was observed during 12 days of cycling (Figure S13, Supporting Information). The same experiment was carried out at 100 mM 2i concentration (equivalent to 200 mM of transferable electrons) in a flow battery setup to investigate the stability of 2i at an increased concentration under more realistic RFB conditions. Various types of membranes were initially evaluated. Mesoporous membranes (i.e., Celgard) led to fast crossover and a rapid fade of capacity. As 2i is reduced to an anionic state, a cation exchange membrane was considered. However, commercial cation exchange membranes are typically designed for proton transfer and using them with other cations generally leads to high conductivity losses. Using a cationic membrane (Fumasep FKE-50), no significant conductivity could be obtained with the tested system. Among various anion exchange membranes, a Fumasep FAA-3–50 proved to be suitable for the symmetric flow cell experiment shown in Figure 5. After 10 days of cycling, a capacity loss of only 0.09% per cycle (0.6% per day) was observed and the Coulombic efficiency (CE) remained 99.8%. This demonstrates the excellent stability of 2i in cycling between neutral, singly reduced, and doubly reduced states.
    The use of 2i as a prime candidate was then evaluated in a proof-of-concept RFB to investigate the performance at higher concentrations as well as in a full system with a catholyte present. As the cycling performance of the anolyte is of interest within this study, a stable and known catholyte, N-(ferrocenylmethyl)-N,N-dimethylethanaminium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide Fc1N112-TFSI, was chosen and used in slight excess. (61−65) Fc1N112-TFSI exhibits a chemically reversible oxidation at E1/2 = 0.23 V vs Fc/Fc+. A mixed system was used with anolyte and catholyte present in both reservoirs to minimize capacity loss due to crossover of active species. The RFB electrolyte solutions contained 0.25 M 2i (equivalent to 0.5 M of transferable electrons), 0.6 M of Fc1N112-TFSI, and 0.6 M of N112.1O2FSI as electrolyte salt in MeCN. The electrolyte has a large electrochemical potential window (Figure S14, Supporting Information). The theoretical energy density for this battery is 12.1 Wh/L at an average Vbat of 1.8 V (Figure S15, Supporting Information).
    The RFB was galvanostatically cycled at 10 mA/cm2, and the resulting cycling data are shown in Figure 6a,b. A capacity loss of 0.8% per cycle and 4.2% per day was determined after the initial equilibration period during the first few cycles. After cycling for 50 h, a gradual mass transfer from the anolyte to the catholyte reservoir was observed. Possibly, a difference in osmotic pressure between the compartments due to unwanted crossover of (ionic) active species could induce solvent transfer between the two sides. The observed electrolyte transfer likely plays a role in the observed capacity loss and may relate to the unusual behavior in voltage vs capacity curves that suggest mass transfer-induced overpotentials occurring on a longer timescale (Figures 6b and S16, charging of second redox couple cycle 3 to 10, Supporting Information). We observed this type of cycling behavior more often in high-concentration nonaqueous RFB systems, and the phenomenon warrants further investigation in the future. Furthermore, a slow increase of the area-specific resistivity (ASR) was seen from about 8 to 17 Ω cm2 after cycling, which is tentatively attributed to membrane degradation (Figure S17, Supporting Information). Overall, the battery showed an excellent capacity retention which is more than one order of magnitude higher when comparing the decay per day with the best monoimide nonaqueous RFB systems reported to date, while it also outperforms the corresponding Coulombic and energy efficiencies (Table S2, Supporting Information). (19) The performance is on par with other high-performing nonaqueous RFBs (Table S2, Supporting Information). (9,15,18,27,30,32,34,42,46) The average material utilization is 75% at a current density of 10 mA/cm2. The maximum power density of the RFB reaches an impressive 160 mW/cm2 at a current density of 140 mA/cm2, which is attributed to the highly conductive anion exchange membrane used and fast electrokinetics of redox-active materials (Figure 6c). This outperforms most of the nonaqueous RFBs to date (Table S2, Supporting Information) and is even comparable to some aqueous RFBs. (19,34,66−70) The Coulombic efficiency reached 97%, while voltaic and energy efficiencies (VE and EE) remained at 75 and 73% after 50 h of cycling.
    To perform a more rigorous analysis of redox chemistries, a battery cycling experiment at similar concentration (0.2 M diimide 2i) was performed (Figure S18, Supporting Information) in which CV and NMR measurements were conducted prior to and after cycling. These confirmed the previous observation that the redox chemistry of the actives stays mostly unimpaired by the cycling and that the capacity fade is mainly caused by crossover through the membrane.
    Further increasing the concentration of the RFB to an equivalent of 1 M of transferable electrons was not possible in a mixed system because the solubility of the oxidized catholyte becomes limiting under these conditions. However, in a nonmixed system using 0.5 M of 2i with 1.2 M N112.1O2FSI as the anolyte and 1 M ferrocenium and N112.1O2FSI as the catholyte resulted in the highest energy density reported so far for a nonaqueous RFB of 24.1 Wh/L (Table S2, Supporting Information). A more selective Fumasep FAPQ-375-PP anion exchange membrane was used in an attempt to slow down crossover. The battery was galvanostatically cycled at 7.5 mA/cm2 with additional voltaic holds at 3 and 0 V to maximize material utilization (Figure S20a,b, Supporting Information). Initially material utilization reached 92% with CE = 98% and EE = 58% after which the capacity loss was dictated by crossover of the catholyte material as evidenced by CV measurements, and due to an increased ASR from about 52 to 148 Ω cm2 accompanied by lower achievable charge capacities (Figure S20c,d, Supporting Information). RFBs at even higher concentrations were prohibited due to the limited solubility of the oxidized catholyte. Overall, these cycling experiments highlight that the diimide compounds are highly suitable for usage in nonaqueous RFB and outperform monoimide derivatives in terms of stability. The details of cycling high-concentration nonaqueous RFBs and limiting their capacity loss due to unwanted crossover will require further research and the development of new membrane separators.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In developing and implementing multielectron redox materials for nonaqueous RFBs, we studied phthalimide, pyromellitic diimide, and mellitic triimide derivatives. Increasing the number of imide groups is an effective strategy to increase the number of electrons that can be stored reversibly on these molecules. The average reduction potential of multielectron systems was slightly higher than that of the monoimide. By optimizing the solubility via the use of branched aliphatic and oligo(ethylene)glycol chains, high volumetric capacities for the mono-, di-, and triimide derivatives up to 65 Ah/L in electrolyte mixtures were achieved. Bulk-electrolysis cycling and UV–vis–NIR spectroscopy showed that the diimide electrolyte solutions displayed superior cycling and shelf-life stability compared to the monoimide and triimide derivatives in their fully reduced state. The combined insights resulted in the development of diimide 2i, as a highly stable, soluble, multielectron anolyte for nonaqueous RFB application. In a proof-of-principle high-concentration mixed RFB with a ferrocenium-based catholyte, the newly developed anolyte 2i showed good capacity retention resulting in the successful demonstration of a nonaqueous RFB with a high power density of up to 160 mW/cm2 as well as a high CE, VE, and EE. Finally, in a nonmixed RFB system, an energy density of 24.1 Wh/L could be achieved by utilizing the newly developed anolyte in combination with a ferrocene catholyte.

        Long-Cycling Aqueous Organic Redox Flow Battery (AORFB) toward Sustainable and Safe Energy Storage
        Hu, Bo; DeBruler, Camden; Rhodes, Zayn; Liu, T. Leo
        Journal of the American Chemical Society (2017), 139 (3), 1207-1214CODEN: JACSAT; ISSN:0002-7863. (American Chemical Society)
        Redox flow batteries (RFBs) are a viable technol. to store renewable energy in the form of electricity that can be supplied to electricity grids. However, wide-spread implementation of traditional RFBs, such as Vanadium and Zn-Br2 RFBs is limited due to a no. of challenges related to materials, including low abundance and high costs of redox active metals, expensive separators, active material crossover, and corrosive and hazardous electrolytes. To address these challenges, a neutral aq. org. redox flow battery (AORFB) technol. is demonstrated utilizing a newly designed cathode electrolyte contg. a highly water-sol. ferrocene mol. Specifically, water sol. (ferrocenylmethyl)trimethylammonium chloride (FcNCl, 4.0 M in H2O, 107.2 Ah/L, and 3.0 M in 2.0 NaCl, 80.4 Ah/L) and N1-ferrocenylmethyl-N1,N1,N2,N2,N2-pentamethylpropane-1,2-diaminium dibromide, (FcN2Br2, 3.1 M in H2O, 83.1 Ah/L, and 2.0 M in 2.0 M NaCl, 53.5 Ah/L) were synthesized through structural decoration of hydrophobic ferrocene with synergetic hydrophilic functionalities including an ammonium cation group and a halide anion. When paired with Me viologen (MV) as an anolyte, resulting FcNCl/MV and FcN2Br2/MV AORFBs were operated in noncorrosive neutral NaCl supporting electrolytes using a low cost anion exchange membrane. Notably, the FcNCl/MV AORFBs (demonstrated at 7.0 and 9.9 Wh/L) exhibited unprecedented long cycling performance, 700 cycles at 60 mA/cm2 with 99.99% capacity retention per cycle, and delivered power d. up to 125 mW/cm2. These AORFBs are built from earth abundant elements and environmentally benign, thus represent a promising choice for sustainable and safe energy storage.
