Imidazolium cation enabled reversibility of a hydroquinone derivative for designing aqueous redox electrolytes

Abstract

Redox flow batteries are promising devices for large-scale stationary energy storage. One remaining challenge towards their broad application is the rational selection of electrolyte components, composed of redox-active species, and the supporting reaction media. Particularly, good compatibility among the different components should be addressed. Compared to transition metal-based redox electrolytes, green and sustainable organic redox-active materials are becoming favourable due to their low cost, structural diversity and possibly higher volumetric capacity. Herein, we study a hydroquinone species, 2-[(2,5-dihydroxyphenyl)sulfanyl]ethan-1-aminium chloride (SEAHQ), which has high solubility in water, but rather poor chemical and electrochemical stability. Such properties preclude its practical applications. To remove such limitations, we shed light on the unique role of the aqueous supporting electrolytes. Ionic liquids, widely investigated as green reaction media, contain commonly used organic cationic groups and may have better affinity with organic redox materials. This is successfully demonstrated by combining water-soluble imidazolium-based ionic liquids and SEAHQ. Electrochemical characterization experiments showed excellent stability and reversibility of SEAHQ in the studied supporting electrolytes. Redox flow cell tests using SEAHQ as the catholyte with concentrations up to 1 M revealed high cycling efficiencies with current densities up to 100 mA cm−2 over 100 cycles. It is considered that the capacity loss over cycling is related to a continuous decrease in the utilization of the electrolytes, rather than a structural degradation of SEAHQ.

1. Introduction

Nowadays high-performance energy storage devices are becoming increasingly vital in the utilization of renewable energy and in grid stabilization.1,2 Among several electrochemical energy storage devices, redox flow batteries (RFBs) are promising for stationary large-scale application in terms of cost, reliability and safety.3–7 Although conventional metal-based RFBs such as vanadium RFBs have been extensively investigated,8,9 the high system costs including vanadium electrolytes and perfluorinated ion exchange membranes hinder their wide applications.10

Organic redox-active materials are drawing more attention due to their low price, abundance and structural diversity.11–13 Benzoquinones and their reduced forms (hydroquinones) are well-known in reversible redox systems.14–16 However, unmodified benzoquinone cannot show sufficient water solubility (only 0.1 M),17 making it less attractive for high volumetric energy density. Therefore, benzoquinones were functionalized with various groups in order to increase their stability and water solubility, aiming to achieve higher capacity and cycling efficiency of the aqueous RFBs.17–21 In addition, benzoquinones were found to suffer from structural degradation in aqueous media due to the Michael addition reaction with water molecules,18–20,22,23 as shown in Scheme 1. In addition to the molecular engineering of organic materials, the influence of supporting electrolytes, including solvents and conducting ions, on the physiochemical and electrochemical properties of organic redox-active materials needs to be considered, but is less addressed so far.


Scheme 1 Redox reactions between the hydroquinones and benzoquinones with proton-coupled electron transfer and Michael addition reaction of benzoquinones with water molecules in aqueous reaction media.

Water, organic solvents and ionic liquids have been often used for formulating electrolytes.24 Organic solvents have problems of flammability and toxicity, whereas high viscosity and low ionic conductivity are the main drawbacks of typical room temperature ionic liquids,25–28 although they can offer high cell voltage. New strategies using mixtures of these solvents may exhibit interesting properties.29–31 For instance, the viscosity of ionic liquids can be reduced by adding co-solvents such as water or acetonitrile.32–35 Mixtures of water and hydrophilic ionic liquids may form a homogeneous phase with the merits of a broad electrochemical stability window, good ionic conductivity and flowability.33,34 These properties are favourable for fluidic systems and promising in realizing high-voltage aqueous batteries.24,36 High-voltage aqueous Zn/Ce RFBs,33 and membrane-free aqueous biphasic RFBs,37 as well as supercapacitors38 have been demonstrated utilizing “water-in-ionic liquid” electrolytes. Electroactive organic materials may have enhanced molecular interactions with the organic cationic groups of the ionic liquids. However, the role of “water-in-ionic liquids” as reaction media for redox-active organic materials has not yet been explored.

In this work, we report how supporting electrolytes containing aqueous imidazolium chloride can enable the reversible electrochemical redox reaction of the hydroquinone derivative 2-[(2,5-dihydroxyphenyl)sulfanyl]ethan-1-aminium chloride (SEAHQ). A comparative investigation was performed by using supporting solvent and electrolytes without imidazolium chloride. Moreover, by pairing with a commercial vanadium electrolyte and a compatible anion exchange membrane, redox flow cells with different concentrations of SEAHQ from 0.08 to 1 M were successfully tested. High current density up to 100 mA cm−2 can be applied under acidic conditions with steady cycling efficiency over 100 charge/discharge cycles. The excellent electrochemical reactivity and stability of SEAHQ in aqueous imidazolium chloride may arise from strong interactions between the imidazolium cationic groups and the organic redox solute. These results are suggestive for rationally designing green and sustainable redox electrolytes to tackle the material challenges we are currently facing for grid-scale electrochemical energy storage.

2. Experimental

2.1 Preparation of electrolytes

Water-miscible 1-butyl-3-methylimidazolium chloride powder (BMImCl, 98%, Sigma-Aldrich) was used as received without further purification. Supporting electrolytes with a neutral pH value were prepared by adding BMImCl into water. Due to the large volume increase, the content of ionic liquids in such a system is denoted as molality (m, mol kgwater−1), instead of concentration (M, mol Lsolution−1). For instance, 10 m aqueous ionic liquid (aq-IL) was obtained by mixing 10 mol BMImCl with 1 kg water. The redox-active organic material SEAHQ (Scheme 2) was supplied from CMBlu Energy AG, synthesized as described elsewhere.39 Briefly, cysteamine chlorohydrate and benzoquinone were mixed stoichiometrically in methanol and stirred at 25 °C for 24 h. After filtration, methanol was removed under reduced pressure, yielding pure SEAHQ as a powder.
Scheme 2 Redox reaction mechanism of SEAHQ with proton-coupled two-electron transfer.

The solubility of SEAHQ was determined at room temperature by adding SEAHQ stepwise into 1 mL water or aq-IL under stirring with an increment of 0.1 mmol, until the organic solute cannot be dissolved anymore. The final volume of the solutions was measured for roughly quantifying the maximal solubility of SEAHQ. Note that due to the dark colour of the solutions, the maximal solubility can be underestimated. To examine the influence of the pH value, the solubility measurements were also performed under acidic conditions by adding HCl (with a final concentration of 1 M) into the aq-IL.

2.2 Electrochemical characterization

All electrochemical characterization experiments were performed using a BioLogic VMP-3 potentiostat controlled via EC-Lab software. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements were performed in a three-electrode cell, composed of a glassy carbon rod working electrode, a Pt foil counter electrode and a Ag wire quasi-reference electrode. For the measurement in pure water and 1 M HCl, a Ag/AgCl reference electrode (Ag/AgCl wire immersed in a glass tube with a frit filled with 4 M KCl) was used.

A homemade flow cell (with an active area of 4 cm2) was assembled with two pieces of graphite felt (GFD4.6 EA, SGL Carbon, Germany) as electrodes, which were pre-treated in a concentrated H2SO4 solution and then thermally processed at 500 °C for 30 h in static air. A crosslinked and methylated meta-polybenzimidazole (PBI) anion-exchange membrane40 was soaked at room temperature in a 3 M NaCl solution for 24 h prior to use. Alternatively, a Fumasep FAA-3-30 anion exchange membrane (FumaTech BWT GmbH, Germany) was used and pre-processed by soaking in 1 M KOH for 1 h, and then in 0.5 H2SO4 for 1 h.

SEAHQ of different concentrations in 10 m aq-IL containing 1 M HCl was employed as the catholyte, unless otherwise stated. Some comparative experiments were also performed using 2 M H2SO4. A vanadium electrolyte with V3+ was used as the anolyte, which was obtained through electrochemical reduction of the commercial vanadium electrolyte of V3.5+ (1.6 M, VO2+/V3+, 1:1, in 2 M sulfuric acid, GfE Metalle und Materialien GmbH, Germany) in a flow cell and then diluted to a suitable concentration. Saturated NaCl was added to the anolyte side to balance the osmotic pressure between the two electrolytes. The catholyte and anolyte with four different combinations were studied, as summarized in Table 1. Both electrolytes (10 mL each) were sealed with a top layer of paraffin oil (Roth, Germany) in glass vials. During cycling the electrolytes were pumped through the flow cell by using a peristaltic pump at a flow rate of 35 mL min−1. The galvanostatic battery charge/discharge tests were carried out between 0.3 and 1.8 V at different current densities ranging from 10 to 100 mA cm−2. A different charge/discharge test protocol by controlling a constant charge capacity was also applied, denoted as “capacity control”.

3. Results and discussion

SEAHQ has a high solubility of up to 2 M in pure water, but forms an oily phase in 1 M HCl. In contrast, a homogeneous solution can be obtained if SEAHQ is added into our aq-IL supporting electrolyte with or without HCl. Furthermore, the solubility of SEAHQ can reach up to 1.2 M and 1.0 M in neutral and acidic 10 m aq-IL, respectively. The final solutions were a little viscous and dark in colour.

The electrochemical redox behaviour of SEAHQ in both pure water (Fig. 1a) and 1 M HCl (inset in Fig. 1a) was first investigated by means of CV at a scan rate of 20 mV s−1. In pure water, a current response for an oxidation reaction during the first anodic scan was observed and the current decreased over the subsequent CV scans. In 1 M HCl, an oxidation peak centered at about 0.85 V vs. Ag/AgCl can be well resolved. However, the electrochemical reversibility is still rather poor with a very weak reduction peak, indicating instability of the oxidation product in the employed aqueous reaction media. Additionally, the peak current decreased significantly after about 20 CV scans. For the previously reported benzoquinone derivatives,19,22,23 the instability is attributed to the Michael addition of water to benzoquinones (Scheme 1) and the hydroxylated product shows redox activity that can be recognized from the CV curves. However, SEAHQ may have a different decomposition mechanism,41,42 which is yet to be determined. It has been reported that various para-benzoquinone-based structures can prevent the side reaction with water molecules and thus offer steady cycling behaviour in aqueous electrolytes.43,44 Note that these derivatives have a common molecular design in that their aromatic rings are fully substituted. In contrast to the previous molecular engineering strategies to suppress Michael addition, our work demonstrates an easy and alternative approach through adjusting the components of the aqueous supporting electrolytes.


Fig. 1 CV curves of 0.1 M SEAHQ in (a) water and 1 M HCl (inset), (b) 10 m aq-IL with or without 1 M HCl, and (c) 3 m aq-IL.

Interestingly, we found that SEAHQ has high chemical stability and electrochemical reversibility in 10 m aq-IL (Fig. 1b). Highly reversible redox peaks were observed at about 0.8/0.21 V vs. Ag. In the presence of HCl, the redox peaks shifted about +0.1 V to higher positive potentials. In addition, the redox peak separation reduced slightly, indicating improved reaction kinetics. Note that when the CV measurements were performed in an aq-IL solution with a low molality of 3 m, poor electrochemical activity and reversibility of SEAHQ were observed (Fig. 1c). Thus, a hypothesis for the stabilization of SEAHQ in aq-IL could be that SEAHQ molecules have a strong interaction with the BMIm+ cations. At a sufficiently high molality of 10 m, the BMIm+ cations outnumber the water molecules in the electrolytes.33,36 Consequently, the BMIm+ solvating shell can shield SEAHQ sterically against nucleophilic attack by water molecules. The redox reactions of SEAHQ in the aq-IL medium are analogous to those for other benzoquinone derivatives, undergoing a proton-coupled two-electron transfer reaction, as shown in Scheme 2.

Owing to the two-electron transfer mechanism and high solubility (1.0 M), SEAHQ has a high theoretical volumetric capacity of 53.6 A h L−1. To study the feasibility of SEAHQ as a catholyte for RFBs, flow cell tests were then performed by pairing SEAHQ with acidic vanadium anolyte (V3+), using a PBI-based anion exchange membrane40 to separate the two compartments and to conduct the anion charge carriers (i.e., mixed sulphate and chloride anions) (Scheme 3). The PBI membrane has better ionic conductivity under acidic conditions than under neutral pH conditions, and can effectively inhibit the crossover of the vanadium species from anolyte to catholyte.40,45–47


Scheme 3 A schematic illustration of a redox flow cell with SEAHQ in aq-IL with 1 M HCl as the catholyte and vanadium anolyte, separated by an anion exchange membrane. The electrolytes are externally stored in tanks and pumped through the two electrochemical half-cells for the redox reactions.

A SEAHQ/vanadium flow cell (RFB-1, as summarized in Table 1) with 0.08 M SEAHQ in 10 m aq-IL containing 1 M HCl as the catholyte and diluted vanadium anolyte (0.16 M) was first investigated at a low current density of 10 mA cm−2. Fig. 2a shows the galvanostatic charge/discharge voltage profiles of the 20th, 50th, 80th and 100th cycles. The steady and well-defined charge/discharge curves indicate an excellent cyclability of SEAHQ as the catholyte. Fig. 2b shows the charge/discharge capacity and the cycling efficiencies over 100 cycles. After about 15 initial cycles, the system reached a steady state with constant deliverable capacity and cycling efficiencies. A high coulombic (CE), voltage (VE) and energy efficiency (EE) of about 97%, 69% and 67% was observed, respectively. In order to check whether a change in electrochemical behaviour occurs, the catholyte after 100 cycles was collected for a CV measurement (Fig. 2c). The redox peaks can still be well-resolved and are similar to those for the fresh electrolyte, indicating a high chemical and electrochemical stability of SEAHQ in the aq-IL supporting electrolyte. In addition, no redox reaction of the vanadium species was observed from the CV measurement, indicating a negligible level of vanadium crossover through the PBI membrane.


Fig. 2 (a) Charge/discharge voltage profile, and (b) cycling efficiencies and capacities of the RFB (cell RFB-1) operating at 10 mA cm−2 with 0.08 M SEAHQ in aq-IL containing 1 M HCl as the catholyte and 0.16 M vanadium anolyte, (c) CV curves of the catholyte before and after 100 cycles of charge/discharge.

A SEAHQ/vanadium flow cell (RFB-2, as summarized in Table 1) with a higher concentration was then tested at an increased current density of 25 mA cm−2 utilizing 0.5 M SEAHQ and 1.6 M vanadium electrolyte with saturated NaCl (Fig. 3). Similar to the previous one, the system can deliver steady capacity after about 15 cycles. The voltage curves overlapped well, indicating a good long-term cycling stability of SEAHQ. Compared to the 0.08 M system with a steady capacity of about 3 mA h at 10 mA cm−2, a higher capacity of about 22 mA h was observed (Fig. 3a). This seems to be an improvement, because the theoretically possible capacity increased by a factor of 6.25, and an increase in the applied current density usually lowers the capacity because the cut-off voltages are reached earlier. The energy density accordingly increased to about 1.4 W h L−1. In addition, a high CE of 99% in the following cycles was observed up to the 100 cycles. At 25 mA cm−2, the VE remains about 62%, which is slightly lower than that for the low concentration electrolyte.


Fig. 3 (a) Voltage profile and (b) cycling efficiencies and capacities of the RFB (cell RFB-2) operating at 25 mA cm−2 with 0.5 M SEAHQ in aq-IL containing 1 M HCl as the catholyte and 1.6 M vanadium anolyte.

When the SEAHQ concentration is further increased to 1.0 M in the catholyte (Fig. 4, RFB-3, as summarized in Table 1), the steady capacity can reach about 50 mA h at 100 mA cm−2 and about 60 mA h at 50 mA cm−2. Thus, it can be seen that the system shows even better deliverable capacities at increased concentrations of SEAHQ. Such observations are distinct from many of the previous reports that the utilization of organic electroactive materials often becomes poor by increasing the concentration.48–51 An increased polarization was observed by increasing the current densities, with a downshift of the discharge curve and an upshift of the charge curve, as shown in Fig. 4a. Consequently, a low VE of 45% at 50 mA cm−2 was observed. At a current density of 50 mA cm−2, the energy density reaches to about 3.2 W h L−1. Moreover, it was found that, independent of the applied current densities, the CE of the flow cell remains about 97.5%.


Fig. 4 (a) Voltage profile and (b) cycling efficiency and capacities of the RFB (cell RFB-3) operating at 100 mA cm−2 during the first 35 cycles, then at 50 mA cm−2 during the following cycles with 1.0 M SEAHQ in aq-IL containing 1 M HCl as the catholyte and 1.6 M vanadium anolyte.

Fig. 5 shows the comparison of the capacity fade in a logarithmic scale as a function of cycling time, i.e. a temporal metric,52 of the three studied flow cells. It can be seen that the RFB utilizing the lowest concentration of SEAHQ has the fastest initial capacity fade rate. Furthermore, it was found that the charge capacity of the next cycle mostly equals the discharge capacity of last cycle, indicating that stability of SEAHQ is related to the state of charge (SOC) of the cell, i.e., the concentration of the oxidized SEAHQ species. This is in agreement with a previous observation by Aziz et al.53


Fig. 5 Capacity fade as a function of cycling time of the flow cells RFB-1, -2 and -3, as studied in this work.

In order to examine the possible influence of the types of the ion exchange membranes and acid additives on the cycling performance of SEAHQ, the PBI membrane and HCl were replaced with an FAA-3-30 anion exchange membrane and 2 M H2SO4 (RFB-4, as summarized in Table 1), respectively. The charge/discharge voltage profiles remain unchanged with a comparable VE. However, similar capacity fading was still observed during the first few cycles. Moreover, by adding more fresh vanadium anolyte during the operation, the capacity can be recovered at first and then dropped to a steady value after another about 15 cycles. Thus, we deduce that the initial capacity fading is neither related to the degradation of the ion exchange membrane nor the type of the acid additive.

To further study the origin of the initial capacity loss during cycling, a comparative charge/discharge experiment was carried out by forcing a constant charge capacity for each cycle, namely a “capacity control” protocol, differing from the cycling between a cut-off voltage range (“voltage control”). As shown in Fig. 6 (RFB-4), the “capacity control” test strategy, during the first 20 cycles and from the 70th to 90th cycles, can well maintain the initial capacities. The “voltage control” from the 20th to 70th cycles again showed the initial capacity fading down to 3 mA h at the end of the 60th cycle. Surprisingly, from the 70th to 90th cycles, a steady and even higher deliverable discharge capacity of 11.5 mA h can be observed, if we apply a higher constant charge capacity (13.4 mA h). Thus, we conclude that the capacity loss during cycling is related to a continuous decrease in the utilization of SEAHQ, rather than a continuous decomposition of the redox-active SEAHQ. Some changes in the active sites of the electrode surface54 and in the ion channel of the membrane may affect the reaction turnover rate.55 Future work is needed to clarify these underlying mechanisms and to improve the reaction kinetics. In addition, by applying “capacity control”, a low CE of about 86% was observed, indicating that some parasitic reactions are involved.


Fig. 6 Dependence of the deliverable capacity and CE on the electrochemical cell test protocol by “capacity control” and “voltage control” for a RFB (cell RFB-4) with 0.08 M SEAHQ in aq-IL as the catholyte containing 2 M H2SO4, and 0.16 M vanadium anolyte with an FAA-3-30 membrane, at a current density of 20 mA cm−2. Cut-off voltage for “voltage control”: 0.7–1.15 V (cycle 20–60) and 0.7–1.2 V (cycle 61–69).

4. Conclusions

In summary, we studied a hydroquinone species SEAHQ as a catholyte for redox flow batteries and the critical role of the aq-IL supporting electrolytes. Through comparative CV experiments, it was found that aq-IL at a molality of 10 m can largely enhance the chemical and electrochemical stability of SEAHQ, which is however not possible in pure water, traditional acidic solutions of HCl and H2SO4, and aq-IL with a low molality of 3 m. It is considered that a strong molecular interaction between the BMIm+ cations and the organic redox-active solute may help to suppress the nucleophilic attack of SEAHQ by water molecules. Such results suggest an advantageous design strategy for formulating organic material-based redox electrolytes.56,57 It should be noted that a high molality of the ionic liquids in the supporting electrolytes would cause high material costs and increased viscosity.57 Thus, an optimized molality of ionic liquids should be reached, and a good compatibility between the type of redox-active organic materials and the molality and cationic structure of the ionic liquids should be clarified in the future.57

A flow battery, utilizing the SEAHQ-in-aq-IL catholyte coupled with a vanadium anolyte, demonstrated excellent cyclability of SEAHQ with a concentration of up to 1 M. CV tests for the used catholyte after 100 charge/discharge cycles and the fresh electrolyte showed similar redox peak profiles, indicating that the chemical structure and electrochemical redox properties can be well preserved. After a few initial charge/discharge cycles, the flow cell using SEAHQ catholyte showed steady capacities and cycling efficiencies, independent of the types of membrane, acid additives, and the concentration of SEAHQ. By increasing the concentration of SEAHQ, a high deliverable capacity was observed. A comparative study by applying “capacity control” and “voltage control” elucidated that the capacity loss during initial cycling is related to a continuous decrease in the utilization of the electrolyte, instead of the structural decomposition of SEAHQ, leaving room for further optimization of organic redox-active compounds using aq-IL supporting electrolytes. Supplementary spectroscopic analysis of the studied organic materials should be performed in the future. Moreover, some computational work on the interactions among water, ILs and organic redox-active molecules in the future could guide the design of better-performing redox electrolytes.
