    Abstract

    Non-aqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs) are promising energy storage devices owing to the broad electrochemical window of organic solvents. Nonetheless, the wide application of these batteries has been limited by the low stability and limited solubility of organic materials, as well as the insufficient ion conductivity of the cell separators in non-aqueous electrolytes. In this study, two viologen analogues with poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) tails are designed as anolytes for non-aqueous RFBs. The PEGylation of viologen not only enhances the solubility in acetonitrile but also increases the overall molecular size for alleviated crossover. In addition, a composite nanoporous aramid nanofiber separator, which allows the permeation of supporting ions while inhibiting the crossover of the designer viologens, is developed using a scalable doctor-blading method. Paired with ferrocene, the full organic material-based RFB presents excellent cyclability (500 cycles) with a retention capacity per cycle of 99.93% and an average Coulombic efficiency of 99.3% at a current density of 2.0 mA/cm2. The high performance of the PEGylated viologen validates the potential of the PEGylation strategy for enhanced organic material-based non-aqueous RFBs.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The ever-growing demand for energy has stimulated the rapid discovery of achievable and economical nonfossil fuels. As representative renewable energy sources, solar and wind have been identified as the most promising energy sources due to their abundance, cost efficiency, and environmental friendliness. (1−3) One of the most significant current discussions in the renewable energy area is how to develop effective and economic energy storage devices. (4−16) Redox flow batteries (RFBs) have received an increasing amount of attention as an attractive energy storage technology for stationary energy storage applications due to their safety, cost efficiency, and scalability. (5,17−21) The decoupled control of energy and power of the RFBs enables independent control of the capacity and power to meet different requirements. (10,13,22) Traditional inorganic-based RFBs suffer from major drawbacks, including low cell voltage, high cost, strong toxicity, and corrosivity. (10,17,23−26) Organic material-based RFBs have attracted an increasing amount of interest owing to the synthetic malleability of organic redox-active materials. (27−36) The solubility, redox potentials, and stability of organic materials can be readily tuned by molecular engineering in pursuit of high energy density and long cyclability. (10,37−40) Although significant progress has been made in recent years, the overall performance of organic materials in aqueous RFBs is still limited by the narrow redox-innocent window of water (<1.9 V), (10,17,24,41) thus necessitating the exploration of alternative solvents with an extended redox-innocent window.
    All organic-based non-aqueous RFBs are emerging energy storage devices, wherein organic compounds are used as the energy storage materials and organic solvents are used as the media. (42) Most of the organic solvents used in RFBs, such as acetonitrile (MeCN), carbonate, and ether, have wide redox-innocent potential windows, allowing the development of high-voltage battery systems. (28,29,32,43−46) Tremendous progress has been made on all-organic RFBs; (29,46−49) however, several critical issues still need to be addressed, including the limited solubility of the redox-active materials, the low conductivity of the organic electrolyte, and the lack of suitable ion-exchange membranes. (17,50) Appending electroactive substituents on polymers is an effective strategy for significantly alleviating the crossover issue. Sanford et al. studied the impact of oligomer size and structure on the battery properties using size-exclusion membranes. (51) The group of Helms proposed trimeric molecule structures to alleviate the crossover of molecules. (52) Rodríguez-López et al. studied both monomeric and polymeric viologen and ferrocene molecules. (53) The non-aqueous RFB assembled with electrode materials in polymer form presented a stable open-circuit potential at charge states. All reports suggest that increasing the molecular size of organic compounds can significantly mitigate the crossover issue in the absence of suitable ion-exchange membranes.
    PEGylated electroactive compounds have been studied in previous literature. Zhang et al. systematically studied the effect of lengths and spatial locations of PEG on the electrochemical properties of 1,4-bis(2-methoxyethoxy)benzene. (54) Wei et al. also studied ethoxy 1,4-bis(2-methoxyethoxy)benzene in non-aqueous RFBs with various separators. (28) Helms proposed PEGs as versatile core motifs to improve the solubility of electroactive materials. (55) The branched structure is beneficial for mitigating the crossover behavior of cathode and anode molecules, affording the increased Coulombic efficiency of the asymmetric cell. However, most of the PEGylated compounds are expensive or are formed via a tedious synthesis process. Herein, we report a general molecular engineering strategy for enhancing the solubility and stability and increasing the molecular size of redox-active materials, exemplified by PEGylated viologens. In this report, PEGylated viologens are synthesized in high yields via a one-step, solvent-free method that does not require purification. The product is an ionic liquid and is miscible with MeCN in any proportion, which ensures an improved energy density. In addition, a methodology for the scalable preparation of the composite nanoporous aramid nanofiber (CANF) separator is developed. The CANF separator allows the permeation of supporting ions while limiting the crossover of PEGylated viologen. Paired with ferrocene (Fc, a cathode material), the full organic non-aqueous RFB presents excellent cyclability with a retention capacity of 99.93% per cycle in 500 charge/discharge cycles at a current density of 2 mA/cm2. Furthermore, this non-aqueous RFB possesses a high Coulombic efficiency of 99.3% owing to the high stability of the PEGylated viologen and the high selectivity of the CANF separator. Overall, this work offers a delicate synthetic strategy for designing size-tailorable organic-based redox species. It also presents a highly selective separator for efficient RFBs.

    2.1. Molecular Design and Synthesis

    A one-step synthesis was performed to prepare the viologen derivatives with 12 and 3 oxyethylene units, defined as PEG12 and PEG3, respectively. The reaction of 4,4′-bipyridine with poly(ethylene glycol) methyl ether tosylate (PEG12-OTs) at 80 °C for 48 h afforded the final product, PEG12-V, as an oily brown liquid (Chart S1, Scheme S1, and Figure S1) in quantitative yield. The progress of the reaction was tracked by 1H NMR, 13C NMR, and HR-ESI-MS (Figures S2–S4). The same synthetic procedure was followed to prepare PEG3-V as a viscous brown liquid (Figure S1), which was miscible in any proportion with MeCN, as well. The densities of neat PEG12-V and PEG3-V are 1.16 and 1.26 g/mL, respectively, corresponding to concentrations of 0.79 and 1.60 M, respectively. On the basis of two-electron utilization, the electron densities of PEG12-V and PEG3-V are 1.58 and 3.20 M, respectively.

    2.2. Electrochemical Properties

    The investigation of the PEGylated viologens is initiated with cyclic voltammetry (CV) in MeCN with 0.1 M tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate (TBAPF6) as the supporting electrolyte. Two reversible redox peaks are observed in the CV scans of viologens (Figure S5). During the reduction process, the dication molecular viologen gains an electron to form a cation radical, followed by another electron reduction to give neutral bipyridinylidene (Figure 1a). (23) The redox behaviors of PEG12-V, PEG3-V, and Me-V indicate that the redox potentials of PEGylated viologens shifts anodically 10 mV compared to Me-V due to the electron-withdrawing property of PEG. However, the introduction of the PEG chain has almost no effect on the electrochemical reversibility of viologens, which can be testified by the ratio of cathodic current density to anodic current density [ipc/ipa (Table S1)]. The density functional theory (DFT) calculation of PEG12-V and Me-V on different redox states also shows that the HOMO/SOMO–LUMO gaps are comparable (Figure S6). However, PEG12-V possessed a lower energy level, meaning that PEG12-V was sensitive to being reduced. The PEG12-V/Fc full RFB presents a theoretical voltage of 1.24 V (Figure 1b). Though the cell voltage is not sufficiently high to take full advantage of the wide redox-innocent window of acetonitrile, we demonstrate that molecular engineering via PEGylation is a powerful tool for improving the overall performance in all-organic RFBs, including stability, solubility, and alleviated crossover.
    To investigate the kinetics properties of PEGylated viologens, the CV scans of 5 mM samples in 0.1 M TBAPF6-MeCN are conducted at different scan rates from 5 to 2000 mV/s (Figures S7–S10). The linear plots of the peak current density versus the square root of the scan rate (Figures S8 and S10) indicate that the redox processes are diffusion-controlled. The electron transfer rates are calculated by the Nicolson method (50,56) (calculation details in the Supporting Information). The diffusion coefficients for all of the redox states of the PEGylated viologens are estimated to be between 4.81 × 10–6 and 8.02 × 10–6 cm2/s (Table S1), slightly lower than that of the reported methyl viologen in a non-aqueous electrolyte. (23) This suggests that the introduction of the PEG units will decrease the diffusion of PEGylated viologens in electrolytes due to the increased viscosity (Table S2). The electron transfer rate constants of PEG12-V for the one- and two-electron reduction processes are 0.22 and 0.37 cm/s, respectively, which are close to those of PEG3-V. The fast electron kinetics are presumably attributed to the conjugated structure of viologen, which is beneficial for reducing the overpotentials of RFBs, particularly when operating at a high current density. (50,57) A rotating disk electrode (RDE) study is also conducted to analyze the diffusion coefficients of 1 mM PEG12-V in 0.1 M TBAPF6-MeCN (Figure S11 and Table S3). The diffusion coefficients of PEG12-V for the first and second electron redox reactions are 7.42 × 10–6 and 8.18 × 10–6 cm2/s, respectively, which correlate with the CV results.

    2.3. Separator Preparation

    Ion-exchange membranes, such as Nafion, (5,12,58) Selemion, (59−61) and Fumasep membranes, (62,63) have been widely utilized in aqueous RFBs due to their ultrahigh ionic conductivity in an aqueous electrolyte. (64) However, they are not suitable for use in non-aqueous electrolytes due to either their insufficient conductivity or poor stability in organic solvents. (17,65,66) The Fumasep FAPQ-375-PP was first used in non-aqueous RFBs by the Sanford group, which greatly boosted the development of non-aqueous RFBs. (67−69) However, the cost of the FAPQ-375-PP membrane ($1.5/cm2) might retard its wide application in non-aqueous RFBs. As alternative porous separators, Celgard and Daramic membranes have been studied in non-aqueous RFBs. (28,70,71) Unfortunately, these separators commonly possess large pores ranging from tens of nanometers to several micrometers, resulting in severe crossover issues and low Coulombic efficiency. (23,28,44,54) In addition, the design of symmetrical battery assembled with these large porous separators doubles the usage amount of active materials and then increases the cost of RFBs.
    The Thompson group reported an aramid nanofiber (ANF) separator for applications in non-aqueous RFBs and lithium metal batteries. (17,72,73) However, the preparation process required the repetition of spin-coating steps 20 times. Moreover, the size and uniformity of the obtained separator are limited by the working area of the spin-coater, which prohibits the wide application of this membrane in large-scale RFBs. In this work, a doctor-blading method is developed to fabricate the pristine nanoporous ANF separator (Figure S12). This scalable method can produce pristine membranes with a size as large as 30 cm × 20 cm. In this work, poly(diallyldimethylammonium chloride) (PDDA) and poly(styrenesulfonate) (PSS) solutions are used to tune the pore polarity for improved selectivity. The detailed procedure for the preparation of the CANF separator is presented in the Supporting Information.
    The smooth surface of the CANF indicates the uniformity of the structure, which could be observed in the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image (Figure S13). N2 adsorption–desorption measurements show that both pristine ANF and CANF membranes possess mean pore diameters of 3–6 nm (Figure S14) with minimal amounts of pores larger than 10 nm of diameter, indicating that the treatment of polymer solutions does not alter the pore size. However, the introduction of polymers with charged groups (quaternary ammonium for PDDA and sulfonate for PSS) to the membrane pores changes the polarity of the microenvironment, making the membrane suitable for anion–cation ion exchange. The cross-sectional SEM image shows that the CANF separator has a uniform thickness of 4.7 μm with a layer-by-layer structure (Figure 2a). The ultrathin CANF separator is beneficial for decreasing the membrane impedance and improving the power density of the battery. Benefiting from the abundant hydrogen bonding of the chemical component of the membrane, namely poly(p-phenylene terephthalamide), the ultrathin CANF separator presents excellent mechanical strength for safe battery operation (Figure 2b).
    To investigate the ion selectivity of the CANF membrane for use in RFB, a series of molecules of various sizes, including Me-V, PEG3-V, PEG12-V, Fc, and Fc+, were used to test the selectivity of the CANF separator in H-cells. Each sample with a known concentration of 50 mM was placed in one side of the H-cell, and the permeability was indicated by the concentration of the sample in the counter cell, which was obtained from the peak current in the CV scan together with the calibration curves (Figures S15 and S16). The detailed procedure of the permeability study is provided in the Methods in the Supporting Information. Notably, the permeability value for Fc is 1.089 × 10–7 cm2/s, which is ∼4 times higher than that for Fc+ (2.77 × 10–8 cm2/s) (Figure 2c). The high permeability of Fc is probably due to the fact that the surface of the CANF separator is finally soaked in the polymer solution of PDDA, composed of polymeric backbones with ammonium groups, which can suppress the crossover of positively charged Fc+ by Coulomb repulsion (Figure S17). Likewise, CANF can also lower the permeability of viologens with two positive charges. Although Me-V (without counteranion) is close in size to Fc and Fc+ (Figure 2c and Table S4), the positive charges on the nitrogen atom endow the CANF membrane with a low permeability (2.66 × 10–9 cm2/s). However, Me-V has the highest permeability among the three kinds of viologen molecules because it has the smallest molecular size. The introduction of PEG units can increase the molecular size of viologen, which, in turn, effectively suppresses the crossover of viologen by size-exclusion or steric effects. In addition, the number of repeat units of PEG has a significant impact on the results. The permeability of PEG3-V (1.12 × 10–9 cm2/s) is almost 3 times higher than that of PEG12-V (3.99 × 10–10 cm2/s). Compound PEG12-V gives the lowest permeability, which is 1 order of magnitude lower than that of Me-V and 3 orders of magnitude lower than that of Fc. This significant decrease in the crossover rate, owing to the large molecule of PEG12-V and the small pore size of the CANF separators, reflects the advantages of structural tunability of organic species in RFBs. The diffusion constant/permeability value of PEG12-V is ∼5 times higher than that of Me-V (Table S1), revealing that the introduction of PEG units does mitigate the crossover issue via a highly effective physical blocking mechanism. We also studied the permeability of PEG12-V at a higher concentration (200 mM). CV scans of PEG12-V in 0.1 M TBAPF6-MeCN in the side of the H-cell that originally contained 200 mM PEG12-V indicated that the concentration of PEG12-V slightly decreased due to the increased volume caused by osmotic pressure (Figure S16g). During the calculation of the permeability of PEG12-V, the volume change was ignored due to that the value was <5%. By contrast, the peak current density of PEG12-V in the blank-electrolyte side increased over time (Figure S16h), suggesting that the concentration of PEG12-V increased continuously (Figure S16i). The permeability of PEG12-V for the CANF separator at various times is stable [(7.69 ± 0.5) × 10–11 cm2/s]. However, the value was lower than that in the 50 mM system, perhaps because the increased viscosity suppresses the diffusion of PEG12-V in the electrolyte. The mitigated crossover of PEG12-V is beneficial for improving the Coulombic efficiency and capacity retention in the battery. (10,18) Because of the excellent properties of relatively long PEG chains, PEG12-V was used as the representative PEGylated viologen for the following studies.

    2.4. Electrochemical Performance of the PEG12-V/Fc Battery

    The internal impedance of a separator in the battery environment plays an important role in the battery charge/discharge process. (13,59,74) A low internal impedance allows the battery to be charged or discharged at a relatively high current density with a small overpotential for a high energy efficiency. The internal impedance of the electrolyte with a separator was tested in a homemade RFB with an active area of 28 cm2. The RFB was composed of two metal plates, two polytetrafluoroethylene plates, two copper plates, two graphite current collectors, two polytetrafluoroethylene frames, two graphite-felt electrodes with an area of 28 cm2, two peristaltic pumps, two electrolyte tanks, and some pump tubes. Six bolts were used to complete the cell assembly. Assembly was conducted in an argon-filled glovebox. The Nyquist plots of the PEG12-V/Fc battery with and without active materials show that the introduction of Fc into the catholyte and PEG12-V into the anolyte slightly affects the impedance (Figure 3a). The increased bulk impedance is attributed to the increased viscosity in the anolyte (Table S2), while the increased charge transfer resistance may be due to the interaction of PEG12-V with graphite felt originating from the strong affinity of the PEG units for graphite.
    The open-circuit voltage (OCV) of a battery pertains to the states of charge (SOC) and potentials of the redox species. The OCV of the PEG12-V/Fc battery monotonically increases with the SOC, and a drastic OCV increase occurs at 50% SOC (Figure 3b). Prior to the drastic change, the initial PEG12-V molecule is reduced to a cation radical, corresponding to the redox potential at −0.82 V versus Fc+/0 (Figure 1b). Subsequently, the cation radicals were further reduced to the uncharged bipyridinylidene at a lower redox voltage (−1.24 V vs Fc+/0). The two-electron utilization doubled the energy capacity of the battery compared to the case with the one-electron redox process. The rate performance of a battery refers to the charge/discharge ability of the battery at varied current densities, which is related to the impedance of the battery. (33,75) The polarization tests were conducted at different SOCs, and a peak power density of 3.48 mW cm–2 was obtained at ∼100% SOC (Figure S18a) with a high internal impedance (Figure S18b). At ∼100% SOC, the battery presented a polarization area-specific resistance of 363 Ω cm2. Although the resistance decreases with reduced SOCs, the overall resistance primarily originated from the membrane (Figure S18c).
    The PEG12-V/Fc battery was subjected to galvanostatic charge and discharge at the predefined current densities within the potential range of 0–2 V (Figure 3c,d). All of the curves at varied current densities present two well-defined charge/discharge plateaus due to the two redox potentials of viologens. At 1 mA/cm2, the battery possesses a discharge capacity of 12.5 mAh, which is 93% of the theoretical capacity. The Coulombic efficiency, energy efficiency, and voltage efficiency can be up to 97.2%, 71.4%, and 73.4%, respectively (Figure S19). The discharge capacity and energy efficiency of the PEG12-V/Fc battery decrease as the current density increases from 1 to 3.5 mA/cm2, where 63.7% of the theoretical capacity is used. The current density of a majority of the reported non-aqueous RFBs is <4 mA/cm2, except where Daramic and Celgard types of membranes with large pore sizes were used, which displayed low Coulombic efficiency and severe crossover (Table S5). The excellent rate performance is attributed to the high ionic conductivity of the CANF separator in the electrolyte and the high electrochemical stability of redox-active materials.

    2.5. Long Cyclability of the PEG12-V/Fc Battery

    Long cycling performance is one of the most important indicators because it reflects the cycle life of a battery. To investigate the viability of the PEG12-V/Fc battery, the battery was subjected to galvanostatic cycling 500 times. Figure 4a presents the capacity and Coulombic efficiency versus cycle number and testing time of a PEG12-V/Fc battery and a Me-V/Fc battery at a current density of 2 mA/cm2, prior to which the batteries were cycled five times at 1 mA/cm2 (Figures S20 and S21a). All of the batteries were tested at a potential ranging from 0 to 2 V. The PEG12-V/Fc battery has a discharge capacity of 11.8 mAh at the first cycle, 88% of utilization of total PEG12-V. Additionally, the battery exhibits stable cyclability with a retention capacity of 90% over 150 cycles, 80% over 310 cycles, 70% over 443 cycles, and 66% over 500 cycles, outperforming the Me-V/Fc battery with a retention capacity of only 22% after 205 cycles, even though the Me-V/Fc battery has the same utilization of total Me-V at the first cycle. The discharge capacity versus operating time is another superior parameter to evaluate the working life of a battery. The retention capacity of the PEG12-V/Fc battery was 66% after 185 h (500 cycles), corresponding to a capacity decay of 0.9%/h. In contrast, the Me-V/Fc battery was cycled for 38.4 h (205 cycles) and presented a retention capacity of 22% after 38.4 h and a capacity decay of 2.0%/h. In addition, an average retention capacity of 99.93% per cycle is achieved for the PEG12-V/Fc battery, which is better than those of most reported non-aqueous RFBs (Table S5). Benefiting from the highly selective CANF separator, the battery presents an average Coulombic efficiency of >99.3%, thus outperforming most reported non-aqueous RFBs, as well (Table S5). The Me-V/Fc battery, by contrast, possesses an average Coulombic efficiency of only 98.1%, due to the high crossover rate of two-electron-reduced Me-V. The CV scans of the catholyte and anolyte indicate that the concentration of Me-V in the anolyte is lower than that in the catholyte (Figure S21b). This is probably because the two-electron-reduced Me-V permeates through the CANF separator to the catholyte side and is oxidized back to its original charge state. However, the Me-V at the catholyte is in a dication state and cannot readily permeate through the CANF separator. Consequently, a higher concentration of Me-V in the catholyte is observed, which further decreases the capacity retention of the Me-V/Fc battery. The variation in the charge/discharge profiles of the PEG12-V/Fc battery at different cycle numbers (Figure 4b) suggests that the overpotential of the PEG12-V/Fc battery slowly increases with cycle number, which is related to the increased impedance (Figure 4c). Compared with the Me-V/Fc battery, the PEG12-V/Fc battery presented a lower energy efficiency and a lower voltage efficiency due to the increased impedance that originated from the higher viscosity of the electrolyte and the reduced diffusion constant of PEG12-V (Figure S22).
    The CV scans of the catholyte and anolyte of the PEG12-V/Fc battery after 500 cycles were recorded (Figure 4d). Compared with the CV scan of the initial PEG12-V in TBAPF6-MeCN, no additional peaks were observed in those of the catholyte and anolyte after 500 cycles (Figure S23). However, the CV scan shows that the current intensity of the electrolyte before cycling is higher than the sum of those in the catholyte and anolyte, indicating decomposition of PEG12-V. The concentrations of Fc in the catholyte and anolyte are nearly identical, suggesting that the CANF membrane is not effective for suppressing the crossover of Fc due to its small molecular size. On the contrary, the crossover of PEG12-V is substantially suppressed, indicating that the CANF separator can efficiently suppress the permeation of PEG12-V to the counter cell. The 1H NMR spectra of the PEG12-V solutions before and after the long-period cycling of the battery were recorded (Figure S24). While the major features of PEG12-V remain, the additional satellite peaks in the aromatic region after cycling suggest slight decomposition of PEG12-V. Compound Me-V presents undesirable stability in the catholyte (Figure S25), even though it is stable in the anolyte. The decomposition reaction of Me-V and the resultant concentration gradient would constantly drive the permeation of Me-V through the CANF separator, causing the capacity of the Me-V/Fc battery to deteriorate. Therefore, it is critical to suppress the crossover of viologens in this kind of RFB.
    To pursue a non-aqueous RFB with a higher energy density, a PEG12-V symmetrical battery is studied. The PEG12-V-based symmetrical battery in a concentration of 200 mM presents a very high resistance (500 Ω cm2) (Figure S26a), resulting in inferior charging/discharging performance. However, at 100 mM PEG12-V, the symmetrical battery exhibits a relatively lower internal impedance (300 Ω cm2) (Figure S26a). The reduced impedance may be due to the decreased viscosity of the electrolyte. The PEG12-V symmetrical battery in a concentration of 100 mM still shows a high polarization voltage when charged/discharged at a current density of 1 mA/cm2 (Figure S26b). At a current density of 0.5 mA/cm2, the battery presented stable charge/discharge property at a voltage ranging from 0 to 1.2 V. The discharge capacity of the battery is 2.34 Ah/L (Figure S26c), corresponding to 87.3% utilization of active materials. In addition, the battery delivered a Coulombic efficiency of 97.1%, suggesting the robustness of PEG12-V as a two-electron-active anode material in non-aqueous RFBs.

    3. Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, a PEGylation strategy on the viologen-based anolyte is developed for the enhanced performance of non-aqueous RFBs. The viologen with two PEG12 chains offers two-electron activity, high solubility, and improved stability, which are important requirements for RFBs with high energy density and long cyclability. In addition, the methodology for the scalable preparation of the highly selective CANF separator is developed. The membrane effectively suppresses the crossover of PEG12-V by physical blocking and Coulomb repulsion mechanisms, while allowing the fast transport of the supporting ions between the anolyte and catholyte. Paired with Fc, the non-aqueous RFB delivered an excellent retention capacity of 66% after 500 cycles with an average Coulombic efficiency of >99.3%. Considering the low solubility and serious crossover of Fc, it is essential to design outstanding cathode redox-active macromolecules to further improve the overall performance.
