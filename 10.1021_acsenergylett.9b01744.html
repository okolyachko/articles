    Abstract

    An innovative approach to improve the energy density of redox flow batteries (RFBs) through electrochemically mediated reactions is demonstrated. Soluble anion radical species (biphenyl and pyrene) mediate reversible sodium storage in a red phosphorus (P) anode located in an external packed bed reactor in the absence of binders or conductive additives. Because the anion radical species can be recycled several times through the cell stack during a single charge/discharge cycle, a mediated RFB effectively decouples the battery’s energy density from the redox species’ solubility in the electrolyte. The present study demonstrates the highest capacity reported to date for a mediated anode (up to 800 mAh/gP via reversible formation of NaxP). This approach represents a paradigm shift in the field and can conceivably achieve energy densities exceeding 200 Wh/kg, which is ∼10 times greater than that of conventional RFBs (e.g., 25 Wh/kg for an aqueous vanadium system).

    To enable the widespread adoption of renewable energy technologies, an electrical grid capable of storing intermittent energy sources (solar, wind, etc.) must be developed. Redox flow batteries (RFBs) are promising energy storage devices for grid-level applications due to their extraordinarily long cycle life (resulting from the use of highly reversible redox couples) and the ability to independently scale their energy and power densities. (1)

    The energy density of a conventional RFB is dictated by its capacity (which is directly related to the solubility of the redox species in the electrolyte) and operating potential. Numerous RFB chemistries utilizing both aqueous (2−6) and nonaqueous (7−11) electrolytes have been explored. In general, aqueous systems have low operating potentials of ca. 1.5 V, (12) resulting in poor energy densities (e.g., 25–30 Wh/kg for an all-vanadium RFB). In comparison, the use of organic electrolytes with wider electrochemical windows moderately increases the energy density, (9,11) but these devices are still ultimately limited by the solubility of the redox species in the electrolyte. To circumvent the solubility issue at the anode, some noteworthy studies (8−10) have investigated the use of Li metal anodes in a flow battery configuration. Despite their high energy densities, Li metal anodes present safety concerns due to dendrite formation and irreversible capacity losses caused by anode passivation. A promising yet largely unexplored approach (13−35) for flow batteries utilizes electrochemically mediated reaction(s) to charge and/or discharge an active material, as first introduced by Wang et al. in 2006. (24)

    To produce a mediated RFB with improved energy density, a high-capacity anode with low operating potential is needed. One promising such material is phosphorus (P), which can be charged to form sodium phosphide (Na3P) with a theoretical capacity of 2596 mAh/gP at potentials < ∼1 V vs Na/Na+. (36) Na and P are also inexpensive and abundant resources, which make them ideal candidates for large-scale electrochemical energy storage devices. Despite these attractive properties, the performance of P anodes has been hindered by the large volume changes of P (308% expansion when forming Na3P) during battery operation. (37) Conventional electrodes containing P, conductive additive, and binder exhibit poor cycling stability due to repeated swelling/shrinking of P, which results in electronic isolation of the active material. (37) Numerous studies have explored P-based composites (e.g., carbon matrixes containing P, (38−40) NiP3, (41) and Sn4P3 (42,43)) to accommodate these volume changes, and while these reports have shown promise (e.g., capacities up to 2000 mAh/gP), the overall performance of these materials is not yet competitive with today’s leading Li-ion battery technologies.

    The present work demonstrates a new approach that uses mediated electrochemical reactions involving arene anion radical redox species (biphenyl and pyrene, denoted as ß and π, respectively) to utilize a high-capacity P anode in a redox flow configuration. This concept is illustrated schematically in Figure 1. Rather than using the solvated anion radicals as the primary energy storage medium (as has been reported by Yu et al., (11) for example), herein the anion radicals are transferred to a packed bed reactor containing red P powder that is sodiated/desodiated through mediated electrochemical reactions. During charge, a biphenyl anion radical pair (Na+ß•–) reduces red P powder to Nax+yP, producing the neutral biphenyl species (ß0), which is recycled back to the cell stack where the anion radical pair is regenerated. This process continues until the P powder is fully sodiated. To discharge the Nax+yP powder, a second arene (pyrene, π) is included in the anode electrolyte. Pyrene oxidizes the Nax+yP to NayP (0 < y < 3), yielding the solvated ion pair Na+π•–, which is subsequently oxidized to the parent pyrene (π0) in the cell stack. The overall charge and discharge reactions are illustrated in Figure 1b,c, respectively.

    Mediated RFBs such as the one discussed above offer several major advantages over current state-of-the-art technologies. Because the anion radical species can be recycled several times through the cell stack during a single charge/discharge cycle, a mediated RFB’s energy density is decoupled from the redox species’ solubility in the electrolyte. The use of mediated electrochemical reactions also eliminates issues related to electrical isolation of red P, which undergoes large volume changes during sodiaiton/desodiation. However, it should be noted that red P particle pulverization could affect system performance by (i) increasing pressure drop through the packed bed reactor and/or (ii) disrupting any interfacial layers present on the active material’s surface, which may also be sensitive to mechanical stresses caused by the flowing electrolyte. (44) Finally, the proposed system ensures inherently safe battery operation because an accidental short circuit through the membrane will not result in a dangerous thermal runaway scenario.

    The fundamental redox properties of electrolytes containing biphenyl and/or pyrene dissolved with 0.9 m sodium trifluoromethanesulfonate (NaTFS) in diglyme were first investigated. As shown in Figure 2a, cyclic voltammograms of electrolytes containing either 0.02 m biphenyl or 0.02 m pyrene exhibited half-wave potentials (E1/2, determined from the average of the anodic and cathodic peak positions) of 0.22 and 0.75 V vs Na/Na+, respectively. These results demonstrate that biphenyl and pyrene operate within an appropriate potential range to drive reversible Na storage in a red P anode (wherein most of the capacity is available at ca. 0.1–1 V vs Na/Na+). (37)

    A custom electrochemical cell (Figure S1) was designed to evaluate the redox properties of biphenyl and pyrene in a flow battery configuration. The electrolyte in the working compartment contained 22 mAh of biphenyl + 22 mAh of pyrene (see Table S1) and was cycled between 0.1 and 1.4 V vs Na/Na+ at a current of 5 mA (corresponding to 0.78 mA/cm2 and a C-rate of 0.125C). A Na-ß″ Al2O3 membrane was used to eliminate crossover of the mediator species during battery operation. (45) Representative charge/discharge curves are shown in Figure 2b. The two plateaus near 0.75 and 0.23 V vs Na/Na+ during reduction correspond to formation of Na+π•– and Na+ß•– as given by eqs 1 and 2, respectively.(1)(2)During the oxidation steps, minimal voltage hysteresis (<10 mV) was observed for both plateaus, and each species exhibited very reproducible behavior over several cycles, as shown in Figure 2b and 2c. Interestingly, the reduction and oxidation capacities for each plateau differed by 3–4 mAh/cycle, but the overall irreversible capacity loss was only ∼0.5 mAh/cycle (corresponding to 98–99% Coulombic efficiency). This irreversible capacity loss was most likely due to a side reaction involving pyrene, as is discussed later in the text.

    To demonstrate the feasibility of the mediated RFB anode shown in Figure 1, red P powder (22 mg, corresponding to 57 mAh assuming formation of Na3P) was added to the electrolyte in the working electrode compartment after performing the measurements shown in Figure 2c. To establish proof-of-concept, this work utilized a small quantity of red P (i.e., a theoretical capacity ratio QP/Qß+π ≈ 1.5) that was added directly to the electrolyte rather than a packed bed reactor. It should be emphasized that practical devices should contain higher P loadings to achieve high energy density. The mediated charge and discharge reactions are summarized in eqs 3 and 4(3)(4)where 0 ≤ x + y ≤ 3 depending on the reaction conversion. During galvanostatic reduction (see Figure 3a), pyrene and biphenyl were converted from the neutral species (π0 and ß0) to the anion radical pairs (Na+π•– and Na+ß•–) at ∼0.75 and ∼0.23 V vs Na/Na+, respectively. The Na+ß•– subsequently reacted with red P powder to produce Nax+yP and ß0 (eq 3). The neutral biphenyl was then circulated back to the cell stack to regenerate Na+ß•– until the P was fully sodiated. The mediated sodiation capacity was 1370 mAh/gP (corresponding to formation of Na1.58P), as calculated using the method shown in Figure 3b. During the oxidation steps, Na+ß•– and Na+π•– were oxidized to their neutral forms. π0 subsequently desodiated the Nax+yP to NayP (eq 4), forming Na+π•–, which was recycled back to the cell stack to regenerate π0. The capacity measured at ∼0.75 V vs Na/Na+ during oxidation was much greater than that which can be attributed to the pyrene content (see Figure 3a), demonstrating that the pyrene successfully mediated the desodiation reaction. Figure 3c shows that the initial desodiation capacity was 710 mAh/gP and gradually faded to 670 mAh/gP over nine cycles. When the current density was reduced from 0.78 to 0.16 mA/cm2 (see Figure S2), significantly higher sodiation and desodiation capacities were measured (1900 and 1120 mAh/gP, corresponding to stoichiometries of Na2.20P and Na0.91P, respectively), indicating that the system was limited by the reaction rate between the mediators and P powder. The rate capabilities of the mediated P anode could possibly be improved by (i) applying interfacial coatings to red P to facilitate mediator adsorption/desorption, (ii) using smaller red P particles to reduce Na transport limitations within the active material, and/or (iii) changing the coordination environment of the arene species. Notably, Na+ß•– readily reduces red P in diglyme, but this mediated reaction does not proceed in tetraglyme.

    The RFB results in Figures 2 and 3 had relatively low Coulombic efficiencies (98–99 and ∼93% for nonmediated and mediated RFBs, respectively; see Figure S3), which indicates that parasitic side reactions occurred during cycling. Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) and additional electrochemical experiments were performed to explore possible decomposition routes, including (i) irreversible electrolyte reduction (46) in the presence of Nax+yP and (ii) decomposition of one or both mediator species. As shown in Figure S4, there were no detectable changes in the FTIR spectra of the baseline electrolyte (0.9 m NaTFS in diglyme) when stored over Na1.99P (generated using a Na+ß•– solution at ∼0.2 V vs Na/Na+) for an extended period (17 months), which demonstrates excellent chemical compatibility between these species.

    On the other hand, the electrolyte’s vibrational spectrum showed some changes after performing the mediated cycling tests in Figure 3. These spectra were dominated by the NaTFS and diglyme, but several features associated with the biphenyl and pyrene species could still be resolved, as shown in Figure S5a,b. Compared to the pristine (i.e., uncycled) electrolyte, the cycled solution from the mediated RFB showed several new bands at 785, 1570, and 1582 cm–1, which are attributed to accumulation of soluble decomposition products. Precise identification of these products is difficult due to the low mediator concentration (0.077 m) used in the RFB tests. Notably, no further spectral changes were observed for the cycled solution after extended storage (14 months; see Figure S5c,d), which suggests that the decomposition was an electrochemically driven process. Additional electrochemical experiments described in the Supporting Information (see Figures S7 and S8 for the cell design and cycling results, respectively) indicate that pyrene was decomposed at potentials < 0.5 V vs Na/Na+ possibly due to (i) reductive dearomatization and/or (ii) formation of an unstable dianion radical species. As such, alternative oxidative mediators need to be identified to improve the red P anode’s cycle life and efficiency.

    Despite issues with the pyrene mediator, the performance of the red P anode shown here compares well with previous reports on mediated and redox-targeted approaches for RFBs. Table 1 summarizes state-of-the-art performance of mediated RFBs since this topic was first reported in 2006. Compared to these previous works, the present study provides several significant advancements to the field, including (i) a Na-based mediated electrode operated at the most negative potential to date (e.g., ∼1.3 V more negative than TiO2), (ii) the highest capacity mediated electrode (e.g., 800 mAh/g, which is 5–10× higher than that of all other reported works), and (iii) the first mediated conversion reaction anode, which experiences large volume changes during cycling (308% for red P to Na3P). (37) The RFBs reported here also utilized higher mediator concentrations and current densities compared to most other nonaqueous cells. However, it should be emphasized that mediated RFB technology is in the early stages of development and still requires major advancements in new materials (e.g., membranes), redox chemistries, and system design to be competitive with commercial battery systems. Future work on the mediated P anode presented here should focus on improving the system’s energy efficiency and cycle life by identifying suitable replacements for the pyrene mediator. Full cells that utilize mediated cathodes (e.g., NaxV2(PO4)3 as reported by Zhou et al. (47)) should also be developed.

    To evaluate the anode’s structural evolution during chemical mediation, titrations of red P using biphenyl and pyrene solutions were performed. Red P powder was chemically sodiated using a 2 m biphenyl solution in which the molar ratio of Na+ß•–/ß0 was 2/1. As titrant was added to dry P powder to produce Nax+yP, the solution changed from dark blue (indicating the presence of Na+ß•–) to translucent yellow (indicating the absence of Na+ß•–). The total titrated capacity was 1830 ± 91.9 mAh/gP (average ± standard deviation for three independent titrations), corresponding to an average stoichiometry of Na2.11P. This titrated capacity agrees with that observed in the flow cell experiments (e.g., 1900 mAh/gP at 0.16 mA/cm2). The structure of red P and its sodiated product (Na2.11P) were analyzed using X-ray diffraction (XRD) and Raman spectroscopy. The red P was completely amorphous, as evidenced by the lack of diffraction peaks, as shown in Figure 4a. In comparison, the sodiated powder contained several strong reflections (e.g., at 2θ values of 36.2 and 37.0°) that matched those of Na3P (JCPDS 74-1164) with P63/mmc symmetry. Because the titrated capacity was only 1900 mAh/g (compared to 2596 mAh/g for Na3P), these results suggest formation of Na3P near the particle surface and Na-deficient phases (0 < x + y < 3) throughout the bulk, possibly due to slow reaction kinetics and/or sluggish solid-state Na diffusion as x + y → 3 in Nax+yP. The Raman spectrum of red P (Figure 4b) showed several overlapping peaks in the range of 300–500 cm–1, which agrees well with previous reports, (39,48,49) and the Na2.11P spectrum contained three bands at 156, 225, and 402 cm–1.

    To better understand the P structural evolution during sodiation, a series of powders with known stoichiometry (NaP15, NaP7, Na3P11, Na3P7, and NaP) were prepared by adding an appropriate amount of Na+ß•– to red P powder. Note that these stoichiometries represent all of the known crystalline Na–P compounds that have been reported at room temperature. (50−52) Interestingly, the mediated NaP15, NaP7, Na3P11, and Na3P7 stoichiometric compounds were completely amorphous (see Figure S6a), but the NaP powder showed weak reflections that index to crystalline Na3P. These results demonstrate that the sodiation proceeded through an amorphous Nax+yP solid solution until x + y ≈ 1, at which point crystalline Na3P regions were formed. Raman analysis of the various Na–P stoichiometries (Figure S6b) shows that as P was sodiated up to Na3P7 two key changes in the spectra occurred, namely (i) the band intensity at 355 cm–1 decreased and (ii) a weak peak appeared near 150 cm–1. In comparison, the two most highly sodiated powders (NaP and Na2.11P) exhibited three broad bands positioned at 150, 220, and 400 cm–1. While assignment of these bands to specific phonon modes is outside of the scope of the present study, the Raman data in Figures 4b and S6b reflect how the P bonding environment changed during the chemically mediated sodiation reaction. Interestingly, previous works have shown that sodiation of conventional phosphorus anodes (i.e., those containing active material, conductive carbon, and binder) also proceeds through amorphous NaxP solid solutions followed by formation of crystalline Na3P near the end of charge. (53,54)

    To study the mediated desodiation product, a solution containing 0.5 m π0 in diglyme was added to the Na2.11P powder. The color of the π0 titrant solution immediately changed from translucent yellow to dark red, indicating formation of the Na+π•– anion radical pair. Note that an exact end point for this titration could not be determined due to the strong light absorption of Na+π•–. The desodiated powder (reddish/brown in color) was completely amorphous, as shown by the XRD data in Figure 4a. Desodiating the Na2.11P to NayP (y ≈ 0.91 as estimated from the 0.16 mA/cm2 electrochemical data in Figure S2) changed the Raman spectrum (Figure 4b) in the following two ways: (i) the peaks at 150, 220, and 400 cm–1 decreased in intensity and (ii) convoluted peaks near 310 cm–1 and a band at 439 cm–1 appeared. Comparing the Raman spectra of the desodiated product and pristine red P indicates that π0 did not remove all of the Na from the structure, which is consistent with the electrochemical data discussed previously. This effect is illustrated in Figure 5, which provides a summary of the mediated charge/discharge reactions for the red P anode.

    This study demonstrates proof-of-concept for a high energy density mediated red P anode in a RFB configuration. Whereas conventional RFBs utilize the solvated redox species as the energy storage medium, the method presented here uses an external packed bed reactor wherein reversible Na storage in a red P powder is mediated using soluble arene anion radicals (biphenyl and pyrene). Consequently, the energy density of this system increases with the P/mediator mass ratio. Because the mediated electrochemical reactions occur outside of the cell stack, this method allows for efficient utilization of active materials that are difficult to incorporate into conventional electrodes due to large volume changes, low electronic conductivity, etc. This technology can be adapted to include either Na-based or Li-based anodes and cathodes by selecting appropriate mediators that bound the operating potential window of the active material. For example, pairing a mediated P anode with a mediated sulfur cathode is expected to produce energy densities exceeding 200 Wh/kg (see the Supporting Information and Figure S9 for details). Ultimately, such mediated electrochemical reactions can be used to develop low-cost, high-energy-density nonaqueous RFBs for megawatt-scale energy storage.
