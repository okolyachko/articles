Abstract

With the resonance strategy, a tetraethylthiuram disulfide molecule that shows high solubility in various organic solvents is explored as a promising active material for nonaqueous redox flow batteries based on the reversible redox chemistry of disulfide bonds. This work presents useful insights into designing stable organic redox-active molecules in nonaqueous systems.

Abstract

Nonaqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs) have great potential to achieve high-energy storage systems. However, they have been limited by low solubility and poor stability of active materials. Here we demonstrate organosulfides as a new-type model material system to explore the rational design of redox-active molecules in nonaqueous systems. The tetraethylthiuram disulfide (TETD) molecule shows high solubility in various common organic solvents and achieves a high reversible capacity of ca. 50AhL1 at a high concentration of 1M. The resonance structures in the reduced product endow the molecule with high electrochemical stability in different organic electrolytes. The underlying mechanism in redox chemistry of organodisulfides involving the cleavage and reformation of disulfide bonds is revealed by material/structural characterizations. This study provides a new perspective of molecule designs for the development of redox-active materials for high-performance nonaqueous RFBs.

Introduction

The redox flow battery (RFB) as an important energy storage system is considered as a promising alternative for large-scale energy storage applications owing to the advantageous features of high scalability, flexible modular design, and decoupled control of energy and power densities.1 According to supporting electrolytes, it can be mainly divided into two types: aqueous and nonaqueous systems. Aqueous RFBs have attracted extensive interests in the past decades because of their low cost, environmental benignity, and high conductivity of electrolytes. However, the low energy density, which is mainly limited by the low solubility of active materials and electrochemical window of water, is a big barrier for their further development to achieve high-energy storage. In comparison, nonaqueous RFBs with the feature of a wide potential window could offer a higher voltage, resulting in an enhanced energy density.1d Nevertheless, the high solubility of active materials in the presence of supporting salts and long cycling stability demonstrated at high concentrations are still challenging. Thus, designing highly concentrated electrolytes with long-term chemical and electrochemical stability is highly desired for advanced nonaqueous RFBs. Many different strategies have been utilized to improve the molarity of active materials, such as molecular engineering,2 deep eutectic solvents,3 or solid suspension electrolyte.4

Recently, compared to the inorganic counterparts, organic active materials have attracted increasing attention as competitive candidates in the energy storage field due to their cost effectiveness, structural diversity, and flexible tunability of properties.2a, 5 To date, most of the reported organic molecules for RFBs are focused on metallocene,6 TEMPO,7 viologen,8 and quinone9 compounds. Besides, the molecular natures of quinone-,10 flavin-,11 phenazine-,2b phenothiazine-,2c and viologen-based12 organic compounds have shown their great potential as promising active materials for designing high-performance aqueous RFBs. However, the application of their derivatives in nonaqueous systems is still challenging considering the limited solubility, low demonstrated concentration and poor stability. Although several organic molecules showed high solubility (>1M) in common organic solvents, poor stability of radical molecules during cycling and serious capacity decay at high concentrations are still daunting problems in nonaqueous systems.13 Besides, it is necessary to pair different redox species in the same organic electrolyte for the RFB operation, but the rational design of redox-active molecules that can stably work in multiple nonaqueous electrolytes has not been explored. Hence, exploring new organic molecules with high solubility and excellent stability is a promising direction to realize high-capacity and long-life nonaqueous RFBs.

With the merits of high capacity and low cost, sulfur-containing materials have been successfully applied in energy storage, especially for Li-S batteries.14 Inheriting the merits of sulfur, the organosulfur-based chemistry is emerging in RFBs.15 To seek stable and highly soluble organic molecules for nonaqueous RFBs, inspired by high solubility and reversible redox chemistry of polysulfides, organosulfides (R-Sn-R) attract an increasing interest because of their multi-electron transfer capability, structural diversity, and cost effectiveness.15 Though several organodisulfides (R-S-S-R) and organotrisulfides (R-S-S-S-R) have been explored, the low utilization of capacity, unstable cycling, and possible Li2S formation are still not well addressed, and the reaction mechanism with structural effects has not been comprehensively understood.16

In this study, we take advantage of organodisulfides to design stable and highly concentrated electrolytes. Though organodisulfides (two-electron transfer reaction) show a lower capacity in comparison with trisulfide compounds (four-electron), it is able to circumvent the possible formation of Li2S (solid phase) to ensure the high reversibility and high utilization of active materials.16c Herein, tetramethylthiuram disulfide (TMTD) and tetraethylthiuram disulfide (TETD) are adopted as new-type redox-active molecules for nonaqueous RFB design given their ultralow material cost and stable resonance structures, which can provide good electrochemical stability in different organic electrolytes. TMTD shows a limited solubility while TETD with the local asymmetry has a high solubility (>1M) in various common organic solvents. With the resonance structure to handle negative charges in the reduced product, the good electrochemical stability of TETD can be realized in several organic solvents. The TETD-based battery achieves high capacity retention (99.9% per cycle) at both low and high concentrations along with high utilization of capacity (ca. 95%). And the reversible and stable redox chemistry in TETD molecules involving the cleavage and reformation of disulfide bonds is clearly revealed by spectroscopic analyses, suggesting the resonance approach in the molecular design represents a promising strategy to tackle the fundamental challenges of redox-active molecules in nonaqueous RFBs.

Results and Discussion

Among different organosulfides, phenyl disulfide (PDS)-based molecules had been recently explored as a new class of active materials for high-energy nonaqueous RFBs.16c Though the allyl-activation strategy improves the reaction kinetics of PDS, the synthetic procedure is time-consuming and the capacity utilization and reversibility are still unsatisfied (low Coulombic efficiency). And the reported cycling performance with large fluctuations may indicate complicated reaction chemistry. Herein, to circumvent the formation of solid Li2S and increase the resonance stability, TMTD and TETD are adopted as potential redox-active candidates for nonaqueous RFB applications. The thioketone structure linked with N heteroatoms could increase the radical stability after the cleavage of disulfide bonds in nonaqueous systems.17 Besides, as shown in Figure1a, based on the same source, the purchase unit-price of TMTD or TETD is several orders of magnitude lower than many other available organosulfides. Even compared with PDS that is widely applied in energy storage, they show a much lower material cost. On the other hand, solvent screening is conducted to identify their solubility in different common organic solvents, which is important for realizing the high energy density. Intriguingly, TMTD shows a limited solubility (<0.25M) in all selected solvents except for dichloromethane (DCM; Supporting Information, FigureS1), while TETD achieves a high solubility (>1M) in all solvents (Figure1b; Supporting Information, FigureS2), including acetonitrile (ACN), dimethylformamide (DMF), and 1,2-dimethoxyethane (DME), which are widely used for nonaqueous RFBs demonstration. According to the dielectric constant, all the solvents selected here are polar. By replacing methyl groups with ethyl groups, the asymmetry of molecular structure would increase accompanied by the increasing polarity. Thus, the TETD molecule shows an enhanced solubility in these polar solvents. As TETD shows much higher solubility in nonaqueous medium, the further electrochemical characterization is mainly focused on TETD molecules.

To evaluate the electrochemical property of TETD in different electrolytes, the cyclic voltammetry (CV) is carried out (Figure1c; Supporting Information, FiguresS3 and S4). In a wide potential range, two pairs of major redox peaks showed up in most cases. For DCM solvents, owing to the difficulty of dissolving lithium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiTFSI) as supporting salts, the electrochemical activity of TETD cannot be detected. Using LiTFSI as supporting salts, we find electrolyte-dependent reversibility of TETD, and according to the CV curves in the first 5 cycles, the good electrochemical reversibility can be obtained in ACN-, DME-, or DMF-based electrolytes. Additionally, taking ACN electrolyte as the example, by comparing the shape and intensity ratios of redox peaks, it is also found that the reversible reaction of TETD molecules is corresponding to the redox peaks located in lower potentials while the peaks located in higher potentials should be assigned to an irreversible reaction (Figure1c). To identify the mechanism underlying reversible and irreversible reactions, we further conducted the CV tests of TMTD and tetramethyl thiourea (TMT) in 0.5M LiTFSI ACN electrolytes (Supporting Information, FigureS5a,b). Comparing their CV curves and molecular structures, it implies that the reversible reaction could be ascribed to the cleavage and reformation of the disulfide (SS) bond18 and the irreversible reaction stems from the electron transfer of N heteroatoms (Supporting Information, FigureS5c). Because of the possible tautomerism after losing electrons in nitrogen atoms, the generated sulfur radicals may lead to the poor reversibility and potential instability of oxidized products. Moreover, considering the possible influence of supporting salts on the electrochemical reversibility, we also apply tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate (TEABF4) as the supporting salt to study the electrochemical behavior of TETD in ACN or DMF electrolytes, respectively (Supporting Information, FigureS6). The CV analysis indicates that the same reaction mechanism remains when using TEABF4 supporting salts, and it is beneficial to improving the electrochemical kinetics, as indicated in the reduced potential separation, which can be attributed to the higher conductivity of TEABF4 in ACN or DMF solvents. Given the low concentration of active materials and low scan rates in the CV analysis, the notable potential hysteresis of the reversible reaction should be attributed to the sluggish kinetics,19 which is a common shortcoming in most of organosulfides. Furthermore, the diffusion coefficient can be calculated by the RandlesSevcik equation since the reversible reaction of disulfide bonds is a diffusion-controlled process as indicated by the linear relationship between peak currents and square roots of the scan rates (Supporting Information, FiguresS7 and S8). When using TEABF4 supporting salts, the determined coefficient values in ACN electrolytes are about 2.58106cm2s1 (anodic) and 1.12106cm2s1 (cathodic), which are comparable to those of organic redox species in nonaqueous systems. And the diffusion coefficients in ACN electrolytes are slightly higher than DMF electrolytes, consistent with the higher conductivity of ACN electrolytes. In general, if the reduced product has a smaller size, its coefficient should be larger than that of organic molecules applied, which is confirmed by the derived coefficient results in ACN and DMF electrolytes.

The tested cell is designed based on lithium metal anode and TETD-based catholyte (Figure1d). For charging and discharging tests, the solid ceramic separator is adopted to avoid the influence of crossover on the electrochemical behavior and capacity loss, providing an approach to assess the intrinsic cycling stability of TETD in nonaqueous electrolytes. As illustrated before, the reversible reaction of TETD is based on the reversible redox chemistry of disulfide bonds (Figure2a). At relatively low scan rates, sometimes we can observe two cathodic peaks (Figure2b) during the reduction that may indicate a two-step process for the electron transfer in the cleavage of disulfide bonds. In the oxidation procedure, the thiolate anions lose electrons to reform the TETD molecule, resulting in one anodic peak during the positive scan. Figure2c shows the galvanostatic voltage profile of 0.1M TETD in LiTFSI-ACN supporting electrolytes at a current density of 0.1mAcm2, achieving a high capacity utilization of over 95%. Two voltage plateaus and one flat plateau are observed in the discharging and charging, respectively, which is identical to the CV analysis. The rate capability of TETD electrolytes is also studied under various current densities. As displayed in Figure2d, when the current density is increased to 0.6mAcm2, the discharge capacity can retain as high as about 84% of that at 0.1mAcm2. We find the potential separation in the galvanostatic test is smaller than that in the CV analysis. Considering that the Super P carbon coated on Ti plate is used as current collectors in our homemade cylinder cells, it may influence the electrochemical behavior of TETD. With coating different carbon materials on the working electrode in the three-electrode testing system (Supporting Information, FigureS9), the CV tests demonstrate that Super P carbon should play a role in facilitating the reaction kinetics by the possible catalyst effect. It gives a hint that we may have the opportunity to address the sluggish kinetics of organosulfides by further developing current collectors decorated with appropriate catalysts.

We first studied the electrochemical stability of TETD at low concentrations by the cycling test at various current densities. As presented in Figure2e, it achieves stable cycling at the low current density of 0.1mAcm2, showing high capacity retention of nearly 91% after 50cycles. When the current density increases to 0.2mAcm2, the high utilization of active materials is kept and meanwhile, the cell also exhibits relatively high capacity retention of 80% over 200cycles with a capacity loss rate of 0.10% per cycle or 0.57% per day (Figure2f). Figure2g shows the corresponding cycling capacity and Coulombic efficiency to the rate test in Figure2d, which is followed by a continued cycling test at the constant current density of 0.4mAcm2. With a high capacity utilization of 88%, the long-term stable cycling is obtained for 670 cycles. The average Coulombic efficiency exceeds 99.5% that is an indicative of high electrochemical reversibility of TETD in nonaqueous systems, and the low capacity loss rate of 0.037% per cycle confirms the good electrochemical stability of TETD. Overall, the demonstrated cell performances at low concentrations are superior to most of the reported nonaqueous RFB systems in terms of cycling stability. Multiple cells are assembled and tested repeatedly to show that the excellent rate capability and cycling stability of TETD are reproducible and easily accessible (Supporting Information, FigureS10). Moreover, when the cutoff potentials in the charging and discharging test are set to be 4V and 2.7V, respectively, the exhibited Coulombic efficiency is very low (<80%) along with a serious capacity decay in a few cycles, which is in accordance with the irreversible reaction observed in the CV analysis (Supporting Information, FigureS11).

Except for LiTFSI-ACN supporting electrolytes, the cycling stability of TETD in other organic solvents is also investigated with the use of LiTFSI supporting salts. Balancing the charge carriers through the applied solid ceramic separator, the voltage profile of 0.1M TETD in 1M LiTFSI DMF supporting electrolytes has a slightly higher discharge potential than ACN electrolytes (Supporting Information, FigureS12), which can be ascribed to the solvent effect. Though it also achieves a high capacity utilization of approximately 92% at the current density of 0.1mAcm2, the rate capability of DMF electrolyte is inferior to that of ACN electrolyte. In addition to the lower conductivity, the major reason might be the lower diffusion coefficient of active materials in DMF electrolytes. The relatively stable cycling in DMF supporting electrolytes is also obtained, showing capacity retention of about 80% after 150cycles at 0.1mAcm2 (Supporting Information, FigureS12). To study the electrochemical stability of TETD in a wide selection of solvents, we further conducted the charging and discharging test of 0.1M TETD in 1M LiTFSI DME electrolytes (Supporting Information, FigureS13). At the current density of 0.2mAcm2, capacity retention of about 78% after cycling for 100cycles is obtained. Furthermore, to confirm the excellent electrochemical stability of disulfide bonds in the designed molecular structure, we measured the cycling stability of TMTD in 0.5M LiTFSI ACN supporting electrolytes. Due to the limited solubility of TMTD, it is only able to dissolve 0.05M TMTD in supporting electrolytes as the catholyte. Consistent with the TETD molecule, TMTD is also capable to achieve stable cycling over 250cycles with a low capacity loss of 0.08% per cycle (Supporting Information, FigureS14), suggesting high electrochemical stability of the designed molecular structure. The possible mechanism is that the resonance structure in the reduced product can handle the negative charge efficiently, accompanying with the electron delocalized in the molecule, which significantly reduces side reactions with organic solvents or supporting salts, thus enabling the stable cycling in multiple nonaqueous electrolytes, which is rarely reported for other redox-active organic molecules (Figure4a).20 Besides, the advantage of sulfur atoms in the resonance structure is that it has a large atomic size to help decrease the charge density, resulting in the better-stabilized electron in the molecular structure.

Realizing the stable cycling at high concentrations is another critically important feature for the development of advanced RFBs. To this end, the electrochemical stability of highly concentrated TETD electrolytes (0.5 and 1M) is also evaluated using the LiTFSI supporting salts and ACN solvents. The charging and discharging profile of 0.5M TETD ACN electrolyte versus time at the current density of 0.3mAcm2 are shown in the Supporting Information, FigureS15. In the initial few cycles, it may experience an activation process and then shows a stable voltage profile with an average discharge voltage of around 2.4V. Intriguingly, at the high concentration of 1M, the rate capability of TETD electrolyte (Figure3a) is even slightly better than that at 0.1M, implying that improving the molar moiety of TETD molecules might facilitate the reaction kinetics because of the increased chance and shortened diffusion for disulfide bonds reformation. At the current density of 0.1mAcm2, the 1M TETD electrolyte delivers a capacity of 50.3AhL1 with high capacity utilization of 94% and retains about 90% of capacity when the current density increases to 0.6mAcm2. And, with an operating voltage of about 2.5V, it is able to produce a high energy density of 125WhL1 based on the catholyte. The stable cycling of 0.5M and 1M TETD electrolytes is successfully demonstrated in Figure3b and c, respectively. Both of them can be stably cycled over 1000hours with a low capacity loss. The 0.5M TETD electrolyte has a capacity fade rate of 0.14% per cycle over 100cycles and the 1M electrolyte obtains high capacity retention of 90% after 50cycles. Such good cycling stability at high concentrations has been rarely achieved in other reported redox-active molecules for nonaqueous systems. To further show its potential, the Li/TETD flow cell was tested by using a Li-graphite hybrid anode, showing capacity retention of 94% after 100cycles at 10mAcm2 with the corresponding energy efficiency of about 60% (Figure3d; Supporting Information, FiguresS16 and S17). Consistent with the CV results, the large overpotential is observed due to the sluggish kinetics of TETD, which results in the relatively low efficiency. But when using the appropriate positive species as the catholyte and TETD as the anolyte, the maximum power density can reach 115mWcm2 (Supporting Information, FigureS17c), which is comparable to some demonstrated aqueous systems. As summarized in Figure4b and the Supporting Information, TableS1, the unprecedented cycling stability of TETD-based electrolytes exceeds most of the reported nonaqueous systems using organic molecules as active materials. To further confirm the electrochemical stability of TETD at high concentrations, the repeated cycling test of 1M electrolytes is conducted. As shown in the Supporting Information, FigureS18, the stable cycling at a high concentration of 1M is reproducible. Besides, it is worth mentioning that the rapid capacity decay observed in 1M TETD electrolytes is usually caused by the crack of solid ceramic separator occurred during the cycling or problems in the anode part (electrolyte leaking or depletion of Li metal). And if the solid separator keeps a good condition, it may have an opportunity to resume the capacity by refreshing the anode part (Supporting Information, FigureS18c).

To comprehensively understand the reaction mechanism of TETD molecule, we take the 0.1M TETD ACN electrolyte as the representative sample to conduct the UV/Vis and NMR characterizations at different charge and discharge states (Figure5). As mentioned before, the proposed mechanism suggests the thiolate anion as the reduced product. Thus, the purchased sodium diethyldithiocarbamate (SDDC) sample is utilized as the standard reference in the UV/Vis and NMR spectra to identify the product after discharging. As displayed in Figure5a, the 0.1M TETD ACN electrolyte shows three major peaks in the UV/Vis spectra, which are mainly located in the range from 190 to 300nm, corresponding to possible * and n* transitions in the compound. According to the molecular structure of TETD, the absorption peak at about 218nm should be assigned to the n* transition of disulfide bonds and the other two absorptions (251 and 282nm) are related to N heteroatoms and * transition in thiocarbonyl groups. With understanding the absorption assignments, it is clearly revealed that the disappear of the absorption peak at 218nm after the first discharging suggests the cleavage of disulfide bonds. Referenced to the UV/Vis spectra of SDDC, the remained two absorptions in the range from 250 to 300nm after the discharging proves the formation of thiolate (diethyldithiocarbamate) anions as the reduced product. What is more, the reversible change of UV/Vis spectra during the repeated charging and discharging confirms the highly reversible reaction of TETD molecules in ACN electrolytes. The chemical stability of TETD molecules during cycling is also studied by the UV/Vis analysis. After resting in the glove box for over 7days, the UV/Vis spectra of 0.1M TETD electrolytes before and after charging do not have any peak shift or evolution (Supporting Information, FigureS19), confirming the good structure stability of TETD during the electrochemical test. The slightly increased peak intensity should be attributed to the potential evaporation of ACN solvents. For the reduced product, the identical spectra at different discharging states versus time are observed, suggesting the role of resonance structure in the good electrochemical stability of molecules (Supporting Information, FigureS20). After standing for 24hours in the glove box, the discharging samples maintain almost the same spectra despite the fact that the peak intensity shows a slight decrease with the elongation of the standing time (168hours), hinting that a slight decay of the reduced product may occur in a long period. But it should be noted that the transient exposure to air or tiny oxygen in the glove box during the transfer of samples may also affect the analysis. On the other hand, the identical 13C and 1HNMR spectra of TETD molecule and TETD electrolyte further confirm the structure stability of TETD in nonaqueous systems (Figure5b; Supporting Information, FigureS21). In the 13CNMR spectra of 0.1M TETD electrolyte, excluding the peaks from supporting electrolyte (LiTFSI and ACN) and DMSO (39.5ppm), the other five peaks observed are completely consistent with the result of TETD powders, indicating good chemical stability in nonaqueous electrolytes, which is further confirmed in the proton NMR result (Supporting Information, FigureS21). After discharging, the reduced product obtained after the bond rupture has a symmetric structure, leading to the reduced number of C peaks, which is identical to the molecular structure of the SDDC reference. The C position connected with S atoms experiences a downfield shift after the cleavage of disulfide bonds due to the resonance effect. After charging, the position and number of C peaks recover to the original state. In a word, using the SDDC structure as the reference, the reversible evolution of NMR spectra observed in 0.1M TETD electrolytes after charging and discharging not only validates the proposed reaction mechanism but also indicates the excellent electrochemical reversibility and stability of TETD during cycling.

Conclusion

By taking advantage of the reversible redox activity in designed organosulfides, we have demonstrated the resonance strategy in redox-active molecules can enable the design of stable and high-capacity nonaqueous energy storage systems. The disulfide bond as the redox center in the molecular structure of TETD shows a highly reversible reaction in nonaqueous electrolytes with sufficiently high chemical and electrochemical stability. Compared with the redox center of carbonyl, oxygen radicals, or N heteroatom in most reported molecules, the synergy of resonance structure and S heteroatom with the increased atomic size is capable to handle the negative charge more efficiently, enabling good electrochemical stability in multiple nonaqueous electrolytes. With a high solubility in nonaqueous electrolytes, the stable cycling of highly concentrated TETD electrolytes is successfully demonstrated, delivering a high reversible capacity of 50AhL1 at the concentration of 1M. Combining the CV analysis and exsitu UV/Vis and NMR characterizations, this work presents a useful perspective into the redox chemistry of organosulfides with a comprehensive understanding of reversible and irreversible reactions in the designed molecular structures, which involves the redox activity of disulfide bonds and diethylamide groups, respectively. Beyond the TETD model material system, the resonance strategy demonstrated in this work can be generalized to design other promising redox-active molecules for large-scale energy storage systems.
