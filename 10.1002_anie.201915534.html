Abstract

A persistent distonic radical cation was prepared through one-electron oxidation of an N-heterocyclic carbene–carbodiimide (NHC–CDI) adduct bearing pyrene substituents. Application of this compound in an organic redox-flow battery was demonstrated, setting the stage for the development of novel redox-active NHC–CDIs for various applications.

Abstract

We present the discovery of a novel radical cation formed through one-electron oxidation of an N-heterocyclic carbene–carbodiimide (NHC–CDI) zwitterionic adduct. This compound possesses a distonic electronic structure (spatially separate spin and charge regions) and displays persistence under ambient conditions. We demonstrate its application in a redox-flow battery exhibiting minimal voltage hysteresis, a flat voltage plateau, high Coulombic efficiency, and no performance decay for at least 100 cycles. The chemical tunability of NHCs and CDIs suggests that this approach could provide a general entry to redox-active NHC–CDI adducts and their persistent radical ions for various applications.

Synthetic strategies to mask the inherent reactivity of organic radicals1, 2 have been explored for fundamental interests and potential applications in organic synthesis,3, 4 polymerization,5-7 energy storage,8-10 and biological imaging.11-13 Carbene derivatives have been employed to stabilize a wide variety of otherwise reactive main-group radicals.14-20 To the best of our knowledge, all of the nitrogen-centered, carbene-stabilized radicals reported to date (for example, i and ii, Figure 1 a) exhibit significant resonance delocalization of the unpaired electron into the carbene moiety.15, 16 Alternative modes of stabilization that do not rely on the π-accepting abilities of carbenes will pave the way to create novel carbene-derived open-shell species with unique electronic structures.

We envisioned that persistent radical species could be accessed through the one-electron oxidation of N-heterocyclic carbene carbodiimide (NHC–CDI) zwitterionic adducts (1, Figure 1 b).21 Distinct from previously reported NHC-stabilized radicals,14-20 unpaired electron delocalization into the NHC is not possible in the case of 1.+. Instead, this radical cation possesses an electronic structure in which the charge and spin occupy spatially distinct regions within the same molecule. Such “distonic” radical cations, although traditionally studied in the gas phase22-24 or as reactive intermediates,25, 26 exhibit characteristic differences in stability and reactivity relative to conventional radical cations.27 Additionally, NHC–CDI adducts have been employed as metal ligands28, 29 and as components of structurally dynamic polymers;30 thus, the development of persistent open-shell NHC–CDI derivatives presents exciting opportunities for the development of advanced organic materials.

Herein, we report on the synthesis and characterization of NHC–CDI derived distonic radical ion 1.+ (Figure 1). The N-pyrene substituents of the amidinyl component of 1.+ stabilize the unpaired electron; oxidation of analogous NHC–CDI adducts derived from diphenyl CDIs (2 a, 2 b, 3, Scheme 1) generated radical cations that were too reactive to be directly observed at room temperature, quickly degrading into EPR-silent products consisting of dicationic dimers and hydrogen atom abstraction products (see the Supporting Information, Figure S1).

The syntheses of NHC–CDI adduct 1 and distonic radical cation 1.+ are shown in Scheme 2. Thiourea 4 was first prepared by the addition of commercially available 1-aminopyrene to 1-isothiocyanatopyrene. Dehydrosulfurization of 4 in o-dichlorobenzene provided carbodiimide 5 as a sparingly soluble yellow/green solid. When 1,3-bis(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)imidazol-2-ylidene (SIMes) was added to a stirred benzene dispersion of 5, homogenization and an accompanying red color change were observed followed quickly by the formation of a dark precipitate. The precipitate could be recrystallized from acetonitrile providing lustrous black crystals of 1.

The structure of NHC–CDI 1 was unambiguously determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction (Figure 2). The molecule adopts two different, alternating conformations in the solid state, one of which possesses C2 symmetry while the other is distorted from C2 symmetry (Figure 2 shows the C2 symmetric component).

Cyclic voltammetry (Figure 3) of 1 shows a reversible one-electron oxidation (E1/2=−0.17 V vs. Fc/Fc+) corresponding to 1 → 1.+ (Figure 1 b). The redox reversibility is maintained at low scan rates (Supporting Information, Figure S5), indicating a relative persistence of the distonic radical cation 1.+ at the electrode surface. On the other hand, 2 a and 3 undergo chemically irreversible oxidation, with anodic peak potentials Epa=−0.14 V and +0.40 V, respectively. These observations imply a stabilizing influence of the pyrene substituents of 1.+ relative to phenyl substituents of 2.+ and 3.+.

Chemical oxidation of 1 (Scheme 2) was carried out using silver bistriflimide (AgTFSI) in THF (E°′=0.41 V)31 at room temperature, which induced the formation of a deep brown color as well as Ag precipitate. After filtration, the resulting solution was NMR-silent and EPR-active, suggesting the formation of distonic radical cation 1.+. EPR spectroscopy (Figure 4) of 1.+ revealed extensive delocalization of the unpaired electron throughout the pyrene groups. The spectrum can be simulated32 with two relatively small, equivalent 14N hyperfine splittings (3.2 G) and 5 sets (2 protons per set) of 1H hyperfine splittings ranging in size from 0.8–1.9 G. This agreement is consistent with C2 symmetry in solution, where the unpaired electron is coupled to the two equivalent amidinyl nitrogens as well as 10 protons, corresponding to the peripheral π-sites with significant spin density on each pyrene ring. Importantly, the observed hyperfine coupling constants are in good agreement with those predicted from density functional theory (DFT) at the UB3LYP/6-311++G(d,p) level of theory (Figure 4, bottom).

Consistent with EPR results, DFT calculations support the classification of 1.+ as a distonic radical cation. Both the spin density (Figure 5 a) and the singly occupied molecular orbital (Supporting Information, Figure S9) of 1.+ are entirely constrained to the amidinyl and pyrene groups of the molecule, with insignificant contributions from the cationic imidazolium component.

Although 1.+ was prepared under inert atmosphere, exposure of EPR samples to atmosphere for several hours at room temperature caused no significant changes in the EPR spectral intensity (see the Supporting Information, Figure S10). In addition, unlike the phenyl-substituted analogs (Scheme 1), mass spectrometry indicated that 1.+ is not readily prone to dimerization. We hypothesized that the persistence of 1.+ arises from increased resonance stabilization of the unpaired electron afforded by the N-pyrene amidinyl substituents. DFT studies support this notion; the spin density of 1.+ (Figure 5 a) is much more diffused compared to that of a model phenyl-substituted distonic radical cation (Figure 5 b). By computing atomic spin densities (using Becke's multicenter integration method),33 we observed that the spin density at amidinyl nitrogens and aryl sites of 1.+ is reduced by nearly a factor of two relative to phenyl-substituted analogs (see the Supporting Information, Figure S11 for numerical values).

To demonstrate a potential application of this new type of persistent, distonic radical cation, 1 was used as a catholyte in an organic redox-flow battery. N-Methylphthalimide (N-MPI) was chosen as the anolyte.34 During the first charging cycle, 1 showed a very flat voltage plateau around 150 mV versus Ag/Ag+ (Figure 6 a). This flat plateau and low voltage hysteresis during discharge are attractive features for battery active materials leading to stable cell potentials and high round-trip efficiency. After the first 20 cycles, the Coulombic efficiency of the cell reaches an average value of 94.6 % (Figure 6 b), indicating the high reversibility of the redox events when up to 90 % of the theoretical capacity (0.06 mAh) is utilized. The low catholyte concentration (5 mm) used here would limit the energy density of a flow battery, but we anticipate that NHC–CDI adducts with enhanced solubility may enable high energy density redox-flow batteries in the future.

In summary, a π-conjugated distonic radical cation (1.+) was prepared by one-electron oxidation of a novel NHC–CDI adduct bearing pyrene substituents. This distonic radical cation was found to be persistent under ambient conditions and could be used as a novel catholyte species for an organic redox-flow battery. This work sets the stage for the development of NHC–CDI adducts for a range of applications that leverage redox events.
