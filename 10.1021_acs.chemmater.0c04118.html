    Abstract

    Nonaqueous organic redox flow batteries (NAORFBs) show great promise for grid energy storage but are currently facing key challenges such as high electroactive material cost and low energy density. Herein, we report the electrochemical properties and the potential application of a series of cost-effective electroactive nitrobenzene molecules in NAORFBs. Pairing the low-cost miscible liquid nitrobenzene (NB) with 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1-methoxy-4-(20-methoxyethoxy)-benzene (DBMMB) resulted in a flow battery that provides a high theoretical cell voltage of 2.2 V and a calculated energy density of 192 Wh L–1. In the charge–discharge testing, this battery delivers a stable cycling capacity retention of 99.5% per cycle over 100 cycles and a 70% energy efficiency at 40 mA cm–2 operation current density, verifying that liquid nitrobenzene is a promising low-cost electroactive anode molecule for NAORFBs.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Redox flow battery (RFB) technologies offer great potential to store renewable energy. In these batteries, the externally stored redox-active electrolytes circulate through the positive and negative electrode compartments and react on the electrodes for energy storage. (1−3) This unique technical architecture permits the merits of energy-power decoupling and flexible scalability. The past decades have witnessed great progress in inorganic (4−10) aqueous RFBs (ARFBs) (e.g., vanadium RFBs, Zn–Br2 RFBs, Fe–Cr RFBs). As one of the most studied ARFBs, vanadium RFBs have been successfully commercialized because of their high power and robust chemistry. However, the extensive application of conventional inorganic ARFBs is hindered by expensive redox-active metals and corrosive electrolytes. In addition, their energy densities are limited by the intrinsically low operating voltage window of water (<1.5 V). To overcome these limitations, tremendous efforts have been made in developing aqueous organic (11−17) redox flow batteries (AORFBs) and nonaqueous organic RFBs (NAORFBs) that employ sustainable and abundant organic molecules as electroactive species for green energy storage. Nonaqueous organic RFBs are especially attractive organic medium, as they enable a much wider electrochemical window of up to 5 V, providing a promising pathway in the pursuit of high-energy-density RFBs. (18−20) More remarkably, organic molecules in nonaqueous systems display high chemical and structural tunability, enabling the optimized design to increase the solubility and redox potential, and thus maximize the energy density of flow batteries. (21,22) It should be noted that nonaqueous systems may suffer from problems such as high electrolyte resistances, chemical instability, and crossover of the redox-active materials.
    Several class of high-performance electroactive molecules in NAORFBs have been studied, including N-methylphthalimide (MePh), (23) 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BzNSN), (24,25) 9-fluorenone (FL), (26,27) azobenzene, (28) and acetylpyridinium compounds (29) as anolytes and phenothiazine, (30−32) phenazine, (33,34) 2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-1-piperidinyloxy (TEMPO), (35) and 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1-methoxy-4-[20-methoxyethoxy]benzene (DBMMB) (23,25,26) as catholytes. Meanwhile, remarkable progress has also been achieved in the development of nonaqueous organometallic complexes. (36−40) Although organic electroactive molecules have the potential to enable cost-effective grid storage in NAORFBs, the field is relatively young, and economically feasible systems with performance that meets the technoeconomic cost target of $100 per kWh have yet to be realized. (25,41) Cost analysis indicates that redox-active molecules with low equivalent weight (<150 g (mol e–)−1), high solubility (>5 M), high separated redox potentials (>3 V), and superior chemical stability are favored to approach to the battery cost target of $100 per kWh. (24) Of the redox-active organic molecules explored for NAORFBs, the anolyte 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BzNSN) has the potential to approach this requirement for NAORFB applications, as it possesses an equivalent weight of 136 g (mol e–)−1, a high solubility of 5.7 M in acetonitrile (MeCN), and a low redox potential at −1.58 V (vs Ag/Ag+). (25) However, this molecule has a high material cost that cannot meet the target cost (please see the cost analysis in the Supporting Information), which would largely impede its practical application.
    In our effort to search for alternative promising redox-active candidates for nonaqueous RFBs with low cost and high energy density, we have explored the possibility of using nitrobenzene and its derivatives as promising anode materials for NAORFBs. As a common starting material for the production of aniline, nitrobenzene and its derivatives exhibit good electrochemical reversibility and stability and can undergo one- or two-electron transfer reaction at a redox potential of around −1.5 V vs Ag/Ag+. More remarkably, the compound is readily available and the material cost can be as low as $11.8 mol–1, which is an order of magnitude lower than that of the present reported nonaqueous electroactive molecules. To the best of our knowledge, applications of nitrobenzene-based electroactive molecules in NAORFBs have been rarely explored, although a hybrid organic–inorganic RFB that applied nitrobenzene and iodine in asymmetric solvents with a solid-state electrolyte has been reported. (42,43) There still remains a critical knowledge gap in understanding the electrochemical and cycling performance of nitrobenzene and its derivatives in NAORFB applications.
    In this work, we have studied the electrochemical properties and solubility of a series of representative nitrobenzene derivatives, which indicates that these compounds have reversible and stable electrochemical properties and good solubility in MeCN and are promising electroactive materials for NAORFB application. Subsequent experiments demonstrated that adopting liquid nitrobenzene as the anolyte and DBMMB as the catholyte provided a flow battery with a high cell voltage of 2.2 V and a high theoretical energy density of up to 192 Wh L–1. The optimized cell exhibited stable cycling capacity retention in tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate (TEABF4)/MeCN supporting electrolyte with 50% capacity retention after 100 cycles, corresponding to 99.5% capacity retention per cycle within 100 cycles.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The electrochemical properties of nitro compounds in media with proton donors have been extensively studied. In such media, nitro compounds are usually reduced to nitroso compounds or phenylhydroxylamine by the addition of electron and proton. (44) There are also few studies on the electrochemical properties of nitro compounds in aprotic nonaqueous media with varying electrodes and supporting electrolytes. (45,46) These studies have revealed that nitrobenzene and some of their derivatives can undergo reversible redox reactions in aprotic nonaqueous media. (43,47,48)
    To explore the possibility of using nitrobenzene and some of their derivatives as anolytes in NAORFB, we have studied the electrochemical properties of a series of nitrobenzenes (1–6) by cyclic voltammetry in MeCN containing TEABF4 as the supporting electrolyte, the common medium for flow battery. The half-wave potentials were calculated by averaging the oxidation and reduction peaks of each couple vs the Ag/Ag+ reference electrode and the reversibility of each couple was quantitatively assessed using the peak current ratios. As shown in Figure 1, nitrobenzene derivatives (1–3) bearing substituents such as H, CH3, and Br displayed one well-defined reversible redox couple at low potentials (−1.36 to −1.5 V vs Ag/Ag+) in the CV tests, while compounds 4–6 with groups of NO2, CHO, and COCH3 exhibited two redox couples in the range of (−1.0 to −1.8 V vs Ag/Ag+). The trend in the measured redox potentials is in agreement with the calculated LUMO energies (Figure S1). The appearance of two redox couples for compounds 4–6 can be explained by the formation of radical anions and quinoid dianions (see Supporting Information S2), and the para-carbonyl or nitro substituents are crucial for the second-electron reduction because the C═O or N═O groups could accept one more electron so as to form quinoid dianions. The CV experiments with different scan rates showed that the peak current has a linear relationship with the square root of scan rate for all compounds, indicating a diffusion-controlled redox process (Figures S3 and S4). According to the Randles–Sevcik analysis, the diffusion coefficients for each active species were obtained and are summarized in Table 1. It was found that the diffusion coefficient values are within the same order of magnitude and larger than those of most reported electroactive species in NAORFBs (e.g., 8.38 × 10–6 cm2 s–1 for N-methylphthalimide; 7.18 × 10–6 cm2 s–1 for 2-methylbenzophenone). (23,49) This CV scan rate study also indicated good electrochemical reversibility of these compounds, since the peak separations, peak potentials, and peak current ratios remained unchanged at different scan rates.

    The solubility of redox-active molecules is one of the critical factors determining the theoretical energy density of the battery. We have therefore investigated the solubility of these nitrobenzene-based RAMs (1–6) using the UV–vis spectroscopic method (see Table 1 and Figures S5–S9). In this investigation, MeCN was selected as the solvent due to its wide electrochemical window (∼5 V), high conductivity, and low viscosity, which are the coveted features for nonaqueous flow battery operation. The results indicated that the solubility varies with the substituents of the aromatic ring. Nitrobenzene (1) is a pale yellow liquid at room temperature and is miscible with MeCN. It can be demonstrated that it is possible to prepare a MeCN solution containing 6.5 M nitrobenzene and 0.5 M TEABF4 supporting electrolyte, corresponding to a theoretical capacity of 174 Ah L–1. Compounds 2–6 are solids. p-CH3–C6H4–NO2 is also highly soluble in MeCN (with a solubility of 5.4 M). The compounds 3 and 4 bearing a Br or NO2 substituent have relatively low solubilities of 0.9 and 0.7 M, respectively. The compounds 5 and 6 bearing functional groups CHO and COCH3 have moderate solubility (1.5, and 3.0 M, respectively). The solubilities of these compounds appear to be closely related to the polarity of the molecules (the dipole moments are 4.28 D (1), 4.40 D (2), 2.69 D (3), 0 D (4), 2.65 D (5), 3.26 D (6), respectively). Additionally, the solubilities of compounds 2–6 in 0.5 M TEABF4/MeCN were also examined. It was found that the solubilities of all the nitrobenzene derivatives slightly decrease in MeCN containing the supporting salt (see Table 1), agreeing with the prior studies on other organic compounds. (30)
    The CV and solubility properties of nitrobenzene derivatives suggest that nitrobenzene derivatives are promising candidates as electroactive materials for NRFBs. In this work, we selected the most soluble nitrobenzene to demonstrate the feasibility. As electrochemical kinetics play critical roles in RFB performance, the electrochemical kinetic property of nitrobenzene was then examined using the rotating disk electrode (RDE) tests. As can be seen in Figure 2, the LSV curves display well-defined mass-transport-controlled limiting current (iL) plateaus in the rotation rates ranging from 300 to 2100 rpm. The charge-transfer rate constant, which contributes to the reaction rate of the electrode was evaluated by the Koutecky–Levich analysis. Figure 2b shows the linear relationship between i–1 and ω–1/2 at a series of overpotentials. The kinetic-controlled currents (ik) were obtained as the reciprocal of the Y-axis intercepts and were then plotted as a function of the overpotential (Figure 2c). Finally, the electrochemical kinetic rate constant (k0) was calculated to be 3.57 × 10–2 cm s–1 for the reductive reaction of nitrobenzene. This kinetic parameter is greater than those of the reported redox-active molecules 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (1.0 × 10–2 cm s–1) and 9-fluorenone (1.18 × 10–2 cm s–1) (25,33) applied in NAORFBs, and is therefore expected to lead to fast electron transfer processes in the battery test.
    To demonstrate the viability of using nitrobenzene as an anolyte in NAORFBs, we employed DBMMB as the catholyte, which has demonstrated reliable performance in NAORFBs. Figure 2d displays the CV curves of a mixture containing equal molarities of NB and DBMMB in 0.5 M TEABF4 in MeCN. The CV curves of nitrobenzene almost overlapped with each other after 200 cycles, suggesting its high electrochemical stability in the CV test. The data indicates that the system can fabricate a redox flow battery with a cell voltage of 2.2 V and a high theoretical energy density of 192 Wh L–1 based on the solubility of the anolyte (6.5 M). Before the cycling test, we adopted the rapid and simple bulk electrolysis technique to assess the charge and discharge abilities of this cell design at a diluted level (Figure S10). In an H-cell electrolysis experiment, galvanostatic cycling was controlled by setting the capacity limit of the theoretical value of 1.34 mAh and voltage limit of 1.8–2.5 V. The resulting data showed one well-defined charge and one well-defined discharge plateau in the voltage–time curves (Figure S11), indicating one stable electron transfer reaction and viability of this battery design.
    The NB/DBMMB flow cell, in which a Celgard 4560 porous separator (thickness 135 μm, pore size 64 nm, porosity 53%) was used to separate the cathode and anode compartments, is shown in Figure 3. We first examined the conductivities of solutions composed of solvents such as MeCN, propylene carbonate (PC), 1,2-dimethoxyethane (DME), and supporting electrolytes such as TEABF4, tetrabutylammonium tetrafluoroborate (TBABF4), lithium tetrafluoroborate (LiBF4), lithium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiTFSI), and tetraethylammonium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (TEATFSI). As shown in Figure 4a, the MeCN solutions, in general, have a higher conductivity than that of the DME solutions, and solutions containing TEABF4 salt are more conductive than those with TEATFSI and LiTFSI salt, consistent with the previously reported observation. (26) The cycling stability of NARFBs is closely related to the stability/reactivity of radical ion species, which can be influenced by the structures of the electroactive species, solvents, and supporting electrolytes. In this regard, several frequently used solvents including DME, PC, and MeCN and supporting salts including lithium tetrafluoroborate (LiBF4), TEABF4, TBABF4, TEATFSI, and LiTFSI were selected to test the impact of solvent and supporting electrolyte on the cycling performance. It can be seen in Figure 4b,c (also see S12) that different supporting salt and solvent bring different discharge capacities. For instance, systems with supporting salts LiTFSI and LiBF4 have no discharge capacity, whereas the system with MeCN as the solvent and TBABF4 as the supporting electrolyte provided the highest discharge capacity and displayed a much slower capacity decay. The screening experiments revealed that TEABF4/MeCN is the optimal supporting electrolyte/solvent combination, which offers a good capacity retention. After optimizing the supporting salt and solvent, the cycling performance was then evaluated using 0.05 M mixed NB/DBMMB electrolytes on both sides to minimize species crossover with 0.5 M TEABF4/MeCN. This battery has a low area-specific resistivity of 3.8 Ω cm2 (see Figure 4a). We first studied the effect of current density on capacity (Figures 5a and S13). As shown in Figure 5b, the Coluombic efficiency increased from 60% at 10 mA cm–2 to 90% at 50 mA cm–2. A decline in the voltage efficiency from 95 to 82% was observed because of the increased overpotential between the charging/discharging processes. The capacity utilization dropped with the increasing current density from 10 to 50 mA cm–2, corresponding to a material utilization ratio of ∼82.1 and ∼74.6%, attributed to the elevated polarization/overpotential within the cell. From the rate study, we chose the current density of 40 mA cm–2, which can realize good electrolyte utilization and energy efficiency to demonstrate long-duration charge–discharge cycling. Figure 5c displays the charge/discharge capacities and efficiencies as a function of cycle number. This flow cell achieved a good material utilization of ∼75% at the performed current density and a gradually increased CE above 85% and EE of 70% throughout the 100 cycles. This cell exhibits capacity retention of 50% after 100 cycles, corresponding to a 99.5% capacity retention per cycle. Figure 5d compares the theoretical energy densities, capacity retentions, and material costs of our RFB and the representative NAORFBs. The theoretical energy density and capacity retention of our system are comparable with these of representative NAORFBs. Redox-active materials cost is one of the major contributors to the total battery costs. It is noted that the price of nitrobenzene ($12 mol–1) is the lowest among the redox-active molecules used in NAORFBs.
    We have also tested the cycling performance at higher electrolyte concentrations (0.1, 0.5, 1.0, and 2 M). The cells at these concentrations can all be safely charged and discharged. It was observed that the cell efficiency is decreased when the electrolyte concentration increases probably due to the increased electrolyte viscosity, material crossover, and cell polarization (Figure S14a). (25) The capacity retention and material utilization rates also decrease with the increase in the electrolyte concentration. When the electrolyte concentration is increased to 2 M, the cell exhibits very low material utilization.

    Decay Mechanism Studies

    It was noted that the battery suffered from low cycling capacity retention at a higher electrolyte concentration. The decay may be related to the decomposition of the electroactive material and/or the cell design. In this work, we have investigated the stability of nitrobenzene in the cell cycling to determine if the decomposition of the electroactive material is the major cause.
    We first studied the properties of nitrobenzene radical anion (NB•–) by a computational study. The structure of the NB•– radical anion was optimized at B3LYP/6-31G* level, and the natural bonding orbital (NBO) charges at the redox-active sites of NB•– were computed. For comparison, similar calculations were also performed for several reported anode candidates in NAORFBs, including 9-fluorenone (9-FL), N-methylphthalimide (MePh), and 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BzNSN) (Figure S15). The O atoms in the radical anions of 9-fluorenone (9-FL), N-methylphthalimide (MePh), and 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BzNSN) exhibit a high electron density with the NBO charge between −0.659 and −0.715. In contrast, the anionic character of the O atom in the NB•– radical anions is much lower (−0.588). The calculated spin density of the NB•– radical anions indicates that the radical is mainly localized on C1 (0.243), followed by N1 (0.200) and O1(O2) (0.202) (Figure S1). The calculated spin distribution together with the LUMO of the NB and the SOMO of NB•– radical anion indicate that the NB•– radical anions exhibit extensive electron delocalization over the whole nitrobenzene skeleton.
    To determine the stability of nitrobenzene in cell cycling, we have analyzed the composition of cycled electrolytes (0.5 M NB/DBMMB in 1 M TEABF4). The crude mixture of the cycled electrolytes was first analyzed by 1H NMR spectroscopy and homonuclear correlation spectroscopy (COSY) (Figures 6a and S16). Both 1H NMR and 1H–1H COSY spectra reveal that a small fraction of nitrobenzene was decomposed. The major decayed product displays two sets of multiplets at ∼7.6 and 7.9 ppm. The major decayed species can be isolated and was identified to be azobenzene (AB) by 1H NMR (Figure S17) and high-resolution mass spectroscopy (HRMS-CI) (Figure S18). The compound displays molecular ion peak at m/z = 182 in HRMS analysis. Azobenzene (AB) was likely formed by a plausible mechanism shown in Figure 6b. In the presence of water, NB•– could react with H2O to form nitrosobenzene via second-electron reduction, which subsequently undergoes further reduction to afford phenylhydroxylamine. Condensation of nitrosobenzene and phenylhydroxylamine would generate azoxybenzene, which can be further reduced to azobenzene (AB). A similar process has been proposed for the electrochemical reduction of nitrobenzene to aniline. (50)
    As inferred from the proposed mechanism, water is essential in the decomposition of nitrobenzene. The trace amount of H2O in our system could come from contamination (by water) of the anolyte, catholyte, electrodes, cell pipes, membranes, solvent, and supporting salt. Since only a small fraction of nitrobenzene was decomposed after cycling, we believe that the decomposition of nitrobenzene is not the major cause of capacity fading at high-concentration conditions in our system.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we have investigated the solubility and electrochemical properties of a series of nitrobenzene-based electroactive molecules. By employing the readily available, low-cost, and highly soluble nitrobenzene as the anolyte and DBMMB as the catholyte, we fabricated a flow cell with a high voltage of 2.2 V and a stable cycling capacity retention with 99.5% capacity retention per cycle within 100 cycles. Further work will be conducted on the optimization of the electrolyte and cell design to achieve a more stable cycling performance at high electrolyte concentrations.

    Materials

    Chemicals, including nitrobenzene derivatives, were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, stored in an argon glovebox, and used directly without further purification. DBMMB and TEATFSI were synthesized according to the previously reported procedure. (26) Celgard 4560 separators and Daramic porous polyethylene/silica separators were obtained from Celgard LLC (Charlotte, NC) and Daramic LLC (Owensboro, KY), respectively. All electrochemical characterizations were operated under an argon atmosphere. The ultraviolet–visible spectrum was investigated by PerkinElmer Lambda 365 UV–vis. 1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker DPX 400 spectrometer at 400 and 100 MHz, respectively. Chemical shift (δ, ppm) was reported with references to tetramethylsilane (1H and 13C). Mass spectra were collected on an Agilent GC/MS 5975C system, or an API QSTAR XL system. Note that nitrobenzene and some of its derivatives are toxic and can be readily absorbed through the skin. Experiments shall be handled in accordance with the strict laboratory safety rules.

    Solubility Tests

    The solubilities of solid nitrobenzene derivatives in acetonitrile and MeCN containing 0.5 M TEABF4 were measured by ultraviolet–visible spectroscopy (UV–vis) method. First, suitable standard solutions were prepared by the serial dilution of the freshly prepared solutions. Then, the absorbance of the standard solutions was measured at a characteristic wavenumber and used to obtain a calibration curve. Finally, the freshly supersaturated solutions in pure MeCN or MeCN containing 0.5 M TEABF4 were prepared after removing undissolved material and diluted 20 000 folds to afford the absorbance within the range of the calibration curve. The solubility of a solid nitrobenzene derivative was then determined based on the UV–vis absorbance of the diluted solution and the calibration curve. The solubility of liquid nitrobenzene in a 0.5 M TEABF4/MeCN solution was obtained by adding portions of 0.25 mL of nitrobenzene and 27 mg of TEABF4 to 0.5 mL of 0.5 M TEABF4/MeCN solution, until the appearance of precipitation. Based on the total mass of the nitrobenzene added, the solubility was calculated to be 6.5 M in 0.5 M TEABF4/MeCN solution.

    Flow Cell Test

    A redox flow battery system with a flow-through configuration (RFB) for the electrolyte flow was used.
