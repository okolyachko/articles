Abstract

Polyimide was employed as a low-cost and high-capacity solid material to boost the capacity of pH-neutral 2,7-AQDS electrolyte to 97 Ah L−1. Through in situ FTIR spectroscopic study, a hydrogen-bonding mediated reaction mechanism was disclosed.

Abstract

Aqueous organic redox flow batteries (AORFBs) have received considerable attention for large-scale energy storage. Quinone derivatives, such as 9,10-anthraquinone-2,7-disulphonic acid (2,7-AQDS), have been explored intensively owing to potentially low cost and swift reaction kinetics. However, the low solubility in pH-neutral electrolytes restricts their application to corrosive acidic or caustic systems. Herein, the single molecule redox-targeting reactions of 2,7-AQDS anolyte are presented to circumvent its solubility limit in pH-neutral electrolytes. Polyimide was employed as a low-cost high-capacity solid material to boost the capacity of 2,7-AQDS electrolyte to 97 Ah L−1. Through in situ FTIR spectroscopy, a hydrogen-bonding mediated reaction mechanism was disclosed. In conjunction with NaI as catholyte and nickel hexacyanoferrate as the catholyte capacity booster, a single-molecule redox-targeting reaction-based full cell with energy density up to 39 Wh L−1 was demonstrated.

Large-scale energy storage systems are essential for smooth migration from the fossil-fuel-based to a renewables-based economy. The unique operating principle and modular design provide redox flow batteries (RFBs) a decoupled energy/power scaling feature as well as superior safety over static batteries.1 As the most mature RFB technology, the vanadium flow battery (VFB) attracts the most commercialization interests owing to the alleviation of the electrolyte intermixing issue by using one common element on both sides.2 However, the commercialization and market penetration of VFB are severely obstructed by the high economic and environmental cost of vanadium and the corrosive nature of the strong acid electrolyte used in the battery.3-5

As alternatives, metal-free organic compounds consisting of earth-abundant elements with synthetic tunability represent a promising direction for low-cost RFBs.6 Since the first report of 9,10-anthraquinone-2,7-disulphonic acid (2,7-AQDS),7 aqueous organic redox flow battery (AORFB) and organic redox molecule (ORM) have attracted considerable attention. These ORMs can be categorized into three classes (which may overlap): 1) aromatic ORMs like quinone and alloxazine derivatives undergoing proton-coupled redox reaction with good electrochemical reversibility and fast kinetics;8-10 2) radicals like TEMPO and viologen derivatives exhibiting high solubility and fast reaction kinetics in neutral electrolytes,6 but the durability of the former remains to improve11, 12 and the toxicity of the latter15 (although relatively stable13, 14) remains a concern; 3) organic polymers, enabling low-cost size-exclusion membrane, whereas the contradiction between viscosity and volumetric capacity needs further exploration.16-18

The anthraquinone derivatives stand out owing to excellent stability, potentially low cost, and good kinetics. However, the pH-dependent solubility limits their application to the corrosive or caustic electrolyte environment that brings about safety concerns in case of leakage. For instance, while the solubility of 2,7-AQDS is about 1 m and about 0.55 m in 1 m H2SO4 and 1 m KOH, respectively, it reduces to less than 0.58 m in pure water and less than 0.30 m in 1 m KCl.8 Attempts have been made to explore the quinone derivatives in weak-alkaline conditions.19, 20 Apart from molecular engineering, it was recently found that adding electrolyte additives like ethylene glycol and ammonium ions can also improve the solubility of 2,7-AQDS in pH-neutral electrolytes, by forming hydrogen bonds.21-23

The redox-targeting reactions, where solid materials are used as capacity boosters in the energy storage tank (Figure 1 a; Supporting Information, Figure S1), have been demonstrated as an efficient method to considerably improve the effective solubility of redox species in various RFBs such as nonaqueous redox flow lithium or sodium batteries and metal complex-based aqueous RFBs, and so on.24-28 In this study, the redox targeting reaction of quinone with polyimide was investigated to solve the low-solubility hurdle of 2,7-AQDS in pH-neutral electrolyte system.

As shown in Figure 1 b and the Supporting Information, Figure S2, 2,7-AQDS, the benchmarking ORM with rapid and reversible 2 e− redox reaction,7, 29 has a redox potential at −0.25 V vs. SHE in pH-neutral conditions. Compared with the strong acidic electrolyte, the 2,7-AQDS in pH-neutral anolyte is more energetically favorable and environmentally friendly, though the solubility is compromised.7, 21 NaI, with a redox potential at 0.62 V vs. SHE is served as catholyte, which together forms a full cell with a cell voltage of 0.87 V.

For a minimal potential loss and fast reaction kinetics, we screened solid materials based on potential matching and structure similarity; the former is the basis of the single mediator redox targeting (SMRT) reaction while the latter may facilitate the reaction as suggested by the previous Prussian blue-ferrocyanide system.28, 30 Polyimide (PI), a low-cost polymeric material with tunable potential, was chosen here as the capacity booster for anolyte. The 1,4,5,8-naphthalenetetracarboxylic dianhydride (NTCDA) derived PI was synthesized, as confirmed by the FTIR spectrum with matched characteristic peaks31, 32 (Supporting Information, Figure S3). It shows two waves at −0.27 V and −0.21 V vs. SHE (the two reduction peaks merged into one), both rather close to that of AQDS in the pH-neutral electrolyte (Figure 1 b). Based on the reported specific capacity (150 mAh g−1) and density measured by pycnometry method (2.84 g mL−1),4 the volumetric capacity and effective concentration of PI are as high as 434 Ah L−1 and 16 m, respectively. The adoption of solid-state capacity booster would not only bypass the solubility limit, but it may also alleviate the issues of the concentrated electrolyte such as concentration-intensified degradation, attenuated utilization, escalated crossover, and so on.

Prior to the battery testing, a spectroelectrochemical study was conducted to validate the SMRT reaction and reveal the underlying mechanism. With the in situ attenuated total reflection (ATR)-FTIR setups shown in Figure 2 a–c, the bonding information of either redox mediator or solid material upon CV scans could be detected in real time. As shown in Figure 2 d, with PI coated on the ATR window as the reactive substrate (Figure 2 c condition), the FTIR spectra of PI responded accordingly to the CV scans of the mediators, of which the trend of changes, especially those at 1400–1800 cm−1 reversed when the CV was scanned beyond the half-wave potential (around −0.5 V vs. Ag wire) of 2,7-AQDS and when the current started to reverse, attesting to the reversible SMRT reactions between 2,7-AQDS and PI in both cathodic and anodic directions. Comparing the experiment without PI substrate (Supporting Information, Figure S4) with the one with PI substrate (Supporting Information, Figure S6), the substantially enhanced current (from 10−2 to 10−1 mA) and altered scan rate-peak current dependence (from scan rate dependent to scan rate independent) also confirmed the SMRT reaction and the regeneration of 2,7-AQDS on PI substrate.

A close scrutiny of the upper and lower spectrum in Figure 2 e shows that the C=O and C−O stretching band of the 2,7-AQDS/2,7-AQDS2− at 1679 and 1364 cm−1 became indistinguishable in the PI+AQDS spectra. In contrast, the IR signals of AQDS in the fingerprint region (800–1200 cm−1) and the O−H stretching vibration (2800–3800 cm−1) were obviously enhanced when the spectra were examined on the same scale (Supporting Information, Figure S7), which implies the adsorption of AQDS on PI and the interaction between them. Comparing the electrochemically reduced PI (middle) with that via SMRT reaction (lower) in Figure 2 e, the similar interconverted changes of imide carbonyl C=O bands, namely the increase at 1703 cm−1 and those decreases at 1579/1514 cm−1, suggest that the PI in SMRT reaction undergoes the same reduction and re-oxidation as the electrochemically reduced PI.33 Interestingly, in the presence of 2,7-AQDS, the original O−H stretching band of reduced PI at 3200 cm−1 was indistinguishable. The dominated bands centered at about 3050 and 3450 cm−1, though resemble the hydrogen-bonded bands of 2,7-AQDS2−, were both suppressed when the CV was scanned to the half-wave potential and reappeared afterward. This could be rationalized by assuming that those bands were correlated to the interaction between AQDS and PI and the dimer formation reduces the interaction between AQDS and PI when the concentration of 2,7-AQDS and 2,7-AQDS2− are comparable at the half-wave potential.34, 35 The dimer formation was also hinted in the pure 2,7-AQDS experiments in Figure 2 e, where nonmonotonous conversion at 1400–1600 cm−1 was observed. Besides these similarities, the band centered at about 3450 cm−1 showed a quite different response to CV scan compared to that at 3050 cm−1 (Figure 2 d), indicating that the two bands could be assigned to different hydrogen-bonded functional groups. However, the accurate assignment remains to be elucidated through more systematic experiments.

DFT calculations also corroborate that the adsorption of 2,7-AQDS2− on polyimide via hydrogen-bonding is indeed energetically more favorable than the direct adsorption without water molecules (Supporting Information, Figure S8). By further taking account of the outer-sphere 2 e− reduction of 2,7-AQDS and the dianion formation under the [2,7-AQDS]>[H+] condition,7, 29 all the correlations between the status of 2,7-AQDS and PI delineates a reaction mechanism as shown in Scheme 1. Given that the charge transfer is influenced by both the reorganization energy and distance between the redox-active center as depicted by the Marcus theory, the hydrogen bonding mediated reaction mechanism implies that the solvation environment may play an important role in determining the SMRT reaction rate and thus affect the power-to-energy ratio.30

As for the catholyte counterpart, in view of the compositional versatility and facile SMRT reaction of hexacyanoferrate-based materials as demonstrated recently,28, 36 nickel hexacyanoferrate (NiHCF) was selected among various cathode materials, considering its identical redox potential to NaI (Figure 1 b), superior stability as well as fast kinetics.37, 38 Ex situ FTIR and XPS measurements were conducted to validate the reaction in the NiHCF-NaI/NaI3 electrolyte system. As shown in the highlighted region at around 2166 cm−1 in Figure 3 a, a characteristic IR band in NiHCF appeared in NaI3 electrolyte while disappeared after reaction with NaI, which is assigned to the evolution of FeIII-CN bonding and implies the two-way SMRT reaction. The fitted XPS spectra in Figure 3 b further proves that the low spin FeII was oxidized to FeIII after the redox-targeting reaction, as indicated by the increased FeIII/FeII ratio in NaI3-oxidized NiHCF sample.

Note that the CV peak current responses for both PI and NiHCF at varying scan rates exhibit a mixed ion diffusion and interfacial charge transfer-controlled kinetics behavior (Supporting Information, Figures S9, S10). It is not surprising for PI to have the surface-dominated behavior considering the fast capacitive ion doping of polymeric materials in the electrolyte.21 Similar phenomena have also been reported for NiHCF in other water-based systems.13 This feature may allow a high SMRT reaction rate since it has been suggested to be limited by the ion diffusion in the solid matrix in the actual working conditions.39, 26

Volume-unbalanced while compositionally symmetric cells were fabricated to evaluate the catholyte performance and NiHCF utilization without crossover interference. As shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S11, the elongated voltage profile was a result of the two-way SMRT reactions of NiHCF-NaI/NaI3. However, the NiHCF utilization was only 35 % (based on the specific capacity of 60 mAh g−1) owing to the low achievable SOC of NaI/NaI3. Considering the equilibrium constant of triiodie formation (Kep≈700 m−1) and the solubility of iodine in water (1.33 mm at room temperature),40 the iodine starts to precipitate when the [I3−]/[I−] ratio reaches about 0.94, an SOC of about 74 %. This is consistent with the voltage profile (Supporting Information, Figure S11), where a kink at around 70 mAh (73 % SOC based on the theoretical capacity of NaI/NaI3) indicates the formation of less conductive iodine on the current collector. This phenomenon was also reported by others in a conventional iodide RFB.15 The conventional iodide-based RFBs are operated under SOC-cutoff mode to prevent precipitation and clogging issues. However, sufficiently high SOC is required here to realize adequate utilization. To enlarge the achievable [I3−]/[I−] ratio and thereby increase the utilization of NiHCF, bromide ions were used as a complexing agent to stabilize the oxidized iodide by forming I2Br− or IBr2− ions, which has recently been demonstrated in Zn-I2 RFB.41 However, the reported mechanism of 2 I−+Br−→I2Br−+2 e− cannot account for the significant capacity increase, considering the equilibrium constant for the formation of I2Br− is only 12.2 m−1, corresponding to only 16 mM I2Br−.42 To rationalize the origin of the stabilization effect, we propose that the formation of IBr2− should also be taken into account, which has a much higher equilibrium constant of 3.7×1012 m−1.43

In the presence of 1 m NaBr, the NaI symmetric cell (Figure 3 c) indeed exhibited a capacity exceeding both the I3− stability limits and I−/I3− theoretical capacity, which leads to a higher achievable SOC. After adding 1 equivalent capacity of NiHCF, the SMRT reaction boosted the capacity to 1.7 times higher, corresponding to 67 % utilization of NiHCF under 10 mA cm−2 (based on 60 mAh g−1) and a power-to-energy ratio of about 0.2 h−1.

To examine the full cell and the anodic side performance, a PI/2,7-AQDS∥NiHCF/NaI full cell was designed to be anolyte limited. The cycling test of 0.1 m 2,7-AQDS in 0.5 m unbuffered Na2SO4 solution revealed a rapid capacity decay and eventually stabilized to 0.72 e− per mole (Supporting Information, Figure S12). The low molecule utilization was attributed to the pronounced dimerization when pH is shifted to >12 after cycling, which might arise from the alkalinity of 2,7-AQDS itself and the hydroxyl products of dissolved oxygen in the first charging cycle.21, 34, 35 Although the accessible concentration of 2,7-AQDS was low, the elongated plateaus after adding PI substantiates a reasonably high PI utilization of 66 % under 5 mA cm−2, as shown in Figure 4 a. Loaded with more granules, the cell was tested for another 500 cycles at which the capacity decayed to 65 % of the initial value (Supporting Information, Figure S13). The long-term cycling along with the unchanged FTIR spectra of cycled PI granules (Supporting Information, Figure S14) show that the redox-targeting reaction and active material are reasonably stable. The capacity drops (Figure 4 b; Supporting Information, Figure S13) were presumably caused by the hydrolysis degradation of PI,44, 45 as well as the potential shift of 2,7-AQDS with pH variation.

In contrast, by adjusting the anolyte pH to around 6–7 with the addition of H2SO4 and 0.1 m buffer (NaAc+HAc=1:9), the accessible capacity of 2,7-AQDS increased to the theoretical 2 e− per mole with stable cycling performance (Supporting Information, Figure S15), indicating the considerably suppressed side reaction at buffered pH-neutral electrolyte. Besides, the solubility of the reduced 2,7-AQDS2− in NaAc+HAc buffered electrolyte was found to be higher than that in the unbuffered electrolyte, which was <0.2 m. The increased solubility might be attributed to the additional hydrogen bonding from acetate ions, as that of the recently reported ammonium-containing electrolyte.23 With the accessible concentration of 2,7-AQDS increased to 0.2 m, the utilization of PI was also increased up to 83 % at 10 mA cm−2 and 61 % at 30 mA cm−2 (in terms of 150 mAh g−1), as shown in Figure 4 c. In the buffered pH-neutral environment, the cycling performance was also substantially improved as shown in Figure 4 d, which presented negligible capacity decay after 60 cycles. The acetic buffer with pKa≈4.8 and the carbonate impurities with pKa1≈6.4 and pKa2≈10.3 are expected to moderate the basicity and provide protons without causing large pH variation in the pH range of 5–10. As a result, the pH of buffered electrolyte after the last cycle remained at about 6. Furthermore, the short discharge plateau at lower voltage (Figure 4 c) was presumably attributed to a slower redox-targeting reaction of 2,7-AQDS with PI at a higher potential, as the second oxidation peak of PI shown in Figure 1 b.

For SMRT reaction-based RFBs, the power-to-energy ratio, which was determined by the SMRT reaction kinetics, is an important indicator of the rate performance.30 With the similar utilization of PI, the power-to-energy ratio was much improved from about 0.7 h−1 in the unbuffered cell to about 2 h−1 in the buffered cell, which implies a much-improved SMRT reaction kinetics. This also confirms the speculation that the solvation environment affects the hydrogen-bonding mediated reaction. It should be clarified that the unsatisfied voltage and energy efficiencies at high current density (Supporting Information, Figure S16) were not due to the hysteresis of the redox targeting reaction, but the cell internal resistance (Supporting Information, Figure S17). The cell stack optimization, such as membrane and flow channels engineering, is however beyond the scope of this work.

Excluding the capacity and volume of electrolyte outside the tank, the cell in Figure 4 d delivered a tank volumetric capacity of around 22 Ah L−1. The tank capacity can be feasibly enhanced by increasing the granule loading.28, 30 Taking the achieved 83 % material utilization, the maximum achievable granule loading ratio of about 50 % and active material ratio (52 vol %) into the calculations, 0.2 m 2,7-AQDS anolyte exhibits an achievable volumetric capacity of about 97 Ah L−1 (calculation method can be found in the Supporting information). Paired with the NiHCF/NaI catholyte, the full cell energy density can reach 39 Wh L−1. Apart from the high volumetric capacity and capacitive charge transfer kinetics, the organic solid material also has the advantage of potential-insensitivity to counter ions, which allows facile integration to different systems without the additional potential-matching process. In conjunction with the ion-insensitive mediators, we envisage that the PI/2,7-AQDS electrolyte system can work as a universal anolyte for the previously studied potassium systems, such as the KBr/PB and TEMPTMA/CuHCF.28, 46

To prove the universality as well as increase the theoretical energy density, a full cell of PI/2,7-AQDS anolyte paired with NH4Br/Br2 catholyte was tested, which provided a higher cell voltage of around 1.3 V and promoted the theoretical tank energy density to 127 Wh L−1. As shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S18, although the full cell test showed a comparable utilization (62 %) at 5 mA cm−2 as the PI/2,7-AQDS∥NiHCF/NaI cell, the pH variation in 2,7-AQDS∥NH4Br cell was found to be serious due to the acidic nature of bromine, which drove the pH of anolyte to around 1 after cycling, even in the presence of 0.1 m NH4H2PO4/(NH4)2HPO4 buffer. Such a pH change altered the redox potential of 2,7-AQDS, as reflected by the constantly decreasing cell voltage (Supporting Information, Figure S18), which eventually reduced the reversibility of SMRT reaction. Therefore, further optimization and stability tests are required to validate the viability of the 2,7-AQDS/PI-Br2 system.

In conclusion, we demonstrated a pair of robust and facile SMRT reactions for the 2,7-AQDS∥NaI based AORFB, which presented considerably enhanced volumetric capacity. For the anodic side, polyimide promoted the capacity of 2,7-AQDS based electrolyte by over 8 times in buffered pH-neutral solution. For the cathodic side, nickel hexacyanoferrate was explored as the capacity enhancer for NaI, with bromide as the additive to enlarge achievable SOC of NaI. The full cell energy density was improved to 39 Wh L−1 via the redox-targeting reaction. With in situ ATR-FTIR measurement, for the first time, we were able to examine the bonding environment during the SMRT reaction. A hydrogen-bonding mediated reaction mechanism was proposed for PI/2,7-AQDS, which may help to explain the enhanced SMRT reaction kinetics. This work also reveals that additives and solvation environment could play an important role for SMRT reactions. Particularly, the buffer additives (NaAc+HAc) were found effective in promoting the SMRT reaction of the PI/2,7-AQDS system. It is expected that the insights gained here can also be applied to tackle capacity issues of other redox-active organic molecules through the redox-targeting reactions.
