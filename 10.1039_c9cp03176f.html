Theoretical exploration of 2,2′-bipyridines as electro-active compounds in flow batteries†

Abstract

Compounds from the 2,2′-bipyridine molecular family were investigated for use as redox-active materials in organic flow batteries. For 156 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives reported in the academic literature, we calculated the redox potential, the pKa for the second deprotonation reaction, and the solubility in aqueous solutions. Using experimental data on a small subset of derivatives, we were able to calibrate our calculations. We find that functionalization with electron-withdrawing groups leads to an increase of the redox potential and to an increase of the molecular acidity (as expressed in a reduction of the pKa value for the second deprotonation step). Furthermore, calculations of solubility in water indicate that some of the studied derivatives have adequate solubility for flow battery applications. Based on an analysis of the phyisco-chemical properties of the 156 studied compounds, we down-select five molecules with carbonyl- and nitro-based functional groups, whose parameters are especially promising for potential applications as negative redox-active materials in organic flow batteries.

1 Introduction

Flow batteries have received significant attention in recent years due to the promise of delivering low-cost energy storage solutions for large-scale applications such as the integration of intermittent renewable generation sources and the deferral of transmission and distribution infrastructure investments.1 The power capacity (unit: kW or MW) is determined by the surface area and the number of cells in the flow battery stack, whereas the energy capacity (unit: kW h or MW h) is defined by the characteristics of the liquid electrolytes (electrolyte volume, concentration of the redox-active compounds, and cell voltage). State-of-the-art flow battery electrolytes are aqueous due to the low cost of water and the compatibility of aqueous electrolytes with stack components. A review on the progress of flow battery stack and system configurations as well as flow battery electrolytes can be found in ref. 2–4.

A quest for low-cost and non-corrosive electrolytes has directed many researchers to investigate organic molecules for use as low-cost flow battery redox couples. The advantages of this approach are multi-fold: molecular redox-active compounds have the potential to be produced from low-cost and earth-abundant resources; furthermore, the flexibility in varying the molecular composition allows for tailoring of the physicochemical properties. A tunable redox potential allows one to maximize the cell voltage, and functionalization of organic molecules with polar side groups can increase the solubility in water. Such molecular modifications can result in more selective operation of the ion-exchange membranes within the flow battery stacks due to enhanced steric or electrostatic exclusion.5,6 Finally, using organic redox-active compounds potentially enables non-corrosive electrolytes.

An important aspect of the above-mentioned exploration of novel molecular flow battery redox couples is the deployment of computational techniques to expedite the down-selection of promising new redox compounds. Synthesis, electrochemical evaluation, and cell testing are all costly activities and as such, theoretical screening can be helpful in targeting only the most promising new compounds. Calculations are performed to evaluate the redox potential, the solubility and the electrochemical and chemical stability as a function of molecular configuration. These parameters have been explored in high-throughput calculations in which “molecular sieve” techniques are used to identify especially promising compounds within large molecular spaces.7,8 Alternatively, computational techniques (e.g. density functional theory, DFT, calculations) are performed on a relatively small number of predefined molecular derivatives (10–100) to calculate the parameters of interest.9–14 To date, computational approaches have been utilized to guide research efforts in identifying benzoquinone,13 anthraquinone,7,9,10 thiophenoquinone,8 alloxazine,11 and 4,4′-bipyridinium7,12 derivatives with enhanced properties for use in flow batteries.

As negative redox-active compounds, several molecular families have been studied, including quinones (e.g. benzoquinones, naphtoquinones, and anthraquinones),7–10,13–16 alloxazines,11 and bipyridiniums.3,5–7,12,17–23 As for the positive electro-active compounds, academic studies have focused on TEMPO-derivatives,3,5,18,19,22 with TEMPO being (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl.

One of the baseline molecules in flow battery studies of organic compounds is methyl viologen (MV, dimethyl 4,4′-bipyridine),5,17,21 a molecule that has been used as a redox indicator and in opto-electronic applications.24 As can be seen in Fig. 1a, the oxidized form of the molecule (MV2+) has a net charge of 2+, and a first reduction step (at −0.45 V vs. the normal hydrogen electrode, NHE) results in the formation of MV+, which can subsequently be reduced to the neutral MV0 species at −0.76 V vs. NHE.12 MV exhibits outstanding solubility (3.0 mol L−1 in water) and excellent electrochemical reversibility for the first electron-reducing step. The electrochemical reversibility for the second electron-reducing step, however, is poor due to the limited solubility of the neutral MV0 species.12 Lastly, MV molecules are prone to chemical degradation due to a second-order decomposition mechanism.20

(b) Structure of 2,2′-bipyridine (Bpy), including labels for the positions on the two aromatic rings. (c) General structure of an n,n′-disubstituted-2,2′-bipyridine for R and R′ functional groups. To date, all research on bipyridinium-based redox compounds for flow batteries has focused on MV and MV-derivatives in which the functional groups are attached to the nitrogen atoms at the 4,4′-position. No attention has been directed to 2,2′-bipyridine (Bpy) compounds for flow battery applications, in spite of the excellent electrochemical reversibility reported for some compounds25,26 and an exhaustive body of literature on synthetic approaches that yield functionalized 2,2′-bipyridines.27 Interestingly, the addition of functional groups to 2,2′-bipyridine typically takes place on any position of the aromatic rings in contrast to 4,4′-bipirydine that is typically functionalized on the N-position, opening up a much larger molecular space. In contrast with 4,4′-bipyridine derivatives such as methyl viologen, 2,2′-bipyridines, shown in Fig. 1b, have the potential to exhibit two reduction and two protonation processes. Fig. 2 depicts all possible reactions for the various electron transfers (pH-independent equilibria 1–6), proton transfers (reactions A–F) and proton-coupled electron transfers described by processes 1A, 2C, 3B, and 4D (concerted or discrete electron transfer and proton transfers). In the present report, we have analyzed computationally all possible electron, proton and proton coupled electron transfers.


Fig. 2 All possible reactions for the various pH-independent electron transfer (reactions 1–6), and pH-dependent proton (reactions A–F) and proton-coupled electron transfer (equilibria 1A, 2C, 3B, and 4D) processes that occur in 2,2′-bipyridines.

The exploration presented in this article aims at predicting several physicochemical properties for 156 known 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives reported in the academic literature.27 The properties calculated are the redox potential, the acid dissociation constant in terms of pKa and the solubility (logS). Selected experimental measurements were carried out to support all the calculations performed. The study of E as a function of pH in aqueous solution was also used to—for the first time to our knowledge—infer the pKa of the 2,2′-bipyridine reduced species (HBpy) in Fig. 2. Our predicted E0 and pKa values for the set of 156 bipyridines were used to visualize the effect of the Hammett substituent constant on both these parameters. These results—in combination with solubility calculations—served as a guide to down-select several concrete 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives to be further explored in experimental flow battery studies.

2 Experimental and computational methods

Cyclic voltammetry (CV) electrochemical studies were performed using a CH Instruments potentiostat (model CHI760E). The three-electrode setup comprised a glassy carbon working electrode (active area: 3 mm diameter), a Ag/AgCl/KCl 0.1 mol L−1 pseudo-reference electrode and a platinum wire as counter electrode. The acquisition of all voltammograms was initiated at open circuit potential (OCP) and collected in positive and negative directions. The ferricinium/ferrocene redox couple was used as a reference and the potential values were corrected in all voltammograms. For CV measurements of 2,2′-bipyridine and its derivatives, the electrodes were immersed into a solution containing 1 mmol L−1 concentrations of these compounds in a H2SO4 solution (except for compound 4 in Table S8, where a saturated solution at lower concentration was used, ESI†). Experiments to study the dependence of the redox properties as a function of pH were carried out using a 1 mmol L−1 solution of 2,2′-bipyridine in different media (H2SO4 for pH 1.04 and 1.62, a chloroacetic acid/chloroacetate buffer for pH 2.58, 2.79, 3.00, 3.25, 3.40 and 3.56 and acetic acid/acetate buffer for pH 3.68). All experiments were performed at room temperature, under a nitrogen gas atmosphere, and using a scan rate of 100 mV s−1.

The molecular structures of the 156 derivatives investigated are summarized in Tables S1–S6 of the ESI.† All these compounds are n,n′-disubstituted-2,2′-bipyridines, where n = n′ = 3, 4, 5 and 6 (Fig. 1c). First, molecular information in the SMILES code was used as the input in Open Babel 2.4.028 to find the minimum energy conformer using the MMFF9429–33 force field. The generated geometries provided the cartesian coordinates that were used to generate the Gaussian input file. In some cases, it was necessary to apply a preliminary minimization using pseudopotentials (PM734) before performing the actual DFT calculation. Molecular energies of the 156 2,2′-bipirydines were obtained using the Gaussian 16 suite35 at DFT, with the B3LYP36 hybrid functional and 6-31G(d) basis. Analysis of vibrational frequencies was performed to verify the existence of an energy minimum. All geometries were optimized using both the polarizable continuum model (PCM)37,38 and the solvation model based on density (SMD)39 with water as solvent; the results presented below correspond to SMD as it is better correlated with experimental values (see Section S2 in the ESI†).

Next, redox potentials for the first and second electron transfers and the acid dissociation constants expressed in logarithmic form (pKa) were calculated for the set of 156 n,n′-disubstituted-2,2′-bipyridines. In order to obtain the best values, a benchmark study was performed to determine the best approximations. Redox potential was calculated via the Born–Haber cycle shown in Fig. 3, which has been widely used to estimate redox potentials with accurate results.7,8,40

Finally, we need to consider aqueous solubility for the species present at the selected pH operating conditions. Solubility, calculated as the logarithm of intrinsic solubility (logS), was obtained using the ChemAxon suite,42 which implements the structure–property relations described by Xu et al.43,44 Calculations were performed at pH values one unit below the first pKa for each molecule, in order to guarantee that the redox reaction studied corresponds to the mono-protonated species (see reaction 3 in Fig. 2). A calibration of this methodology was performed using Xu's44 and Yalkowsky's45 training sets in order to verify to what extent this approach is able to predict solubility (for details, see Section S3 in the ESI†).

3 Results and discussion

The starting point of our exploration of the 2,2′-bipyridine molecular family is the unsubstituted 2,2′-bipyridine molecule itself. Our initial focus is on the first reduction step of HBpy+ into HBpy, although the H2Bpy2+ and Bpy species are in principle promising species for flow battery applications as well. To be able to calibrate our theoretical calculations of E0 with experimental data, it is important to accurately measure the redox potential for a well-defined equilibrium reaction. As shown in Fig. 2, the reduction process of HBpy+ can occur via reaction 3 (pH-independent electron transfer) and/or a proton-coupled electron transfer. For a process described by equilibrium 3B to take place, the operational pH needs to be in the range pKoa1 < pH < pKra1, where pKoa1 and pKra1 are the negative logarithms of the first acidity constants for the oxidized and first reduced species, respectively. Under these pH conditions, HBpy+ would be protonated into H2Bpy+ species during the reduction step. As such, it is important to understand how the pH affects the different reactions in Fig. 2 when experimentally determining the redox potential. For this reason, the redox potential of 2,2′-bipyridine (pKoa2 = 4.3 and pKoa1 = −0.246) as a function of pH was experimentally determined from pH = 1 to 4, where we expected to observe the change from the pH-dependent electron transfer equilibrium (process 3B) to the pH-independent equilibrium (reaction 3). Fig. 5 shows a Pourbaix diagram for the first reduction step of the HBpy+ system in which two separate regimes are observed: a pH-dependent region for values lower than 2.98 (pKra1) with a slope of −0.06 in agreement with this process and a pH-independent region for values between 2.98 and 4.3. In the first region, the predominant process is 3B, whereas reaction 3 dominates in the pH-independent regime. These results allow us to conclude that HBpy+ is a weaker base than its reduced species HBpy; since the pKa1 increases from −0.2 to 2.98 upon reduction, this behavior can be explained by the presence of a whole extra negative charge that will have a stronger interaction with a cation.47 The trends observed in Fig. 5 allow us to assume a pH interval in which the first reduction step of the HBPy+ species is dominated by reaction 3 (Fig. 2). Specifically, we assumed that for all derivatives investigated, the experimental redox potential is exclusively associated with reaction 3 (Fig. 2) at a pH value one unit lower than the pKa2 value of each derivative.
Fig. 5 Experimental diagram of Evs. pH for unsubstituted Bpy in different electrolytic solutions for the first reduction process. At pH < 3, the redox reaction was observed to be pH-dependent, whereas E was observed to be independent of pH at pH values >3.

3.1 pKa calculations

In the thermodynamic cycle shown in Fig. 4, the introduction of a reference species allows us to get a pKa value without the need to calculate the proton energy directly. In order to get accurate pKa values, this reference must be structurally similar to the systems under investigation.48,49 For this reason, we chose 2,2′-bipyridine as the reference system. Using this approach, the pKa values are related to ΔG0s by eqn (5), and pKHBpya corresponds to the experimental value of the reference system (a value of 4.30 for the second deprotonation step of unsubstituted 2,2′-bipyridine).

Fig. 6 shows the predicted pKa values (associated with reaction A in Fig. 2, pKa2) for a variety of 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives in the oxidized form. In the case of substitutions with electron-donating functional groups (Hammett substituent factor < 0), a slight decrease of the acidity of the nitrogen atoms on the ring resulted on average in somewhat higher values for pKa2 (maximum change of 2 pH units), although this trend does not seem very pronounced. When adding both electron-attracting and electron-withdrawing substituents, the values of pKa2 trend below 4.3 (the pKa2 value for 2,2′-bipyridine). Finally, substitutions with only electron-withdrawing functional groups (Hammett substituent factor > 0) resulted in a marked change towards lower pKa2 values (maximum change of 8 pH units). This last observation can be rationalized as follows: when side groups are strongly electron-withdrawing, the electron density on the nitrogen atoms on the rings is reduced, making it easier for protons to dissociate, which is expressed in a lower value for pKa2. Conversely, we had expected that the addition of electron-donating side groups would inhibit the dissociation of protons, resulting in higher values for pKa2. This expected trend was only clearly observed for two derivatives with strongly electron-donating side groups on the 6,6-position (two purple dots in the upper right-hand side of Fig. 6) but in general, functionalization with more moderately electron-donating groups (Hammett constants between −0.2 and 0) only leads to a small change of the pKa2 value.


Fig. 6 Predicted values of pKa1 for several n,n′-disubstituted-2,2′-bipyridine derivatives, presented in four blocks according to the position of the side-group functionalization on the aromatic ring. The coloration of the data points represents the Hammett substituent constant, which indicates how strongly the side groups are electron-attracting or electron-withdrawing.

3.2 Calculated standard redox potentials of 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives

The calculation of standard redox potentials for the organic redox-active molecules has been widely described in the academic literature.7,8 In the Born–Haber cycle, ΔG0,redoxs is directly related to the redox potential (eqn (1)) and can be obtained from energy differences of the oxidized (Ox) and reduced (Red) species involved in the cycle. For each derivative, we consider both electronic (Eelec) and Gibbs free energies (G), with the latter concept comprising the vibrational and thermal corrections (as can be seen in eqn (2)), which should in principle result in a better approximation. In addition, to include solvent effects, two different implicit solvation models were used: PCM and SMD, along with eqn (3).

Theoretical calculations allow for the estimation of standard redox potentials referenced to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE), but the absolute values of such results are often unreliable due to offsets, the magnitude of which depends on the exact computational methodology that is employed. Usually, these offsets can be corrected by using a calibration curve, which is an empirical relation between experimental and theoretical values. We obtained such a calibration curve by calculating the redox potential for five specific 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives (for the pH-independent reaction 3) and by measuring the experimental redox potential for the same derivatives at a pH value one unit lower than its pKa2 value (see Fig. S1, ESI†). In Table S8 (ESI†), we report the estimation of pKa1 for the reduced species. It is observed that the value of pKa1 of the reduced species is at least one pH unit lower than the pKa2 value of its corresponding species in the oxidized form, as in 2,2-bipyridine. Under these conditions, the selected pH values in the experimental studies resulted in the exclusive occurrence of the pH-independent reaction 3 (Fig. 2). Correcting the calculated values for the standard redox potential with the calibration curve of Fig. S2 (ESI†) allowed us to estimate the reduction potential for all reactions of Fig. 2 for the 156 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives of interest. For each reaction in Fig. 2, Fig. 7 shows the statistical variations of the predicted standard reduction potentials, presented in four blocks according to the position on the aromatic ring of the side-group functionalization for the SMD solvation model. Redox potential values obtained for first reduction step of the protonated species H2Bpy2+ to H2Bpy+ and HBpy+ to HBpy (described by reactions 5 and 3, respectively) exhibit less negative redox potential values compared to the reduction of the deprotonated species Bpy to Bpy− (reaction 1), which can be explained by the stability added to the reduced species in the presence of a proton. Relative to the first reduction step, the calculated redox potential for the second reduction step occurs at more negative potential values for all protonation states. Comparing the values of E0 obtained for reactions 1, 3 and 5, it can be concluded that on average, the redox potentials associated with reaction 5, and especially reaction 3, are optimal for use in aqueous flow batteries. For reaction 1, the predicted redox potentials are very negative (≪−1.0 V vs. SHE), which will result in significant hydrogen evolution (i.e. water reduction) on carbon based electrodes that are typically used in aqueous flow batteries.50 In contrast, the redox potentials associated with reaction 5 are rather positive (>−0.5 V vs. SHE), which will avoid the hydrogen evolution reaction but may result in a sub-optimal cell voltage and electrolyte energy density. In addition, reaction 5 is associated with extremely acidic electrolyte conditions, which may be undesirable from an engineering perspective. The values of E0 for reaction 3, on the other hand, lie on average between −0.5 and −1.0 V vs. SHE, with values around −0.5 V being optimal in the context of optimizing electrolyte energy density while avoiding excessive parasitic reactions such as hydrogen evolution. Also, reaction 3 is accessible under moderately acidic electrolyte conditions (see Fig. 6), which is desirable from an engineering point of view. All the calculated redox potentials as well as the pKa values are summarized in Tables S1–S6 of the ESI.† It must be noted that the above discussion does not exclude the use of reactions 1 or 5 for flow battery applications. The above-mentioned observations are high-level trends, but deviations to these trends can be observed in Fig. 7, and as such, the above-mentioned trends should be used as a guideline but not as definitive information to exclude the use of reactions 1 and 5 for 2,2′-bipyridines for flow battery applications. During the analysis process to produce Fig. 7, molecules considered outliers were removed from the data set. In-depth analysis of these outliers led us to conclude that the extreme values observed for E0 of these outliers were due to optimizations in the calculations that lead to the breakage of a chemical bond in the reduced species. Such phenomena would be indicative of possible decomposition reactions associated with the electron transfer. Some examples of these decomposition reactions are described in more detail in Fig. S3 (ESI†).

E0 corresponds to standard redox potentials, calculations based only on electronic energies, while G to calculations that consider Gibbs free energies.

3.3 Functional group effect in 2,2′-bipyridines

According to Schrier, the addition of different functional groups on thiophenoquinone aromatic rings results in shifts of redox potential value of up to 0.8 V.8Fig. 8 displays the estimated reduction potential of 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives for reaction 3 (Fig. 2), where each value is identified by the Hammett substituent constant (σ) associated with each functional group and to discriminate between electron donating or withdrawing groups (σ ≤ 0 and σ > 0, respectively). It can be observed that electron withdrawing groups increase the reduction potential (E0) with respect to the unsubstituted 2,2′-bipyridine. This response can be attributed to the stabilizing effect conferred by these groups to the aromatic ring. Contrarily, electron donating groups hinder entry of electrons due to conferring charge density on the aromatic ring; therefore, E0 is shifted to more negative values. The latter are significant observations, since electron withdrawing groups could increase reversibility of 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives by stabilizing the electron in the reduction process. Fig. 8 also shows that in molecules with different substituents on each ring (σ1 ≠ σ2), the electron withdrawing effect is predominant, that is, the reduction potential values are mostly shifted in the positive direction above −0.826 V.
Fig. 8 Predicted E0 values for various n,n′-disubstituted-2,2′-bipyridine, presented in four blocks according to the position on the aromatic ring of the side-group functionalization. The coloration of the data points represents the Hammett substituent constant, which indicates how strongly the side groups are electron-attracting or electron-withdrawing.

Finally, it is noteworthy that the electron withdrawing effect is more significant than the electron donating one: for electron-donating groups with σ = −0.6, the maximum decrease of the downward shift of the reduction potential is only 0.25 V with respect to unsubstituted 2,2′-bipyridine, whereas electron-withdrawing groups with σ = 0.7 can lead to an upward shift of up to 0.78 V.

3.4 Solubility

Theoretical solubility (S, intrinsic solubility in moles per liter) of molecular species can be obtained as51
 
ΔGdissolution = ΔGsub + ΔGsolv = −RTlnSVm(8)
where (ΔGsub) is the sublimation free energy, (ΔGsolv) is the solvation free energy and Vm is the molar volume of the crystal. For novel compounds, however, limited data exist on ΔGsub and Vm, which can complicate the prediction of compound solubility. However, the solvation free energy of a molecule can be used to approximate trends in solubility when screening large databases of molecules.7,8 On the other hand, as mentioned above, methods that implement structure–property relations have been used to estimate the solubility of novel compounds.8 Solubility values (logS) were obtained with the ChemAxon suite and compared with ΔGsolv—calculated via DFT—in order to correlate tendencies between both thermodynamic properties. Results for reacting species, HBpy+ and HBpy, presented in Fig. 9, show that oxidized species have larger absolute values of ΔGsolv than neutral species, while maintaining a similar scattering for all molecules (see Section S3 and Fig. S8 in the ESI,† for more information).
Fig. 9 Theoretical molar solubility (S) of molecular species, represented as logS versus Gibbs free energies for the mono protonated species of the first redox couple involved in reaction 3 (HBpy+/HBpy).

In Fig. 9, three different regions can be resolved along the y-axis for both species. The first region (logS < −5.0) corresponds to molecules with small absolute values of ΔGsolv, indicating a correspondence between poor solubility and weak ΔGsolv values, confirming the undesirable low solubility of these molecules. A second region can be defined for the interval log S = [−5.0, −1.0] where most candidates are concentrated. Molecules belonging to this region should be studied in more detail in order to establish more accurate values of solubility (perhaps experimentally). The last region (logS > −1.0) contains the derivatives with the highest solubilities; better candidates should be those that also have larger absolute values of ΔGsolv (dots in the top-left corner of each data set). These results allow us to apply the present methodology for estimating the solubility as a rapid preliminary screening of candidate molecules.

3.5 Selection of molecules as possible negolytes in flow batteries.

Selecting suitable redox couples for use in flow batteries usually results in a trade-off between various physico-chemical properties. On the one hand, an ideal molecule for aqueous flow batteries must exhibit a redox potential within the electrochemical window of stability of the solvent (i.e. water) on graphitic electrodes that are typically used in flow batteries. In other words, parasitic solvent dissociation reactions such as water reduction or oxidation must be avoided to prevent challenges from a battery operation point of view. On the other hand, the difference between the redox potential for the negative and positive electro-active compounds (the cell voltage) should be sufficiently high to ensure high electrolyte energy density. Furthermore, the molecule must exhibit high solubility in water, again to ensure high energy density.

We attempted to visualize this trade-off between redox potential and energy density in Fig. 10, in which each dot represents one 2,2′-bipyridine derivative (its color represents the Hammett substituent factor). The blue horizontal line intersects the y-axis at a value of −0.5 V vs. SHE, below which we assume the parasitic reaction of water reduction (i.e. hydrogen evolution) to be problematic. Similarly, the red vertical line intersects the x-axis at a value of logS = −1, below which the compound solubility is deemed to be unattractive from an electrolyte density point of view (logS = −1 corresponds to a solubility of 0.1 mol L−1). As such, the dots located in the upper right quadrant of Fig. 10 represent the most interesting compounds from an operational stability (no hydrogen evolution) and electrolyte energy density point of view.


Fig. 10 Standard reduction potential values of 2,2′-bipyridines as a function of logarithmic solubility values (logS) calculated with ChemAxon software and their corresponding Hammett substituent factor.

As can be observed in Fig. 10, molecular derivatives with electron-withdrawing groups exhibit on average more positive redox potentials, reducing the impact of parasitic hydrogen evolution. In contrast, no clear trends can be observed with respect to the effect of the addition of electron-withdrawing or electron-donating side groups on the solubility (logS). As such, the nine candidate compounds in the upper right quadrant are all functionalized with electron-withdrawing groups. For compounds B4455 (E0 = −0.31 V, and pKa2 = −1.23) and B5522 (E0 = −0.29 V, and pKa2 = −1.89), the low values of pKa2 would require operation in extremely acidic electrolyte (>17 mol L−1) for reaction 3 to be the predominant redox reaction. A similar behavior is observed for compound B6635 (E0 = −0.45 V, and pKa2 = −3.94) and B4454 (E0 = −0.30 V, and pKa2 = 0.18), as a result of which these compounds would also require operation in highly concentrated acidic media. On the other hand, the compounds functionalized with carbonyl groups (B4446, B4452, B4461, and B5519) and nitro substituents (B5543) have acidity constants greater than 1, which would ease the requirement of the electrolyte acidity (<1 mol L−1). Reduction potential values for these compounds fluctuate within the range of −0.34 to −0.45 V, which makes them promising candidates with respect to the methylviologen benchmark, which has a reduction potential of −0.45 V. Using any of these compounds as the negative electrolyte, a flow battery with for instance TEMPO-based molecules or the Br2/Br− redox couple on the positive side should have an open circuit potential greater than 1.0 V, which is on a par with or better than state-of-the-art organic flow batteries.52 In addition, this last set of molecules should be the one with the best reversibility due to stability conferred by the electron withdrawing group. As a consequence, the carbonyl- and nitro-functionalized 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives presented in Fig. 11 constitute the most promising candidates for further evaluation in flow battery testing.

4 Conclusions

Redox properties, acidity and solubility of 156 substituted 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives were studied for their possible application as active materials in organic flow battery electrolytes. The study of the acidity constants showed that functionalization with electron-withdrawing groups increases the acidity with respect to unsubstituted 2,2′-bipyridine, as evidenced by a decrease in predicted values for pKa constants of such compounds. In contrast, functionalization with electron-donating groups increases the basicity of the substituted molecules, increasing the values of the second acidity constant. At the same time, we found that the reduction potential of the monoprotonated 2,2′-bipyridine derivatives shifts to more positive values (relative to unsubstituted 2,2′-bipyridine) when the molecular backbone is functionalized with electron-withdrawing groups. Compounds functionalized with electron-donating groups, however, exhibit a slight negative shift decrease in the reduction potential, the magnitude of which was less significant relative to the positive shift observed for functionalization with electron-withdrawing groups. These results can be rationalized by analyzing the molecular structure of the investigated molecules, in particular with respect to the value of the Hammett substituent factor of the functional groups of each 2,2′-bipyridine derivative. The analysis of the estimated redox potential and solubility values for the 156 investigated compounds indicates that compounds with carbonyl- and nitro-based functional groups (B4446, B4452, B4461, B5519, and B5543) are the most promising candidates within the set of investigated molecules for successful application as negative electrolytes in organic flow batteries.
