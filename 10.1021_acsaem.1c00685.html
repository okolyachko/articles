    Abstract

    Analyte concentration effects on the first reduction process of methyl viologens and diquat redox flow battery electrolytes were examined by cyclic voltammetry in aqueous media. A simple one-electron transfer mechanism to form radical cations was detected for diquat, 4,4′-dimethyl diquat, and bis(3-trimethylammonio)-propyl viologen compounds. The radical cations attach to the electrode surface when the source of their electrogeneration is methyl viologen molecules bearing PF6– ions as a counterpart. However, this inner sphere reduction mechanism was not observed in methyl viologen having an I– counterion. For the latter compound, as well as for 5,5′-dimethyl diquat and 1,1′-bis(3-sulfonatopropyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium, a piece of experimental evidence for unexpected, fast, and reversible dimerization interactions between their electrogenerated radical cations is presented. To get information on these bimolecular interactions, a screening methodology (using different levels of theory) was employed in finding suitable dimeric structures and their related interaction energies. By using diquat as a reference system, a relationship between calculated interaction energies and the corresponding experimental dimerization constants was obtained. The examination of redox-active molecules using this experimental and theoretical approach will allow a better selection of redox flow battery electrolytes.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Electron transfer reactions of organic redox-active materials are key processes for controlling the energy conversion and storage in natural and artificial systems. (1−3) One recent advancement to storage wind and solar electricity is aqueous organic redox flow batteries (RFBs), where reversible oxidation and reduction reactions at electrodes are fundamental processes to charge and discharge the redox-active electrolytes of the device. (2−4) These electrolytes can be composed of a wide range of organic redox-active molecules, so the development of organic RFBs is continuously assisted by a selection process to find the best electroactive species before being tested on a pilot scale. For simplicity, the screening process is typically carried out combining experimental and computational studies of the redox potential of different molecules in solution, (5−7) but the mechanism controlling this parameter in the system should be correctly evaluated and calculated so as not to fail in the predictions. Within this context, methyl viologens (MV2+) and diquat (DQ2+) derivatives are widely used in formulations as quick acting desiccant herbicides. (8−10) They exert their poisonous effect by uptaking electrons from photosystem I, where NADP+ reduction is inhibited and the plant dies. (8) Due to such strong electron-withdrawing abilities, MV2+ and DQ2+ derivatives (represented in a general way as A2+) have been extensively used in chemistry to develop functional materials of specific applications, including sensors, electrochromic display devices, diodes, molecular machines, and electrolyte solutions to storage negative charge in efficient, cheap, and durable aqueous organic RFBs. (11−18) Such A2+ structures can undergo two single-electron transfer reactions to form first radical cations (A+•) and then neutral species (A0). Under specific conditions, A+• electrogenerated species are stable in solution and formed by a completely reversible process; therefore, this first reduction process is assumed to control the energy conversion in a variety of methyl viologen and diquat RFB electrolytes.
    For instance, Janoschka et al., (17) tested a RFB composed of a TEMPO derivative and N,N′-dimethyl-4,4′-bipyridinium dichloride at a concentration of 2 mol L–1. The cell was efficiently and safely charged–discharged with no degradation observed in the electrolyte, suggesting that MV+• electrogenerated species are stable in solution. Similar effects were detected by introducing pendant propyl ammonium groups (NPr) (3,19,20) or sulfonate groups (SPr), on the MV2+ base structure. (15,21−23) The resulting (NPr)22+–Vi2+ and (SPr)22––Vi2+ compounds exhibited good cycling stability (up to 99.99%) upon acting as negolyte electrolytes in RFBs. Likewise, Liu et al., (5) considered a simple one-electron reduction process to experimentally and theoretically analyze the kinetics and thermodynamics of different MV2+ structures. Despite these remarkable advances, the reduction mechanism of MV2+ and DQ2+ derivatives continues to be unclear.
    Other studies of MV2+- and DQ2+-based batteries detected efficiencies and storage capacities smaller than expected. (3,14) As this evidence questions the supposed high stability of electrogenerated A+• species in solution, the authors invoked second-order degradation mechanisms to explain the batteries fade, but they remained aware that these proposals were not conclusive. For example, the mechanism of bimolecular degradation in the MV2+ compound was based on slight changes observed (as a function of the time) in the UV–vis spectra of very dilute (C* close to 0.0001 mol L–1) solutions of chemically generated A+• species in solution. (18,20) These authors cite in turn previous electrochemical and UV–vis studies performed of methyl viologens, where dimerization and also dismutation reactions are described, but the latter reactions were not considered to be irreversible at least at low analyte concentrations. (24−26) On the other hand, Geraskina et al., (27) concluded that methyl viologen cation radicals interact via π-bonded dimers, but they also claim that the binding constant of this interaction is independent of the nature of each analyzed substituent group. Likewise, we previously demonstrated the high stability of electrogenerated radical cations from diquat compound in acetate buffer (4) and evidenced an adsorption process of N,N′-dimethyl-4,4′-bipyridinium bis(hexafluorophosphate) radical cations (MV+•) at the glassy carbon electrode. Finally, Liu et al., (28) avoided this adsorption ability by using N,N′-dimethyl-4,4′-bipyridinium diiodide as both negolyte and posolyte electrolytes in a symmetric cell, which suggests that counter ions significantly affect the reduction mechanism of methyl viologen.
    From the above discussion, more mechanistic information is required to improve the development and computational screening of methyl viologen and diquat RFB electrolytes. As RFBs work on electrochemical principles, well-defined and executed electrochemical experiments of their electroactive materials undergoing charge transfer reactions should provide crucial information in designing more efficient systems. To differentiate between adsorptive, (4) first-, and second-order mechanisms; (29,30) these experiments should be performed as a function of different analyte concentrations. In the present work, analytic concentration experiments were performed for a series of methyl viologens and diquat structures (Scheme 1) in aqueous media, as model-active components of RFB electrolytes. The results obtained exhibit strong effects of chemical structure nature on the first electron transfer mechanism of compounds studied. For some systems, the high stability of electrogenerated A+• species in solution is confirmed. The effect of PF6– and I– counterions on the adsorptive abilities of MV+• species at the glassy carbon electrode is discussed. Finally, an experimental evidence for unexpected dimerization interactions between the reduced form of MV2+, (SPr)22––Vi2+, and 5,5′-DMDQ compounds is presented. The binding constants obtained for these interactions are discussed on the basis of electronic structure calculations, observing a correlation with the calculated interaction energies of dimeric structures selected via a screening process.

    Experimental Evidence for Fast and Reversible Dimerization Interactions

    Before testing an organic RFB, its redox active electrolyte solutions are typically characterized at low analyte concentrations close to 0.001 mol L–1. (29,30) Under these conditions, the electrochemistry of compounds studied (Scheme 1), (4) also exemplified by the responses obtained from methyl viologen compound derivatives (Figure 1), exhibits a first reversible one-electron transfer process in aqueous media to form radical cations A+•. Based on this experimental evidence, methyl viologen and diquat RFB electrolytes are assumed to operate in line with the mechanism described by eq 1, upon charging and discharging the device. (3,15−23)(1)
    The compounds studied do not exhibit important differences in potential values to modulate the voltage in a battery. However, it is known that chemical structure nature significantly impacts the stability of A+• electrogenerated species in the solution of RFBs. (3,15,19−23) The problem arises when increasing the initial analyte concentration C* of a given A2+ compound to test the battery. The responses obtained from diquat solutions were taken as reference signals expected for simple one-electron transfer mechanisms since previous works have shown that electrogenerated diquat radical cations are stable in solution even at high concentrations of C* (0.03 and 0.10 mol L–1). (4,13) For this compound, reversible and diffusion-controlled one-electron voltammetric waves were detected in a wide range of analyte concentrations prepared (Figure 2A). The signals exhibited marginal changes in potential values and normalized peak current intensities versus C*, as described by the Randles–Cevcik equation in simple one electron transfer reactions; (31) this result is disclosing the expected high stability of electrogenerated DQ+• species in solution. A similar behavior was detected also for 4,4′-DMDQ (Figure 2B) and (NPr)22+–Vi2+ (Figure 2C) compounds, suggesting that electrogenerated 4,4′-DMDQ+• and (NPr)22+–Vi+• species are also stable in solution. The latter result is in line with previous discussions about RFB electrolytes, (3,15,16,19,20) where compounds worked efficiently and avoided dimerization. However, the electrochemistry of MV2+ species was strongly dependent on PF6– or I– counterions.
    The MV bis(hexafluorophosphate) compound dissolved in 1 mol L–1 NaCl exhibited a similar behavior to the one detected in previous experiments carried out in acetate buffer as solvent electrolyte. (4) The systematic increase in MV2+ concentration caused strong effects in potential values and in the wave shape (Figure 2D); the voltammograms moved toward less negative potential values and for the higher quantities used in the experiment (tending to 0.01 mol L–1), the oxidation signal broadened (exhibiting a kinetic effect), and some overcrossings on the cathodic branches were noticed. The latter phenomenon is typically related to the existence of a nucleation process at the electrode surface, (4,32) and the general observations are consistent with an inner sphere electron transfer process, that form MV+• species, which attach to the glassy carbon electrode. (4,32,33) As the electrochemistry of these compounds was not affected by the solvent electrolyte, acetate buffer was selected to continue with the analysis. On the other hand, the voltammetric wave of MV diiodide remained as reversible and diffusion-controlled even at 0.01 mol L–1 of the analyte (Figure 3A), which suggest that PF6– ions of the compound studied in NaCl are significantly influencing the stability of electrogenereted species. In order to rationalize this phenomenon, the effect of PF6– ions was also examined by adding equivalents of NH4PF6 salt to 0.01 mol L–1 solutions of MV diiodide, diquat, 5,5′-dimethyl diquat, and 1,1′-bis(3-sulfonatopropyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium compounds. The voltammograms obtained are presented in Figure 3.
    In the absence of NH4PF6, the compounds exhibited typical diffusion-controlled one-electron voltammetric waves. Additions of salt to a solution of the 1,1′-bis(3-sulfonatopropyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium compound caused marginal changes in the system. However, the signals obtained from the remaining compounds studied in the presence of 1 and 3 equiv of NH4PF6 salt exhibited a similar behavior to the one detected for the MV bis(hexafluorophosphate) compound dissolved in NaCl (Figure 2D), which is also consistent with the electrogeneration process of radical cations that attach to the electrode surface. Methyl viologens have also been extensively used in supramolecular chemistry as selective anion recognition elements. (34,35) Ji et al., (34) studied the anion recognition of a methyl viologen derivative for ions 2Br–, 2I– and 2PF6–, reporting the following binding constants for interactions in a pH = 7 phosphate buffer solution: 2.37 × 105, 3.37 × 104 and 1.92 × 105 mol L–1, respectively. Based on this data and our cyclic voltammetry results, we believe that adding enough NH4PF6 salt to the solutions of the compounds studied generates an ion-exchange process. As a result, MV bis(hexafluorophosphate), diquat bis(hexafluorophosphate), and 5,5′-dimethyl diquat bis(hexafluorophosphate) species are formed (before starting the electrochemical experiment). An exception to this process is the 1,1′-bis(3-sulfonatopropyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium compound, whose charge stabilization does not require a counterion. Besides, the presence of PF6– ions not only generates adsorption of species at the electrode surface but also a significant loss in reversibility. This fact is in line with previous studies that demonstrate the influence of PF6– ions on the electron transfer kinetics of methyl viologens. (34,35) The results exhibit a critical role of Br– and I– ions in DQ2+ and MV2+ structures, respectively, as they prevent adsorptive interactions of DQ+• and MV+• electrogenerated species at the glassy carbon electrode.
    The MV diiodide compound exhibited also strong C*-dependent effects in cyclic voltammetry (Figure 4A), but the electrochemical behavior was different. Upon increasing concentrations of C*, the reduction and oxidation signals shifted toward less negative potential values, while the voltammetric responses remained as reversible without exhibiting significant changes in both their wave shapes and normalized peak current intensities versus C*. This compound was also examined as a function of the scan rate (Figure 4B), noticing that at least in the range of 0.1–10 V s–1, the peak voltammetric currents varies linearly with v1/2, which is in agreement with the theoretical behavior described for a diffusion-controlled mechanism. (4,31)
    A similar behavior was detected in experiments performed with (SPr)22––Vi2+ and 5,5′-DMDQ compounds (Figure 5), the former case was also examined in unbuffered acidic conditions (1 mol L–1 H2SO4), where the linear dependence of their peak voltammetric currents versus v1/2 was reported previously. (4) These observations are similar to the ones detected by Compton et al., (36) upon studying the reduction mechanism of N,N′-dimethyl-4,4′-bipyridinium dichloride in aqueous media with a platinum working electrode. They related this behavior to the occurrence of a fast and reversible dimerization process between electrogenerated MV+• species to form (MV+•)2 adducts in solution, which is represented in a general case by eq 3. (36,37) Therefore, and inspired by the latter reported work, we conducted a strategy based on the use of voltammetric simulations to analyze this mechanistic possibility (involved by eqs 1 and 3) for compounds MVI2, (SPr)22––Vi2+, and 5,5′-DMDQ, as suggested by Evans et al.; (38) however, some considerations should be taken into account.(3)
    The peak potential values of voltammograms for reversible electrode reactions followed by a fast and reversible dimerization are independent on the scan rate but depends on the dimensionless binding parameter KeqC*. For reduction reactions, Epc – E1/2° varies linearly with log(KeqC*) at a rate of 30/n mV per unit log C* at 25 °C, in the anodic direction as C* is increased. (39)n is the number of electrons and E1/2° is the half-wave potential for the A+• monomer formation. In this work, E1/2° values to form MV+•, (SPr)22––Vi+•, and 5,5′-DMDQ+• species were approximated to −0.689, – 0.760, and −0.772 V (vs Ag/AgCl), respectively, by plotting Epc versus C* (Figure 6), extrapolating to “infinite dilution” of C* and adding 28 mV to each extrapolated Epc value, as suggested by Naz and Mohammad. (37)
    The binding reactions should occur as a fast and reversible process, for which the relative rates of both dimer formation and dissociation reactions (kf2 and kb2) should be very large being the system in equilibrium during the binding process. (38,39) To achieve this condition, both rate constants were fixed at values near the diffusion limit rate constant for bimolecular reactions (108 s–1). (40) To fully prevent kinetic limitations, a sufficiently large electron transfer standard rate constant (kS = 100 cm s–1) was also supposed for simulations. With this information and considering the dimensionless binding parameter log(KeqC*), (38,39,41) voltammetric simulations of the mechanism involved by eqs 1 and 3 were carried out using the commercial software DigiElch 8FD. The resulting working curves of the comparisons between simulated Epc – E1/2° and C* variations are represented in Figure 7.
    As seems in Figure 7, the monomer A+• should be stable at low data (less than −1) of the thermodynamic parameter log(KeqC*), where the potential values are practically the same; this should be fulfilled at low concentrations of C* and/or small values of Keq. The potential shifts are expected by further increasing C*, because the monomers begin to interact to form (A+•)2 dimers. Therefore, the well-fitting experimental results to the working curve, in the region where Epc – E1/2° varies linearly with log(KeqC*) at a rate of 30/n mV per unit log C*, reinforces the proposal that electrogenerated MV+•, (SPr)22––Vi+•, and 5,5′-DMDQ+• species undergo fast and reversible dimerization interactions (eqs 1 and 3) to form (A+•)2 adducts. In that sense, the experimental voltammograms (Figure 5) shifted to lower potential values because the concentration of A+• products was diminished by (A+•)2 dimerization interactions.
    An important advantage of this analysis strategy is that experimental Keq values can also be abstracted from the fittings. For (5,5′-DMDQ+•)2 interaction, this parameter approached Keq = 490 L mol–1, while in the case of MVI2 interaction (MV+•)2, Keq resulted (820 L mol–1) significantly higher to the one (720 L mol–1) reported by Compton et al., (36) to the MVCl2 compound; which suggest that counter ions in MV2+ structures not only control the adsorption abilities of electrogenerated MV+• species at the electrode surface, but also the strength of (MV+•)2 dimerization interactions. Finally, Keq of compound [(SPr)22––Vi2+] is the same regardless of the solvent used and approaches 3000 L mol–1. Interestingly, the latter value is at least three-fold the one estimated (Keq = 820 L mol–1) for interactions in the MVI2 compound, which seems not to be in line with the reported discussions about RFB electrolytes, (13−15,21−23) where dimerization interactions in methyl viologen compound are believed to be responsible for fading the battery and where the compound (SPr)22––Vi2+ is thought to efficiently store and discharge negative charge by preventing dimerization.
    As it is well documented, (39,41) kinetic-controlled dimerization mechanisms, such as those proposed (eq 2) to explain the fading of RFBs (13−15,21−23) should influence the cyclic voltammetry responses similarly as simple irreversible charge transfer reactions. This is because of the small rate constant for the dimer dissociation. As dimer products are favored at high concentrations of C*, such kinetic effects should be visually detected by increasing C*, because a transition from a quasi-reversible behavior toward an irreversible one is expected; which is not the case (Figures 4 and 5). The only case exhibiting a kinetic-controlled system at high concentrations of C* was methyl viologen bearing PF6– ions as a counterpart (Figure 2D), but in this case, the loss in reversibility was caused by an adsorption process of MV+• electrogenerated species at the glassy carbon electrode. The reversibility of the latter system can be recovered with I– (Figure 4) and Cl– counterions, (36) and with the proper selection of a working electrode, as other studies showed that MVCl2 electrogenerated species are not adsorbed on a platinum working electrode but on a mercury one. (27,42) These results suggest that reversibility of charge transfer reactions in methyl viologen compound derivatives are mainly affected by adsorptive interactions of their electrogenerated species. Therefore, we believe that dimerization interactions occurring as a fast and reversible process, such as those detected during the first reduction process of MV2+, (SPr)22––Vi2+, and 5,5′-DMDQ2+ compounds, should not have such a pronounced effect on the electrode kinetics of RFB electrolytes, because the very fast kinetics of dimerization and dissociation reactions keep the system in equilibrium (at the electrode surface) during the electron transfers. In order to get structural information on the noncovalent interactions discussed in this section, electronic structure calculations were performed.

    Calculations for Structural and Energetic Parameters of Dimers

    To gain structural information about the possible formation of dimers in each species after the first reduction process that agreed with the experimental evidence, we performed a screening process to down-select the best dimers. For each dimer, the interaction energy was estimated through a supramolecular approach, obtaining the electronic energy difference between the dimer and the monomer calculated at the same level of theory, according to eq 4.(4)
    The first step was a conformational evaluation of the non-covalent interactions of the species in water. For this purpose, we used the semiempirical tight-binding method implemented in the Grimme’s Conformer–Rotamer Ensemble Sampling Tool (CREST), (43) as described in section Computational Methods. The analysis resulted in an energy distribution within 6 kcal mol–1, as shown in Figure 8A. We observed that species (NPr)22+–Vi+• and (SPr)22––Vi+• generate the highest amount of conformers for the dimers (n = 734 and n = 136 respectively), which is due to a higher degree of freedom generated by the pendant groups. Thus, these species have conformers close in energy that can populate high energy states. On the other hand, MV+•, DQ+•, 4,4′-DMDQ+•, and 5,5′-DMDQ+• have fewer conformers with a more localized density of states. A sample of the adducts obtained is shown in Figure 8B, where we can identify different types of interactions (see Figure S1 for an extended sampling). For all species, we identified a π–π stacking between the viologen or diquat cores. This type of dimer is known as a π-dimer or pimer. The viologen derivatives can form pimers with different arrays, such as parallel face-centered, parallel off-centered, and canted, as can be seen for MV+•. Additionally, the (SPr)22––Vi+• and (NPr)22+–Vi+• species can stabilize other types of interactions (due to the charges localized in the terminal positions of the alkyl chains). For (SPr)22––Vi+•, the negatively charged sulfonate groups favor the intermolecular interaction with the cation radical core giving rise to the formation of a π-anion dimer. However, the positively charged ammonium groups in (NPr)22+–Vi+• have a repulsive effect with the viologen core, identifying perpendicular off-centered and edge-to-face (T-shaped) interactions. In the case of DQ+•, 4,4′-DMDQ+•, and 5,5′-DMDQ+•, the principal adducts observed correspond to an antiparallel-face-centered and a parallel-off-centered pimers.
    For a better description of the dimeric structures obtained, we selected sets of dimers for its evaluation through a density functional (DFT) approximation with the ωB97X-D (44) functional and considering water solvent effects through the SMD implicit solvent model. (45) A subset of ca. 20 conformers was chosen for each species to perform single-point (SP) calculations and estimate the associated interaction energies (ΔESP), as shown in Table S1. Based on the structure and ΔESP values, we picked smaller subsets (2–3 dimers) for further full geometry optimization (Table S2). At first glance, the negative value of ΔEOPT in all cases could mislead the interpretation toward the formation of dimers. At this point, it is worth noting the accuracy of the method. The functional employed has proved to accurately describe C–C bond lengths (46) and non-covalent distances such as those present in the ferrocene system (47) thanks to the inclusion of dispersion corrections. Despite the adequate performance on the geometry, Fokin et al., reported an overestimation of a few kcal mol–1 in the description of rotational barriers. (46) Geraskina et al., had a deviation on the order of 10 kcal mol–1 in the binding energy for the MV+• pimer. (27) To correct this error, we introduced the calculated interaction energy of the DQ+• dimer as an internal reference. This system is suitable for this purpose since the experimental results suggest high stability, and therefore, no dimerization is expected. Figure 9 shows the calibrated interaction energies (ΔECAL) and the optimized structure for the final dimers.
    The screening process resulted in canted and parallel off-centered pimers for MV+•. The former agrees with the minimum energy conformation obtained by Geraskina et al., (27) in a rotational study performed from parallel to perpendicular face-centered arrays. Additionally, a parallel off-centered conformation was identified, which is 0.2 kcal mol–1 lower than the canted. Both energies (−1.9 and −2.1 kcal mol–1, respectively) agree with the order of magnitude reported by the same authors. For (NPr)22+–Vi+•, containing positive pendant groups, the two main conformations correspond to perpendicular off-centered (−2.0 kcal mol–1) and canted (1.1 kcal mol–1). The increase in energy for the latter could be attributed to a greater repulsion between the end groups. When moving from ammonium to sulfonate groups, the (SPr)22––Vi+• species has the possibility of stabilizing the π-anion (Table S2) as well as π–π interactions. However, pimers are strongly favored with energies of −3.0 and −8.7 kcal mol–1 for parallel off-centered and canted arrays, respectively. Finally, for diquat derivatives, the position of methyl groups seems to affect the dimers formed. The 4,4′-DMDQ+• stabilizes an antiparallel face-centered array at −4.7 kcal mol–1, where the methyl groups point in opposite directions. On the other hand, 5,5′-DMDQ+• stabilizes a canted conformation with higher energy (−2.8 kcal mol–1) due to steric hindrance generated by the methyl groups. In both derivatives, off-centered arrays take place at −1.8 and −1.1 kcal mol–1.
    The interaction energies obtained are in the order of a few kcal mol–1 in all cases. This result is expected due to the non-covalent nature of the interactions. Despite the small differences, the trend of the interaction energies is consistent with experimental results. Highly stable compounds, where dimerization is not favorable, have high energy conformations. Particularly, the (NPr)22+–Vi+• dimer has a conformation with positive interaction energy. In contrast, compounds undergoing fast and reversible dimerization processes (MV+•, (SPr)22––Vi+•, and 5,5′-DMDQ+•) have conformations with more stable interaction energies. In order to set a quantitative estimation of dimerization constants, we correlate the calibrated interaction energies with the dimerization constants determined experimentally. The selected values for the correlation are in Table 1. The correlation plot obtained is shown in Figure 10, where we found an R2 = 0.9994 between calculated ΔECAL and experimental Keq data, suggesting a strong correlation between these two properties. This fact supports the methodology used to find accurate interaction energies and suggests the possible dimeric conformations adopted by this species. This correlation can be extended to more species and used as a prediction tool for dimerization constants of electrolytes for flow batteries.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    The first reduction process of methyl viologens and diquat redox flow battery electrolytes was analyzed by cyclic voltammetry in aqueous media, as a function of both the scan rate and initial concentration of each studied compound, finding simple one-electron transfer reactions in diquat, 4,4-dimethyl diquat, and bis(3-trimethylammonio)-propyl viologen compounds, to form radical cations. The reduction mechanism of methyl viologen structure MV is strongly dependent on its counterparts PF6– or I–. The presence of PF6– ions in MV molecules leads to a kinetic-controlled deposition mechanism of electrogenerated radical cations to the electrode surface, while I– ions as a counterpart suppressed those adsorption abilities and maintain the voltammetric responses as reversible in all concentrations studied. For the latter system (MVI2), including 5,5-dimethyl diquat, and 1,1′-bis(3-sulfonatopropyl)-4,4′bipyridinium structures, an experimental evidence for fast and reversible dimerization interactions between their electrogenerated radical cations is presented.
    Screening methodology through different levels of theory was performed for the latter experimentally detected bimolecular interactions to find suitable dimeric structures and interaction energies. By using diquat as a reference system, a relationship between calculated interactions energies and the corresponding experimental dimerization constants was obtained, so the structural information of each interacting species was discussed. For the redox-active molecules evaluated, the correlation of the experimental and theoretical properties can be used as a prediction tool for dimerization constants of new electrolytes for flow batteries.

    Methods

    Chemicals

    Electrochemical experiments were carried out using solutions of previously synthesized diquat and methyl viologen compound derivatives dissolved in acetate buffer (pH = 5), considering different concentrations of the analyte ranging from 0.0005 to 0.1 mol L–1. The complete procedure for the synthesis of studied compounds was previously reported and widely detailed in ref (4). All of the solutions were maintained in an inert atmosphere by saturation with high-purity nitrogen (Praxair grade 5.0) at room temperature.

    Instrumentation

    Concentration and scan rate-dependent experiments were performed by cyclic voltammetry using an AUTOLAB PGSTAT100N potentiostat/galva–nostat interface equipment interconnected to a laptop computer. IR drop compensation was determined (Ru values ∼40 Ω) from positive feedback measurements (51,52) and applied to each performed experiment. A GC disk (d = 3 mm) electrode was used as the working electrode; its surface was polished with 0.25 μm diamond powder (Büehler) and rinsed with distilled water. An aqueous reference electrode Ag/AgCl and a platinum wire were used as the reference and auxiliary electrodes, respectively.

    Computational Methods

    We calculated the first reduced species MV+•, (NPr)22+–Vi+•, (SPr)22––Vi+•, DQ+•, 4,4′-DMDQ+•, and 5,5′-DMDQ+• and the corresponding dimers with the following specifications. All DFT calculations were carried out with the Gaussian 16 package. (53) We considered the long-range-corrected ωB97X-D functional (44) with dispersion corrections and the triple zeta valence basis set def2-TZVP from Ahlrichs and Weigend (54) for all atoms. The solvent effects were considered through the SMD solvation model (45) in water. For both monomers and dimers, initial geometries were pre-optimized in water with Grimme’s xtb program. (55,56) Using the xtb-optimized geometries as input, we performed a conformer search with the CREST, (43) implemented in the same package, with the specification of sampling for non-covalent interactions for the dimers. The threshold value for the conformer sampling was 6 kcal mol–1 and corresponds to the default value set by the program. Subsequent to the conformational analysis, we selected a sample of 10 monomers and ca. 20 dimers (when available) for further SP DFT calculations. Finally, we picked a smaller set of 2–3 conformers with the lower SP energies for full DFT geometry optimization. The interaction energies were referred to the value calculated for the diquat dimer.
