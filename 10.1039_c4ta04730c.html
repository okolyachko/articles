A metal-free and all-organic redox flow battery with polythiophene as the electroactive species†

aEnergy Storage Laboratory, Korea Institute of Energy Research, Daejeon 305-343, Republic of Korea

Abstract

A metal-free and all-organic redox flow battery is proposed and investigated. It employs polythiophene microparticles dispersed in electrolyte solutions as the redox couple and does not depend on limited metal resources. It shows a high cell potential and stable charge/discharge performance with a high energy efficiency of 60.9%.

The surge in demand for electrical energy has been a common concern around the world. In addition, greenhouse-gas emission is another challenge. As a solution to these problems, large-capacity energy-storage systems, combined with renewable energy and smart grids, have been proposed. Among various large-capacity energy-storage systems, the redox flow battery (RFB) is one of the most viable alternatives. Compared to other secondary batteries such as lithium-ion and sodium–sulfur batteries, RFBs in the range of 10 kW to 10 MW are the most competitive in terms of cost, flexibility, rapid response, and safety.1–3 In particular, the power and energy capacity of a flow battery can be controlled independently by maintaining the electro-active species in the flow.

There are two classes of RFBs: aqueous and organic (Table S1†). The aqueous RFB takes a variety of forms depending on the redox couple employed: all vanadium (VRB),4,5 zinc–bromine (ZBB),3,6 iron–chromium (ICB),7 polysulfide–bromine (PSB), and so on. Among them, VRBs, ZBBs, ICBs, and PSBs have been commercialised at power levels of a few hundred kilowatts and even multi-megawatts.4 According to several feasibility tests, however, the commercialisation of aqueous RFBs is hindered by the high price of electrolytes which contain transition metals.8,9 In addition, another limitation of the aqueous redox flow battery stems from the electrolysis of water, which has a theoretical electrochemical potential of 1.229 V and prevents the cell potential from increasing above the hydrogen or oxygen evolution voltage. In fact, ICBs and ZBBs are hindered by H2 evolution on the anodic side.

Therefore, the organic redox flow battery, which is free from the water electrolysis problem, has been studied extensively in recent years. Organic molecules can be synthesised from low-cost, lightweight, and widely available materials. In addition, their electrochemical properties can be easily adjusted using well-established organic chemistry data.10–13 By taking these attributes of organic materials, it may be possible to fabricate a redox flow battery with very high energy densities and power densities. However, organic RFBs are encumbered by low electrical conductivity, and therefore low current density and low power density.

In this study, we used polythiophene as the redox couple in an organic flow battery. Polythiophene is one of the π-aromatic, redox-active organic molecules. It holds two opposite electrochemical properties: it becomes conducting through both n-doping and p-doping. It can accommodate one additional electron for every thiophene repeating unit at an extremely negative potential below −2.0 V vs. Ag/Ag+. It also loses one electron for every repeating unit when a positive potential above +0.5 V vs. Ag/Ag+ is applied. Based on theoretical calculations, it can offer a high specific capacity (∼319 mA h g−1) that is nearly double that of LiCoO2 in a lithium-ion battery.14 Therefore, we can use these polythiophene microparticles as both anodic and cathodic redox couples. In addition, polythiophene is one of the representative conducting polymers. By using these conductive particles dispersed in an organic electrolyte, we can overcome the low electrical conductivity of organic RFBs.

The electrochemical characteristics of polythiophene were studied using cyclic voltammetry (CV) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). Polythiophene prepared by two different methods, electrochemical and chemical polymerisation, was compared in terms of its electrochemical properties. Using the chemically synthesised polythiophene particles, their stability and performance as a redox couple in a flow battery were confirmed by a charge/discharge test.

The thiophene monomer was first electrochemically polymerised at a specific potential in the range of 0.0–2.2 V. A thin layer of the resulting polythiophene was then coated on the carbon electrode surface with an effective area of 0.0707 cm2. To investigate the n-doping characteristics of the electrochemically synthesised polythiophene, we applied a cyclic potential of −1.2 to −2.3 V to the polythiophene-coated carbon electrode. Fig. 1a shows the cyclic voltammograms obtained at various scan rates. The peak current density for n-doping on polythiophene was observed in the potential range of −2.2 to −2.0 V, which is in agreement with previously reported values in the literature.15–18 Although polythiophene was deposited on the electrode surface, the cathodic peak current density increased linearly with the square root of the scan rate (Fig. 1c). A likely reason is that, in general, the polythiophene redox process is accompanied by incorporation of counter ions into the polymer network, and if relatively large ions diffuse into the network, the rate of diffusion would determine the overall redox rate. According to Lin et al.,19 the ionic sizes of tetraethylammonium cations (TEA+: 0.67 nm) and tetrafluoroborate anions (BF4−: 0.48 nm) are considerably larger than those of general supporting electrolytes (e.g. Li+: 0.18 nm). Because tetraethyl ammonium tetrafluoroborate (TEABF4) was used as the supporting electrolyte, the redox rate would have been determined by the rate of diffusion of TEA+. Furthermore, when the polythiophene layer became thicker than a certain value, the charge-transfer reaction for n-doping was sharply weakened (Fig. S1†).


Fig. 1 Cyclic voltammograms, obtained at various scan rates, of electrochemically polymerised polythiophene on the carbon electrode surface. Cyclic voltammograms for (a) n-doping and (b) p-doping. (c) Scan-rate dependence of the cathodic peak current density in the n-doping process. (d) Scan rate dependence of the anodic peak current density in the p-doping process.

Fig. 1b shows the cyclic voltammograms obtained to investigate the p-doping characteristics of the same polythiophene layer. The anodic redox reaction occurred in a broad potential range of 0.2–0.8 V. In this case, the anodic peak current density increased linearly with the scan rate (Fig. 1d), which fits the general behaviour of a deposited electroactive layer. Here, since the relatively small tetrafluoroborate anions diffused into the polythiophene network more easily, the overall redox rate was determined by the characteristics of charge-transfer between the electrode and polythiophene layer. Unlike the case for n-doping, the anodic peak current density for p-doping increased linearly with the deposition charge over the entire range from 10.2 to 110.7 mC (Fig. S1†).

To compare the chemically synthesised polythiophene with its electrochemically synthesised counterpart, we pasted a thin layer of the chemically synthesised polythiophene microparticles on the titanium electrode (Fig. 2a). We then analysed the CVs of the polythiophene particle layers with different thicknesses (Fig. 2b). When compared with the CVs of the electrochemically synthesised polythiophene, the overpotential was higher and the peak current was less distinct because of the contact resistance between the polythiophene particles and the titanium electrode surface. The current densities for both n-doping and p-doping increased as the layer thickness was increased. However, when the layer was thicker than 500 μm, the current was sharply decreased.

(b) Cyclic voltammogram for n-doping and p-doping of polythiophene microparticle layers with different thicknesses at a sweep rate of 100 mV s−1. (c) Bond-length deviation of charged polythiophene with respect to neutral polythiophene. Averaged bond lengths of neutral polythiophene are a = 1.74 Å, b = 1.39 Å, and c = 1.41 Å. (d) XRD pattern of a 200 μm film of cast polythiophene particles before doping, after n-doping, and after p-doping.

The chemically synthesised polythiophene showed little structural change before and after the doping process. We calculated polythiophene structures using density functional theory (DFT) to further examine its electronic redistribution by either n-doping or p-doping and the corresponding structural change. As shown in Fig. 2c, the structural changes of polythiophene via n- and p-doping were insignificant: the maximum change in bond length was about 0.04 Å (2.9%) with respect to neutral polythiophene. More specifically, in the n-doping case, there were bond extensions in S–C (shown by bond a in the inset in Fig. 2c) and C–C (bond b in the inset), bond shrinkage in C–C (bond c in the inset), and a decrease in the inter-ring distance (denoted by d in the inset). A similar trend was observed for the p-doping case, except that the changes in bond length were localised in b, c, and d: there was no bond-length change in a, and the magnitudes of bond-length changes in b, c, and d were larger in p-doped than n-doped polythiophene. The bond angles (Table S2†) also showed trends similar to those of the bond lengths. Despite doping, the angular deviations of the carbon ring were within 1° (maximum change: −0.99°).

The X-ray diffraction (XRD) pattern also shows an insignificant change in the polythiophene structure by either n-doping or p-doping, except for a strong peak at 2θ = 13.8° (Fig. 2d). This peak corresponds to a distance of 6.4 Å between diffracting planes. When polythiophene was n-doped or p-doped, the polymer chain could have been rearranged by the charge repulsion between the thiophene repeating units, producing some crystallite structures.

Therefore, we can assume that polythiophene was stable during the redox process. The above results indicate that chemically synthesised polythiophene particles also showed strong activity in n-doping and p-doping. We then decided to apply these particles as the redox active couple in the redox flow battery because it was easier to disperse them in an electrolyte.

To investigate the availability of polythiophene microparticles as the redox couple, a charge/discharge test was conducted using a non-flow cell. A carbon felt electrode with a cross-sectional area of 12 cm2 and thickness of 0.45 cm was soaked in an electrolyte consisting of polythiophene (8.41 g L−1) and TEABF4 (1 mol L−1) in propylene carbonate (PC). The amount of absorbed electrolyte was controlled to be 3 mL. Afterwards, the charge and discharge capacities were found to decrease steadily (Fig. 3a), possibly because of the sedimentation of polythiophene particles in the absence of an electrolyte flow. If particles accumulate on the bottom of a cell, the upper part of the carbon felt electrode will not participate in the electrochemical reactions owing to the lack of the active material. As a result, the electrode overpotential will increase and the charge or discharge capacity will decrease. This problem was solved by circulating the electrolyte in the flow cell and the (dis)charge capacity ceased to decrease.


Fig. 3 (a) Plot of capacity vs. cycle number for a non-flow cell using polythiophene dispersed in 1 M TEABF4–PC as both the catholyte and anolyte. (b) Efficiencies during charge/discharge tests at different current densities. (c) Representative charge/discharge profile of the non-flow cell at 0.5 mA cm−2 and 25 °C.

The voltage efficiencies at current densities of 0.5 and 1.0 mA cm−2 remained high at >80% (Fig. 3b) because a carbon felt electrode with high surface area was used. The charge and discharge voltages were around 2.7 and 2.4 V, respectively (Fig. 3c). However, the voltage efficiency and charge/discharge capacities at a current density of 5.0 mA cm−2 decreased dramatically owing to the high overpotential and IR drop across the electrolyte and separator. This was also a reason for using a low current density below 1 mA cm−2 in previous studies on other organic redox flow batteries,20,21 indicating that the low electrical conductivity of the electrolyte in an all-organic redox flow battery is a common challenge that awaits a solution.

An all-organic redox flow battery can be constructed using polythiophene microparticles as both anolyte and catholyte active couples, and the electrolyte does not include any metal ingredients. In our study, polythiophene (8.41 g L−1) and Ketjenblack (2 g L−1) were dispersed in a 1 M TEABF4–PC solution, and 40 mL of this mixed solution was circulated through the flow cell, as shown schematically in Fig. 4. This polythiophene concentration corresponds to 0.1 eq. L−1 of thiophene repeating units. To avoid potential side reactions of polythiophene, we used a cutoff voltage of 3.0 V.

(b) Flow single cell consisting of a copper current collector, bipolar plate electrode, PTFE flow frame, and anion-exchange membrane. (c) Charge/discharge profile (at 0.5 mA cm−2 and 25 °C) of a flow cell using polythiophene (0.1 eq. (d) Current, voltage, and energy efficiencies at different current densities.

Fig. 4c shows stable charge/discharge performance of the battery at a current density of 0.5 mA cm−2, using 34.5% of the theoretical capacity. The relatively low utilisation of the battery capacity can be explained by the polythiophene conductivity (Fig. S8†). Conducting polymers such as polythiophene, polypyrrole, and polyaniline show low conductivities in the range of 10−10 to 10−5 S cm−1 in their neutral states. They are converted to semiconductive or conductive states with conductivities of 1 to 104 S cm−1 through n-doping or p-doping.17 Neutral polythiophene is converted to its doped state from the exterior to the interior of the particle during charging. This process generates a conductive shell, which helps with additional doping of the particle inside. Therefore, most of the polythiophene could be doped during the first charging process in our test. On the other hand, a doped polythiophene particle is converted to its neutral state in the form of a non-conductive shell during discharging, which hinders further reaction of the particle inside. The discharge takes place within the thickness through which electrons can hop. This is the reason for the significantly low current efficiency during the first few cycles in our test (Fig. S8†). The charge capacities for the first three cycles were 77.6, 62.2, and 44.3% of the theoretical capacity. The low capacity utilisation by the non-conductive shell is expected to be improved by using nanoscale instead of microscale polythiophene particles.

The galvanic discharge capacity retention (discharge capacity ratio of the present cycle compared to the previous cycle) was as high as 100.2 ± 2.4% at a current density of 0.5 mA cm−2 (Fig. 4c). The efficiencies of current, voltage, and energy were 77.5 ± 1.3, 78.6 ± 0.6, and 60.9 ± 1.1%, respectively. Even though the carbon felt electrode was not used, the voltage efficiency remained high (>75%) at current densities of 0.2, 0.5, and 1.0 mA cm−2 (Fig. 4d). This can be explained by the network between the conductive polythiophene particles and Ketjenblack during the electrolyte circulation.

The above results were compared with previous reports on non-aqueous systems to demonstrate their good performance.20–29 This performance is expected to be further enhanced in the future as the materials (polythiophene derivatives, particle size, conductive additives, etc.) and process variables (cell design and operating conditions) continue to be studied and optimized. Furthermore, it is possible to disperse the polythiophene particles so that the concentration exceeds 2.0 eq. L−1 of thiophene repeating units, thus increasing the energy density of the battery. Because the same initial electrolyte composition is used in both the anolyte and catholyte, each half-cell electrolyte can be remixed to recover the initial electrolyte state in the event of solution crossover.

Conclusions

The all-organic redox flow battery has been extensively studied to attain a higher cell potential and to remove the reliance on limited metal resources. We used polythiophene microparticles as both anodic and cathodic redox couples in our study. Polythiophene showed electrochemical redox activity at −2.0 and +0.5 V vs. Ag/Ag+ (n-doping and p-doping, respectively), which means we can operate this all-organic RFB at a cell potential of 2.5 V. This is, to the best of our knowledge, the highest cell potential in metal-free and all-organic RFBs. The CV and EIS results were analysed to investigate the electrochemical activity of the polythiophene that was synthesised electrochemically and chemically. Both types showed strong activity for n-doping and p-doping. However, this activity started to decrease above a critical thickness of the polythiophene layer. Therefore, we dispersed the polythiophene microparticles in a 1 M TEABF4–PC solution and circulated it through the flow cell. It showed stable charge/discharge performance, with a high energy efficiency of 60.9% at 0.5 mA cm−1.

Furthermore, as one of the representative conducting polymers, polythiophene was helpful in reducing the electric resistance inside the cell. Another merit was that the same initial electrolyte composition was used for the anolyte and catholyte. If solution crossover had occurred, each half-cell electrolyte could have been remixed to recover the initial electrolyte state. As the size of the polythiophene particle is expected to have a strong effect on the battery performance, we plan to synthesise polythiophene nanoparticles for this organic RFB in the future.
