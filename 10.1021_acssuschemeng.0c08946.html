    Abstract

    Aqueous organic redox flow batteries (AORFBs) employing synthetically tailorable organic electroactive compounds have received significant attention for energy storage technologies. There have been many efforts in developing electroactive materials for AORFBs with anion-exchange membranes. On the contrary, electroactive compounds that are compatible with cation-exchange membranes in AORFBs are less studied. Here, we report an electroactive 4-carboxylic-2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-N-oxyl (4-CO2Na-TEMPO) molecule for neutral AORFBs. The compound exhibits a good solubility of 1.5 M in an aqueous sodium-based solution, which is 3 times more than that of the pristine 4-hydroxy-2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-oxyl (4-OH-TEMPO). When paired with a 1,10-bis(3-sulfonatopropyl)-4,4′-bipyridinium (SPr)2V anolyte, the resulting RFB operating through a cation-exchange membrane achieved an open-circuit voltage of 1.19 V and a high energy density of 14.7 W h L–1. In the long-term cycling study, the RFB features a stable capacity retention of 99.94% per cycle over 400 cycles with nearly 100% Coulombic efficiency.

    Synopsis

    A redox flow battery operating through a cation-exchange membrane is designed for sustainable energy storage.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Redox flow batteries (RFBs) using dissolved redox-active species have received considerable attention owing to the potential to store intermittent energy such as solar and wind energy. (1−4) The technology has the merit of energy–power decoupling, which enables high flexibility in the design to meet various application requirements. RFBs relying on inorganic electroactive materials (e.g., all-vanadium RFBs and Fe–Cr RFBs) have been extensively investigated. However, the development of such RFBs is hindered by several critical issues, including the adoption of expensive electroactive metal materials and corrosive acid supporting electrolytes. (5−10)
    To address the shortcomings faced by inorganic RFBs, recent efforts have targeted RFBs employing sustainable and tunable organic electroactive molecules due to their high structural tunability, cost-effectiveness, high natural abundance, and safety features. (11−15) Apart from the general technical strengths of organic RFBs, aqueous organic RFBs have several additional attracting merits including incorporation of well-developed selective ionic membranes and non-flammable and highly conducting supporting electrolytes. (16−19) Great efforts have been taken to develop AORFBs by utilizing water-soluble organic anolytes (e.g., quinones, (20−22) viologens, (18,23,24) alloxazines, (25) and phenazines (26)) and catholytes (e.g., TEMPO (24,27−29) and ferrocenes (30,31)). It should also be pointed out that remarkable development has also been achieved in non-aqueous organic RFBs (NAORFBs). (32−41)
    Comparing with acidic or alkaline aqueous RFBs, neutral AORFBs have been proven to be a better choice because they are non-corrosive and safer, and as such, they have been extensively researched. (18,42,43) Liu, Schubert, Aziz, and other researchers have employed 4-OH-TEMPO, ferrocene, and viologen and their derivatives to realize stable and high-energy-density pH-neutral AORFBs. (18,29,42) Among these molecules, 4-OH-TEMPO has been widely employed as a stable electroactive cathode material due to its fast and reversible redox reaction. (24) However, its low solubility in a sodium-based supporting electrolyte (0.5 M in 1.5 M NaCl aqueous solution) prevents its practical application. To improve the solubility, Schubert et al. and Xu et al. prepared TEMPO derivatives with positively charged hydrophilic trimethylammonium groups. (27,29) These molecules with positively charged trimethylammonium groups are well-suited for flow battery application with anion-exchange membranes but not with cation-exchange membranes. RFBs could also be constructed with cation-exchange membranes such as Nafion, which is readily available and also exhibits superior conductivity and durability. Therefore, it is of interest to design negatively charged TEMPO derivatives that could enhance the solubility of 4-OH-TEMPO and also make it compatible with cation-exchange membranes in AORFBs. Although Schubert et al. developed a 4-OSO3--TEMPO molecule for a semi-organic zinc hybrid RFB, (28) as far as we know the electrochemical and cycling performances of negatively charged TEMPO in all-organic full-flow AORFB applications remain unknown.
    Herein, we report the use of a carboxyl-functionalized TEMPO-based (4-CO2Na-TEMPO) catholyte for cation-exchange pH-neutral AORFBs. The introduced hydrophilic sodium carboxylic group (−COONa) significantly enhances the solubility in water (2.5 M, corresponding to 67 A h L–1 capacity) and in sodium-based supporting electrolytes (1.5 M, corresponding to 40.2 A h L–1 capacity). Pairing with a sulfonate-functionalized viologen anolyte leads to a flow battery that delivers stable cycling performance with a capacity retention of 99.94% for a single cycle, demonstrating the superior cycling performance of this electroactive molecule.

    2.1. Design and Synthesis of 4-CO2Na-TEMPO (3)

    We anticipated that functionalization of TEMPO with negatively charged hydrophilic moieties could enhance the solubility and also make it compatible with the cation exchange membrane. Therefore, we decided to prepare a TEMPO derivative functionalized with an ionic carboxylic group (−CO2Na). Although Zhou et al. have examined the electrochemical properties of a series of TEMPO derivatives and concluded that 4-COOH-TEMPO is not suitable as an electroactive catholyte for aqueous RFB applications due to its poor solubility, (44) it was believed that the solubility of the TEMPO compound could be largely enhanced via installation of the carboxylic group −CO2Na, which has been demonstrated in the phenazine compound. (26) Additionally, the counter-sodium ion can act as a charge carrier to enhance the ionic conductivity. As shown in Scheme 1, the desired compound 4-CO2Na-TEMPO (3) can be synthesized following a modified literature route. Treatment of the low-cost 4-oxo-2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine (1) with tosylmethyl isocyanide produced 4-cyano-2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-piperidine-N-oxyl (2). Treatment of 2 with Ba(OH)2 followed by NaOH gave the desired compound 4-COONa-TEMPO (3). The synthesis can be carried out at an amount of 10 g with a good overall yield of 81%. Compound 3 has been fully characterized by HR-MS and 1H NMR. UV–vis measurements (Figure S1) show that compound 3 possesses a good solubility of 1.5 M in NaCl aqueous solution and 2.5 M in water. Thus, compound 3 is significantly more soluble than the pristine 4-OH-TEMPO (Figure S1), benefiting from the highly hydrophilic carboxylate group (Table 1). In addition to the improved solubility of 3 compared with that of 4-OH-TEMPO, compound 3 also has a reduced permeability across cation-exchange membranes, attributed to the larger molecular size and the presence of the negative charge. The permeabilities of 4-OH-TEMPO and 4-CO2Na-TEMPO crossing through a Nafion 212 cation-exchange membrane were calculated to be 1.75 × 10–8 and 2.25 × 10–10 cm2 s–1, respectively. The permeability of 3 is 2 orders of magnitude lower than that of 4-OH-TEMPO, meaning that it has a lower tendency to cause cross-over.

    2.2. Electrochemical Characterization of 4-CO2Na-TEMPO (3)

    The basic electrochemical properties of 3 have been studied by cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements. As shown in Figure 1a, a reversible redox peak appeared at 0.60 V versus Ag/AgCl with a cathodic-to-anodic peak current ratio of ∼1.0. The compound has a good electrochemical stability as no changes were detected in the CV curves after 200 scans. When the scan rates increased, the peak potentials stayed identical, while the peak currents linearly increased with the square root of the scan rate, indicating a diffusion-controlled process (Figure S2). Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) was carried out to study the electrochemical kinetics of 3. Figure 1b depicts the LSV scans for the oxidation of 3. The diffusion coefficient was calculated to be 5.45 × 10–6 cm2 s–1 from the slope of Levich plots (Figure 1c) by applying the Levich equation (Experimental Section). The diffusion coefficient is in the usual range of TEMPO-based electroactive molecules. Through Tafel and Butler–Volmer analyses (Figure S3), the reaction rate constant (k0) was calculated to be 2.1 × 10–3 cm s–1, approaching those of 4-OSO3–-TEMPO (1.91 × 10–3 cm s–1) and TEMPTMA (4.2 × 10–3 cm s–1).

    2.3. Initial Flow Battery Studies and Optimization of the Electrolyte

    Figure 2 shows the outline of the AORFB cell design. 4-CO2Na-TEMPO was used as the catholyte and (SPr)2V as the anolyte. A cation-exchange membrane (Nafion 212) incorporated with pendant sulfonated anions was utilized to allow Na+ transport and to suppress the cross-over of negatively charged active molecules. Figure 2 gives the half-cell reactions of the anode and cathode in the flow battery. In the charging process, the colorless (SPr)2V in the anode side was reduced to a deep purple radical, (SPr)2V–, at −0.59 V; 4-CO2Na-TEMPO was oxidized to oxoammonium at +0.6 V in the cathode side. The voltage of this redox flow battery is 1.19 V. In the initial battery test with 0.4 M of 3, it was found that aggregates gradually formed, and a slow drop of the discharge capacity occurred within 25 cycles (Figure S4). Formation of precipitates was also observed in some TEMPO-based RFB systems. (28,45) In our case, the precipitates formed are soluble in chloroform (and dichloromethane) and insoluble in water and were identified by 1H NMR to be 4-CO2H-TEMPO (Figures S5–S7). The formation of 4-CO2H-TEMPO is likely caused by the pH drop of the solution as a result of the reaction of the oxidized TEMPO derivative with water, which consumes OH– (Figure S8). (46,47) To prevent the precipitation due to the pH change, we adopted an effective strategy by using a phosphate buffer solution to maintain the neutral pH value.

    2.4. Flow Battery Studies and Cycling Performance

    After electrolyte optimization, the (SPr)2V/4-CO2Na-TEMPO full-flow RFB was demonstrated using 0.05 M (SPr)2V and 0.05 M 4-CO2Na-TEMPO in 1.5 M NaCl buffered with 0.01 M phosphate, resulting in a theoretical capacity of 1.34 A h L–1. The rate performance was first evaluated at current densities of 10, 20, 30, and 40 mA cm–2 with the voltage ranging from 0.7 to 1.5 V. Figure 3a displays the charge and discharge capacities and capacity retentions at each current density. A high material utilization of 94% was attained at 10 mA cm–2 (Figure S9). The battery delivered nearly 100% coulombic efficiency at these current densities. However, with the increasing current density, the voltage efficiency decreased (being 90, 81, 72, and 63% at 10, 20, 30, and 40 mA cm–2, respectively) (Figure 3b). To reveal the long cycling stability, an extended battery cycling test was conducted at 30 mA cm–2 over 400 cycles (Figure 3c). The cell shows nearly 100% coulombic efficiency in the entire cycling, which indicates low species crossing rate and insignificant side reactions. Notably, this cell delivered impressive stable capacity retention after 400 cycles with a capacity retention of 99.94% per cycle, evincing good cycling stability of the 4-CO2Na-TEMPO cathode molecule. High-resolution mass spectrometry (HRMS), 1H NMR, and CV analyses were conducted after the cycling test. As can be seen in Figures 3c–e and S10, negligible chemical degradation of the electroactive electrolyte occurred in the cathode under the battery condition. Additionally, the UV–vis spectra are almost identical before and after cycling, verifying the good stability of 4-CO2Na-TEMPO (Figure S11).
    The cell performance has also been investigated at 4-CO2Na-TEMPO concentrations of 0.4 and 1 M, corresponding to theoretical capacities of 10.72 and 26.8 A h L–1, respectively. Figure 4 shows the charge and discharge performance of 0.4 M 4-CO2Na-TEMPO, and the voltage–time curves versus varied current densities are displayed in Figure S12. The battery presented a high electrolyte utilization of 93% at 10 mA cm–2. The electrolyte utilization slightly decreased to 89% at 40 mA cm–2 due to the increased over-potential. Benefiting from the low cell resistance of 5.4 Ω cm2 (Figure S14), the battery exhibits a high energy efficiency of 83.5% at 10 mA cm–2 and a moderate energy efficiency of 64.0% at 40 mA cm–2 (Figure 4b). The voltage efficiency dropped when the current density increased. The voltage efficiencies could be enhanced by adding more supporting salt to reduce the voltage loss. For example, the voltage efficiency of a cell with 2 M NaCl (78%) is appreciably higher than that of a cell with 1.5 M NaCl (<75%) at the same current density (Figures S13, S15, and S16). The long-term cycling performance has been examined at 30 mA cm–2. As shown in Figure 4c, the flow battery manifests a good electrolyte utilization of 90% and a capacity retention of 99.8% per cycle within 50 cycles without observed precipitations. The HRMS (Figure S17), NMR data (Figure 4d), and CV (Figure 4e) curves indicate that decomposition of the electroactive materials is not significant after 50 cycles.
    The cycling experiment with a higher concentration (1 M) of 4-CO2Na-TEMPO revealed a demonstrated capacity of 24.5 A h L–1 (14.7 W h L–1) and ∼80% material utilization (Figures S18 and S19). However, the capacity gradually dropped to 67% of its initial value after 20 cycles. NMR data of the cycled electrolyte reveal that decomposition of the active material is also insignificant (Figure S20). We noted that some aggregates formed, which might be the major cause for the decline of capacity retention. Further work will be carried out to optimize the battery design to realize a better cell performance at high electrolyte concentration.

    3.1. General

    All solvents and chemicals are commercially available and used as received. All reactions were carried out under a N2 atmosphere without moisture and air using standard Schlenk techniques. All electrochemical CV characterizations were operated under a N2 atmosphere. Pure water was purged with N2 to remove O2 before use. All RFB tests were carried out in a glovebox filled with argon. 13C and 1H NMR spectra were collected with a Bruker DPX 400 spectrometer at 100 MHz and 400 MHz, respectively. The chemical shift (δ, ppm) was reported with reference to tetramethylsilane. Mass spectra were recorded on an API QSTAR XL system and an Agilent GC/MS 5975C system. UV–visible spectra were collected with a PerkinElmer Lambda 365 UV–vis spectrometer.

    3.2. Synthetic Procedures

    3.2.1. 1,1′-Bis[2-sulfonatopropyl]-4,4′-bipyridinium, (SPr)2V

    The compound was prepared according to the literature procedure. (23)
    Propanesultone (9.7 g, 76.8 mmol) was dissolved in 70 mL of toluene and was heated to reflux in a 500 mL N2-purged Schlenk flask. 4,4′-Bipyridine (6.0 g, 38.4 mmol) was dissolved in 50 mL of toluene and added dropwise to the refluxing solution. The flask was cooled to room temperature after maintaining the reaction at 110 °C for 4 h. The resultant white precipitate product was collected by filtration, washed with acetonitrile (2 × 40 mL), and dried under vacuum. Yield, 9.8 g, 70.7%. 1H NMR (400 MHz, D2O): δ 9.16 (d, J = 6.3 Hz, 4H), 8.57 (d, J = 5.5 Hz, 4H), 4.89 (t, J = 7.4 Hz, 4H), 3.03 (t, J = 7.2 Hz, 4H), 2.68–2.35 (m, 4H).

    3.2.2. 2,4-Carboxy-2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-piperidin-N-oxyl (4-CO2Na-TEMPO) (3)

    This compound was prepared by a modified literature procedure. (48)

    3.2.3. Synthesis of 4-Cyano-2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-piperidin-N-oxyl (4-CN-TEMPO) (2)

    2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-N-oxyl-4-one (1) (2.0 g, 11.8 mmol) was dissolved in 100 mL of DME in a two-neck round-bottom flask, and tosylmethyl isocyanide (2.4 g, 12.4 mmol, 1.05 equiv) was added at 0 °C, after which t-BuOK (1.32 g, 11.8 mmol) in DME/t-butanol (1:1, 40 mL) was added to the reaction mixture. The reaction mixture was stirred for 1 h at 0 °C and for further 1 h at room temperature. The reaction was quenched by H2O, and the product was extracted with Et2O (3 × 100 mL). The combined organic layers were dried over MgSO4 and filtered, and the solvent was removed under reduced pressure. The product 4-cyano-2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-piperidin-N-oxyl (2) was obtained (1.5 g, 8.25 mmol, 70.5%) as a red solid. HR-MS (CI) m/z calcd for C10H17N2O+ 181.1341; found, 181.1332.

    3.2.4. Synthesis of 4-Carboxy-2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-piperidin-N-oxyl (4-CO2H-TEMPO)

    4-Cyano-2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-piperidin-N-oxyl (2, 1.5 g, 8.3 mmol) was dissolved in methanol (25 mL), and a mixture of Ba(OH)2 × 8H2O (9.8 g, 31.2 mmol) and NaOH (0.522 g, 13.05 mmol) in H2O (75 mL) was added. After refluxing for 24 h, the mixture was cooled to room temperature and extracted with CHCl3 (3 × 80 mL). The aqueous layer was acidified with HCl (aq. 10%) to pH = 2 and extracted with CHCl3 (3 × 80 mL). The combined organic layers were dried over MgSO4 and filtered, and the solvent was removed under reduced pressure. The product 4-CO2H-TEMPO was isolated (0.99 g, 4.9 mmol, 60%) as a red solid.

    3.2.5. Synthesis of 4-Carboxy-2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-piperidin-N-oxyl (4-CO2Na-TEMPO) (3)

    4-CO2H-TEMPO was dissolved in water and treated with NaOH to give the sodium-based salt 4-CO2Na-TEMPO. One drop of phenyl hydrazine was added to the sample to quench the radical to give 4-CO2Na-TEMPOH for 1H NMR measurements. 1H NMR (400 MHz, D2O): δ 2.62 (t, J = 12.9 Hz, 1H), 1.87 (d, J = 10.7 Hz, 2H), 1.60 (t, J = 13.2 Hz, 2H), 1.20 (d, v = 11.5 Hz, 12H). HR-MS (CI) m/z calcd for C10H18NO3+ 201.1281; found, 201.1286.

    3.3. Solubility Tests

    The solubility of 4-CO2Na-TEMPO in aqueous solutions was evaluated by the UV–vis method. First, standard solutions (0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, and 0.8 mM) were made. Then, the absorbance values of these solutions were collected at a wavelength of λ = 243 nm and were subsequently utilized to prepare a calibration curve. Next, supersaturated solutions were prepared after centrifuging for 20 min, and the supernatant liquor was acquired and diluted 10,000-fold to afford the absorbance.

    3.5. CV Studies

    Electrochemical CV experiments were performed on Autolab PGSTAT30. A glassy carbon electrode (ϕ = 3 mm), a platinum column, and an aqueous Ag/AgCl electrode were employed as the working electrode, counter electrode, and reference electrode, respectively. The glassy carbon electrode was polished with Al2O3 powder before use, and the silver wire in the reference electrode was coated with AgCl in the 3.5 M KCl electrolyte. All potentials reported herein are referenced to Ag/AgCl (E = 0.199 V vs NHE). All CV experiments were carried out with a 0.5 M NaCl aqueous solution purged with N2 before testing.

    3.7. Flow Battery Test

    Flow batteries were assembled with two aluminum end-plates, two gold-coated copper current collectors, two graphite-plate electrodes, and two polytetrafluoroethylene gaskets, sandwiching a Nafion 212 membrane. Graphite plates with a serpentine flow field (2 cm × 2 cm) were used. The electrode on each side comprised three stacked sheets of SGL 39AA carbon paper electrodes (heated to 400 °C for 6 h prior to use). All electrolytes were purged with argon to completely remove O2 and then placed into an argon-filled glovebox to prevent atmospheric oxygen. A Masterflex L/S peristaltic pump (BX100J-1A) was applied to pump and circulate the electrolytes with a flow rate of 30 mL min–1. The flow battery tests were performed on Arbin BT2000. The polarization curve and electrochemical impedance spectra were collected using an Autolab (PGSTAT30) workstation. For the 0.05 M cell, the catholyte comprises 10 mL of 0.05 M 3 in 1.5 M NaCl aqueous solution and 0.1 M phosphate buffer solution (adjust the pH ≈ 7.2), and the anolyte comprises 15 mL of 0.1 M (SPr)2V in 1.5 M NaCl aqueous solution. For the 0.4 M cell, the catholyte comprises 10 mL of 0.4 M 3 in 1.5 M NaCl aqueous solution and 0.4 M phosphate buffer solution (adjust the pH ≈ 7.2), and the anolyte comprises 15 mL of 0.4 M (SPr)2V in 1.5 M NaCl aqueous solution. Post 1H NMR and CV studies of cycled 3 were conducted for both 0.1 M and 0.4 M electrolytes. For the 1 M cell, the catholyte comprises 8 mL of 1 M 3 in 1.5 M NaCl aqueous solution buffered with 0.5 M phosphate (adjust the pH ≈ 7.2), and the anolyte comprises 16 mL of 0.8 M (SPr)2V aqueous solution. Excessive amounts of anolytes were used in order to minimize the effects of anolytes on capacity loss so that we can focus on evaluating the stability of the catholyte.
