    Abstract

    Applications of conducting carbon materials for highly efficient electrochemical energy devices require a greater fundamental understanding of heterogeneous electron-transfer (ET) mechanisms. This task, however, is highly challenging experimentally, because an adsorbing carbon surface may easily conceal its intrinsic reactivity through adventitious contamination. Herein, we employ nanoscale scanning electrochemical microscopy (SECM) and cyclic voltammetry to gain new insights into the interplay between heterogeneous ET and adsorption of a Co(III)/Co(II)-complex redox couple at the contamination-free surface of electron-beam-deposited carbon (eC). Specifically, we investigate the redox couple of tris(1,10-phenanthroline)cobalt(II), Co(phen)32+, as a promising mediator for dye-sensitized solar cells and redox flow batteries. A pristine eC surface overlaid with KCl is prepared in vacuum, protected from contamination in air, and exposed to an ultrapure aqueous solution of Co(phen)32+ by the dissolution of the protective KCl layer. We employ SECM-based nanogap voltammetry to quantitatively demonstrate that Co(phen)32+ is adsorbed on the pristine eC surface to electrostatically self-inhibit outer-sphere ET of nonadsorbed Co(phen)33+ and Co(phen)32+. Strong electrostatic repulsion among Co(phen)32+ adsorbates is also demonstrated by SECM-based nanogap voltammetry and cyclic voltammetry. Quantitatively, self-inhibitory ET is characterized by a linear decrease in the standard rate constant of Co(phen)32+ oxidation with a higher surface concentration of Co(phen)32+ at the formal potential. This unique relationship is consistent not with the Frumkin model of double layer effects, but with the Amatore model of partially blocked electrodes as extended for self-inhibitory ET. Significantly, the complicated coupling of electron transfer and surface adsorption is resolved by combining nanoscale and macroscale voltammetric methods.

    Electrodes based on conducting carbon materials have been widely used for electrochemistry owing to various advantages including low cost, wide potential window, and electrocatalytic activity. (1â3) Nevertheless, our understanding of heterogeneous electron-transfer (ET) mechanisms at carbon electrodes has been limited by the difficulty in measuring their intrinsic electrochemical reactivity (4,5) for comparison with fundamental electrochemical theories such as Marcus theory of adiabatic outer-sphere ET (6) and Frumkin theory of double layer effects. (7) Problematically, the reactivity of an adsorbing carbon surface is quickly compromised by adventitious surface contamination with hydrophobic blocking molecules in air (8,9) and even in ultrapure water. (10,11) It has been highly challenging to obtain and maintain clean surfaces of traditional carbon materials as well as emerging carbon nanomaterials. (8â13) Moreover, an sp2-carbon surface is inductively polarized to adsorb polar redox-active molecules, (14) which complicates the ET mechanism. (15,16) By contrast, the reactivity of a relatively clean carbon surface can be immeasurably high to yield only diffusion-limited ET rates as minimum estimates. (17)
    Herein, we report new mechanistic insights into the interplay between the heterogeneous ET and adsorption of a redox couple at the pristine surface of electron-beam deposited carbon (eC). (18) This amorphous form of carbon contains both sp3 and sp2 hybridization as a 30/70 mix to yield a conducting surface. (19) Advantageously, eC forms extremely flat (root-mean-square roughness of 0.1â0.5 nm) thin films on Au or other metals, and has been used for fabrication in molecular electronics. (20â23) For electrochemical applications, we deposit Au and eC in high vacuum (<6 Ã 10â6 Torr), not only to prepare the eC surface, (19â21) but also to protect the clean surface with a washable KCl layer without breaking vacuum. (18) Subsequently, the protective layer is dissolved in an aqueous KCl solution of a redox-active molecule to expose the clean eC surface, which was never exposed to air, thus avoiding airborne contamination. Moreover, the freshly exposed eC surface is protected from organic contamination in the electrolyte solution, which is prepared from ultrapure water containing an extremely low level of organic impurities, i.e., <1 ppb of total organic carbon. (10)
    Specifically, we reveal that tris(1,10-phenanthroline)cobalt(II), Co(phen)32+, is adsorbed on the pristine eC surface to electrostatically self-inhibit the outer-sphere ET of the Co(phen)33+/2+ couple. Significantly, Co(III)/Co(II)-complex redox couples including the Co(phen)33+/2+ couple have been widely used as promising outer-sphere redox mediators for dye-sensitized solar cells (24) and redox flow batteries (25) based on carbon electrodes. In this work, we prove experimentally that the Co(phen)33+/2+ couple mediates outer-sphere ET at the outer Helmholtz plane (OHP) (26,27) near the eC surface:(1)However, we also find that this reaction is inhibited by Co(phen)32+ adsorbed on the inner Helmholtz plane (26,27) (IHP):(2)Quantitatively, the self-inhibitory effect is characterized by a linear decrease in the standard rate constant of Co(phen)32+ oxidation with a higher surface concentration of Co(phen)32+ at the formal potential. The concentration-dependent ET kinetics is apparently inconsistent with the Marcus theory (6) and cannot be explained by the Frumkin model of double layer effects. (7) We account for the anomalous ET kinetics by extending the Amatore model of partially blocked electrodes. (28â30)
    Experimentally, we employ nanogap voltammetry based on scanning electrochemical microscopy (SECM) and cyclic voltammetry (CV) to quantitatively determine the heterogeneous ET kinetics and adsorption isotherm of Co(phen)32+ at the pristine eC surface. Previously, the cleanliness of the KCl-protected eC surface in solution was ensured by symmetry of SECM-based nanogap voltammograms using reversible redox couples. (18) In the current study, high mass-transport conditions of SECM-based nanogap voltammetry enable us to kinetically resolve slow oxidation and fast adsorption of Co(phen)32+ at the OHP and IHP, respectively, near the eC surface. The ButlerâVolmer ET kinetics and Frumkin adsorption isotherm are employed for finite element analysis of SECM-based voltammograms. (31) The ButlerâVolmer ET kinetics and a kinetic version compliant with Frumkin adsorption isotherm are implemented in a custom advanced CV simulator (16,32,33) for numerical analysis of CVs as obtained at faster scan rates to enhance an adsorption effect. Importantly, a unique set of parameter values can be obtained from good fits of both nanogap voltammograms and CVs of various concentrations of Co(phen)32+ and KCl. Moreover, both techniques demonstrate the lack of the adsorption of Co(phen)33+, which excludes the inner-sphere ET of the adsorbed Co(phen)33+/2+ couple and the self-exchange ET between adsorbed and nonadsorbed couples. (17,34)

    Electrochemical Measurements

    A home-built SECM instrument with an isothermal chamber (35) was used as reported elsewhere. (10) Glass-sealed Pt tips with inner and outer diameters of â¼1 and â¼2 Î¼m were fabricated for SECM by laser-assisted pulling, heat annealing, and focused-ion-beam milling. (42) Both SECM and CV measurements were carried out by using a bipotentiostat (CHI 802D, CH Instruments, Austin, TX), which was modified to eliminate possible causes of tip damage. (17,41,43)
    All eC electrodes employed in this study were overlaid with a protective KCl film to unambiguously demonstrate the self-inhibitory ET of the Co(phen)33+/2+ couple at the pristine eC surface without adventitious contamination, which results in slower ET kinetics. (18) Briefly, eC is an amorphous, 30/70 mix of sp3- and sp2-hybridized carbon with sufficient conductivity to result in negligible ohmic potential losses through a 10 nm thick layer on Au as demonstrated in electronics applications. (20â23) Electron beam deposition of an â¼1 Î¼m thick film of KCl following eC deposition without breaking vacuum at below 6 Ã 10â6 Torr protects the eC surface from impurity adsorption until the KCl layer is dissolved in solution. Exposed eC surfaces were characterized by atomic force microscopy, scanning electron microscopy (SEM), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy, and Auger spectroscopy. (18)

    Data Analysis

    SECM-based nanogap voltammograms and CVs were fitted with those simulated using COMSOL Multiphysics 5.3a (COMSOL, Burlington, MA) and the CV simulator. (16,32,33) Fits were first evaluated visually by comparing experimental and simulated voltammograms. The fits were then improved based on least-squares deviation optimization through using the NelderâMead method present in the Optimization module of COMSOL Multiphysics (31,44,45) or a modified NelderâMead method developed for the custom CV simulator. (46) The least-squares method of COMSOL employed 100â200 iterations to find the best set of parameters from wide ranges of parameter values. The CV simulator fitted simultaneously several (typically three in this work) CVs obtained at different scan rates to find the best single set of parameters.

    Nanogap Voltammetry of the Co(phen)33+/2+ Couple

    We investigated the outer-sphere ET of the Co(phen)33+/2+ couple (eq 1) and the adsorption of Co(phen)32+ (eq 2) on KCl-protected eC electrodes by SECM-based nanogap voltammetry. (47,48) With this method, an â¼1 Î¼m diameter Pt ultramicroelectrode tip was positioned at a nanometer distance from the pristine eC surface to oxidize Co(phen)32+ at the eC surface in the substrate generation/tip collection (SG/TC) mode or at the tip in the feedback mode (Figure 1, parts A and B, respectively). In the SG/TC mode, Co(phen)33+ was voltammetrically generated at the eC surface and amperometrically detected at the tip to yield a nanogap voltammogram based on the tip current against the cycled eC potential. In the feedback mode, tip-generated Co(phen)33+ was voltammetrically reduced at the eC surface to regenerate Co(phen)32+, which was detected at the tip to yield another branch of voltammograms.

    We used a relatively low concentration of 0.1 mM Co(phen)32+ to observe its adsorption on the eC surface as a voltammetric hysteresis in the SG/TC mode. Specifically, a nanogap voltammogram in the SG/TC mode yielded a higher tip current during the forward anodic scan of the eC potential (red solid line in Figure 2A) than the reverse scan (red dashed line), which provides evidence for the adsorption of Co(phen)32+ on the eC surface. During the forward scan, Co(phen)32+ was voltammetrically oxidized to Co(phen)33+ at the eC surface and, subsequently, depleted near the eC surface to desorb Co(phen)32+, which was also oxidized to Co(phen)33+ at the tip to further increase the tip current (Figure 1A). Since Co(phen)32+ was completely desorbed from the eC surface during the forward scan, no Co(phen)32+ was desorbed during the reverse scan, thereby yielding a lower tip current. The lower tip current during the reverse scan also indicates that Co(phen)33+ was not adsorbed on the eC surface. (31)
    A nanogap voltammogram in the feedback mode (Figure 2B) was lower and broader than that in the SG/TC mode (Figure 2A) to yield no plateau current. We attribute the lower and broader nanogap voltammogram in the feedback mode to an electrostatic inhibitory effect from Co(phen)32+ adsorbed on the eC surface. A strong inhibitor effect is expected in the feedback mode, where Co(phen)33+ with higher positive charges must access to the positively charged eC surface covered with Co(phen)32+. Moreover, the coverage of the eC surface with Co(phen)32+ is higher in the feedback mode, where Co(phen)32+ is abundant near the eC surface biased at negative potentials with respect to the formal potential to regenerate Co(phen)32+ from Co(phen)33+ generated at the tip (Figure 1B). Lower and broader nanogap voltammograms were previously observed in the feedback mode based on the reduction of ferrocenium ions at contaminated surfaces of graphene (11) and highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG). (10) The eC surface, however, was not contaminated, because of the protective KCl layer. In fact, KCl-protected eC electrodes yielded symmetric pairs of nanogap voltammograms for (ferrocenylmethyl)trimethylammonium and Ru(NH3)63+ in SG/TC and feedback modes. (18)
    It should be noted that a lower tip current in the feedback mode implies that Co(phen)33+ was not adsorbed on the eC surface. The adsorption of the product on the substrate surface yields a higher transient current during the reverse scan in the feedback mode. (31) We speculate that Co(phen)33+ with a higher charge is more strongly hydrated in the electrolyte solution and, subsequently, is not significantly adsorbed on the eC surface. Importantly, the lack of adsorption of Co(phen)33+ on the eC surface indicates that adsorbed Co(phen)32+ cannot be oxidized through the inner-sphere pathway at the IHP (17,34)(4)or a heterogeneous self-exchange reaction between adsorbed and nonadsorbed redox couples (34)(5)Accordingly, the Co(phen)33+/2+ couple must be an outer-sphere one (eq 1).

    CV of Co(phen)32+ Adsorption

    We employed CV to quantitatively validate adsorption parameters of Îs, Î², and gâ² (Table 1) determined from the hysteresis of nanogap voltammograms in the SG/TC mode. A current response based on the oxidation of Co(phen)32+ desorbed from the eC surface increases at a faster scan rate as shown by background-subtracted CVs depicted for 0.1, 1, and 10 V/s (solid lines in Figure 5). Background-subtracted CVs from 0.1 to 10 V/s fit well with quasi-reversible CVs simulated without the adsorption of Co(phen)32+ and Co(phen)33+ (Figure S7).

    We measured nanogap voltammograms of different concentrations of Co(phen)32+ in the SG/TC mode to confirm the self-inhibitory effect of Co(phen)32 adsorbates on Co(phen)32+ oxidation, which became slower as the bulk concentration of Co(phen)32+ increased.     Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Here, we combined SECM-based nanogap voltammetry and CV to reveal the self-inhibition of the outer-sphere Co(phen)33+/2+ couple by the adsorption of Co(phen)32+ on the pristine eC surface. The electrostatic inhibition caused slower reduction of Co(phen)33+ than the oxidation of Co(phen)32+. Importantly, the resultant asymmetric pairs of nanogap voltammograms were unambiguously attributed to the self-inhibitory effect by using the pristine eC surface, whereas the contamination of carbon surfaces caused asymmetry of nanogap voltammograms. (10,11) Strong electrostatic repulsion among Co(phen)32+ adsorbates was also observed as a highly negative gâ² value of â4.6 in the Frumkin isotherm. This unfavorable interaction was overcome by an attractive interaction between Co(phen)32+ and the eC surface to achieve high coverage of the eC surface with Co(phen)32+. Without the attractive interaction, Co(phen)32+ can approach the OHP only among voids of Co(phen)32+ adsorbates with an effective radius of 1.25 nm, which is larger than an actual radius of 0.65 nm. (56) The electrostatic blocking effect was implemented into the Amatore model (28â30) to explain linear relationships between k0 and Î¸A at the formal potential, which exemplifies that k0t is underestimated, not overestimated, (54,55) without considering the self-inhibitor effect of surface adsorption.
    This work demonstrates the power of transient SECM measurement to investigate complex electrode reactions coupled with surface processes as recently reported for microscale SECM of electrodeposition and electrodissolution. (31) The transient approach contrasts to the steady-state mode of SECM, which has been dominantly used to investigate ET reactions without perturbation by surface processes such as adsorption and desorption. (58) In this work, we employed SECM-based nanogap voltammetry to resolve kinetically the interplay between the heterogeneous ET and adsorption of the Co(phen)33+/2+ couple as a promising redox mediator for carbon-based electrochemical energy devices. (24,25) Substantial hysteresis based on Co(phen)32+ adsorption was observed by nanogap voltammetry despite a slow scan rate using low Co(phen)32+ concentrations in contrast to previous studies based on this technique. (10,11,17,18,36) An inhibitory effect was not obvious in CV of Co(phen)32+ even at fast scan rates, which may be attributed to difficulties in precise background subtraction at the low-capacitive eC surface interfering in the quantitative analysis of the voltammograms. SECM-based nanogap voltammetry is free from background and will be useful to quantitatively study coupled ET and surface steps under high mass-transport conditions when fast scan rates are employed.
