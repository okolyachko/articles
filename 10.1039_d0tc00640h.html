Neutral pH aqueous redox flow batteries using an anthraquinone-ferrocyanide redox couple

Abstract

Anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid (2,7-AQDS) and ferrocyanide including potassium and sodium salts are used as a redox couple for neutral aqueous redox flow batteries (ARFBs). In 1 M potassium chloride (KCl) electrolyte of neutral pH, the electron transfer rate and redox reactivity of 2,7-AQDS are better than those in acidic electrolyte. Furthermore, ferrocyanide is attacked by the hydroxyl group within the alkaline electrolyte, however this does not happen in KCl because there are no hydroxyl groups in KCl. The low solubility of 2,7-AQDS is enhanced in 1 M KCl using an ethyleneglycol (EG) additive and the solubility of the ferrocyanide also increases when using the mixed cations of potassium and sodium. This benefit arises because of the fact that the EG additive forms hydrogen bonds with water within the electrolyte and 2,7-AQDS, while ferrocyanide mixed with different cations increases the solubility due to ion pair formation. Based on that, the ARFB using 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide shows high coulombic efficiency (98% at 40 mA cm−2) and capacity (17.8 A h L−1) with a low capacity decay rate (>99% until 100 cycle).

1. Introduction

Redox flow batteries (RFBs) are an example of energy storage systems (ESSs) that store renewable energy and produce electrical energy as needed.1–8 The RFB consists of two tanks of active species within electrolyte, two electrodes, and a membrane placed in between the two tanks.9,10 In terms of the performance of the RFB, the charge/discharge capacity is determined by the volume of the electrolytes, and the power density is dictated by the size of the RFB stack.11,12 Intensive research has been done thus far toward the viability of commercial application and usage. With much trial and error, the vanadium redox flow battery (VRFB) has been considered as a solution for ESSs because vanadium ions – which are the active species of the VRFB – have four different oxidation states.13–18 This means that differently oxidized vanadium ions can be used as both the anodic and cathodic active species, while the utilization of the same vanadium ions induces less cross-over, which is a leading cause of coulombic efficiency (CE) decay.19–24 However, since vanadium is a rare metal and very expensive, its viability as the main active species for the RFB is questionable and there have been many attempts to replace it.25–29

As an alternative, researchers have been considering the organic active material-based redox flow battery. When organic materials are utilized as the active species for the RFB, there are notable benefits, such as a cheaper cost when compared to metal competitors, eco-friendly properties, and increases in the solubility and redox potential by changing the functional group of the individual organic components.30–37

Two very different types of RFBs are possible when considering organic materials as the active species: non-aqueous electrolyte-based and aqueous electrolyte-based.38–41 Due to the requirements of having high current density and secure operational conditions, the aqueous electrolyte can be considered a better option.42,43 When the aqueous electrolyte is utilized, the pH of electrolytes from strong acids – such as sulfuric acid (H2SO4) – to strong bases – such as potassium hydroxide or sodium hydroxide (KOH or NaOH) – is important to determine the performance of the RFB.44–46 For example, Narayanan et al. suggested a quinone-based aqueous organic redox flow battery (AORFB) where the quinone redox couple was dissolved in H2SO4.44 Its open circuit voltage (OCV) was 0.76 V and CE was 99%. Furthermore, Aziz et al. introduced a combination of 2,6-dihydroxyanthraquinone and ferrocyanide including potassium hydroxide where the redox couple was dissolved in KOH.45 This AORFB exhibited a 1.20 V OCV and a CE of 99%. Although the strong acid and base electrolytes are very effective for improving the performance of the AORFB, they are usually toxic and can produce undesirable side reactions.46,47 For example, Liu et al. found that when KOH was used as the electrolyte, ferrocyanide could be attacked by the –OH group and form toxic materials like KCN, which leads to a severe capacity decay.48

Therefore, in this study, we suggest an ARFB using 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide with potassium and sodium salts dissolved in a neutral electrolyte (KCl). Here, 2,7-AQDS is used as the negative active species because its KCl solubility is the highest of all anthraquinone derivatives (∼0.3 M). However, this solubility is still low for accomplishing the high energy density needed for the ARFB, so ethylene glycol (EG) is added to increase the 2,7-AQDS solubility in KCl. Ferrocyanide is used as the positive active species but its KCl solubility is low (∼0.4 M). To increase its solubility from 0.4 to 1.1 M, an additive consisting of a mixture of potassium and sodium is added. The schematic of the cell and the mechanism of the redox reactions for 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide are shown in Scheme 1. As the main originality of this manuscript, different from other related results, we succeed in increasing the solubility of 2,7-AQDS in the neutral supporting electrolyte (KCl) by using an EG additive. Based on that, the ARFB using 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide shows high coulombic efficiency (98% at 40 mA cm−2) and capacity (17.8 A h L−1) with a low capacity decay rate (>99% until 100 cycles).

2. Experimental

2.1 Materials

Anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid disodium salt was purchased from Santa Cruz Biotechnology Inc., potassium ferrocyanide, sodium ferrocyanide, and amberlyst proton ion exchange resin were purchased from Alfa Aesar, and potassium chloride and ethylene glycol were purchased from Samchun Chemical. Anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid (2,7-AQDS) was made by using amberlyst resin with the anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid disodium salt solution.49

2.2 Solubility measurement

To evaluate the effects of EG and the mixed cations consisting of potassium and sodium salts on the solubility of 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide in KCl, UV-vis spectroscopy was utilized. Initially, to examine the effect of the EG additive on the solubility of 2,7-AQDS, two different samples were prepared: (i) a sample consisting of 5 mL of 1 M KCl and (ii) a sample consisting of 2.5 mL of 1 M KCl and 2.5 mL of 1 M EG. The excessive amount of 2,7-AQDS powder was dissolved in both solutions and stirred overnight. After stirring, 400 μL was collected from the top layer and diluted with DI water until the solution was detected by UV-vis spectroscopy. To examine the mixed cation effect on the solubility of ferrocyanide, three different samples were prepared: (i) a sample consisting of pure potassium ferrocyanide, (ii) a sample consisting of pure sodium ferrocyanide, and (iii) a sample consisting of potassium and sodium ferrocyanide in a one to one ratio in 5 mL of 1 M KCl. The equivalent amount of each ferrocyanide was dissolved in 1 M KCl and the next steps were the same as the procedure for 2,7-AQDS.

2.3 Electrochemical characterization

For evaluating the electrochemical performance of 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide, various half-cell tests were performed by using a computer connected potentiostat (SP-240, BioLogic). The reference and counter electrodes were Ag/AgCl (soaked in 3.0 M NaCl) and Pt wire, respectively, while a glassy carbon electrode with an active area of 0.1936 cm2 was used as the working electrode. The cyclic voltammetry (CV) tests were performed under the conditions of 0.01 M 2,7-AQDS and 0.005 M potassium ferrocyanide dissolved in 1 M KCl to measure OCV and other electrochemical properties. In addition, CV tests for 0.01 M 2,7-AQDS dissolved in 1 M H2SO4 solution were also performed to compare the electrochemical performance in different supporting electrolytes. Lastly, CV tests for the solution with the EG additive and the solution with different cations were also implemented. Regarding the scan rate, 100 mV s−1 was considered.

ARFB full cell tests were performed by using charge–discharge equipment (Wonatech, WBCS3000). Two different concentration combinations of active species were used for the tests to evaluate the effect of the concentration of active species on the performance of the ARFB. First, the catholyte was prepared by dissolving 0.3 M potassium ferrocyanide into 20 mL of 1 M KCl solution, while the anolyte was prepared by dissolving 0.1 M 2,7-AQDS into 20 mL of 1 M KCl solution. Second, the catholyte was prepared by dissolving 0.6 M potassium ferrocyanide/0.6 M sodium ferrocyanide into 20 mL of 1 M KCl solution, while the anolyte was prepared by dissolving 0.4 M 2,7-AQDS into the solution consisting of 10 mL of 1 M KCl solution and 10 mL of 1 M EG. The concentration conditions of the anolyte and the catholyte adopted for this study are summarized in Table 1. Carbon felt (by Toyobo) was used for both electrodes and Nafion 212 membrane was used as a separator. The active area was 4 cm2. The cut-off voltage was 0.2–1.6 V and a current density of 40 mA cm−2 was used.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Benefits of 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide in KCl electrolyte

Although the cell voltage of the 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide redox couple in 1 M KCl solution is 0.76 V (Fig. 1a), there are three main benefits of using a neutral supporting electrolyte like KCl for the ARFB. First, a pH neutral solution is eco-friendlier than alkaline and acidic solutions. Second, the electrochemical performance of 2,7-AQDS in KCl solution is better than that in H2SO4 solution. According to the electrochemical activity and reversibility measurements of 2,7-AQDS, the anodic peak current of 2,7-AQDS in KCl solution was 0.61 mA and the peak potential separation was 0.08 V, which were better than those observed in H2SO4 solution (0.31 mA and 0.45 V, respectively) (Fig. 1b). This benefit is well preserved when the EG additive, which is added to enhance the solubility of 2,7-AQDS, is further included (Fig. 1c). Third, ferrocyanide may produce toxic byproducts like KCN in an alkaline (KOH) solution,48 and the toxic byproducts can be prevented by replacing KOH with KCl. In the KCl solution, ferrocyanide and ferricyanide that is the oxidized form of ferrocyanide are alternatingly exchanged with high reversibility without producing toxic products like KCN (Fig. 2a). This was proven by 13C NMR measurements of ferrocyanide dissolved in KCl (Fig. 2b and c). According to Fig. 2b and c, in ferrocyanide dissolved in KCl, toxic byproducts were not observed.

Fig. 2 (a) Mechanisms of the undesirable side reaction of ferrocyanide taking place in KOH solution and the redox reaction between ferrocyanide and ferricyanide in KCl solution, and the C-NMR data of ferrocyanide dissolved in 1 M KCl solution (b) before and (c) after a few days.

3.2 Solubility enhancement of 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide in the KCl solution

Although there are advantages of using KCl electrolyte, the solubility of 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide in KCl is still low. To increase the solubility of 2,7-AQDS, EG is utilized as an additive. When EG was adopted, the solubility of 2,7-AQDS increased from 0.3 to 0.8 M without having any undesirable impact on its electrochemical performance (Fig. 3a and b). The mechanism explaining the effect of EG on the solubility of 2,7-AQDS is introduced in Fig. 3c. As shown in Fig. 3c, the hydroxyl (–OH) groups of the EG additive and the polar CO groups of 2,7-AQDS interact to form hydrogen bonds, and the non-polar side groups of the EG additive and 2,7-AQDS interact to form nonpolar–nonpolar bonds. In addition, further hydrogen bonding occurs between the hydroxyl group of the EG additive and the aqueous solution.34 Therefore, the EG additive plays a critical role in forming strong interaction between the organic active species and aqueous supporting electrolyte, which leads to better solubility of 2,7-AQDS in KCl.
Fig. 3 (a) UV-vis results of 2,7-AQDS dissolved in 1 M KCl solution with and without the EG additive, (b) CV graphs of 2,7-AQDS dissolved in 1 M KCl solution with and without the EG additive, (c) the scheme showing the role of EG increasing the solubility of 2,7-AQDS in aqueous solution, and (d) UV-vis results of ferrocyanide with different cation salts dissolved in 1 M KCl solution to determine the maximum solubility.

Regarding the solubility of ferrocyanide, when a modified ferrocyanide containing potassium and sodium salts was synthesized, its solubility reached ∼1.1 M in 1 M KCl solution and this is far greater than that of ferrocyanide containing just potassium salt (∼0.4 M) (Fig. 3d).

3.3 Evaluation of performance of ARFBs using 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide

To evaluate the effect of the concentration of active species on the performance of the ARFB, the performances of ARFBs using two different concentrations of active species were measured (Fig. 4). In terms of a low concentration case that is the best concentration combination before this study, the ARFB using 0.3 M potassium ferrocyanide and 0.1 M 2,7-AQDS was measured without additive at room temperature. On the other hand, in terms of a high concentration case that is the newly developed concentration combination by this study, the ARFB using 1.2 M ferrocyanide containing the sodium and potassium salts and 0.4 M 2,7-AQDS was prepared. In this ARFB, 1 M EG additive was further included to enhance the solubility of 2,7-AQDS in KCl and to prevent the precipitation of 2,7-AQDS during cycling. In addition, the ARFB was operated at 40 °C to completely remove the possibility of the precipitation of 2,7-AQDS during cycling. These two additional factors, the use of an additive and the increase in operating temperature, have a positive impact on the energy efficiency (EE) of the ARFB by facilitating the redox reactivity of 2,7-AQDS.50
Fig. 4 (a) Charge–discharge curve measured at the 10th cycle, (b) coulombic efficiency, (c) energy efficiency, and (d) discharge capacity graphs of two different ARFBs. The first ARFB used 0.3 M potassium ferrocyanide and 0.1 M 2,7-AQDS without additive at room temperature, while the second ARFB used 1.2 M ferrocyanide containing the sodium and potassium salts of 1:1 ratio and 0.4 M 2,7-AQDS. In the second ARFB, 10 mL of 1 M EG additive was additionally included for enhancing the solubility of 2,7-AQDS in KCl solution.

According to the results, the charging and discharging time of the ARFB using a high concentration redox couple including 0.4 M 2,7-AQDS was almost four times longer than that of the ARFB using a low concentration redox couple including 0.1 M 2,7-AQDS (Fig. 4a). This means that as the concentration of active species increases, the inner resistance remains almost unchanged. More specifically, regarding efficiency, the CEs of both ARFBs were almost the same (∼98%), while the EE of the ARFB using a high concentration redox couple (81%) was better than that of the ARFB using a low concentration redox couple (77%) (Fig. 4b and c). This result is due to the high operating temperature of the ARFB using a high concentration redox couple that can enhance the redox reactivity and lower the resistance. In addition, the EG additive prompts the flow of the 2,7-AQDS solution. Based on that, the discharging capacity of the ARFB using a high concentration redox couple reached 17.8 A h L−1 with a state of charge (SOC) of 83% (its theoretical capacity is 21.4 A h L−1), while that of the ARFB using a low concentration redox couple was just 4.4 A h L−1 with a SOC of 82% (its theoretical capacity is 5.36 A h L−1).

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, for the first time, 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide were used with KCl electrolyte for ARFB operation. By the utilization of the neutral KCl electrolyte, the electrochemical performance of 2,7-AQDS, and the redox reactivity and stability of ferrocyanide were considerably improved. In addition, the low performance of ARFBs that was attributed to the low solubilities of 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide was enhanced by utilizing an EG additive for 2,7-AQDS and a mixture consisting of two different cations for ferrocyanide. Due to these reasons, the performance of the ARFB using 2,7-AQDS and ferrocyanide was improved. In particular, the discharge capacity and the SOC of the ARFB were as high as 17.8 A h L−1 and 83%, respectively, with a high capacity retention (>99% until 100 cycle). In terms of efficiency, CE and EE of the ARFB were excellent at 98% and 81% at 40 mA cm−2.
