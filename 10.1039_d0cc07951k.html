New phenazine based anolyte material for high voltage organic redox flow batteries†

Abstract

Herein, we report the synthesis and investigation of a novel phenazine derivative M1 with oligomeric ethylene glycol ether substituents as a promising anolyte material for non-aqueous organic redox flow batteries (RFBs). The designed material undergoes a reversible and stable reduction at −1.72 V VS. Ag/AgNO3 and demonstrates excellent (>2.5 M) solubility in MeCN. A non-aqueous organic redox flow battery assembled using the novel phenazine derivative as an anolyte and a substituted triarylamine derivative as a catholyte exhibited high specific capacity (∼93% from the theoretical value), >95% Coulombic efficiency, 65% utilization of active materials and good charge–discharge cycling stability.

The continuously growing demand for renewable energy sources dictates new trends in the development of safe, efficient, stable, and high-capacity energy storage systems.1 Redox flow batteries (RFBs) represent the most promising technology for stationary energy storage due to their unique design: the power (electrochemical cell) and capacity (tanks with electrolytes) parts of RFBs are separated in space, which enables facile upscaling of such systems and tailoring them to match specific customer demands.2

RFBs have seen on-going development since the late 1970s with almost exclusive attention being paid to inorganic aqueous systems such as all-vanadium or Fe–Cr.3,4 Although significant progress has been made in this field, the limited electrochemical stability window of aqueous electrolytes (≈1.5 V), low active material concentration and safety issues limit the ubiquitous application of inorganic RFBs.5

Replacing the toxic and precious inorganic materials by aqueous solutions of environment-friendly organic redox-active molecules may decrease the system cost and solve the safety issues, whereas the problem of low volumetric capacity and specific power remains unsolved.6 To address this challenge, non-aqueous electrolytes based on organic solvents with a wide electrochemical stability window (>4 V) need to be employed.7

Synthetic versatility of organic redox-active molecules enables facile modification of all their properties including the solubility. For example, water-soluble methyl viologen represents a classical anolyte for aqueous FRBs.8,9 However, replacing methyl substituents by the oligomeric ethylene glycol ether chains provided complete miscibility with acetonitrile and enabled fabrication of non-aqueous RFBs with 70% capacity retention after 440 charge/discharge cycles.10

Recently, promising anolyte materials for aqueous RFBs were designed using phenazine derivatives bearing sulfonic acid,11 carboxylic,11 ammonium,12 and nitroxyl13 groups (Table 1). These compounds demonstrated high solubilities12 and a two-electron electrochemical reduction process11,12 at the potentials of −1.1 to 0.56 V vs. Ag/AgCl. Importantly, the reduction potential of phenazine in non-aqueous systems was found to be significantly lower: −1.5 V vs. Ag/AgNO3.14 Therefore, phenazine derivatives can be considered as highly promising anolyte materials for high-voltage and high energy density non-aqueous RFBs, which, to our surprise, have not been investigated so far to the best of our knowledge.

While pursuing this challenge, we designed in this work the phenazine molecule M1 decorated with the oligomeric ethylene glycol ether substituents to boost its solubility in polar organic solvents. The synthesis of M1 was accomplished in two simple steps as shown in Scheme 1.


Scheme 1 Synthesis of the ethylene glycol substituted phenazine compound M1.

First, the condensation of 2,5-dihydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone and o-phenylenediamine produced phenazine-2,3-diol (1) according to the previously reported procedure.15 Then, M1 was obtained by treatment of (1) with 2-(2-methoxyethoxy)ethyl 4-methylbenzenesulfonate in the presence of K2CO3 in anhydrous N,N-dimethylformamide (see detailed description of the Experimental methods in the ESI†).

As expected, the introduction of two ethylene glycol ether substituents in the phenazine core affected considerably the solubility of the resulting compound. M1 showed the solubility of 2.5 M in pure acetonitrile and 1.0 M in a 5.0 M TBABF4 (tetrabutylammonium tetrafluoroborate) supporting electrolyte, which corresponds to the theoretical capacity of at least 26.8 Ah L−1 (Table 1). The optimization of the supporting electrolyte may lead to considerable increase of M1 solubility in salt-containing solutions and the capacity growth up to the theoretical value of 67 Ah L−1.

The thermal stability of M1 was studied by thermal gravimetry analysis (Fig. S5, ESI†). Significant (60%) mass loss associated with the thermal decomposition of the compound is observed in the range from 200 °C to 310 °C. The point of irreversible decomposition (5% mass loss) is reached at 210 °C, which indicates relatively high thermal stability of M1.

While water-soluble phenazine derivatives demonstrate simultaneous two-electron reduction,12 the parent phenazine shows two separate single-electron peaks on the cycling voltammograms (CV) recorded in organic electrolytes,14 which we also reconfirmed experimentally (Fig. S6a, ESI†). Furthermore, we observed a fully symmetrical peak corresponding to the first electron transfer and an unsymmetrical second peak, indicating the poor reversibility of the second-electron transfer process. However, continuous cycling of the phenazine within a narrower potential range (from −1.3 to −1.8 V, Fig. S6c, ESI†) revealed high electrochemical stability of the single-electron reduction process.

The cyclic voltammograms of M1 were obtained for 3 mM solutions of this substance in MeCN with 0.1M TBABF4 as a supporting electrolyte. A stable quasi-reversible process at E1/2 = −1.72 V vs. Ag/AgNO3 with the reduction and oxidation peak splitting of 0.24 V was observed (Fig. 1a). Thus, the introduction of the electron-donor ethylene glycol ether chains in M1 resulted in the decrease of the reduction potential by 0.2 V as compared to the parent phenazine (Fig. S6, ESI†).

To estimate the diffusion coefficients of M1, CV measurements were performed at various scan rates (Fig. S7a, ESI†). A linear correlation of the peak currents and the square root of the scan rates was revealed for both the reduction and re-oxidation processes (Fig. S7b, ESI†). The diffusion coefficients were determined using Randles–Sevcik analysis.17–19 Thus, we obtained the value of 4.26 × 10−6 cm2 s−1 for the reduction and 5.30 × 10−6 cm2 s−1 for the re-oxidation processes, which are comparable with the diffusion coefficients reported for some commonly used organic redox-active compounds.20–22 Nicolson analysis (Table S2, ESI†) yields electro-chemical rate constants of 2.84 × 10−3 cm s−1 for the reduction of M1.

To evaluate the performance of M1 as an anolyte in redox flow batteries, we applied a highly soluble triarylamine derivative M2 as a catholyte component (Fig. 1b and Fig. S4, ESI†).23 Before running the RFB experiments, multiple cycling voltammetry tests were performed for the investigation of the electrochemical stability and mutual compatibility of M1 and M2 redox species. The oxidation of M2 and reduction of M1 are separated by ∼2.3 V, which is the feasible potential of the redox flow battery based on this redox couple (Fig. 1c). The CV curves overlap for the first 10 scans, whereas a small decrease in the intensity of an M1 peak is observed after the 20th cycle. Such behavior might suggest a partial degradation of the anolyte material. Still, the revealed electrochemical stability of the M1/M2 redox couple can be considered as sufficient for performing further RFB tests.

The performance of M1 was investigated in the symmetric flow cell (Fig. 2a and Fig. S1, ESI†) with the 10 mM concentrations of the active species and 0.1 M of TBABF4 in anhydrous MeCN. The symmetric electrolytes were used to discriminate the capacity loss due to the crossover effect. The cell was charged at 2 mA current until the upper cutoff voltage (2.7 V) and then was discharged at the same current. After reaching the lower cutoff voltage (1.9 V), the potential was held for 2000 seconds to achieve deeper discharge of the cell. Fig. 2b shows the voltage and current vs. time profiles for the first and tenth cycles. Well-defined plateaus are observed at ∼2.5 V and ∼2.0 V for the charge and discharge steps, respectively. A considerable potential drop can be attributed to high resistance of the used graphite-PTFE (20/80) current collectors and Neosepta AHA membrane (cell resistance ∼180 Ω, Neosepta AHA membrane resistance ∼110 Ω, Fig. S8, ESI†). Fig. 2c shows the evolution of the charge and discharge capacities and Coulombic efficiency of the battery upon charge–discharge cycling. The RFB cell showed Coulombic efficiency of ∼80% at the first cycle, which was improved to >95% and remained constant after the 20th cycle. The initial charge capacity was found to be 250 mA L−1, which is close to the theoretical value of 268 mA h L−1, so corresponds to 93% utilization of the active materials. The flow cell demonstrated noticeable capacity loss to ∼178 mA h L−1 during the first 20 cycles. However, the charge and discharge capacities stabilized at ∼175 mA h L −1 (65% from maximum value) by the 20-th cycle and no further capacity decay was observed.


Fig. 2 (a) Schematic layout of the used RFB cell with M1 and M2 used as active materials, (b) cell voltage and current versus time for first and tenth cycles and (c) evolution of the Coulombic efficiency, charge and discharge capacities upon charge–discharge RFB cycling.

To summarize, we designed and investigated a novel highly soluble phenazine derivative M1 as a promising anolyte for RFBs. The designed material exhibited reversible and stable one-electron reduction at −1.72 V vs. an Ag/AgNO3 electrode, relatively high diffusion coefficient of 2.25 × 10−6 cm2 s−1 and excellent (>2.5 M) solubility in acetonitrile, thus enabling a theoretically feasible anolyte capacity of ∼67 Ah L−1. A non-aqueous organic redox flow battery based on M1 anolyte and appropriate triarylamine-based catholyte M2 exhibited the potential of 2.3 V, high capacities, >95% coulombic efficiency and good charge–discharge cycling stability after the initial 20 cycles. The obtained results feature highly soluble phenazine derivatives with low reduction potentials as a highly promising family of anolyte materials for high-voltage and high energy density organic redox flow batteries.
