    Abstract

    Redoxmers are organic molecules that carry electric charge in flow batteries. In many instances, they consist of heteroaromatic moieties modified with appended groups to prevent stacking of the planar cores and increase solubility in liquid electrolytes. This higher solubility is desired as it potentially allows achieving greater energy density in the battery. However, the present synthetic strategies often yield bulky molecules with low molarity even when they are neat and still lower molarity in liquid solutions. Fortunately, there are exceptions to this rule. Here, we examine one well-studied redoxmer, 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole, which has solubility ∼5.7 M in acetonitrile at 25 °C. We show computationally and prove experimentally that the competition between two packing motifs, face-to-face π-stacking and random N–H bond piling, introduces frustration that confounds nucleation in crowded solutions. Our findings and examples from related systems suggest a complementary strategy for the molecular design of redoxmers for high energy density redox flow cells.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Redox flow batteries (RFBs) are electric energy storage devices that have drawn increasing attention because of their potential scalability, sustainability, and cost efficiency. (1−5) In these devices, the charge carriers contained in the liquid electrolyte are generated in electrochemical stacks and stored in external reservoirs. During electrochemical cycling, the battery fluids are circulated between these two locations using a pump, providing new possibilities when compared to more conventional energy storage devices, such as the ability to continuously monitor and adjust the storage medium. Flow cells are highly scalable to match supply and demand in the electric grid when intermittent power sources are involved.
    While presently most RFBs use aqueous electrolytes, the possible next frontier proposes nonaqueous electrolytes as their wide electrochemical stability windows of 2–3 V allow increasing cell voltage and, thus, stored energy density. (6) Polar, aprotic organic solvents (such as acetonitrile) dissolve ionized salts that support electric conduction during cycling, while the redox-active molecules (redoxmers) reversibly charge and discharge. For economic and sustainability reasons, it is preferable for the redoxmers to be organic. (1) Such redoxmers can be small molecules or ions, but they can also be macromolecules consisting of multiple redox-active units attached to a polymer scaffold. These molecules/ions/polymers typically have π-conjugated heterocyclic cores that readily donate or accept 1–2 electrons per unit. To obtain high energy density, these units should have high redox potential in a positive charge carrier and low redox potential in a negative charge carrier. The charged states of these energy-rich redoxmers need to be chemically and electrochemically stable. (7)
    Presently, hundreds of candidate redoxmer molecules have been examined, but only a small fraction of these molecules have the desired redox potential and sufficient electrochemical stability. Still fewer of these redoxmer molecules can be used at the high concentrations (0.5–2 M) required for nonaqueous RFBs. (8,9) This is unsatisfying, as at lower concentrations such RFBs may not have significant cost advantages over the aqueous RFBs. (6) There is certain chemical logic to this difficulty. The heteroaromatic cores of redoxmers typically have low solubility (<0.1 M) in polar solvents, so they need to be modified with pendant groups to improve solubility. Charged groups and ethylene oxide chains are common examples of such modifications. (1) The resulting molecules are more soluble but their cores still tend to aggregate, and the solubility can decrease precipitously when these cores become charged and pair with electrolyte ions. The aggregation of planar aromatic and heterocyclic molecules into pancake, staggered, or T-shaped stacks (i.e., π-stacking) is a ubiquitous phenomenon in chemistry and biophysics. (10) While this stacking can be reduced by introducing steric hindrance, such modifications increase synthetic costs and further decrease solubility. The synthetic control over the redoxmer solubility remains an art form.
    This study was conceived as an attempt to rationalize an exception to the general trend, a negative charge carrying (anolyte) molecule 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BzNSN, see Figure 1a for the molecular structure). This molecule is commonly used in RFB designs (11−13) as it has low one-electron redox potentials and high stability in either state of charge. (14,15) It is also one of the most soluble redoxmers (∼5.7 M in acetonitrile at 25 °C).
    This high solubility of BzNSN is surprising. Typically, crystallization is prevented by reducing molecular symmetry (oddly shaped, flexible molecules, and ions can be difficult to pack into a crystal) and/or by frustrating intermolecular interactions. (16,17) BzNSN is a small planar molecule with a C2h symmetry that forms infinite staggered face-to-face stacks in the crystal. (18) This molecule can be classified as mesoionic (19) and has a strong dipole moment (∼2 D) along the axis of the symmetry. Potentially, such dipoles can form a frustrated glassy state; however, because of dipole pairing in solution, most of heterocyclic redoxmers, even when their aromatic cores have large dipole moments, still have low solubility. BzNSN is an exception, which makes it unusual. We reasoned that learning the “secret” of this molecule could teach us how to achieve higher solubility in a more general case.

    Computational Methods

    MD calculations were performed using Gromacs v5.1.4, (20) all calculations were at 300 K. For acetonitrile (CH3CN), we used the force field developed in our recent publication. (21) For BzNSN, the force field originated from a refined OPLS-AA force field. (22−24) To this end, a range-separated hybrid functional method (wb97xd) (25) with the aug-cc-pVDZ basis set was used to optimize the geometry in the CH3CN solution (modeled at CPCM dielectric continuum) (26) using Gaussian 09 suite, (27) and then, a single-point Hartree–Fock calculation was used to compute partial electrostatic charges using the CHelpG method. (28,29) The bond and angle parameters from these calculations were used to define the equilibrium geometry in MD simulations, while the force constants came from the OPLS-AA parameterization. (22−24) The Gromacs topology input files are given in the Supporting Information, Section S1.
    The MD calculations were performed using a 6 nm cubic periodic box; the initial configurations were obtained using a Packmol code (30) followed by energy minimization. The computational approach was similar to the one described in ref (21). To generate solid amorphous BzNSN, the crystal was melted in silico at 1300 K, followed by rapid quenching of the melt and equilibration at 300 K. For the experimental solutions, the experimental densities were used for NVT (constant number density) ensemble calculations; for other systems, constant-pressure NPT ensembles were used first, and then NVT calculations were used for the equilibration (∼2 ns) and production (∼4 ns) runs. The experimental and NPT ensemble calculated densities were in good agreement, as shown in Figure 1b. The analyzed frames were equidistant 20 fs snapshots from a single trajectory; these frames were used to calculate statistical quantities of interest. These include the weighted radial distribution functions (RDFs) seen in Figure 2a,b, neighboring and clustering statistics, small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS) spectra, and so forth. For the neighboring statistics, the molecules with the interatomic contacts <2.8 Å were defined as the neighbors, which is suggested by the crystal structure (see Figure 3 ). This proximity criterion excludes the π-stacked molecules, quantifying only randomly piled molecules as neighbors.
    To count stacks, we used a different proximity criterion (we stress that all such criteria are necessarily subjective, e.g., see refs (31) and (32)). The imidazolium cations in ionic liquids give a “classic” example of frustrating π-stacking interactions though H-bonding. (33) The original criterion was suggested for the imidazolium cations by Fayer and coworkers (34) and it is modified here (Figure 1a). For each heterocycle, a centroid (c) was computed and the gyration tensor Gij = ∑mwmδri,mδrj,m/∑mwm was calculated, where m is the atom index, wm is the weight, and δri,m is the radius vector from the centroid to this atom. The eigenvector corresponding to the lowest eigenvalue of this tensor minimizes the weighted sum of scalar products between a unit vector and the radius vectors, giving the “best” normal vector fit to a slightly nonplanar molecule.
    For any two solute molecules, we compute the distance r between their centroids and the parameter ξ = 1 – |cos(n1,n2)|, defined as a complement to the absolute cosine of an angle between the two normal vectors (Figure 1a). For parallel molecules, this parameter is zero. The molecules form a stack if their centroids are sufficiently close to each, while their planes are sufficiently parallel to each other, which implies the upper limits for the c–c distance r and the angle parameter ξ. The first limit (r < 4.5 Å) is easy to obtain as it corresponds to the first minimum in the RDF functions, as shown in Figure 2a. To define the second limit, we computed two-dimensional frequency (r,ξ) maps for the different solutions shown (Figure S1). These false color maps visualize how many BzNSN pairs with the given r and ξ occur in the system. For many such pairs (including all N–H bonded pairs), these two parameters are random as there is no preferred orientation of the molecules in these pairs, but there is a subset of pairs that is seen as a narrow ridge near the r axis that includes the stacked molecules. The width of this sharp ridge does not change with the concentration (Figure S1), and from the width of this feature, we set ξ < 0.1 as the stacking criterion; this corresponds to an angle <15° between the molecule planes. Using this criterion, our software identifies and labels all stacked molecules in the periodic cell and calculates their mole fraction, coordination, connectivity, and so forth. Figures 4 and 5 below show thus classified stacked molecules for three CH3CN solutions at 300 K. A network of such molecules will be referred to as the “stacked-molecule” network that complements the “piled-molecule” network.
    Graph theory methods were used to analyze the networks using iGraph package. (35) Each molecule is represented by a node in an undirected graph; two molecules making contact give the graph edge. The coordination number is the average degree of the node (the number of molecular contacts, see Figure 6a–d below). The graph connectivity is characterized by the global and mean local connectivity clustering coefficients C2 and C3. The C2 gives the probability of the three nodes making a triangle if two of them make a wedge. The C3 expands this definition to a tetrahedron. (36) These coefficients can be counted globally for the entire graph or locally for each node and then averaged.
    Another way to characterize the connectivity is by computing the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the adjacency matrix, in which 1 corresponds to an edge between the nodes (i.e., the two molecules contacting each other) and 0 is for no connection. The need to involve such less intuitive quantities results in a wide distribution of the node degrees (i.e., the contacts coordination numbers) in the real-life graphs (see Figure 6b,c below for an example); the spectral graph theory can give estimates of connectivity taking this wide distribution into account. The largest eigenvalue (λmax), which is also called the spectral radius of the graph, characterizes the extent of connectivity and the inverse participation ratio (ipr) for the corresponding eigenvalue vq (defined as (∑qvq4)−1) characterizes the spread of the connected cluster over the graph. (37) These metrics are averaged over the MD trajectory. We also plotted the binned connectivity spectra, as their smoothness indicates a transition from isolated clusters to a contiguous contact network (Figure 6a,b below). Isolated clusters of molecules can be counted directly; for these clusters, the average Z-weighted gyration radii rg were computed to characterize the physical size (as the X-ray scattering is proportional to the electron density). Such clusters are observed only at lower concentrations, at high concentrations they overlap with few if any isolated clusters still remaining.
    To compute SAXS spectra, the system was regarded as a periodic cubic lattice, which limited the allowed transfer vectors q of length q in the reciprocal lattice to the multiples of 2π/b, where b is the unit parameter of the cubic cell. (38) The complex phases were calculated using the Cromer–Mann atomic form factors and positions for all atoms, and their amplitudes were averaged over the ensemble to compute the structure factor as a function of q, the resulting amplitudes I(q) were then corrected by the polarization factor. This approach results in poor directional averaging for the vectors with the smallest Miller indices resulting in the graininess of the computed spectra for low transfer vectors q.

    Small Angle X-ray Scattering

    The BzNSN solutions were prepared by adding the known weight of the crystalline material to the known weight of CH3CN. The density of the solution was determined, and the composition was checked using 1H NMR. At 7 M, a metastable supersaturated solution was obtained by rapid cooling from 60 °C to 25 °C. The supercooled solution is stable for 10–20 min before it begins to crystallize. SAXS experiments were performed at Argonne’s Advanced Photon Source (APS), sector 12ID, stations B and C. The liquid samples were contained in thin-wall quartz capillaries. The data were collected on a Pilatus 2 M area detector (DECTRIS Ltd.), selecting the incident energy of 18 keV. The scattering vector q (0.06–1 Å–1) was calibrated using silver behenate. All spectra were obtained at 300 K, and the signal from the container was subtracted. (39) The spectra are given as the log–log plots for the scattering intensities I(q) versus the length of the transfer vector q.

    Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Below, we demonstrate that the peculiarity of BzNSN solutions is in the frustration of molecular ordering through a competition between two packing motifs. We first consider the crystal structure, (18) then proceed to examine the solution structure, and finally review the experimental data that validate our structural insights.

    Stacking Versus Piling in the Crystal

    In the crystal structure shown in Figure 3a, the BzNSN stacks form infinite chains with the shortest centroid-to-centroid (c–c) distance of 3.85 Å. (18) Each BzNSN molecule has 16 neighbors, only two of which are stacked face-to-face. The shortest c–c distance of 6.4–6.8 Å is for molecules from the neighboring π-stacks, as shown in Figure 3b, an aromatic H in the benzene ring of the BzNSN molecule in one stack forms a hydrogen bond with the nitrogen in the molecule from another stack (Figure 3b). The shortest of these N–H bonds is ∼2.57 Å (Figure 3b). There are also multiple contacts between the H atoms in the benzene ring with the H and C atoms from other benzene rings (N–H piling). The longest of these contacts are ∼2.8 Å (Figure 3b), and, thus, neighboring BzNSN molecules are defined as within this distance. In essence, there is a dichotomy of ordering in the crystal state; the BzNSN molecules interact in both highly directional π-stacking and more random N–H bond piling. Similar effects are observed in polar solution.

    Stacking Versus Piling in Solution

    Akin to its crystalline form, BzNSN molecules in the CH3CN solution also cluster in π-stacks and N–H piles. In both crystal and solution, there is always a dichotomy of states as observed in the MD snapshots. An illustrative MD snapshot of 3 M BzNSN is shown in Figure 1a, while Figure 1b shows experimental and computed density of the solutions extended to simulate supersaturated solutions. The crystalline and amorphous BzNSN (see the Methods section for details) are also included for comparison. We estimate that in this amorphous state, at most, 45% of the molecules are in stacks (Figure 1b) as opposed to 100% of the molecules in the ordered state. The mole fraction of the stacked molecules in solution (Figure 1b) increases linearly with the molar concentration of BzNSN until ∼6 M and then reaches a plateau, signifying that further increase in concentration does not increase stacking. The computed threshold for this transformation is close to 5.7 M, which is the experimental estimate for the BzNSN solubility in CH3CN at 298 K. Thus, the experimentally observed onset of crystallization coincides with the attainment of critical stack density in solution; these stacks form seeds for nucleation.
    The experimentally measured densities are close to the MD estimates in the 0–6 M range. Given this agreement, we presume the extrapolated densities for >6 M hold true. The computed densities have linear behavior versus the molarity. Of note, this trend line clearly misses the crystal density (the points in the oval in Figure 1b), either computed from crystallographic data or MD simulation. Instead, it passes through the quenched melt state, which is a vitreous polymorph, suggesting that the extreme concentrated, crowded solutions are closer to this disordered state than to the ordered crystalline state. This disordered state is the ultimate limit for crowded solutions, as it preserves random aggregation of BzNSN molecules which is characteristic of the liquid solutions.
    Figure 2a shows the RDF for the c–c distances in CH3CN solutions simulated using our MD model. At all concentrations (for more extensive RDF data including the quenched melt, see Figure S2), and even in the melt, there are two distinct maxima, one corresponding to the most probable distance of 4.0 Å in the π-stacks and the 6.7 Å maximum that corresponds to “piling” of BzNSN through the N–H bonds. These two peaks are seen at all BzNSN concentrations, and they become sharper at higher concentrations, indicating increasing spatial ordering of the molecules. The similarity with the crystal follows from the approximate 1:7 integration ratio for the two peaks in the RDF trace, as shown in Figure 2a, it is the same ratio as in the crystal.
    Figure 4a,b shows the snapshots of BzNSN molecules in a periodic cell corresponding to 3 and 5 M, respectively, which is below the critical concentration of ∼6 M. Even at 3 M, while ∼28% of the BzNSN molecules are in stacks, most of these stacks are isolated from each other, as can be seen in Figure 4a where these isolated stacks are colored red. At 5 M (37% molecules in stacks), the density of stacks becomes sufficiently high for them to aggregate further. However, most of this aggregation is not through the stacks combining into staggered chains but rather through N–H piling of the molecules in the stacks (Figure 4b). As we increase to 7 M (Figure 5a,b), the aggregation becomes pronounced, and now staggered chains of stacks of four or more molecules can be found in the cell. The resulting network becomes nearly contiguous as (almost) all of the molecules in the network may be connected by their stacking contacts. Following this trend, the spectral radius λmax, which quantifies the extent of connectivity in the stacks (see the Methods section for more details), increases from 1.2 at 1 M to 1.76 at 7 M and then stabilizes at ∼1.75–1.80 as the concentration increases further (λmax = 2 in the crystal), as shown in Figure S3. It is seen that this metric of connectivity behaves in the same fashion as the stacking fraction plotted in Figure 1b.
    As the solution thickens, not only are there more stacks but also there is more N–H bond piling. Figure 6a shows the average number of the solute (i.e., BzNSN) and solvent molecules contacting a given BzNSN molecule in solution. The contacts between solute molecules increase linearly with the concentration, reaching ∼2 contacts at 5 M and ∼3 contacts at 7 M, while the number of contacted CH3CN molecules decreases from ∼5 contacts at 0.5 M to ∼2 contacts at 7 M. Thus, at 7 M on average there are more neighboring solute molecules than there are solvent molecules. Importantly, the coordination numbers shown in Figure 6a are the ensemble averages; as illustrated in Figure 6,c, there is a Poisson-like distribution of the coordination numbers for individual BzNSN molecules, which becomes progressively wider as the BzNSN concentration increases. At 7 M, ∼15% of BzNSN molecules have no solvent in their coordination sphere and the number of BzNSN neighbors varies from none to seven, while the average coordination number is ∼3 (Figure 6a). Plotting the mole fractions of BzNSN molecules with different coordination numbers as a function of molarity (Figure 6d), we see that the maximum probability to have exactly one BzNSN neighbor peaks at around 3 M, two neighbors at 5 M, and three neighbors at 7 M. The transition from the average coordination number of 2 (chains) to 3–4 (mesh) indicates the emergence of a 3-dimensional contiguous network.
    This transition is also seen in the spectra for the adjacency matrices, as shown in Figure 7a,b, that characterizes contact or no contact between molecules (for details, see the Methods section). For small molecular clusters, the largest eigenvalues λmax can only attain distinct values, so the eigenvalue histograms averaged over the ensemble look like line spectra. (37) As the connected clusters include more molecules and these connections become more random, the spectrum becomes continuous. For BzNSN clustering, the transition from the (mainly) stick spectrum in weak solutions to the (mainly) continuous spectrum in thick solutions is seen in Figure 7a, while the opposite trend is observed for CH3CN clustering, as shown in Figure 7b. The latter means that the solvent clusters are fragmented into increasingly smaller domains by the solute molecules. Another way to observe the growth of the contact network is by plotting λmax and the inverse participation ratio ipr (see the Methods for the definition) versus the concentration (Figure S4), as these two metrics reflect the widely dispersed coordination number distributions, as shown in Figure 6b,c. The λmax increases in the same fashion as the coordination number eventually reaching ∼5.5 in the melt. This is close to the maximum allowable number of molecular contacts for the BzNSN molecule (six total, via the two nitrogens and four hydrogens), that is, all atoms except sulfur in BzNSN become involved in forming the piled-molecule network. At this point, the coordination can increase further only through face-to-face π-stacking. At the solubility limit, the λmax is close to 4, which is sufficient to form a dense 3-dimensional structure. The corresponding ipr rapidly increases from ∼12 to ∼20, indicating the formation of large aggregates (Figure S4).
    At low concentrations (<3 M), most aggregates are isolated at any given time. The fraction of larger aggregates rapidly decreases with their size (Figure S5); there are no peaks suggesting the formation of micelles or other well-defined aggregates. At 1 M, the clusters include <8 molecules (and such relatively large clusters account for <0.5% of the total molecules), while 60% molecules are isolated and 20% are paired. Thus, even this 1 M solution (that for many redoxmers would be their solubility limit) is still a dilute solution. At 3 M, the clusters of 20–30 molecules emerge while 25% of the molecules remain isolated. The Z-weighted gyration radius rg for these clusters (which characterizes low-q X-ray scattering amplitude by these clusters) rapidly increases with the number of molecules, reaching 1.0–1.5 nm for the largest clusters (Figure S5), and above 3 M these molecular aggregates fuse into a single network filling the entire periodic cell.
    As we determined from Figure 2a, the first computed centroid-to-centroid RDF maximum at 4.0 Å is from the π-stacked BzNSN molecules. When the contact radius in the nearest-neighbor analyses is increased to 4.0 Å to include these stacked molecules, the average number of the BzNSN neighbors increases to ∼9 at 7 M (Figure 6a). Continuing this trend to the amorphous BzNSN, as shown in Figure 1b, one obtains the average of ∼16 molecules (Figure S4), which equals the coordination number in the crystal. The same coordination number is obtained by counting neighbors with the c–c distance <8.4 Å, which corresponds to the second minimum in the RDF traces. When defined in this way, the ipr for the contact network shoots to over 200 at 5 M (Figure S6). That is, considered in toto, at a critical concentration of ∼6 M (at which the maximum fraction of stacking is first attained), the piled and stacked molecules fuse into a single network that includes all solute molecules. The corresponding average local and global clustering coefficients (Figure S6) converge to the values typically observed for the jammed spheres, (40,41) and the concentration range of this convergence (5–7 M) coincides with the solubility limit. We remind that these clustering coefficients give the probabilities of the pairwise contacting molecules to arrange into complete triangles and tetrahedrons. When these probabilities approach the values observed for the jammed spheres, (40) the molecules begin to pack into lattices and a long order begins to emerge between their positions in space, yielding correlation peaks in the X-ray scattering spectra.

    Computed and Experimental SAXS Spectra

    The tails of the computed RDF functions, as shown in Figure 2b, show damped oscillations whose period (the domain size in the fluid) weakly depends on the solute concentration. These domains give rise to a strong correlation peak at high-q in the X-ray scattering spectra, which is observed in all CH3CN solutions (Figures 8 and 9).
    In the computed SAXS spectra, as shown in Figure 8a, the Lorentzian low-q section does not evolve significantly with the molecular concentration, and the most distinguishing feature (indicated by the arrow in Figure 8a) is a correlation peak at 1.1 Å–1, which is observed at concentrations >3 M. The correlation length 2π/q = 5.7 Å corresponding to this peak position falls right in between the most probable c–c distances of 4.0 Å for π-stacking and 6.4 Å for N–H piling, that is, the observation of this peak is the “smoking gun” for the postulated competition between the two molecule packing motifs. If our MD model is correct, the position of this peak would reflect exclusively the intermolecular interactions between the neighboring BzNSN molecules, and it would not depend on the concentration of these molecules and/or presence of salts in solution.
    The experimental SAXS spectra of BzNSN solutions, as shown in Figure 9a–c, agree with these model predictions. As the BzNSN concentration increases (Figure 9a), the Lorentzian tail in the low-q region does not evolve, whereas the correlation peak gradually emerges over the background from the solvent at 1 M at the predicted 1.1 Å–1. When the signal of the solvent weighted by the solvent mole fraction is subtracted from the SAXS spectrum (Figure 9c), the correlation peak stands out, clearly showing that the peak position does not depend on the BzNSN concentration, that is, the correlation is defined by pairwise intermolecular interactions. With the stacking order partially established and the increasing fraction of ordered molecules at higher concentrations, at 5–7 M, the solution approaches the critical point where nucleation occurs. Figure 9b illustrates another qualitative prediction of the MD model, the position of the correlation peak does not depend on the presence of the electrolyte despite the fact that the introduced salt strongly increases X-ray scattering (due to electron rich atoms in the PF6– anion). This also suggests that the ordering responsible for the correlation peak at 1.1 Å–1 stems exclusively through the short-range interactions of BzNSN molecules. These experimental observations validate our MD model and complete the argument.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In the Introduction section, we asked why a small, symmetric, planar molecule BzNSN that readily forms stacks in a crystal would have the anomalously high solubility in acetonitrile solutions. Our results suggest that it achieves this feat through a tug of war between two kinds of intermolecular interactions, π-stacking and N–H bonding. These interactions occur at all concentrations but are especially prevalent in crowded solutions. Their competition manifests itself on many levels causing frustration that disfavors nucleation of the BzNSN solute and rationalizing its anomalously high solubility in a polar solvent. There is a deep chemical analogy between the BzNSN in liquid solutions and the imidazolium cations in ionic liquids. (33,34) Both heteroaromatic species avoid crystallization through competitive interactions of two packing motifs. This propensity explains why imidazolium-based ionic liquids represent the largest class of commercially available ionic liquids. (17) The ability of imidazolium ions to form multiple types of bonds has been used by Huang et al. to boost solubility (∼4.3 M) of a nitroxide redoxmer in aqueous solution of an imidazolium ionic liquid used as an electrolyte in a flow cell. (42)
    Such telling examples provide valuable insights as to how higher redoxmer solubility can be achieved in general. The presently used synthetic approaches focus almost exclusively on improving solute–solvent interactions; these approaches are successful in raising the solubility from <0.1 to 1–2 M. However, surpassing this concentration can be problematic. The derivatization makes the redoxmer molecules larger, which results in lower molarity even when the redoxmers are miscible with the electrolyte. We contend that in many instances, the interactions that limit solubility in concentrated solutions are solute–solute interactions, which are not given adequate attention by researchers.
    The neutral (and even charged) heterocycle cores of redoxmer molecules are vulnerable to stacking interactions; however, many of these heterocycles can form strong H-bonds between each other. The textbook example of balancing these two interactions is the DNA molecule, the H-bonding arranges the nucleotides into complementary pairs, while stacking interactions of the assembled pairs yield the double helix. This arrangement relies on the fundamental property of the heterocycle cores to strongly interact with each other in two fundamentally different ways. The same dichotomy is in play for BzNSN, and we implore synthetic chemists to learn from such examples and implement their lessons in molecular designs.
