    Stable Low-Cost Organic Dye Anolyte for Aqueous Organic Redox Flow Battery

    Abstract

    Aqueous organic redox flow battery represents a potential solution to significantly reduce the cost of flow batteries. An ideal organic redox active material for flow battery application shall possess good stability in supporting electrolyte, fast redox reaction speed, and low manufacturing cost. In this work, a commercially available dye material was modified and evaluated in aqueous organic redox flow battery as anolyte. The desalted Basic Red 5 dye (d-BR5) exhibited reasonable solubility in acidic supporting electrolyte and stable redox performance. Two aqueous organic redox flow batteries with d-BR5 as anolyte and two different catholytes (4,5-dihydroxybenzene-1,3-disulfonic acid (BQDS) and cerium methanesulfonate (Ce(CH3SO3)3)) were evaluated in this work. The later system reached satisfactory stability in long-term cycling experiments. The cell of d-BR5 and (Ce(CH3SO3)3) exhibited an operating voltage ∼ 1.4 V, with 99.9% capacity retention after 200 cycles. The results indicated that organic dye molecules could be potential low-cost active materials for aqueous organic redox flow batteries.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    With the rapid development of renewable energy technologies, the demand for a large-scale energy storage system has increased dramatically in the past few decades. Various energy storage technologies based on thermal, mechanical, and electrochemical mechanisms (1) have been introduced and demonstrated. Among all of the candidates, redox flow battery (2−4) (RFB) emerged as one of the most attractive electrochemical systems for grid energy storage recently. In a redox flow battery, the energy is stored in redox-active fluid outside the power conversion electrochemical cell, where the positive and negative electrolytes are separated by ion exchange membrane. This setup decouples the energy density and power density of electrochemical cells, thus providing flexibility to manipulate device properties to facilitate various applications. The active redox materials are the central thesis of material study in RFB. At the current stage, all commercial RFBs were built on inorganic redox-active materials such as vanadium, (5) chromium, (6) and iron (7) in an aqueous system. However, utilization of inorganic redox ions in RFB has several limitations such as the high cost of the raw materials, the high crossover rate of active materials in the cell, and potential environmental impact. It is a constant driving force to search for low-cost redox materials suitable for RFB technique. One promising direction for low-cost RFB materials is organic redox-active materials, (2,8−11) which could be inexpensive and sustainable. To facilitate the most favorable solvent in RFB, water, the organic redox species were usually designed to have good solubility in an aqueous system, which was categorized as an aqueous organic redox flow battery (AORFB). To date, most studies on organic redox-active materials for AORFB are focused on materials based on quinone derivatives, (12−17) nitronyl nitroxide, (18−21) and viologen. (18,19,22−24) Yet, there has been some discussion on phenazine based materials, which are proven to have good electrochemical reversibility, fast reaction rate, and high solubility for AORFB application. (25)
    In this work, we modified a commercially available industrial dye, Basic Red 5 (BR5), and used it as redox-active anolyte for AORFB. BR5 is widely utilized as a redox indicator in cell staining because it has a fast redox reaction rate and high reversibility toward changing pH value. (26) To evaluate the BR5 derived anolyte, two redox flow battery systems were built and tested in this work. (Figure 1) The first system is an all-organic AORFB with desalted BR5 (d-BR5) as anolyte and 4,5-dihydroxybenzene-1,3-disulfonic acid (BQDS) as catholyte (BQDS/d-BR5). The second system consists of an inorganic catholyte cerium methansulfonate, which forms a hybrid organic/inorganic RFB (Ce/d-BR5). In both systems, the RFBs were operated under acidic condition. The cell voltages were determined by the redox couples in two types of batteries, with 0.7 V for BQDS/d-BR5 AORFB and 1.4 V for Ce/d-BR5 AORFB. The two AORFBs exhibited good stability with negligible capacity degradation after 350 cycles and 200 cycles, respectively.

    2.1. Synthesis and Characterization of NR Derivative

    A series of BR5 derivatives were purified and synthesized in this work including Basic Red 5 (1), desalted Basic Red 5 (2), and Basic Red 5-trifluoromethansulfonic acid (BR5-TfOH, 3) (Supporting Information Figure S1). The detailed synthesis and purification procedure are reported in the Supporting Information (SI). Figures S2 and S3 show the NMR spectra of d-BR5 and BR5-TfOH, respectively. As shown in the NMR spectra, d-BR5 has five distinctive chemical shifts between 6.5 and 8 ppm, which correspond to the aromatic region. The proton on the amine group shows a chemical shift at 4.25 ppm. As compared to d-BR5, BR5-TfOH has identical chemical shifts in the aromatic region around 6.5–8 ppm; however, the -NH2 proton chemical shift is not observed due to triflate counterion attachment. BR5 and its derivatives have relatively good solubility in acidic solution as compared that in basic solution and neutral solution. As shown in Figures S4a, S5a, and S6a, the saturated solutions of BR5, d-BR5, and BR5-TfOH in 1 M H2SO4 were diluted for various times until a similar absorption intensity (compared to a known concentration) was observed. The calculated solubility limitation is 0.10, 0.34, and 0.11 M, respectively (Table S1). BR5 has shown more than three times the solubility enhancement from 0.1 to 0.34 M after removal of hydrochloride counterion. However, no further enhancement was observed by replacement of hydrochloride counterion with more polar triflate counterion. The solubilities of BR5 derivatives in 4 M methanesulfonic acid (MSA) were also studied. The results show that the solubilities of BR5, d-BR5, and BR5-TfOH are 0.054, 0.206, and 0.175 M in MSA, respectively (Figures S4b, S5b, and S6b and Table S1). In both common acidic supporting electrolytes, d-BR5 exhibits higher solubility than BR5 and BR5-TfOH.
    Electrochemical properties of various BR5 were investigated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurement. As shown in the scheme in Figure 2a, all BR5 based molecules have the same redox mechanism, in which process the aromatic ring could host two electrons forming negatively charged ions. The redox potentials of different BR5 in different supporting electrolytes are slightly different (Figure 2b). In 1 M H2SO4 supporting electrolyte, the anodic peaks of BR5, d-BR5, and BR5-TfOH are 0.013, −0.015, and −0.015 V (vs Ag/AgCl), respectively. The corresponding cathodic peaks are −0.012, −0.038, and −0.039 V for BR5, d-BR5, and BR5-TfOH, respectively. The formal redox potentials, which are determined as the averages of cathodic peak and anodic peak, are 0.0005, −0.013, and −0.012 V for BR5, d-BR5, and BR5-TfOH. Table S2 summarized all cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurement data. It is worth mentioning that the peak separation of BR5 derivatives are very small (24–25 mV) in 1 M H2SO4 supporting electrolyte. As compared with the peak separation of well-recognized anolyte anthraquinone-2,6-disulfonic acid (AQDS), 220 mV (Figure S7), the smaller peak separation of BR5 derivatives indicated a better kinetic reversibility. The redox peak of some BR5 derivatives in neutral supporting electrolyte (1 M NaCl) were tested as well (Figure S8). The low current density in the CV spectrum was observed, which could be attributed to the low solubilities (<0.001 M) of BR5 derivatives in 2 M NaCl supporting electrolyte. In this work, MSA was also used as supporting electrolyte; the redox behavior of BR5 derivatives in MSA (4 M MSA in water) has been described in Figure 2b. In 4 M MSA supporting electrolyte, the anodic peaks of BR5, d-BR5, and BR5-TfOH are 0.062, 0.057, and 0.058 V (vs Ag/AgCl), respectively, corresponding to cathodic peaks 0.004, 0.004, and 0.006 V. It is worth mentioning that larger peak separations of BR5s in 4 M MSA were observed as compared to 1 M H2SO4 (Figure 2 and Table S2). Such a phenomenon usually indicated slower redox kinetics or a poorer reversibility of BR5 and its derivative in MSA. The electrochemical test revealed that BR5 derivatives could be a good anolyte candidate under acidic condition. d-BR5 was chosen as the electrolyte for further cell investigation due to its relatively high solubility.

    2.2. BQDS/d-BR5 Aqueous Organic Redox Flow Battery

    Before the AORFB cell was evaluated, a symmetric flow cell with d-BR5 was fabricated to evaluate the stability of the new anolyte. In the symmetric cell, 0.05 M d-BR5 was prepared in 1 M H2SO4. A 5 mL aliquot of electrolyte was used in each electrolyte tank. The cell was tested under the current density of 50 mA/cm2 at the flow rate of 32.5 mL/min. The voltage window of the cell was set to −0.35 to 0.35 V. As shown in SI Figure S14, d-BR5 exhibited 99.96% capacity retention per cycle, which allows it to be cycled for more than 500 cycles. The first AORFB system tested in this work comprises d-BR5 (anolyte) and 4,5-dihydroxybenzene-1,3-disulfonic acid (BQDS, catholyte). H2SO4 (1 M) was used as supporting electrolyte, and the detailed cell fabrication procedure has been described in the Supporting Information. The cell chemistry is described in Figure 3a.
    BQDS is a commercially available redox molecule and has been previously evaluated as a potential catholyte material in AORFB. (12,15−17) The CV spectrum of BQDS in Figure 3a shows a rapid oxidation peak and a slow step reduction peak, which is consistent with literature results. (17) Compared with the well-known organic anolyte in acidic condition, AQDS, d-BR5 shows a deeper negative potential shift by 79 mV (Figure S7), leading to a theoretically higher cell voltage than that of the AQDS/BQDS cell. (16,17) The concentration of active materials used in this work is relatively low (0.05 M) to avoid the possible precipitation of active materials during the cycling. The catholyte tank comprises 7 mL of 0.05 M BQDS in 1 M H2SO4, and the anolyte reservoir contains 10.5 mL of 0.05 M d-BR5 in 1 M H2SO4. The flow cell was initially operated under 10 mA/cm2 for the first 10 cycles. From the results (Figure 3b), the charge/discharge plateau is round 0.7 V during the first cycle, which matches well with the CV results of d-BR5 and BQDS. The first cycle also reached a capacity of 15 mAh, which corresponds to ∼80% state of charge (SOC). However, there is a reduced discharge plateau around 0.5 V developed at the end of the first discharge. This lower discharge plateau was further extended in the following cycles. At the end of the 10th cycle, the major charge/discharge plateau was shifted to ∼0.5 V for the cell. The change of the charge/discharge plateau indicated the formation of new redox-active species. To investigate the change of active species, the anolyte and catholyte solutions were collected after the first, fifth, and 10th cycles and the CV measurements of cycled electrolyte solution were conducted. As shown in Figure S9a, BQDS solution showed an additional redox peak around 0.45 V (vs Ag/AgCl) after cycling, which confirmed the formation of a new redox species. The instability of BQDS during the cycling was reported by literature. (16) It was concluded that nucleophilic Michael addition reaction on BQDS molecule could happen, leading to hydroxyl group substituted compounds. On the anolyte side, the d-BR5 solution is very stable during the first 10 cycles (Figure S9b). Due to the degradation of BQDS, the Coulombic efficiency (CE) of the cell in the first 10 cycles are low, being only ∼70% in the first cycle. The energy efficiency (EE) and voltage efficiency (VE) of the cell are also low in the first 10 cycles (Figure S10). The EE is around 60%, and VE is 93% for the first plateau and 66% for the second plateau in the first cycle. Interestingly, the Coulombic efficiency of the cell increased quickly to over 99% after a few initial cycles and the cell cycling could be stabilized. During the following 350 cycles, the BQDS/d-BR5 system exhibited a relatively good stability (Figure 3c,d). CE exceeded 99.5%, and the SOC maintained a constant of 58%. For comparison, a flow battery with excess amount of BQDS was fabricated as well (10.5 mL of 0.05 M BQDS and 7 mL of 0.05 M d-BR5). A similar degradation/stabilization process was observed with 41.2% SOC after 380 cycles. (SI Figure S15) The reason for the stabilization process may originate from the fact that all of the hydrogen atoms on the benzene ring of BQDS are replaced by hydroxyl groups. The resulting hydroxylated BQDS is still a redox-active molecule, but presents a lower redox voltage due to the additional electron donating groups. The CVs of the anolyte d-BR5 was evaluated after 360 cycle and is identical to the initial d-BR5 CV spectrum (Figure S11). The overall capacity retention of the cell after 360 cycles is 73.3%; however, most capacity loss happened during the first 10 cycles and the following 350 cycles have capacity retention of 99.9%. During the long-cycle test, the voltage efficiency and energy efficiency were dropping. As indicated in Figure 3d, the voltage efficiency is 94.5% in the 10th cycle and gradually drops to 36% in the 360th cycle. Meanwhile, the EE has fallen from 68.1% to 27.3% at the end of the cycling. The exact reason for voltage and energy efficiency degradation is still unknown; we speculate the degradation of active materials could result in an increased internal resistance within the cell during long-term cycling. (27) In addition to the cycle performance, the rateability of the stabilized cells were evaluated. Figure S12 shows the cell performance of the BQDS/d-BR5 system at different current densities of 10, 30, 50, and 70 mA/cm2. The corresponding average discharge voltages are 0.5, 0.41, 0.37, and 0.31 V respectively, with capacities of 13.3, 12.6, 11.5, and 10.5 mAh, respectively. The capacity of the BQDS/d-BR5 system decreases nearly linearly with increasing current density, (28,29) which is believed to be due to the ohmic loss within the cell (Figure S13).

    2.3. Ce/d-BR5 Aqueous Redox Flow Battery

    As shown in the previous section, a challenge for AORFB cathode material is that many organic aromatic materials are subject to nucleophilic substitution during the cathodic redox process. The utilization of a catholyte material with metal ion as redox center could be a viable way to ameliorate this issue. In addition, the use of inorganic salt could also increase redox potential and achieve high cell voltage. In this work, cerium methanesulfonate was used as catholyte to further evaluate the BR5 anolyte in a hybrid AORFB. Cerium methansulfonate is a salt with stable redox reaction and high redox potential. It has been reported in RFB (30) with zinc metal as counter electrode. As shown in the scheme (Figure 4a), the cell voltage of Ce/d-BR5 cell could reach 1.4 V. The supporting electrolyte used in this hybrid cell is MSA, which is a good solvent for both cerium methansulfonate and d-BR5. In addition, MSA also slows the thermodynamically favored oxygen evolution reaction in RFB. (25) According to Figure 4a, the anodic current at the positive potential rises relatively sharply. Such a cyclic voltammogram is typically observed in the system involving oxygen evolution reaction. (31) Previous work also reported that the weak adsorption of Ce3+ ion on the electrode surface could result in large anodic peak in the Ce3+/Ce4+ system. (31) The redox reaction of Ce3+/Ce4+ generally demonstrated good reversibility if the concentration of the solution was not extremely high. (31)
    The flow cell was assembled by using 7.5 mL of 0.05 M Ce(CH3SO3)3 in 4 M MSA as catholyte and 19 mL of 0.05 M d-BR5 in 4 M MSA as anolyte. The rate performance of the cell was evaluated under current density from 30 to 100 mA/cm2 (Figure 4b). From all currents used, the cell exhibited a stable equilibrium voltage ∼ 1.4 V. The increase of current density would lead to the decrease of battery capacity: 0.01 Ah at 30 mA/cm2 (99% state of charge) and 0.004 Ah at 100 mA/cm2 (39% state of charge). The capacity output decrease could be attributed to the increased ohmic loss in the device. As shown in Figure S13a,b, the overpotentials were 6.5 mV at 30 mA/cm2, 43 mV at 40 mA/cm2, 76 mV at 50 mA/cm2, 130 mV at 60 mA/cm2, 180 mV at 80 mA/cm2, and 245 mV at 100 mA/cm2. The overpotential increases linearly with the current density. Compared to the BQDS/d-BR5 system, Ce/d-BR5 exhibited smaller overpotenials across different current densities (Figure S13), indicating that the rate of redox reaction for the Ce/d-BR5 system is more efficient. The long-term cycling test was conducted at 50 mA/cm2 at 85% SOC. The Ce/d-BR5 cell delivered stable cycling performance for 200 cycles. The capacity remained almost unchanged (capacity retention of 99.9% after 200 cycles) with a negligible capacity loss per cycle. Figure 4b showed the representative charge–discharge curve at the rate of 50 mA/cm2. Our data also indicated that the voltage and energy efficiency of the Ce/d-BR5 system remain at around 92% and 80%, respectively, through the cycles. As compared to the BQDS/d-BR5 system, no additional discharge/charge plateau was formed during cycling of the Ce/d-BR5 system, indicating good stabilities of both catholyte and anolyte materials. Table S3 showed the comparison of the Ce/d-BR5 system with other AORFB systems. The results indicated that the Ce/d-BR5 cell had one of the highest cell voltages for AORFB systems. The BQDS/d-BR5 cell showed reasonable per cycle capacity retention (99.88%), and the total capacity retention of Ce/d-BR5 cell is excellent in the first 200 cycles. It is worth mentioning that there is no solubility-promoting functional group on d-BR5 and the unfunctionalized raw material reaches the solubility of 0.34 M in 1 M H2SO4. Therefore, chemical modification of the d-BR5 molecule to achieve high solubility in supporting electrolyte is promising. BR5 is a commercially available dye material which is sold as low as $10/kg, rendering it a potential low-cost AORFB material.

    3. Conclusion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    From this study, commercial Basic Red 5 and derivatives have been demonstrated to be a good source for AORFB anolyte. Compared to AQDS anolyte, d-BR5 showed a more negative shift in redox potential as well as a smaller redox peak separation, which increased the cell voltage and reduced the overpotential, respectively. Two AORFB BQDS/d-BR5 and Ce/d-BR5 cells were tested in this work. The BQDS/d-BR5 cell with 1 M H2SO4 supporting electrolyte suffered from the instability of catholyte, which led to the initial decrease of cell voltage. After the catholyte was stabilized, the cell could deliver a cell voltage of 0.5 V and maintained a stable capacity over 350 cycles. The Ce/d-BR5 cells with 4 M MSA supporting electrolyte, however, showed no voltage decrease during the cycles due to the good stabilities of both anolyte and catholyte. The cell voltage of the Ce/d-BR5 system was around 1.4 V, and the device reached a remarkable stability in the first 200 cycles (over 99.9% capacity retention). Those tests confirmed that BR5 derivatives could be a potential low cost anolyte material in AORFB, particularly under acidic supporting electrolyte conditions.
