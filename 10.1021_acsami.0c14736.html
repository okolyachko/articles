    Abstract

    Two distinct advantages of nonaqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs) are the feasibility of building a high cell voltage (without a constraint of the water-splitting potential) and the operability at low temperatures (without a concern of freezing below 0 °C). However, electrochemically active organic redox couples are usually selectively soluble in specific nonaqueous solvents, and their solubility is relatively low (in contrast to that in aqueous solutions). The selective and low solubility of redox couples seriously constrict the practical energy density of nonaqueous RFBs. Herein, we present a hybrid nonaqueous RFB with a solid zinc anode and a liquid (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl (TEMPO) cathode. Toward accessing a high solubility of the TEMPO cathode and to sufficiently accommodate the discharge products of a Zn anode, asymmetric electrolyte solvents, viz., propylene carbonate (PC) and acetonitrile (ACN), have, respectively, been employed at the cathode and anode. To prevent a mixing of the two asymmetric electrolyte solvents, a NASICON-type Na+-ion conductive solid-state electrolyte (SSE, Na3Zr2Si2PO12) is employed to serve as a mediator-ion separator. The shuttling of Na+ ions through the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 SSE sustains the ionic charge balance between the two electrodes. The Zn-TEMPO nonaqueous cell with a stable energy density of ca. 12–18 Wh L–1 over 50 cycles was demonstrated.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Lithium-ion batteries are one of the most widely deployed electrochemical energy storage technologies so far, and they are presently playing a dominant role in the market of mobile power sources. (1) However, due to a few intrinsic limitations, such as the high cost of some of the cell components and safety concerns, it is difficult for Li+-ion batteries to meet the requirements of large-scale energy storage applications in the long run. (2) On the other hand, the continued advances in the industrial society relies heavily on the support of various forms of energy. (3) Fossil fuels such as oil, coal, and natural gas are by far the largest energy sources. (4) However, due to the negative health and environmental impacts of fossil fuels, there has been a growing focus on the exploration of clean energy sources among the global community. (5) Solar and wind energies are two successful representatives among the new generation of clean energies. (6,7) However, one challenge for the increased utilization of solar and wind energies is their intermittency. (8) Therefore, reliable energy storage technologies are required to facilitate the management of power grids for the efficient utilization of intermittent clean energy sources. (9) Due to the high cost and safety concerns of Li+-ion batteries, (10) alternative affordable, safe, and high-energy electrochemical energy storage technologies are needed. (11)
    Owing to a series of notable advantages in terms of cost, scalability, reliability, operability, and ease of maintenance, redox flow batteries (RFBs) have been regarded as a promising candidate for large-scale energy storage applications. (12) Previously, the majority of research toward developing RFBs have been focused on aqueous systems, where electrochemically active materials are dissolved in water. (13) However, due to the nature of aqueous RFBs, the cell voltage is restricted to the electrochemical reaction window of water splitting and the working temperatures are confined between the boiling and freezing points of water. (14) As a result, there has recently been an increasing interest in exploring nonaqueous RFB to circumvent the abovementioned limitations. (15) Nonaqueous RFBs can operate at a higher cell voltage and a wider range of temperatures. (16,17)
    The foremost parameter of an RFB is its volumetric energy density, which is regulated by the solubility of the redox couples. One concern regarding the nonaqueous RFBs is the considerably low solubility of electrochemically active redox reactants in organic solvents in comparison to that in aqueous solutions. As a result, at the present stage, nonaqueous RFBs are not able to deliver a volumetric energy density comparable to that of aqueous RFBs. (18,19) In addition, nonaqueous redox couples or their oxidation/reduction products sometimes selectively dissolve in specific organic solvents. In such cases, one solvent may not be applicable for both the anode and cathode to access high-concentration fluid electrodes or to sufficiently accommodate their reaction products. If asymmetric solvents are employed in a single cell to formulate the anode and cathode in different organic media, then the above solubility problem can be resolved. However, the different organic solvents in a single cell would tend to mix with each other if a traditional porous polymer separator is employed for the cell operation.
    In view of the above issues, we herein demonstrate a nonaqueous RFB system with an affordable solid Zn anode and a highly active (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl (TEMPO) liquid cathode. A high-concentration TEMPO catholyte is accessed with a propylene carbonate (PC) solvent. To sufficiently accommodate the discharge product of a Zn anode, an anode electrolyte is formulated with an organic solvent of acetonitrile (ACN). To avoid the chemical crossover of the two asymmetric organic solvents, a mediator-ion solid-state electrolyte membrane, viz., a NASICON-type Na3Zr2Si2PO12, is employed as a separator. During the cell operation, Na+ ions shuttle back and forth between the two electrode chambers and through the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane to support the ionic charge transfer for the redox reactions in the anolyte and catholyte.

    2.1. Chemicals and Materials

    Materials for preparing the solid electrodes and current collectors include zinc foil (Online Metals), nickel foam (TMAX Battery Equipment Ltd.), carbon paper (Toray 060, Fuel Cell Store), and titanium wire (McMaster-Carr). Na+-ion conductive solid-electrolyte membranes (Na3Zr2Si2PO12) were purchased from 421 Energy Inc. The as-received Na3Zr2Si2PO12 pellets were 1.0 mm thick, which were polished down to a thickness of ca. 500 μm before using. Chemicals for preparing liquid electrolytes and liquid electrodes include acetonitrile (ACN, Fisher Scientific), propylene carbonate (PC, Fisher Scientific), sodium perchlorate (NaClO4, Fisher Scientific), zinc perchlorate (Zn(ClO4)2, Alfa Aesar), and (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl (TEMPO, Sigma Aldrich). Chemicals for preparing the reference electrode (RE) include silver nitrate (AgNO3, Alfa Aesar) and tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate (TEABF4, Alfa Aesar).

    2.2. Characterization of Materials

    A Quanta 650 scanning electron microscope (SEM) was employed to characterize the morphologies of the solid electrodes, solid electrolyte, and current collectors before and after cycling. A Rigaku MiniFlex Benchtop X-ray diffractometer was used to collect X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the fresh and cycled solid electrolyte and solid electrodes. The instrument was equipped with Cu K radiation. The XRD patterns were collected with a scan step of 0.02° and at a scan rate of 0.4° min–1.
    The ionic conductivity of the solid-electrolyte membranes was characterized and analyzed with electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) on a Solartron 1260 potentiostat. The testing frequency ranges from 0.1 to 1 000 000 Hz. Before the EIS testing, the solid-electrolyte membrane was coated with a thin layer of Au.     2.3. Cyclic Voltammogram (CV) Collection

    Cyclic voltammetry experiments were conducted on an Autolab (Eco Chemie B.V.) potentiostat. The inert working electrode used for the CV experiments was a piece of Pt wire. A large-area Pt mesh was employed as a counter electrode. An in-house designed Ag+/Ag nonaqueous reference electrode was prepared by replacing the liquid electrolyte of a standard AgCl/Ag reference electrode with a nonaqueous electrolyte comprising of 0.1 M tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate (TEABF4) and 0.01 M silver nitrate (AgNO3) dissolved in an acetonitrile (ACN) solvent. This homemade nonaqueous reference electrode was ca. 0.2 V vs the standard saturated calomel reference electrode (SCE). The electrolyte for cyclic voltammogram experiments was prepared by dissolving 0.1 M of TEMPO and 0.5 M of NaClO4 (supporting electrolyte salt) in a propylene carbonate (PC) solvent. The CV curves were recorded after the electrolyte solution was purged with Ar gas for 15 min.

    2.4. Cell Assembly and Testing

    The Zn-TEMPO RFBs were assembled and tested with a lab-made cell. The cell configuration was inherited from a full-flow RFB as described in our previous publications. (20,21) Since a solid negative electrode (Zn) was employed in this study, the cell was modified accordingly, as detailed in Figure S1 in the Supporting Information. A piece of Na3Zr2Si2PO12 pellet was attached to the cell body with a strong glue (marine epoxy) to make a good seal. The anode of the cell was a piece of Zn foil. To assist the homogeneous distribution of a thin layer of NaClO4/ACN liquid electrolyte on the surface of a Zn anode, a thin layer of Ni foam was placed between the Zn-foil anode and the solid-electrolyte membrane. A piece of carbon paper with high surface was used as the cathode matrix to facilitate the redox reaction of the liquid TEMPO electrode. Charge–discharge performance of the Zn-TEMPO cells was tested with an Arbin BT 2000 battery cycler.

    (2,2,6,6-Tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl (TEMPO) has been known for a long time as an electrochemically active moiety. (22,23) The electrochemical reactions of TEMPO can be expressed as in Scheme 1, which depicts the reversible conversion process between TEMPO and cationic oxoammonium. (24) During the forward (anodic) process, the nitroxyl radical of TEMPO is oxidized by accepting one electron and form a cationic species. The produced cation species further combines with an anion in the electrolyte to form an oxoammonium salt. In the cathodic reaction, the reverse process occurs. (25)
    Through a proper selection of organic solvents, a high-concentration TEMPO liquid electrode can be accessed. We have tested a variety of organic solvents and found that propylene carbonate (PC) could dissolve both the TEMPO and a supporting electrolyte salt (NaClO4) simultaneously at a sufficiently high level for operating RFBs (will be discussed later). Before studying the TEMPO as a liquid electrode in RFBs, its electrochemical reaction kinetics in the PC solvent was investigated in detail. The counter electrode for this study was a piece of Pt mesh and an in-house designed nonaqueous Ag+/Ag reference electrode (its preparation is detailed in the Experimental Section) was employed for this set of experiments. The CV data were collected at various scan rates. As seen in Figure 1a, the average oxidation potential of TEMPO and the reduction potential of oxoammonium is ≈ 0.3 V versus the Ag+/Ag nonaqueous reference electrode. Compared to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE), the redox potential of TEMPO would be ca. 0.75 V (the Ag+/Ag reference electrode (RE) is ca. 0.45 V vs SHE, as calibrated with a salt bridge). All CV curves are highly symmetric, indicating an excellent reversibility of the redox reactions. The peak currents of the anodic and cathodic waves are almost identical at each scan rate, denoting the formation of a stable oxidation product of TEMPO. It has previously been reported that the rate constant of electron transfer (K0) for the oxidation–reduction reactions of TEMPO was ca. 0.1 cm s–1. (26) The narrow peak separation between the redox waves at low scan rates (e.g., 50–100 mV s–1) reveals the small polarization of the electrode, which further indicates the fast reaction kinetics of the TEMPO electrode. (31−33) Nonaqueous zinc-ion batteries have recently attracted considerable attention as a promising candidate for large-scale energy storage applications. Based on the electrochemical potentials of Zn (eq 5) and TEMPO (Figure 1a), a Zn-TEMPO redox couple would be theoretically able to provide an expected cell voltage of ca. 1.5 V. Electrolytes used for the development of nonaqueous zinc-ion batteries were usually prepared with a zinc salt dissolved in an organic solvent. (35) For example, Zn(ClO4)2/ACN (acetonitrile) has been regarded as an ideal salt/solvent system for the demonstration of nonaqueous zinc-ion batteries since the solubility of Zn(ClO4)2 in ACN can reach a level of ≥1.1 M. (36) For the development of a practical RFB, energy density is always the primary target and is determined by the solubility of the liquid electrode. Through multiple efforts with both literature search and experimental screening, PC was determined as an ideal solvent for the TEMPO cathode based on its solvation capability for both the liquid TEMPO electrode and supporting electrolyte (sodium salt). The solubilities of TEMPO and the supporting electrolyte salt (NaClO4) are summarized in Table 2. At the anode side, the discharge product (Zn2+) needs to be promptly removed from the surface of Zn to prevent the electrode passivation. Thus, it requires the produced Zn2+ to join with an anion to form a soluble Zn salt in a suitable electrolyte. According to the information learned from literature and the data obtained through experimental screening, NaClO4/ACN was determined as a reasonable anode electrolyte. The oxidation product of an anode (Zn2+) during discharge can easily join with ClO4– to form a highly soluble Zn(ClO4)2 in ACN.     From the discussion above, an anode friendly environment could be created with a NaClO4/ACN electrolyte and a high capacity (high-concentration TEMPO) liquid cathode could be accessed with a NaClO4/PC electrolyte. However, if an asymmetric electrolyte cell is fabricated with a traditional porous separator, the two different electrolyte solvents, PC and ACN, would mix with each other during cell operation. By employing a solid-state ionic electrolyte, the above concern can be circumvented. Nonaqueous RFBs have previously been demonstrated with solid-state electrolytes in which Li+-ion conductive solid-electrolyte membranes (such as Li1–xAlxTi2–x(PO4)3) were mostly employed. (37) The research progress on Na+-ion solid-state electrolytes (Na-SSEs) is relatively behind the Li+-ion SSEs (Li-SSE). However, multiple advantages have been shown for Na-SSEs in contrast to Li-SSEs, especially from the perspective of materials cost. We have previously proposed and demonstrated a concept of mediator-ion RFBs by employing a NASICON-type Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane. However, these mediator-ion RFBs were mostly demonstrated with aqueous or low-concentration nonaqueous redox couples. (20,38) The Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membranes used in this study were purchased from 421 Energy Inc. Ltd. The sturdy pellets made with this material are shown with a picture in the Supporting Information (Figure S2). The as-received solid-state electrolyte pellets are ca. 1 mm thick. The pellets were polished with a fine sandpaper to reduce the thickness to ca. 500 μm. The surface morphology of the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane is shown in Figure S3. Since liquid electrodes were used in this study, there is not an interfacial issue between the electrode and electrolyte. Therefore, the surface of the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 solid electrolyte is not necessary to be exceptionally smooth. The Na3Zr2Si2PO12 solid-electrolyte membranes can deliver a room-temperature Na+-ion conductivity of ca. 1.0 × 10–3 S cm–1 (provided by the supplier and as measured with electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), Figure S4 in the Supporting Information). The Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane also exhibits a wide electrochemical stability window (0–7 V vs Na+/Na, as provided by the supplier). A more important feature of the NASICON ceramic is its ideal mechanical property, which is able to suppress dendrite formation and growth when a metallic electrode is employed.
    Figure 2a shows the schematic of a Zn-TEMPO redox flow cell in which a Na3Zr2Si2PO12 solid-electrolyte membrane is used for the cell fabrication. The Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane serves as a solid-electrolyte separator to preclude the cross-mixing of the asymmetric liquid electrolytes. The shuttling of sodium ions back and forth through the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 solid-state membrane supports the redox reactions between the Zn anode and the TEMPO cathode. NaClO4 serves as a supporting electrolyte to supply sufficient Na+ ions in the anolyte and catholyte for the electrochemical reactions.
    For an efficient utilization of the liquid TEMPO electrode, a high-surface-area and highly conductive electrode matrix is required. In this study, a high-surface carbon paper was used as a cathode matrix. Figure 2b presents the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of the carbon paper (SEM images obtained at high resolutions for this electrode matrix are provided in Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). The fibers of the carbon paper serve as a conductive framework to sustain the electrochemical reactions of TEMPO. The interspace within the fabric framework can provide ample space and adequate channels for both the ionic transport of sodium ions and the mass transport of TEMPO/oxoammonium (cathode material and its discharge product). At the anode side, a thin layer of Ni foam was placed between the Zn-foil anode and the solid-electrolyte membrane to assist the homogeneous distribution of a thin layer of NaClO4/ACN liquid electrolyte on the surface of the Zn anode. Figure 2c shows an SEM image of this nickel foam (high-resolution SEM images are provided in Figure S6). The pore size of the Ni-foam interlayer could provide ample space for a facile transport of the NaClO4/ACN liquid electrolyte, the discharge product of Zn, and Na+ ions.
    The Zn-TEMPO cell was fabricated and tested as a cathode-capacity-limited cell. The anode was a piece of Zn foil with excess capacity. The catholyte was formulated with 1.5 M NaClO4 (supporting electrolyte) and 1.0 M TEMPO dissolved in the PC solvent. Although the solubility of Zn(ClO4)2 can reach 1.1 M in the ACN solvent, an anolyte with an oversaturated Zn(ClO4)2 may hinder the ionic transport of Zn2+ ions during the charge process. The detailed correlation between the Zn2+-ion transport and the concentration of Zn2+ ions will be investigated separately in the future. The anode electrolyte was prepared with 1.0 M NaClO4 (supporting electrolyte) and 0.5 M Zn(ClO4)2 dissolved in the ACN solvent.     The results provided in Figure 3 evidence the feasibility of operating mediator-ion Zn-TEMPO cells with the Na+-ion conductive Na3Zr2Si2PO12 solid electrolyte. The encouraging charge–discharge performance of the cell also reveals that the cell can be operated with a solid zinc anode and a high-concentration liquid TEMPO cathode under the Na+-ion mediation principle.
    The long-term cycling performance of Zn-TEMPO cells was tested at a medium current density of 1.0 mA cm2. Figure 4a displays the consecutive voltage profiles during the charge–discharge of the Zn-TEMPO cell. The average discharge voltage of the Zn-TEMPO cell at this current density is ca. 0.9–1.0 V. Figure 4b presents the volumetric capacities (both charge and discharge) of the TEMPO catholyte throughout 50 cycles. At 1.0 mA cm2, the TEMPO catholyte can deliver a constant volumetric capacity of ca. 20 Ah L–1 (throughout the 50 cycles). Figure 4c shows the Coulombic efficiency of the cell, which varies from cycle to cycle within 91–98%. The relatively low Coulombic efficiency might be due to unknown side relations, which are necessary to be clarified with a rigorous investigation separately in the future.
    For half-flow RFBs demonstrated in this study, the anode of the cell is a solid Zn metal and it only requires a thin layer of liquid electrolyte at the anode. Furthermore, the anode electrolyte is always in the anode chamber rather than being stored in a separate container during the operation of a real cell. Therefore, the Zn anode along with the anode electrolyte, as an integration, can be considered as a stationary part of the cell (The anode is like a current collector and the anode electrolyte is not involved in energy storage). With this consideration, the volumetric storage energy density is determined on the basis of the volume of the liquid TEMPO catholyte. Based on such a calculation, the Zn-TEMPO cell can deliver an energy density of ca. 18 Wh L–1 as presented in Figure 4d.
    With a conservative consideration, if we include the impact from the volume of the anode electrolyte (although it is not involved in the scale-up of energy storage), 1.0 L of 1.0 M TEMPO catholyte requires 0.45 L of the anode electrolyte to complete the balanced redox reactions at the two electrodes (the reduction of 1 Mole TEMPO requires the oxidation of 0.5 mole Zn, and the solubility limit of Zn2+ is 1.1 M). The energy density of the cell calculated on this basis is ca. 12.5 Wh L–1, as presented in Figure S7 in the Supporting Information.
    It is worthwhile to point out here that the Zn-TEMPO chemistry cannot provide a high cell voltage (as seen in Figure 3). Based on the asymmetric electrolyte approach and a mediator-ion concept, the voltage of nonaqueous RFBs can be tuned through a proper selection and management of different redox couples. The cell voltage can be enhanced with an alternative cathode that can provide a high redox potential (such as Unisol blue, (39) octafluoronaphthalene (OFN), (40) bromine, (41) etc.). A high cell voltage can also be accessed by replacing Zn with an alternative anode that exhibits a low redox potential (such as Li).
    The Na3Zr2Si2PO12 solid electrolyte is expected to be both electrochemically and chemically stable under the Zn-TEMPO cell operation conditions, since the electrochemical stability window of the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 material is 0–7.0 V (versus Na+/Na, supplied by the product vendor). The cycled Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane was analyzed with XRD and SEM to investigate the compatibility of the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 solid electrolyte. As seen in Figure 5a–c, there is no phase change of the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane after cycling. In comparison to the fresh Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane (Figure S3 in the Supporting Information), there is no change in the surface morphology of the cycled membrane (Figure 5d) as well. Therefore, both the chemical and the electrochemical compatibilities of this ceramic solid electrolyte are not a concern for the operation of nonaqueous redox flow batteries. Figure S8 compares the surface morphologies of the Zn-foil anode before and after cycling (after 50 cycles). In comparison to the pristine Zn, the cycled anode shows a relatively rough feature, which is likely due to the nonhomogeneous deposition of the Zn metal during the charge process. However, no obvious Zn dendrite formation is observed, indicating a good compatibility of the Zn metal with the ACN solvent. (42,43)
    It should be noted that the Zn-TEMPO RFB demonstrated in this study is actually a hybrid flow battery, which is different from the conventional full-flow batteries. In a full-flow RFB, the power produced depends on the surface area of electrodes and the energy is a function of the catholyte/anolyte volume. For such a case, the power and energy can be scaled independently. For a hybrid flow (half-flow) battery, the storage energy is a function of both the electrode surface area and the catholyte volume. The power and energy cannot be fully decoupled.
    In contrast to the well-developed aqueous-based RFBs, the energy density of Zn-TEMPO cells demonstrated in this study is still significantly lower. Therefore, much more effort is needed for the exploration of highly soluble redox couples in nonaqueous solvents. In addition, it requires a high-concentration supporting electrolyte as well to supply adequate ionic species in liquid electrodes to sufficiently support the electrochemical reactions. The asymmetric-solvent approach validated in this study provides a viable pathway to explore high-concentration redox couples. In addition, the mediator-ion strategy presented here can ensure the application of asymmetric-solvent electrolytes in a single cell. Therefore, it is expected that the cell development strategy proposed and validated here could provide an insightful path for the design and development of nonaqueous RFBs with a variety of highly soluble redox couples.

    With a single-ion (Na+-ion) mediation principle, we have developed a novel nonaqueous redox flow battery with a Zn-TEMPO (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl chemistry and by employing a sodium-ion conductive solid-state electrolyte (Na3Zr2Si2PO12). To access a high solubility of the TEMPO cathode and to sufficiently accommodate the discharge products of a Zn anode, asymmetric electrolyte solvents, viz., propylene carbonate (PC) and acetonitrile (ACN), have, respectively, been employed at the cathode and anode. The Na3Zr2Si2PO12 solid-electrolyte membrane can ensure a nonmixing of the two organic electrolyte solvents. The shuttling of sodium ions back and forth through the Na3Zr2Si2PO12 membrane maintains the ionic charge transfer between the anolyte and catholyte. This research provides a new approach to operate a Zn-anode battery with a sodium-ion conductive solid-state electrolyte. The nonaqueous Zn-TEMPO redox flow cell can deliver a stable volumetric energy density of ca. 12–18 Wh L–1.
