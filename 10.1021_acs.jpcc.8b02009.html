    Abstract

    Nonaqueous redox flow batteries (NRFBs) use energized organic fluids that contain redox active organic molecules (ROMs) and supporting electrolyte. Such all-organic electrolytes have wider electrochemical stability windows than the more familiar aqueous electrolytes, potentially allowing a higher energy density in the solutions of charged ROMs. As this energy density increases linearly with the concentration of the charge carriers, physicochemical properties of concentrated ROM solutions in both states of charge present considerable practical interest. For NRFBs to become competitive with other types of flow cells, the current techno-economic analyses favor highly concentrated solutions (>1 M) with high ionic conductivity (>5 mS/cm). It is not presently clear that such solutions can have the required dynamic properties. In this study, we show that ion diffusivities and conductivities of ROM-containing electrolytes reach maxima around 0.5 M and decrease significantly at higher concentrations; realistic limits are established for variations of these parameters. Furthermore, using closed-shell analogues for open-shell charged ROMs, we show that reconstitution of highly concentrated fluids during electrochemical charging will have strong adverse effects on their properties, including an increase in viscosity and decrease in conductivity and ion diffusivity. Given our results, it appears that the target concentrations of NRFB fluids need to be reconsidered in terms of concentration-dependent conductivity and viscosity.

    Redox flow batteries (RFBs) store electrochemically separated charges in energized fluids that circulate between the compact electrochemical cells and large reservoirs. (1â3) Due to this compartmentalization, RFBs are inherently scalable; hence, the interest in using such systems for energy storage in the electric grid grows. (3,4) In these fluids, positive charges are stored in materials that are called catholyte (C) materials, while negative charges are stored in the so-called anolyte (A) materials. The electrolyte can be either aqueous or nonaqueous. Polar organic solvents, such as acetonitrile and propylene carbonate, have wider electrochemical stability windows, so they potentially allow reaching higher cell voltages and energy densities of charge storage. Below, we consider only such nonaqueous RFBs (NRFBs) and further limit ourselves to redox-active neutral organic molecules (ROMs) as active materials. Importantly, stable organic radicals (e.g., refs (5â7)) and ions (e.g., refs (8and9)) can be used as redox active materials in NRFBs, but such species are beyond the scope of this study. We also do not consider colloidal, polymer, and oligomer redoxmers, for which there are additional complications due to their lower solubility and higher viscosity compared to ROMs. The main attraction of such polymeric materials (10) is using nonselective porous membranes that prevent crossover of the material between the cell compartments, which is a perennial problem with the smaller ROMs.
    The electrolyte in each cell compartment typically consists of ROMs in a polar organic solvent (Figure 1a) also containing ionized X+Yâ salt. Acetonitrile is typically used, as it is the most polar of electrochemically inactive organic solvents, reducing ion association in solutions. Ideally, the membrane separating the two cell compartments transports only solvent molecules and X+ and/or Yâ ions, blocking ROMs from crossing over. However, as these membranes are always imperfect, the same electrolyte composition is typically used in both of the cell compartments to reduce the unwanted mass transport. (1) As the energy stored in the charged NRFBs is proportional to the ROM concentrations, the latter need to be as high as possible without degrading the electrochemical cycling and storage performance. The recent techno-economic analyses (3,4) suggest that, to become competitive with other types of batteries, NRFBs need to contain at least 1â2 M ROMs. For many of the currently used ROMs, such concentrations are close to their solubility limits in one or more states of charge. Naturally, increasing the concentrations of ROMs can cause dramatic changes of the physicochemical properties (including their viscosity and conductivity) of the electrolytes, when new ions are generated during charging. Importantly, in spite of having a high concentration of salts and ROMs, electrolytes need to have sufficiently high ionic conductivity (>5 mS/cm) and low viscosity (<10 cP) in order to minimize hydrodynamic resistance. These performance parameters have been assumed in techno-economic analyses that consider reactor costs and pumping pressures. (11)

    3. Ion Diffusivities in Electrolyte/ROM Solutions

    Concentrated solutions of ROMs (Scheme 1) in acetonitrile become bright yellow (Figure S9), which indicates the formation of a CÎ´+AÎ´â charge-transfer complex and suggests strong interactions between ROM solutes even when they are both in their neutral form. Nonideality of these solutions is also seen in the plots of density vs concentration shown in Figure S10a and b. Even in solutions of, separately, A and C, these concentration dependencies are strikingly nonlinear. For 1:1 equiv solution, the density reaches a plateau at 0.3 M and then changes slowly between 0.3 and 1.5 M. Interestingly, when electrolyte is added to these solutions (1:1:1 equiv), the concentration dependences linearize for c > 0.1 M, which can be due to screening of the induced charges by the ions and strong ROMâion interactions. Also, approximately linear w.r.t. concentration are the diffusivities of ROMs in these solutions (Figure 4). In dilute solutions, the smaller BzNSN molecule is considerably faster than the bulkier catholyte molecule. As the concentration increases, the diffusivity decreases linearly (see traces i and iv in Figure 4). An even greater decrease in the diffusivities of these ROMs and a faster convergence of the corresponding linear plots are observed in the C:A:LiTFSI solutions (traces iii and vi in Figure 4).     5. Effect of Charging on Viscosities of Electrolyte/ROM Solutions

    To assess the effect of electrolysis more directly, we charged electrolyte solutions containing less than 0.25 M ROMs each to 100% SOC, as these solutions can be reconstituted with a high material utilization ratio (>90%) and sufficiently rapidly (5C rate) that the loss of radical ions during this process and subsequent measurements can be neglected. (12,55) In these experiments, a molar excess of salts was used to maintain high ionic conductivity to maintain high charging rate, and the voltage profiles indicated achieving â¼100% charging relative to the calculated theoretical capacity. The persistence of the electrochemically generated radical ions on the time scale of viscosity measurements (15â20 min) has been established in our prior studies. (12,13,55) Using different supporting electrolytes, four series of measurements were taken for 50 mM solutions (using different supporting electrolytes) and one series for 250 mM (using 1 M N4444TFSI as an electrolyte). The NMR method was unsuitable for such charged solutions, due to paramagnetic broadening, line shifting, and the degenerate electron exchange. In Table S4, we compare the viscosities of reduced and oxidized fluids removed from the anode and cathode compartments with the pristine ones. In no instance did we find the changes in viscosity that exceeded three standard deviations of our repeated measurements, but there were instances of viscosity changes exceeding the two standard deviations (Table S4). Remarkably, the changes observed in the 0.25 M solution were even smaller than these changes in the 0.05 M solutions (with the same electrolyte).
    Nothing in our viscosity data suggests that reconstitution dramatically changes rheological properties of cell fluids in these concentration ranges at c < cmax. This agrees with our ion diffusivity and dc conductivity measurements in section 4, suggesting that the reconstitution has minor consequences when c â¤ cmax. As we have stressed above, the consequences become dramatic only for c > cmax.

    6. Li+ BzNSNââ¢ Solution: Strong Ion Association

    Our electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) studies (12) indicate the formation of contact Li+BzNSNââ¢ pairs in acetonitrile (which is observed through a nonzero unpaired electron density on the lithium-7 nucleus) even in 1 mM solutions, suggesting that the ion association constant K is greater than 103 Mâ1. As described in sections 2 and S1, we have isolated a relatively stable [Li(THF)x]+BzNSNââ¢ salt from the THF solutions with a solubility of â¼0.26 M in CH3CN. By addition of acetonitrile followed by the removal of this solvent in a vacuum, it was possible to obtain solutions containing no THF (â¼0.31 M at saturation). However, in this process, some of the BzNSNââ¢ decomposed, so for our dc conductivity measurements the [Li(THF)x]+BzNSNââ¢ salt was used, and these acetonitrile solutions contained some THF.
    Figure 11 shows the dc conductivity Ï and the apparent molar conductivity Î of such Li+ BzNSNââ¢ solutions. Extremely low Ï â¼ 0.53 mS/cm is observed at 0.25 M. Even at 50 mM, the molar conductivity was â¼5 SÂ·cm2/mol (compare that with Figure 9).     The current techno-economic analyses suggest that, for NRFBs to have an edge over the aqueous RFBs, the flow cells need to be operated at high concentrations of the active materials (>1 M) and high voltage (>2.2â2.4 V). (3) While such operation parameters are desirable, it is not clear that they are achievable in practice, as reconstitution of charged fluids at such high concentrations (see eqs 2a and 2b) can adversely affect the electrolyte properties, including viscosity, conductivity, and ion diffusivity. Furthermore, one also needs to maintain high solubility of redox active components in all of their states of charge (as well as the solubility of all potential products of their decomposition). That such a feat can be accomplished in practice is not a given.
    For our study, we chose a ROM (BzNSN in Scheme 1) that is one of the smallest such molecules. In the neutral state, it has very high diffusivity and excellent solubility in acetonitrile (>2 M). Closed shell anions that are structurally similar to BzNSNââ¢ radical anion were used to compose organic salts, and chemical reduction was used to synthesize Li+ BzNSNââ¢. Only organic salts of the mimic anions had sufficient solubility and conductivity in the acetonitrile based electrolyte. Using these salts, we mimicked the composition of charged cathodic fluids on the left-hand sides of eqs 2a and 2b and characterized their dynamical properties. Our results suggest that at concentrations exceeding cmax â 0.4â0.6 M (at which the conductivity reaches maximum) it might be impossible to avoid significant loss of ionic conductivity and increase of viscosity in at least one of the cell compartments. The greatest changes are observed in the cell compartments where the salts become concentrated during charge separation (as in eq 2a). The smallest changes in the conductivity, diffusivity, and viscosity during the reconstitution are observed near cmax. This behavior appears to be general; it is not a specific property of our redox system. Indeed, variations of the size/diffusivity properties of ROMs and supporting electrolyte ions had only a small effect on this behavior.
    Can these dynamic parameters be improved significantly? Our study indicates that, while it is possible to increase ion diffusivity in electrolytes containing tetraalkylammonium cations with certain anions (such as dicyanamide and tetracyanoborate), in the presence of ROMs, this ion diffusivity and ionic conductivity both decrease significantly, so it was impossible to achieve significant improvement. It appears that there is no âmagicâ electrolyte that can mitigate this loss of ion diffusivity and ionic conductivity in the concentrated ROM/salt systems. It is also doubtful that using other electrochemically compatible organic solvents can increase cmax, as the ion pairing in acetonitrile (which is one of the most polar organic solvents, with a dielectric constant of 37.5 vs 80.1 for water) is the weakest among these solvents, while the loss of conductivity is mainly due to increased ion pairing that occurs in the concentrated salt solutions. Although acetonitrile is deservedly called âorganic waterâ, it is no match to water in its ability to solvate ions and screen ion interactions in concentrated solutions; other polar organic solvents used in NRFBs (that all have dielectric constants 2â3 times lower than acetonitrile) perform worse in this respect, and the penalty will be higher.
    Therefore, our study suggests that the currently set NRFB goals may be unattainable using small neutral ROMs due to practical limitations imposed by the currently known solution chemistry, as the reconstitution of such concentrated electrolytes turns them into poorly conducting (<5 mS/cm), excessively viscous (>10 cP) liquids. In terms of the performance, the âsweet spotâ appears to be closer to 0.5 M (â¼cmax), where the fluidity, ionic diffusivities, and ionic conductivities are high, while the changes in these properties after the reconstitution (eq 2) are small. For c > cmax, these changes become excessive, as conductivity and viscosity likely degrade faster than the benefits of increased energy density; issues are further exacerbated by the occurrence of electroosmosis and electroprecipitation. We are agnostic whether these NRFB goals can be achieved with other types of redox active organic materials, including stable radicals, ions, and polymeric redoxmers, but it is obvious that similar concerns arise for these materials, too, and these concerns should be addressed before one transfers from the dilute solutions in NRFB prototypes to the practically relevant high-concentration regimes.
    Our study also indicates that electrolytes that are based on lithium salts are particularly problematic in this respect due to strong ion pairing of the Li+ ion with the radical anions of anolyte molecules, which strongly decreases the solubility and conductivity of negative charge carriers. Using small, diffusive onium cations instead of the Li+ ions in such electrolytes is likely preferable.
