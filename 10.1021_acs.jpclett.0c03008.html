    Abstract

    Aqueous organic redox flow batteries have many appealing properties in the application of large-scale energy storage. The large chemical tunability of organic electrolytes shows great potential to improve the performance of flow batteries. Computational studies at the quantum-mechanics level are very useful for guiding experiments, but in previous studies, explicit water interactions and thermodynamic effects were ignored. Here, we applied the computational electrochemistry method based on ab initio molecular dynamics and thermodynamic integration to calculate redox potentials of quinones and their derivatives. The calculated results are in excellent agreement with experimental data. We mixed side chains to tune their reduction potentials and found that solvation interactions and entropy effects play a significant role in side-chain engineering. On the basis of our calculations, we proposed several high-performance negative and positive electrolytes. Our first-principles study paves the way toward the development of large-scale and sustainable electrical energy storage.

    The development of renewable energy sources, such as solar and wind, to replace current fossil fuels is a very pressing task to enable sustainable development. A big challenge in integrating solar or wind power into electrical grids is the mismatch between electricity supply and demand, which can be solved by scalable electrical energy storage. (1) To buffer the volatility of solar and wind energy, redox flow batteries have shown many appealing features, including scalability, decoupled energy and power output, high depth of discharging, and rapid responsiveness. (2) In addition, if water is used as a solvent to dissolve electrolytes, the safety of flow batteries is greatly enhanced because water is nonflammable and has an unusually large heat capacity.
    Since the 1970s, the vanadium redox flow battery has been extensively studied and is considered to be the closest to being widely used commercially. (3,4) However, the low abundance, high cost, and toxicity of vanadium impede its further application in large-scale energy storage. (5,6) Recently, organic electrolytes have emerged as promising alternatives to vanadium in aqueous flow batteries. (7) In particular, quinones and their derivatives, such as benzoquinones (BQ), naphthoquinones (NQ), and anthraquinones (AQ) in Figure 1, exhibit many appealing properties as redox couples. (8−12) They have no rare metal elements, do not need precious metal catalysts, and have great potential for large-scale industrial production. Their large chemical tunability makes it possible to optimize their performance. In addition, retired organic flow batteries do not need heavy metal removal, and supercritical water oxidation, a well-established green chemistry technology, may be used to dispose of retired organic flow batteries. (13,14)
    The performance of organic flow batteries largely depends on three important properties of electrolytes: redox potential, solubility, and chemical stability. Molecular engineering of side chains is an effective method for tailoring the three properties of quinones and their derivatives. For example, Huskinson et al. showed that the open circuit voltage increases by 11% upon addition of two -OH groups to 9,10-anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonic acid (AQDS). (8) Most of the current experimental studies are limited to some halogen, -OH, or -SO3X (X = H or Na) functionalized quinones, but there are hundreds of thousands of possible quinone/hydroquinone pairs. (15) Computational methods at the quantum-mechanics level can be used to evaluate the performance of quinones. Er et al. used a high-throughput computational approach to study the redox potential and solubility of quinones, which are either singly or fully substituted by one functional group. (15) Tabor et al. used a combination of density functional theory (DFT) and semiempirical calculations to investigate the chemical stability of quinones. (16) In those fast-screening methods, water was treated as an implicit medium and thermodynamic effects were ignored. For some functionalized quinone structures, the experimental and predicted redox potentials differ considerably. (10)
    Here, we chose a variety of functionalized quinone couples with potentially good solubility and chemical stability and applied ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations to calculate their redox potentials. Water molecules were simulated at the atomic level, and no empirical parameter was used. The calculated results agree very well with experimental values for existing quinones. We mixed side chains to tune the redox potential and found that when there are multiple side-chain substitutions, our AIMD results differ from those obtained by the previous model, indicating that explicit water and entropy effects are important in side-chain engineering. We predicted five low-potential negolytes (negative electrolytes) and four high-potential posolytes (positive electrolytes). Our work sheds new light on improving the performance of aqueous organic flow batteries.
    The standard reduction potential of the quinone/hydroquinone (Q/H2Q) couple versus the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) is given by E0=−ΔG°2F, where F is the Faraday constant and ΔG° is the Gibbs free energy of the two-electron, two-proton reaction:
    Q(aq)+H2(g)→H2Q(aq)
    (1)
    We applied the computational electrochemistry method based on AIMD and thermodynamic integration (17−19) (see the Supporting Information for computational details) to calculate E0 for quinones with explicit water molecules using the BLYP, (20,21) PBE, (22) and HSE06 (23,24) exchange-correlation functionals with the D3 van der Waals correction. (25) In Figure 2, we compare our calculated results with experimental data. Both BLYP and PBE functionals underestimate E0, and the PBE values are better than BLYP values. The HSE06 functional gives the best result, whose mean absolute error with respect to experimental data is 0.028 V, similar to the standard deviation (∼0.02 V) from our AIMD simulations. It is known that the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) functionals have a charge localization error that is larger than that of the hybrid functionals, (26) so HSE06 describes the charge-state change in the redox reaction (eq 1) better than BLYP or PBE. In the following part, we mainly used the HSE06 functional to calculate electronic structure.
    Note that the standard potential E0 is obtained at pH 0. According to the Nernst equation, the reduction potential in reaction 1 as a function of pH is (27)
    E=E0−kBT⁡ln⁡10epH
    (2)
    where kB is the Blotzmann constant, T is the temperature, and e is the elementary charge, so the measured potential should decrease linearly with pH. However, the experimental Pourbaix diagram in Figure 3 seems to contain several line segments. The reason is that how the redox reaction happens depends on pH. Figure 3 shows the possible reaction steps involving quinones. We need the dissociation constant of the two reactions below to judge the actual redox reaction:
    pKa1:H2Q(aq)→HQ−(aq)+H+(aq)
    (3)
    pKa2:HQ−(aq)→Q2−(aq)+H+(aq)
    (4)
    The acid dissociation constant is given by
    pKa=−log10[X−][H+][HX]=pH−log10[X−][HX]
    (5)
    where X– denotes HQ– or Q2– and HX is H2Q or HQ–. According to Pauling’s rule for oxoacids, successive pKa values usually increase, so pKa2 is larger than pKa1. When the solution is very acidic and the pH is smaller than pKa1, the concentration of H2Q is higher than that of HQ– or Q2–, so the main reduction reaction is
    Q(aq)+2H+(aq)+2e−→H2Q(aq)
    (6)
    When the pH is between pKa1 and pKa2, the concentration of HQ– is higher than that of H2Q or Q2–, so the main reduction reaction becomes
    Q(aq)+H+(aq)+2e−→HQ−(aq)
    (7)
    When the pH is larger than pKa2, Q2– will be the dominating electrolyte in the solution, so the main reduction reaction is
    Q(aq)+2e−→Q2−(aq)
    (8)
    If in a reduction reaction there are m protons removed and n electrons transferred, the pH-dependent reduction potential in eq 2 becomes
    E=E0−mkBT⁡ln⁡10nepH
    (9)
    so the slopes in the Pourbaix diagram are −kBT⁡ln⁡10e, −kBT⁡ln⁡102e, and 0 corresponding to reactions 6–8, respectively.
    We take AQDS as an example and performed first-principles simulations to calculate the Pourbaix diagram as shown in Figure 3. E0 in reactions 6–8 was calculated to be 0.245, 0.010, and −0.302 V, respectively. The calculated pKa1 and pKa2 are 8.363 and 10.525, respectively (see the Supporting Information), so our calculated Pourbaix diagram contains three segments: pH < 8.363, 8.363 < pH < 10.525, and pH > 10.525. The line slopes are −0.060 V/pH, −0.030 mV/pH, and 0, respectively. Figure 3 shows that the calculated curve agrees very well with the experimental result, which together with the results in Figure 2 gives us confidence to study a variety of quinones and derivatives.
    We apply side-chain engineering to tailor the redox potential of quinone derivatives. The reduction potential of parent quinones has the following order: BQ > NQ > AQ. AQ is often chosen as the backbone of negolytes, and BQ as the backbone of posolytes. We considered five common side chains (-SO3H, -PO3H2, -COOH, -OH, and -NH2), which were predicted to help to dissolve quinones in water. (15) The first three are electron-withdrawing groups (EWGs), and the last two are electron-donating groups (EDGs). Generally, we found that EWGs increase E0, while EDGs decrease E0. In addition, the position of EDGs strongly affects E0. When EDGs are next to the C═O group, E0 decreases more. Our findings are consistent with those of previous studies. (15) There are no experimental data for the -PO3H2, -COOH, and -NH2 substitutions. Our AIMD simulations show that the -PO3H2 group increases E0 slightly more than the -SO3H and -COOH groups do, and the -NH2 group decreases E0 more than -OH does, which were not reported in previous theoretical studies.
    In previous calculations, (15)E0 was calculated by a linear function
    E0=−ΔHfnF+b
    (10)
    where ΔHf is the DFT total energy change of reaction 1 in the gas phase and b is a constant fitted from the experimental E0 data of parent quinones. In Figure 4, we compared the gas-phase model results with our AIMD ones; the mean absolute error is 0.097 V, nearly 3 times as large as that between the experimental and HSE06 results as shown in Figure 2. In particular when there are multiple side-chain substitutions, the difference becomes larger (see also Table Supporting Information). Note that only parent quinones without side chains were used to fit eq 10, (8,15) so side-chain effects may not be captured well.
    In the gas-phase model calculation, we first relax the molecular structure and then calculate the DFT energy. Side chains have freedom to rotate and vibrate; however, the structural relaxation often can only find a local minimum, so depending on the initial configuration, E0 obtained by eq 10 may be very different for quinone isomers. For example, upon variation of the orientation of the O–H bonds in 1,8-dihydroxyanthraquinone [DH(1,8)AQ, dantron], E0 can change by as much as 0.560 V. We may try to find the global minimum structure in the gas phase by starting from different initial configurations, but because of the presence of water, the obtained structure may not be the stable one in the solution. At finite temperature, the rotation and vibration of side chains in water contribute to the entropy part of the free energy of quinones, which, as well as solvation interactions, is included in AIMD simulations, but not in the gas-phase model. Our results suggest that they are important for determining E0. A great advantage of quinones is that we can engineer side chains to optimize their performance. In the case of multiple side-chain substitutions, a general computational method that can properly take into account the solvation interactions and entropy contributions of side chains is essential for making accurate predictions.
    We first mixed the EDGs and EWGs to functionalize AQ to work as negolytes. To increase the open circuit voltage, we look for quinones whose reduction potentials are close to 0 V, but E0 cannot be negative; otherwise H2 gas will be produced in the charging process. We placed EDGs away from the C═O groups in AQ, because previous experimental studies suggest that if EDGs are too close, the solubility is poor. (10)Figure 5 shows five negolyte candidates with potentially high performance, whose E0 is between 0 and 0.1 V.
    It is more challenging to find suitable organic posolytes, because molecules with high reduction potentials are usually not chemically stable in aqueous solutions; for example, as electron deficient molecules, they may react with water via nucleophilic addition. (10,16,28) Here, to enhance chemical stability, we replace all of the C–H groups adjacent to the C═O groups that are susceptible to oxidation with C-PO3H2 or C-COOH groups, as shown in Figure 6. The reduction potentials of fully substituted BQ are slightly lower than 1 V and similar to that of 1,2-benzoquinone-3,5-disulfonic acid [1,2-BQDS(3,5)] reported in the previous experimental study (9) and subject to the Michael reaction. (28) Interestingly, if there are two -PO3H2-group substitutions in 1,2-benzoquinone-3,5-diphosphonic acid [1,2-BQDP(3,5)], the reduction potential can be as high as 1.051 V, which means that E0 does not increase monotonically with the number of EWGs. Through the Michael reaction, the C–H groups on the ring of 1,2-BQDP(3,5) or 1,2-BQDS(3,5) may be substituted with C-OH groups, which will lower their reduction potentials. Thus, there is a trade-off between reduction potential and chemical stability in designing organic posolytes using quinones.
    In summary, we applied the computational electrochemistry method based on ab initio molecular dynamics and thermodynamic integration to calculate the redox potential of quinones and their derivatives. The calculated standard reduction potentials and the theoretical Pourbaix diagram are in excellent agreement with experimental results. We compared our method with the gas-phase model used in previous studies and found that when there are multiple side-chain substitutions, our AIMD results may differ considerably from those obtained by the previous model, indicating that solvation interactions and entropy effects are very important in side-group engineering of quinones. On the basis of our calculations, we proposed five low-E0 negolytes and four high-E0 posolytes.
    The large chemical tunability of quinones shows great potential to improve the performance of aqueous organic redox flow batteries. The computational method shown here may be used to select high-performance electrolyte candidates and accelerate the trial-and-error process in experiments. Our first-principles study paves the way toward the development of large-scale and sustainable electrical energy storage.

    Methods

    Ab Initio Molecular Dynamics. We performed ab intio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations using the CP2K Quickstep package. (29,30) We used Goedecker–Teter–Hutter (GTH) norm-conserving pseudopotentials to describe core electrons (31,32) and a triple-ζ doubly polarized (TZV2P) basis set to expand the Kohn–Sham orbitals of valence electrons. The electron density cutoff is 340 Ry. We used deuterium instead of hydrogen to make use of a larger time step, which is 0.5 fs. One quinone molecule and 49–58 water molecules are in a cubic simulation box with periodic boundary conditions (see Table SI in the Supporting Information). In canonical ensemble (i.e., NVT) simulations, the temperature is 330 K, controlled by the Bussi–Donadio–Parrinello thermostat (τ = 100 fs). (33) We applied the BLYP exchange-correlation functional and the D3 van der Waals (vdW) correction (25) to generate AIMD trajectories, (34) which were used in the following PBE+D3 and HSE06+D3 calculations for the total energies of reactants and products in thermodynamic integration (TI). Each trajectory is at least 5 ps, and in the TI calculations, vertical energy gaps were calculated every 100 AIMD steps. The convergence tests are shown in the Supporting Information. In HSE06 hybrid functional calculations, we used the auxiliary density matrix method (ADMM) (35) to reduce computational costs and the FIT3 auxiliary basis set.
    Classical Molecular Dynamics. We performed classical molecular dynamics simulations using the GROMACS package (36) to prepare initial configurations for AIMD. We used the Gromos 54A7 force field (37) and the SPC/E water model. (38) The force field parameters for quinone molecules were generated by the Automated force field Topology Builder (ATB). (39) We built the simulation box by doubling the box lengths along the x, y, and z directions in AIMD simulations. We carried out 1 ns NPT simulations at 1 bar to determine the size of the simulation boxes and then 500 ps NVT simulations before AIMD simulations.
