    Abstract

    Redox flow batteries (RFB) based on organic redox active species have low-cost potential for large-scale electrical energy storage. Currently, there are a range of organic molecules as candidates for the negative side of the RFB: e.g., anthraquinones and alloxazines. However, for the positive side there are no obvious organic molecules mainly because of stability that is too low and to a lesser extent a low standard potential. Currently, a stable performance has been achieved only with inorganic or organo-inorganic species at the positive side: e.g., bromine, ferricyanide, and water-soluble derivatives of ferrocene. Previous attempts to utilize hydroquinones at the positive side, such as 4,5-dibenzoquinone-1,3-benzenedisulfonate, or water-soluble derivations of 2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-1-piperidinyloxy resulted in unstable battery performance. On the basis of the available literature it appears as if unsubstituted and partially unsubstituted hydroquinones are not stable in water. For this reason a new fully substituted hydroquinone was specifically designed and examined for the positive side of the organic RFB in a symmetric RFB setup. It has four fully protonated morpholino groups, a solubility of 2 M (107 Ah/L) in water, and a formal redox potential of 0.89 VNHE; however, its performance is highly dependent on the pH and the electrolyte composition.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Research on redox flow batteries (RFB) has for the past years increased focus on organic redox molecules that potentially offer tailored properties, such as solubility and redox potential, from side-group exchange on the parent molecules. (1) Organic redox molecules can be designed to have a certain redox potential: one or more positive or negative charges that increase solubility and will make them suitable for use with either cation or anion exchange membranes. Furthermore, organic compounds are abundant and can be synthesized, and costs are primarily determined by the complexity and scale of the synthesis. (2) (1)
    Organic redox species in RFB were initially introduced in 2010 with 4,5-dibenzoquinone-1,3-benzenedisulfonate (TIRON) as a water-soluble cathode material and Pb/PbSO4 as anode. (3) This has been followed by a dramatic increase in work in the field, as discussed in recent reviews. (2,4) Briefly, derivatives of anthraquinones, viologen, phenazines, and aloxazines were applied as anolyte materials, while ferricyanide and derivatives of 2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-1-piperidinyloxy, hydroquinone, and ferrocene were applied as catholyte materials. (5−14) This work is focused on the development of a new water-soluble hydroquinone for the use in RFBs. There is a general consensus that up to now many promising organic molecules for the anolyte side with sufficiently low redox potential have been demonstrated, but for the positive side no stable molecules have been identified. (2)
    Hydroquinone derivatives can be used both as catholyte and anolyte materials; however, all of the unsubstituted carbon atoms in the aromatic ring are highly reactive in water and prone to Michael addition reaction with water. (15,16) If these unsubstituted carbon atoms are blocked, this could lead to more stable molecules, in both acidic and alkaline solutions, as with hydroxylated TIRON and polymerized dihydroxybenzoquinone, respectively. (16−20) However, when these carbon atoms are blocked with electron-donating groups, such as −OH and alkyl groups, this inevitably leads to the reduction of the redox potential of the parent molecule, from 0.75 to about 0.3–0.4 V. In practice this makes them almost useless as catholytes, (15,17) because low cell potential results in lower stack power density and efficiency and increased costs. (21,22) To circumvent this unwanted effect, a strategy can be to block these positions with electron-withdrawing groups, whereby the redox potential should remain unchanged or even increase.
    From a cost perspective, hydroquinones are attractive and they can, in general, be dissolved in both acidic and alkaline solutions up to 4 M (204 Ah/L). (17,18) It is noted here that such high solubility is likely not practical in use with state of the art ion exchange membranes because hydroquinones are small molecules (100–200 g mol–1) which can permeate the membranes and their permeability is increased even further at high ionic strengths, where ion exchange membranes become less selective. (23,24) In this work, a new type of positively charged water-soluble hydroquinone catholyte has been synthesized for its use as a catholyte in redox flow batteries with an anion exchange membrane as a separator. All four unsubstituted carbons were protected with electron-withdrawing morpholino groups which were protonated to create a chloride salt (Figure 1), with a solubility of 2 M. The compound has been electrochemically characterized and used in symmetric redox flow battery tests. (25)

    2.1. Synthesis of Tetramorpholinohydroquinone

    We used a synthesis procedure similar to that first described by Fields et al. (26) Our starting materials were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Co. and were used without further purification. Morpholine (95 g, 1.10 mol) and paraformaldehyde (28 g, 1.00 mol) were combined in a mixture of toluene (140 mL) and triethylene glycol monomethyl ether (140 g) (step 1, Scheme 1). A 100 mL portion of the water/toluene mixture from step 1 was distilled, and then a solution of hydroquinone (22 g, 0.20 mol) dissolved in 10 mL of methanol was added slowly (step 2, Scheme 1), followed by heating of the reaction mixture to 150 °C in an oil bath for 20 h while it was kept in a gentle reflux. Afterward, it was cooled and the precipitate was collected and washed with diethyl ether to give 37.5 g (37%) of a white powder.
    1H NMR (CDCl3): δ 2.503 (s, 16H), 3.667 (s, 24H), 10.992. 13C NMR (CDCl3): δ 52.336 (s), 54.118 (s), 66.399 (s), 120.943 (s), 149.197. ESI-MS: 507 m/z (+).

    2.2. NMR Measurements and LC-MS Measurements

    NMR experiments were performed at 500 MHz (1H) on a Varian VNMR SYSTEM spectrometer. Chemical shifts are referenced to the residual solvent signals: 2.50 ppm for 1H in DMSO-d6, and 7.28 ppm for 1H in CDCl3. The LC-MS measurements were performed independently on two separate instruments: (i) a Shimadzu LCMS2020 System using positive and negative dual ion source (DUIS±) and electrospray ionization (ESI±) and (ii) LC-UV/vis-ESI-MS analytical equipment comprised of a Dionex Ultimate 3000 UHPLC and diode array detector interfaced with a HESII electrospray ion source to a Thermo Finnigan LTQ-XL linear ion-trap mass spectrometer. The chromatographic separation was performed with a Phenomenex RPC18 Kinetic analytical 10 cm column with an internal diameter of 2.1 mm (Product No. 00D-4462-AN). A 25 min gradient elution was performed with a flow of 0.4 mL/min and application of solvents of (A) 10 mM ammonium formiate adjusted to pH 8.3 with NH3 and (B) acetonitrile. Equilibration time was 4 min with 100% A elution before sample injection. One minute after injection B was increased to 5%, linearly increased to 33% in 10 min, and linearly increased to 77% after 17 min and finally to 100% B after 20 min followed by a 1 min return to 100% A. The HESII probe and the heated ESI capillary temperatures were both 270 °C.

    2.3. Water Solubility Measurements

    Here a procedure provided by OECD was used. (27) Four 0.1 g samples of TMHQ and monoprotic strong acid (HCl 37%, VWR Chemicals, AnalaR NORMAPUR) were mixed in molar ratios of 1:1, 1:2, 1:3, and 1:4 to give the mono (TMHQ+)-, di (TMHQ2+)-, tri (TMHQ3+)- and tetraprotonated (TMHQ4+) forms (Figure 1), respectively. These mixtures of TMHQ and HCl were then diluted with portions of 50 μL of MilliQ water (18.3 MΩ/cm) and rigorously mixed, at room temperature, using a vortex mixer until complete solubility was observed.

    2.4. Titration of Aqueous Solutions of TMHQ2+, TMHQ3+, and TMHQ4+

    A Metrohm 916 Ti-Touch autotitrator (Metrohm) was used in dynamic titration mode to measure pH titration curves and corresponding pK values. Aqueous 0.01 M solutions were prepared in Milli-Q water by mixing the clean white powder of TMHQ with 4 mol equiv of HCl. NaOH (0.05 M) was used as a titrant, which had been calibrated against 0.1 M HCl Sigma-Aldrich standard solution.

    2.5. Cyclic Voltammetry

    Cyclic voltammograms (CVs) were recorded with a CH Instruments potentiostat (CHI660E) using a standard three-electrode configuration consisting of an Ag/AgCl (3 M KCl) reference electrode (CHI111) with 0.210 VNHE, a platinum-wire counter electrode (CHI115), and 5 mm glassy-carbon (GC) electrode (CHI104). CVs were recorded in 1 M HCl (pH 0), 0.2 M glycine/HCl buffer (pH 2.7), 0.4 M acetate buffer (pH 4.3), 0.03 M phosphate buffer in 1 M NaCl (pH 7), 1 M KOH (pH reduced to 13.6 due to carbonization), and 1 M NH4Cl. The glassy-carbon electrode was thoroughly polished with polishing pads and aluminum oxide powders with different particle sizes (1, 0.3, and 0.05 μm) each for 2 min on individual polishing pads. The solutions were purged with nitrogen 10 min before electrochemical measurements.

    2.6. Rotating Disk Electrode Measurements

    Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) studies were carried out at room temperature for 1 mM TMHQ solutions at different protonation degrees at four different pHs (0, 2.7, 4.3, and 7) using a rotating disk electrode system (RDE710, GAMRY instruments). The electrode was rotated at 200, 300, 400, 600, 800, 1200, and 1600 rpm.     2.7. Symmetric Cell Redox Flow Battery Tests

    A homemade electrochemical cell consisted of two stainless steel push plates, two 5 mm thick Viton sheets that insulated the push plates from the current collectors, two gold-plated copper plates (current collectors), and graphite blocks with interdigitated flow fields (6.25 cm2, seven channels which depth was 1 mm and thickness of walls between channels 0.7 mm). (28) A Biologic VSP300 Potentiostat with a current booster was used to perform the tests. Carbon papers were heat-pretreated for 5 h at 500 °C, and the membrane was soaked for 24 h in 1 M NaCl before use.
    Two types of tests were made in the electrochemical cell, as follows.

    2.7.1. Oxidation of TMHQ4+ To Form Equimolar Mixture of Its Quinone and Hydroquinone Forms

    A 50 mL solution of 0.1 M TMHQ4+ in 1 M NH4Cl was prepared by reacting 1 mol equiv of TMHQ and 4 mol equiv of either HCl or citric acid and then oxidized against 1 M HCl on platinized paper to get 1:1 molar ratio of hydroquinone to quinone form (50% state of charge (SOC)). The electrochemical cell was assembled using three carbon papers (Sigracet 29AA, 190 um) on the TMHQ4+ side and platinized carbon papers (0.2 mg/cm2 20% Platinum on Vulcan Carbon Paper Electrode, 235 um) on the 1 M HCl side. Platinized papers facilitate the evolution of H2 on the cathode side. A Fumasep FAD55 anion exchange membrane separated the two half-cells. Viton gaskets (0.5 mm) were used to seal the cell. This setup allows for clean oxidation of TMHQ4+ that avoids pollution coming from the cathode side. In total 0.005 mol of e– was drawn, corresponding to 482 C in constant voltage mode at 0.775 V vs hydrogen.

    2.7.2. Symmetric Cell RFB tests. (25)

    A solution from section 2.7.1 was taken. Both half-cells were comprised of 0.05 M TMHQ4+ (hydroquinone form) and 0.05 M TMQ4+ (quinone form) in 1:1 mol equiv ratio in 1 M NH4Cl. However, one tank, the capacity-limiting side (CLS), contained 15 mL while the non-capacity-limiting side (NCLS) contained 20 mL of the solution. The two half-cells were assembled using three Sigracet 29AA carbon papers (190 um) on both sides and sealed with a 0.5 mm thick Viton gasket and separated by the FAD55 membrane. Two tests were made, with difference being the acid used to form a TMHQ salt: (i) HCl (37% VWR) and (ii) citric acid (99% Sigma-Aldrich). The CLS has a theoretical capacity of 80 mAh (289.5 C). The symmetric RFB was cycled in constant voltage mode between 0.3 and −0.3 V, where the current cutoffs were 3 and −3 mA, respectively.

    3.1. Water Solubility of Tetramorpholinohydroquinone

    Tetramorpholinohydroquinone (TMHQ) with a molar mass of 506.63 g/mol is a white powder with poor solubility in water in its nonprotonated form. Increasing the protonation degree of the four morpholino groups increases the solubility up to 2 M for TMHQ4+ (Figure 1). The solubilities of TMHQ2+ and TMHQ3+ are in the range 0.05–2 M, while that of TMHQ+ is below 0.025 M. In terms of capacity 2 M corresponds to 107 Ah/L, which is potentially 2.5 times higher than that of standard 1.6 M vanadium electrolyte considering that two electrons are involved in the redox reaction. The fully protonated compound, as a chloride salt, makes a bright yellow solution, a color similar to that of methyl viologen aqueous solution, that turns brownish after long exposure to ambient air.

    3.2. pK Values of Morpholine Groups in TMHQ

    According to the structure of TMHQ (Figure 1) a total of six pK values can be expected in the fully protonated and reduced form, four from morpholine groups and two from phenols. Phenols usually deprotonate at a pH above 10. The solubility data indicated that it might be challenging to titrate TMHQ+, as it is sparingly soluble, while TMHQ is insoluble in water.

During the third deprotonation the solution starts to become turbid, and at pH ∼8 a heavy precipitation of white powder occurs (Figure 2B), which results in a drop in pH and the solution becomes orange. The precipitation hinders the measurement of the fourth pK value; nevertheless, given that exactly 1 mol of OH– had been used per charged morpholine group, this allowed ua to extrapolate the fourth pK value as shown in Figure 2A.     3.3. Cyclic Voltammetry of TMHQ+, TMHQ2+, TMHQ3+, and TMHQ4+

    Figure 4 shows the cyclic voltammetry of TMHQx+ at pH values (0, 2.7, 4.3, and 7) where only one species is dominant in concentration. The electrochemical performance and the redox potential of the compound are highly dependent on the pH: i.e., the protonation degree of the compound. Ideally for a two-electron process, the peak separation is 29.5 mV at 25 °C; however, we measured 650, 38, 35, and 49 mV at 10 mV/s for TMHQ4+, TMHQ3+, TMHQ2+, and TMHQ+, respectively.
    The compound demonstrates the lowest peak separation at pH 4.3, when most of the species are in the TMHQ2+ form (Figure 3) and the highest separation at pH 0, when nearly all are in the TMHQ4+ form. Here we define voltammograms for TMHQ4+ as irreversible (Table S1.1 in the Supporting Information). On the other hand, other species at higher pHs show quasi-reversible electrochemical performance(Figure 4, Tables S1.2–S1.4 in the Supporting Information). At pH 0, the measured formal redox potential is 0.89 V vs SHE. In Figure 5 a Pourbaix diagram of TMHQx+ species is presented, where x = 4, 3, 2, 1, and ideally for a two-electron/two-proton process the slope should be 59 mV/pH unit at 25 °C. Indeed, this was experimentally verified at pH between 2.7 and 7. Using the slope and measured formal potential at pH 7, a redox potential at pH 0 is estimated as 0.75 VNHE. A formal potential of 0.75 VNHE is a value that would be normally expected for a hydroquinone species with no electron-donating groups attached to the central ring. (15) However, the measured potential at pH 0 is 140 mV more positive. The higher value could have been caused by the four protonations of the morpholino groups, which act more electron withdrawing when all four are protonated.
    One could argue then that another interesting property of quinones was observed: a redox potential can be increased by increasing the number of positive charges on the parent hydroquinone ring. It is not clear though why is it difficult to oxidize the hydroquinone ring at pH 0: i.e., why the kinetics are so sluggish at pH 0 on a glassy-carbon electrode. A plausible cause is a poor proton conductivity of this large molecule due to large pK values of morpholino groups: i.e., the morpholino groups do not easily release protons, and so incoming protons cannot really hop in and hop out, as the “morpholino” proton is sitting fast. During the oxidation of phenol groups TMHQ4+ protons are released, and to complete the oxidation protons must be transported to the bulk solution (eq 3). If this is hindered for any reason, then the oxidation would be hindered.

    3.5. Symmetric Battery Tests with TMHQ4+ and Post Run Analysis

    RFB battery tests for 0.1 M TMHQCl4 in 1 M NH4Cl are presented in Figure 7. First the battery was charged against hydrogen up to 50% SOC, and then the solution was split to capacity-limiting and non-capacity-limiting sides (CLS and NCLS). A value of 92% of the theoretical capacity of CLS is reached in the first charge cycle, after which four to five stable cycles were recorded; at this point the capacity started to decay. The capacity retention was 33% after 100 cycles. The same test was performed for another salt, 0.1 M TMHQ(dihydrogen citrate)4, and there only 1 mAh out of 80 mAh could be charged and discharged.
    The symmetric RFB setup used here allows us to minimize the effect of the crossover and increase resistance of the cell to capacity loss. (32,33) This is due to the fact that the same species are found on both sides of the membranes and the battery is charged and discharged in constant voltage mode having very low currents used as cutoff values (Figure 7B). Other mechanisms of capacity loss that might be present in a symmetric cell are (i) electrolyte unbalance on a CLS which may occur due to the external oxidation (oxygen permeation) or internal reduction (redox reaction with carbon paper in the cell) (25,34,35) and (ii) chemical instability of the species, due to degradation. (19) For example it is well-known that the V(II) cation is particularly sensitive to external oxidation; however, this is also the case with anthraquinones, which is why such symmetric battery tests were performed in a glovebox. (25) (35) We took all of the precautions to minimize the effect of external oxidation: e.g., we used a tank in tank setup, where the external tank was constantly flushed with nitrogen, Teflon and Neoprene pipes, Teflon connections, and Viton gaskets. Nevertheless it is acknowledged here that only the tests performed in the glovebox could completely eliminate the effect of external oxidation. With respect to internal reduction, i.e. reaction of the redox species with carbon paper, this was first reported recently, where ferricyanide (0.44 VNHE) was reduced to ferrocyanide, receiving the electrons from carbon paper. The symmetric battery setup was essentially the same as that used here, and they report a steep capacity loss, similar to that in Figure 7, although ferrocyanide and ferricyanide are chemically robust. (25) In order to deduce whether the capacity loss observed in Figure 5 is due to the electrolyte imbalance or chemical instability, we performed a post run analysis of the electrolytes using LC-MS.

    4. Conclusion

    We have successfully synthesized and characterized a fully substituted hydroquinone with a maximum redox potential of 0.89 VNHE at pH 0, which has not been reported so far in the literature. The compound has four protonation degrees, and it shows different electrochemical performance at different protonations. TMHQ4+ has a solubility of 2 M; however, it is electrochemically irreversible on a carbon electrode in this form. When TMHQ was tested in its 3+ and 2+ forms, it showed quasi-reversible electrochemical performance and good electrode kinetics. Battery cycling data exhibits 70% of capacity loss during 100 cycles. We have performed a LC-MS post run analysis, and the data strongly indicate that the capacity loss was mainly due to the electrolyte unbalance, which most likely occurred due to electrochemical reduction of the corresponding quinone to its hydroquinone form by a carbon paper. However, a control experiment with carbon paper has not been performed to verify this conclusion. Electrolyte composition has a large influence on the chemical stability and battery cycling of the compound. A severe chemical degradation was observed for the TMHQ3+ citrate salt at a pH of around 3, while it was minimal, yet present, for the TMHQ chloride salt at pH 1.7. It is shown here that full substitution of the hydroquinone ring leads to a good electrochemical performance; however, a more careful choice of substituent groups is recommended to get chemically more robust compounds for a better battery performance.
