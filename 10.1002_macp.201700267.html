Abstract

A phthalimide-containing redox-active polymer is designed and synthesized in particular for the application in all-organic redox-flow batteries. 4-Vinylbenzyl phthalimide is prepared in a one-step reaction and copolymerized with a solubility promoting comonomer. The novel polymer is electrochemically characterized in various electrolytes via cyclic voltammetry and features a quasireversible reduction reaction at E0½ = −1.89 V versus AgNO3/Ag. Afterward, the polymer is applied as anode-active material in a first preliminary battery test in combination with a 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperdinyl-N-oxyl-containing polymer as cathode-active material and a porous membrane as separator, yielding a polymer-based all-organic battery system with a high cell voltage of 2.0 V.

1 Introduction

The amount of electricity produced from renewable sources like wind power and solar energy increases continuously. However, the electricity production from these sources is intermittent and energy storage technologies are necessary to guarantee a continuous electricity supply and a stable grid operation.1 A suitable storage system for “green” electricity is the redox-flow battery (RFB), which consist of an electrochemical cell and tanks for the storage of the redox-active electrolyte.2-4 This design enables a superior flexibility in comparison to solid-electrode batteries; capacity (size of the storage tanks) and power (size of the electrochemical cell) can be scaled independently of each and thus can be precisely adapted to the associated generator unit. Furthermore, RFBs can feature a reasonable cost advantage to lithium ion batteries.3-6 In addition, RFBs can be constructed in the form of sea containers; by this manner an easy transportation and flexibility of the battery system is given, which benefits the usage as decentralized energy supply in remote regions. Conventional flow battery technologies rely on expensive and toxic redox-active materials, e.g., vanadium or bromine. These drawbacks can be overcome with the utilization of organic-active materials.5-19 In particular, the polymer-based RFB (pRFB) offers cost-efficient active materials as well as the utilization of a low-priced membrane and features an additional cost advantage.20 Nevertheless, current well-operating pRFB systems are limited to a cell voltage of 1.2 and 1.6 V.13, 14, 20 Accordingly, the energy density can be increased with a higher cell voltage that can be achieved by the utilization of an organic anode-active material with a low redox-potential. First attempts were conducted, but the resulting systems featured certain disadvantageous, like steep voltage curves in battery applications or elaborate synthesis effort.15, 21

N-Substituted phthalimide derivatives undergo a chemically reversible reduction reaction in cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements. Their application as charge storage material in flow batteries was first reported by Li et al.22 The authors used N-methyl phthalimide as anode material and 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperdine-N-oxyl (TEMPO) as cathode-active material. The resulting battery featured voltages of 1.65 and 1.36 V for the charging and discharging procedure, respectively. Recently, Wei et al. reported an all-organic flow battery utilizing N-methyl phthalimide and 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1-methoxy-4-[2-methoxyethoxy]benzene as anode and cathode-active material, respectively.16 The stability of this system was dependent on the used electrolyte and only in one particular electrolyte constant capacity retention was achieved. To obtain a phthalimide-containing polymer, which fulfils the requirements of pRFBs, a phthalimide styrene derivative was selected as appropriate anode-active monomer. This compound features a half-step redox potential of −1.89 V versus AgNO3/Ag. In combination with TEMPO methacrylate as cathode-active material a cell voltage of 2.2 V in a respective battery is reachable, leading to a high voltage all-organic pRFB system.

2 Experimental Section

Chemicals and Materials: Propylene carbonate (anhydrous ≥99%), acetone, N,N-dimethyl formamide (DMF), tetrahydrofuran, tetrabutylammonium iodide, tetrabutylammonium perchlorate, potassium carbonate, sodium hydride, azobisisobutyronitrile (AIBN), 4-Vinylbenzyl chloride, triethylene glycol monomethyl ether, and phenazine were purchased from commercial sources. A porous polyolefin foil was used as separator (3419s, Celgard LLC, USA). Graphite felt was cut in appropriate pieces (2.5 × 2.5 × 0.6 cm3, GFA6, SGL Carbon SE, Germany). Poly(TEMPO methacrylate-co-poly(ethylene glycol) methacrylate) was prepared as described in literature.23

Syntheses: N-Vinylbenzyl phthalimide 3

The monomer was synthesized according to literature with modifications to simplify the purification procedure.24 Phthalimide (1 g, 6.8 mmol), potassium carbonate (3.76 g, 27 mmol), and tetrabutyl ammonium iodide (25 mg, 68 µmol) were dissolved in dry acetone (25 mL) and stirred for 25 min under Ar atmosphere. 4-Vinylbenzyl chloride (1.45 g, 9.5 mmol) was slowly added to the suspension. The reaction mixture was stirred at 50 °C for 24 h. After cooling to room temperature the reaction mixture was concentrated to dryness. The remaining yellowish solid was washed with water (4 × 100 mL) and was recrystallized from methanol yielding 3 as colorless powder (1.27 g, 71%). 1H NMR (CDCl3, 300 MHz, ppm): δ = 7.75 (ddd, J = 44.0, 5.1, 3.1 Hz, 4H), 7.37 (q, J = 8.0 Hz, 4H), 6.66 (dd, J = 17.6, 10.9 Hz), 5.71 (d, J = 17.6 Hz, 1H), 5.21 (d, J = 10.9, 1H), 4.82 (s, 2H); 13C NMR (CDCl3, 75 MHz, ppm): δ = 168.03, 137.25, 136.38, 135.94, 134.02, 132.17, 128.91, 126.54, 123.37, 114.20, 41.38; high resolution ESI-MS: calculated m/z = 286.0838, found m/z = 286.0846 [M + Na+].

Vinylbenzyl triethylene glycol monomethyl ether (TEGSt) 5

Compound 5 was prepared as described in literature with slight modifications in order to increase the yield.15, 25 The reaction flask was charged with sodium hydride (203 mg, 5.1 mmol, 5 eq.), purged with argon for 20 min and afterward dry tetrahydrofuran (8 mL) was added as solvent. Triethylene glycole monomethyl ether (200 mg, 1.2 mmol, 1.2 eq.) was added dropwise; the reaction mixture was stirred for 30 min at room temperature. 4-Vinylbenzyl chloride (140 µL, 1 mmol) was diluted in 200 µL dry tetrahydrofuran and added slowly to the reaction mixture and was stirred at 60 °C for 20 h; the color of the suspension turned from gray to brown. The reaction mixture was concentrated by half and afterward unreacted sodium hydride was neutralized with diluted hydrochloric acid. The aqueous phase was extracted two times with ethyl acetate and one time with dichloromethane (100 and 10 mL, respectively). The organic phases were dried over magnesium sulfate and reduced to dryness. Purification was performed via column chromatography on silica gel and ethyl acetate/n-hexane (1:1) yielding 5 (280 mg, 1 mmol, yield: 98%) as yellowish oil. 1H NMR (CDCl3, 300 MHz, ppm): δ = 7.34 (dd, J = 25.4, 8.2 Hz, 4H), 6.70 (dd, J = 17.6, 10.9 Hz, 1H), 5.73 (dd, J = 17.6, 0.8 Hz, 1H), 5.23 (dd, J = 10.9, 0.8 Hz, 1H), 4.55 (s, 2H), 3.75–3.49 (m, 12H), 3.37 (s, 3H); 13C NMR (CDCl3, 75 MHz, ppm): δ = 137.93, 136.95, 136.56, 127.93, 126.20, 113.72, 72.94, 71.96, 70.66, 70.65, 70.55, 69.41; high resolution ESI-MS: calculated m/z = 303.1567, found m/z = 303.1569 [M + Na]+.

Poly(N-vinylbenzyl phthalimide-co-TEGSt) (P1–P4; general description)

Compounds 3, 5 and AIBN (5 mol%) were dissolved/suspended in dry dimethylformamide, with initial molar monomer ratios of 1:1, 1:2, and 1:4 (3:5) with molalities according to Table 1. The reaction mixture was purged with argon for 30 min and stirred for 24 h at 75 °C. The resulting clear solution was diluted in acetone (10 mL) and dialyzed against acetone and dichloromethane for 4 and 2 d, respectively. After concentration to dryness the polymers were obtained as colorless viscous solids. See Table 1 for polymer composition and molar mass characterization.

Electrochemical Characterization: CV and charge–discharge tests were conducted on a VMP3 potentiostat/galvanostat (Biologic, France). For CV measurements, a standard three electrode setup with a glassy-carbon disk working electrode (diameter 1.6 mm), an AgNO3/Ag reference electrode, and a platinum wire counter electrode were used. Evaluation of the diffusion coefficient D was performed via analysis of the limiting current ilim versus square root of the scan rate by using Randles–Sevcik equation with n = 1, Faraday's constant F = 96 485 C mol−1, electrode surface A of 0.02 cm2, temperature T = 302 K, gas constant R = 8.3145 VAs K−1 mol−1 and the bulk concentration c0 = 8.57 mmol L−1 of the redox-active repeating unit.

Characterization Techniques: Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) was performed on an Agilent 1200 series system (degasser, PSS; pump, G1310A; auto sampler, G1329A; oven, Techlab; DAD detector, G1315D; RI detector, G1362A; eluent, DMAc + 0.21% LiCl, 1 mL min−1; temperature, 40 °C; column, PSS GRAM guard/1000/30 Å), PSS standard. 1H and 13C NMR measurements were performed on a Bruker RC AC 300 (300 and 75 MHz) at 298 K. Chemical shifts are reported in parts per million (ppm, δ scale) referred to the residual signal of the deuterated solvent.

Battery Tests: The battery measurement was performed under Ar atmosphere in a glove box. The electrochemical cell was designed and constructed in a flat cell type with a membrane active area of 5 cm2 (JenaBatteries GmbH, Germany). For static, nonpumped charge/discharge experiments, both half cells were filled with 4 mL electrolyte solution using a syringe. The battery was charged/discharged with constant current and the resulting potential was measured over time. Potentiostatic electrochemical impedance spectroscopy was measured in the range of 100 mHz to 200 kHz. Cell resistance was determined at zero crossing.

3 Results and Discussion

3.1 Synthesis and Polymer Characterization

Redox-active polymers for flow battery applications have to feature a high solubility within the utilized electrolyte. Common solvents are organic carbonates, ethers, and in exceptional cases dimethylformamide or acetonitrile. For this reason the redox-active phthalimide monomer 3 was copolymerized with the solubility promoting vinylbenzyl triethylene glycol monomethylether (TEGSt, 5) in varying ratios. Monomer 3 was synthesized in a one-step reaction from phthalimide and 4-Vinylbenzyl chloride 1; analogously, 5 was synthesized from 1 and triethylene glycol monomethyl ether 4 (Scheme 1). The polymerization was first of all performed in bulk. 3 was dissolved in 5 at the reaction temperature of 75 °C and AIBN was used as initiator for the free radical polymerization. The ratio between the redox-active monomer and the solubility promoting comonomer was varied in the range of 1:1 to 1:4 (3:5).

However, the bulk polymerization led to an insoluble solid. Therefore, dimethylformamide was used as solvent in order to reduce the molar mass, but a mostly insoluble polymer was obtained at a high monomer molality of 19 mol g−1; therefore, the molality was further decreased to 2.6 mol g−1 to achieve molar masses of <20 kg mol−1. The reaction solution was dialyzed against acetone with dialysis tubes having a molecular weight cut-off (MWCO) of 1000 g mol−1 to remove the solvent and eventually incurred oligomers, yielding highly viscous oils. By increase of the temperature above 30 °C the viscosity decreased noticeably. Afterward, SEC measurements were performed to estimate the relative molar masses and the dispersity. The synthesized polymers revealed molar masses in the range of 6–8 (Mn) and 14 to 19 (Mw) kg mol−1 according to a polystyrene standard as well as a dispersity in the range of 2.2 and 2.4 (see Figure S1 in the Supporting Information, for SEC traces). SEC traces showed monomodal distributions. The polymer composition was determined via nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy (see Figure 1). Characteristic signals for monomer 3 and monomer 5 were selected, integrated, and were related to each other. The signals in the range of 7.8–7.6 ppm (H6 and H7) and 3.4–3.3 ppm (H11) were used for this purpose and the obtained ratios are summarized in Table 1. The polymers were obtained as highly viscous oils and a decrease in viscosity of the polymers was observed with increasing temperature. The polymers are miscible in polar solvents in every ratio. P3 was chosen for a detailed electrochemical characterization, because it features the best compromise of specific capacity, solubility and distance between the individual redox-active units.

3.2 Electrochemical Characterization

Phthalimide and its analogs are known to undergo side reactions with electrophiles in their reduced state.16, 26 In order to develop a suitable electrolyte, the redox-reaction of P3 was investigated in several electrolytes to identify the most suitable one. For this reason, the polymers were electrochemically investigated via CV measurements and a strong influence on the redox-reaction by the solvent and the supporting electrolyte was observed. CV measurements were first conducted in acetonitrile with tetrabutylammonium perchlorate (NBu4ClO4) and tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate (NBu4PF6). If NBu4ClO4 was used as supporting electrolyte an irreversible redox process accompanied with quickly decreasing peak currents were observed (Figure 2a). The peak potentials were at −1.86 and −1.38 V versus AgNO3/Ag for the reduction and consecutive reoxidation reactions in the first cycle at a scan rate of 500 mV s−1. From the third cycle onward no redox signals were detected due to a precipitate on the electrode. If NBu4PF6 was used as supporting electrolyte a quasireversible redox-reaction with peak currents at −1.89 and −1.82 V versus AgNO3/Ag was detected at a scan rate of 500 mV s−1 (Figure 2b). However, the peak potentials of both redox processes decreased rapidly with every cycle. This is an indication for either a further reaction or a forming precipitate on the working electrode. If propylene carbonate is used as a solvent in combination with NBu4PF6 as supporting electrolyte, a quasireversible redox reaction is observed (Figure 2c). Peak potentials are slowly shifting to more negative potentials with increasing scan rate for the reduction reaction, but are slowly shifting to more positive potentials with decreasing scan rate for the subsequent reoxidation. This is a first indication of a precipitation or adhesion/association of the polymer on the working electrode. However, a nearly linear function for the peak currents versus the square root of the scan rate was obtained. The redox reaction is significantly more stable in propylene carbonate compared to acetonitrile. In addition, the peak potentials are shifted to more negative potentials and are located at −2.0 and −1.87 V versus AgNO3/Ag for the cathodic and anodic process at 50 mV s−1, respectively. However, only for slow scan rates decreasing peak currents with proceeding cycle number were measured.

Because of this, the redox reaction of P3 was investigated in an electrolyte consisting of dimethyl formamide as solvent and NBu4ClO4 as supporting electrolyte. A quasireversible redox-reaction was observed with slowly increasing peak splits with increasing scan rate (Figure 3a). The peak split amounts 84 mV at a scan rate of 50 mV s−1. Peak potentials are at −1.93 and −1.85 V versus AgNO3/Ag at a scan rate of 50 mV s−1. Furthermore, a strict linear trend of the peak currents versus the square root of the scan rate was measured (Figure 3b). The slope of this linear trend was k = −2.48 10−5 A s0.5 V−0.5 and was used to calculate the diffusion coefficient using the Randles–Sevcik equation. The diffusion coefficient of P3 amounts 2.33 × 10−7 cm2 s−1 (see the Supporting Information for detailed calculation) and is similar to previously investigated redox-active polymers.13, 14, 20, 23 The redox stability of the polymer was further examined by continued cycling (Figure 3c). Stable peak currents were measured for 50 cycles and a precipitation of the redox-active polymer on the electrode surface was not observed. Further investigation of the stability of the polymer was performed via CV measurements with a hold time of 60 s at the vertex potentials of –0.5 and −2.3 V at a scan rate of 50 mV s−1 (Figure 3d). Conditions more similar to bulk electrolysis and battery operation were investigated with this experimental design. The redox reaction is still present, however, the shape of the reduction reaction is slowly shifting with increasing cycle number, but, more importantly, the subsequent reoxidation reaction is not qualitatively affected and is clearly present at every cycle. In conclusion, the redox reaction of P3 in the electrolyte consisting of DMF and NBu4ClO4 shows a promising stability and can be a suitable electrolyte for battery tests.

A preliminary all-organic battery test was performed in a nonpumped solution-based battery set-up using a porous polyolefin foil as separator. In a first experiment dimethylformamide was utilized as solvent and NBu4ClO4 as supporting electrolyte (Figure 4a). P3 was used as anode-active material and a TEMPO-containing copolymer as cathode-active material. The previously reported poly(TEMPO methacrylate-co-poly(ethylene glycol) methacrylate) was used as cathode material (see Figure S2 in the Supporting Information, for a schematic battery representation).14, 23 Well-defined charge–discharge voltage curves were obtained at consecutive charging and discharging featuring voltage limits of 2.3 and 1.2 V for charging and discharging, respectively. The mean charging voltage was 2.2 V and the mean discharging voltage was 2.1 V. This yields a voltage efficiency of 95.5%. However, a slowly decreasing capacity of the battery was observed, despite the good results from the CV measurements.

In a second test, in reference to the research findings by Wei et al., an electrolyte consisting of dimethoxyethane and LiTFSI was also investigated (Figure 1). Well-defined charge–discharge voltage curves were obtained at consecutive charging and discharging featuring voltage limits of 2.2 and 0.8 V for charging and discharging, respectively. The mean charging voltage was 2.0 V and the mean discharging voltage was 1.94 V. This yields a voltage efficiency of 97% and is an indication for a low cell resistance. This was also confirmed via potentiostatic electrochemical impedance spectroscopy and a cell resistance of 1.3 Ω (6.5 Ω cm2) was measured (Figure S3, Supporting Information). Further detailed studies will be performed in near future, to investigate the behavior of this polymer in battery applications.

Dimethoxyethane in combination with lithium bistrifluoromethanesulfonimidate represents a commonly used electrolyte in batteries utilizing phthalimide and its derivatives as redox-active materials.16, 26 The reduced phthalimide species is very reactive against positive partial charges and also solvent- or supporting electrolyte molecules that are in principle stable in the applied potential range can be attacked. However, dimethylformamide and dimethoxyethane are nonreactive against the reduced pththalimide radical anion; similar accounts to tetrabutylammonium hexafluorophosphate, -perchlorate, and lithium trifluoromethanesulfoneimidate (Figure 5).

4 Conclusion

A novel phthalimide containing copolymer in particular designed for the application in redox-flow batteries was synthesized and electrochemically investigated in this study. Several copolymers with varying comonomer composition were synthesized via free radical polymerization initiated by AIBN. N-vinylbenzyl phthalimide was utilized as redox-active monomer and was copolymerized with the solubility promoting monomer vinylbenzyl triethyleneglycol monomethyl ether. The resulting polymers featured molar masses in the range of 14–19 kg mol−1 (Mw) and a dispersity of 2.2 and 2.4 in reference to a polystyrene standard. CV measurements revealed a low reduction potential of E0½ = −1.89 V versus AgNO3/Ag reference. Systematic electrolyte analysis was performed to evaluate the influence of the solvent and the supporting electrolyte on the chemical reversibility of the phthalimide redox couple. In this respect, dimethylformamide and dimethoxyethane in combination with tetrabutyl hexafluorophosphate and lithium bistrifluoromethanesulfonimidate revealed best results on cycling stability in CV experiments. In a first preliminary all-organic battery experiment well-defined charge–discharge voltage curves were measured with a maximum mean discharging voltage of 2.2 V. Further studies will be performed to increase the cycling performance of the battery experiments.
