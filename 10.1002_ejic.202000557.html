Abstract

Organoboron analogues of viologens were synthesized and characterized structurally, photophysically, and electrochemically. Bipyridine-based N^N chelates demonstrate two reversible reduction events, demonstrating their applicability for use in redox-flow batteries.

Abstract

This work presents the use of organoboron compounds as anolytes in redox flow batteries. Through the study of their structural, electrochemical, and photophysical properties, four bipyridyl boronium salts show potential for application in such batteries. The compounds were synthesized in moderate to high yields by previously published methods. We find when using chelating bipyridyl-based substituents, the complexes undergo two reversible reductions; however, when switching to phenanthroline-based substituents, the reduction events are no longer reversible. Density-functional theory calculations indicate that the chelating nitrogen heterocycle is the locus of reduction and fluorescence.

Introduction

Interest in diquat, viologens, and similar compounds arises from the photophysical and electronic properties these purely organic molecules possess.1 Where these compounds have been explored for applications across many different fields, including redox flow batteries, medicine, photocatalysis, and sensing, examples of boronated derivatives remain underexplored. Such compounds complement conventional metal-based electronics, reducing high manufacturing costs and toxicities, while enhancing our ability to tune properties, such as solubility and electronic structure.1, 2 Of particular interest, boronium complexes bearing a bipyridine-type ligand remain underinvestigated, with only a few published reports dating back more than half a century.3-8 Most recently, Higuchi and co-workers reported the synthesis and characterization of boronium complexes bearing 2,2'-bipyridyl moiety.7 These authors report photo-induced solid-state color changes upon formation of a stable radical, and they suggest that these boronium species undergo electronic transitions similar to those of viologens and diquat. Their work explores these compounds using coordinating and active counterions such as triflate, iodide, and chloride. Further, the authors report no electrochemical data, disclosing only photophysical results and EPR spectra.

Whereas viologens and their progeny are promising as active species in redox flow batteries (RFBs),9-11 their organoboron analogues have yet to be explored for similar applications. Viologens have been extensively explored as nonaqueous electrolytes, highlighted by results from Sanford and co-workers,12 who have shown such compounds to be viable catholytes. Redox flow batteries are attractive devices for the grid-scale integration of intermittent renewable energy sources.13, 14 These batteries store energy in solvated redox-active species, termed anolytes and catholytes, allowing for the decoupling of energy and power. This decoupling allows flow batteries to be more cost-effective at scale when compared to solid-state architectures.13-15 Energy density is one of the most important factors in measuring any storage system's economic efficacy. The energy density of RFBs can be maximized by maximizing the open-circuit voltage (OCV), the concentration of redox active compounds, and the number of transferrable electrons per molecule.16 Further, techno-economic (TE) models set an equivalent weight target of 150 g mol–1 e–1 for redox active molecules.17 By introducing multiple electron transfers, we are able to reduce the equivalent weight close to 150 g mol–1 e–1. However, examples of multielectron cycling of nonaqueous anolytes remain rare. Such systems tend to have limited stability and/or solubilities on top of incomplete discharge through each redox event.11, 18 To summarize, a catholyte for nonaqueous redox flow batteries should have a highly negative reduction potential, a low molecular mass per electron transferred, and good solubility in all redox states. Sanford and co-workers have shown the viability of using diquat and viologens as catholytes in RFBs.12 Boron dipyrrins have shown promise as active species in RFBs,19 however that work has not been extended to bipyridine systems. Boron-centered molecules have shown great promise in energy related applications.20 As far as we know, organoborane adducts of nitrogen heterocycles have yet to be explored for such applications.19, 21, 22

Herein, we examine boronium bipyridyl adducts, with attention to their electronic properties using inert, non-coordinating anions. Being boron-based analogues of viologens and diquat, we chose to study derivatives of (2,2'-bipyridine-N,N')(cyclooctane-1–5-diyl)boronium hexafluorophosphate because a fundamental understanding of the structural, photophysical, and electrochemical properties of these compounds could give rise to applications in areas such as RFBs, electrochromic devices, photocatalysis, and sensing. The reversible two-step redox system when using bipyridine-based N^N chelating ligands shows these materials have promise for usage in nonaqueous RFBs.

Results and Discussion

Syntheses

Compounds 1–4 are given in Figure 1. The heterocycles 4,4'-di-tert-butyl-2,2'-bipyridine and 4,4'-di-methoxy-2,2'-bipyridine were chosen to tune the electronic properties of the boronium complex by substitution on the heterocycle backbone. Likewise, in comparison to bipyridine, 1,10-phenanthroline possesses an extended π-system and a more rigid framework. The alkylated ligand tetramethyl-1,10-phenanthroline, is more reducing than phenanthroline, and potentially more solubilizing.

Compounds 1–4 were synthesized as demonstrated in Scheme 1, by dissolving the bipy or phen ligands in toluene followed by the dropwise addition of a 1.0 m solution of B-bromo-9-BBN in dichloromethane. The reaction mixture was stirred at room temperature for 30 min, followed by anion exchange with aqueous NH4PF6. The product was then filtered and washed with water, methanol, and ether. X-ray diffraction quality crystals were grown by vapor diffusion of diethyl ether into concentrated acetone solutions.

Products were isolated as white powders. Upon exposure to light, the powders became pink. This color change occurs both under ambient atmosphere as well as an inert atmosphere. Likewise, the color change occurs both in solution and in the solid state. The solutions of all compounds are colorless prior to exposure to light. This phenomenon has been explained as resulting from the formation of air-stable radicals and thus was not further explored.7 All compounds are air and moisture stable.

The 11B NMR spectra show resonances at δ(11B) between 8.51 (1), 8.47 (2), 8.73 (3), and 12.63 (4) ppm, indicating the presence of tetracoordinate boron. 1H and 13C NMR spectra match those of authentic samples.7

Structures

Colorless diffraction-quality crystals of 1 (monoclinic, space group P21/c), 2 (monoclinic, space group P21/n), and dark red crystals of 3 (monoclinic, space group P21/c) and 4 (monoclinic, space group P21/c) were grown by vapor diffusion of diethyl ether into a concentrated acetone solution. All four compounds exhibit an essentially tetrahedral boron center. Thermal ellipsoid depictions of 2 and 4, which are representative of the compounds in this paper, appear in Figure 2. Structural depictions of 1 and 3 are reproduced as Figures S3 and S9, Supporting Information.

The chelated bipyridine-type ligands of 1 and 2 are effectively planar, with torsion angles between both pyridyl being 2.339 °C for 1 and 1.850 °C for 2. The two heterocyclic ring systems in 1 shows boron–nitrogen bond lengths that are not statistically different [B–N(1), 1.61(1) Å; B–N(2), 1.60(6) Å]. A similar conclusion pertains to the boron–nitrogen bond lengths of 2 [B–N(1), 1.61(4) Å; B–N(2), 1.60(9) Å]. The B–N bond lengths indicate dative, rather than covalent B–N bonds.23 The coordination of the small boron atom results in a slight distortion of the chelating 2,2'-bipyridine ligands. In the case of 1, the angles C(4)–C(5)–C(6) and C(5)–C(6)–C(7) on the outer frame of the aromatic system are stretched to 129.2(7) °C and 129.1(4), respectively. Consequently, the angles N(1)–C(5)–C(6) and N(2)–C(6)–C(5) are compressed to 109.6(7)°C. In both compounds 1 and 2, crystal packing shows no intermolecular interactions between molecules.

With respect to the phenanthroline-derived boron species 3 and 4, both compounds exhibit planarity across the phenanthroline substituent with torsion angles of 0.22(7)°C and 1.26(1)°C, respectively. The B–N bond lengths in compound 3 are equal at 1.63(3) Å, whereas compound 4 possesses two statistically different B–N bond lengths of 1.63(2) Å and 1.62(8) Å. Again, these bond lengths indicate dative B–N bonds. As expected, the phenanthroline derivatives do not show significant distortion across the backbone [C(6)–C(7)]. In both compounds 3 and 4, crystal packing shows no intermolecular interactions between molecules. Important bond lengths and angles are given in Table 1.

Cyclic Voltammetry

Figure 3 shows the cyclic voltammograms of 1, 2, 3, and 4 in 0.1 m solutions of TBAPF6 in acetonitrile. Table 2 gives formal electrode potentials of all events observed in the measured voltammograms. The bipyridyl species 1 and 2 both show two reversible reduction events. For compound 1, the first reduction event occurs at –1.550 V with the second occurring at –2.244 V. In changing the substituents from tBu to OMe, we see no significant shift, with reduction potentials at –1.67 V and –2.29 V. The similar structure of the voltammograms as well as the similar reduction potentials indicate substitution on the chelating bipyridyl ligand does not significantly affect the electronics of these molecules. These voltammograms are consistent with other reported boron bipyridyl species. Both 1 and 2 show an irreversible oxidation event occurring at +1.56 V and +1.39 V, respectively.

Compounds 3 and 4 show significantly different voltammograms compared to 1 and 2. Compound 3 shows irreversible reduction events at –1.42 V and –1.95 V. Introducing four methyl substituents across the phenanthroline backbone leads to a significant shift and quasi-reversibility in the reduction events of compound 4 at –1.71 V and –2.49 V. Compound 3 also shows three irreversible oxidations occurring a –0.96 V, +0.53 V and +1.58 V, whereas compound 4 shows two irreversible oxidations centered at +0.50 V and +1.52 V. The consistency of an irreversible oxidation event occurring near +1.5 V suggests that event could result from the borabicyclo[3.3.1]nonane component of the molecule. Overall, the effect of the substituents at boron on the oxidation potentials is small.

Optical Spectroscopy

The investigations of the photophysical properties were performed at room temperature in dilute solutions (~10-6 M). Compounds 1, 2, 3, and 4 are all white powders or colorless crystals in the solid state, and colorless in solution. Compounds 1 and 2 exhibit similar absorption spectra as shown in Figure 4, likewise 3 and 4 exhibit nearly identical absorption spectra. All compounds follow Beer's law. The λmax for 1 occurs at 215 nm with a molar extinction coefficient in the range of 3 × 104 m–1 cm–1. Compound 1 also possesses a shoulder at 245 nm and an additional absorption at 312 nm. Compound 2 exhibits a λmax at 231 nm with a molar extinction coefficient in the range of 4 × 104 m–1 cm–1 with a shoulder around 253 nm and a secondary absorption event at 297 nm. Upon switching from tBubpy to OMebpy, we observe a significant increase in the molar extinction coefficient as well as a red-shifting of the λmax to 231 nm. The secondary maximum conversely blue shifts to 300 nm. Because of the similarity of the two spectra, we attribute these absorption bands to π–π* transitions of the bipyridine scaffold. Both compounds 3 and 4 show high-energy absorption maxima at wavelengths below 200 nm. Both compounds show a shoulder at 223 nm and a secondary maximum at 278 nm. We attribute these high energy transitions again to π–π* transitions of the phenanthroline scaffold.

Emission measurements were collected in degassed and air-equilibrated solutions at room temperature. All four compounds show strong structureless emission between 481 and 508 nm; emission profiles are insensitive to oxygen. Photophysical data are summarized in Table 3. Normalized emission spectra of 2 appear as Figure 5. Broad, unstructured emission sets in near 425 nm and extends past 575 nm in all solvents. In the less polar media 2-methyltetrahydrofuran and dichloromethane, a high energy emission band appears for compounds 2 and 3, which is also unstructured as demonstrated in Figure 5 and Figure 6, respectively. We tentatively attribute this higher-energy feature to monomer emission in the less polar media, and the longer-wavelength emission to excimer luminescence in all solvents. Absorption and emission spectra of all four compounds show a slight red-shift in polar solvents (positive solvatochromism).

Density-Functional Theory Calculations

Static and time-resolved density-functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed on cations 1–4 and the corresponding one-electron reduced radicals. Cation 1 is representative. Its geometry was optimized with imposed C2v symmetry and found to be a minimum by harmonic frequency calculation. A re-optimization without imposed symmetry returned identical results. All calculations proceeded in continuum acetonitrile solvent. Figure 7a depicts a partial Kohn–Sham orbital energy level diagram, with plots of selected orbitals appearing in Figure 7b. The highest occupied Kohn–Sham orbital (HOMO) is delocalized across the cation, with 86 % of its density on the boronium fragment and 14 % on the di-tert-butylbipyridine ligand. The lowest unoccupied Kohn–Sham orbital (LUMO) resides on the bipyridine ligand, which accounts for 95 % of its orbital density. Both HOMO and LUMO transform as b1 in C2v symmetry. The calculated HOMO-LUMO gap, 4.30 eV, is substantial.

Geometries of the neutral radicals 1·–4· were optimized in spin-unrestricted calculations, without imposed symmetry. Results for 1· are again typical. Only the di-tert-butylbipyridine ligand experiences significant metric distortions. Figure 8 depicts line drawings of cation 1 and reduced 1·, with bond lengths in Å indicated. A frontier Kohn–Sham orbital energy level diagram of 1· appears as Figure 9a. The spin-unrestricted nature of the calculation leads to differing energies of spin-α and spin-β orbitals. The singly occupied Kohn–Sham orbital (SOMO) corresponds to the LUMO of the closed-shell cation. Unpaired spin concentrates on the di-tert-butylbipyridine ligand, Figure 9b. The energy gap between the SOMO and the highest occupied spin-β orbital is 2.78 eV, some 1.52 eV smaller than the HOMO–LUMO gap of cation 1.

Time-dependent DFT calculations on cationic 1 reveal Franck–Condon singlet excited states at energies 3.53 eV and greater, consistent with the colorless appearance of the authentic salts. The Franck–Condon S1 state consists (99 %) of a LUMO←HOMO one-particle transition, hence this state has substantial charge transfer character.24 Calculated transitions are summarized in Tables S2–S5, Supporting Information.

Calculated sums of electronic and thermal free energies were gathered for all cations and radicals. These quantities, in principle, parallel observed reduction potentials. The calculated values, which appear in Table S5, Supporting Information, capture the experimental trend in the first reduction potentials of 1–4: 4 < 2 < 1 < 3, but the calculated energy differences are poor approximations to half-wave potentials. These results indicate that 1,10-phenanthroline is the most electron-withdrawing of the four heterocycles, and 3,4,7,8-tetramethyl-1,10-phenanthroline is the most electron-releasing.

Conclusions

Herein, we describe the synthesis and characterization of four boronium bipyridyl complexes. The compounds are synthesized as previously reported.6 The four compounds are reported with crystallographic, electrochemical, and optical characterization. The new compounds can be doubly reduced through successive one-electron reductions. Electrochemical reductions of substituted bipyridine chelates are reversible, whereas phenanthrolines undergo irreversible reduction. The new complexes show unstructured fluorescence at 298 K. Density-functional theory calculations indicate that the nitrogen heterocycles are the sites of reduction. Time-dependent calculations support the hypothesis that unstructured fluorescence originates from a charge transfer (boronium→bipyridine or phenthroline heterocycle) singlet excited state. These results have implications for flow battery design: bipyridines-based compounds are likelier to offer reversible electron uptake as compared to phenanthroline ligands, despite the longer conjugation and more positive cell potentials of phenanthroline ligands. Further, derivatization of the bipyridine scaffold in the 4,4'-position allows for the tuning of properties, such as solubility, without significantly impacting the electrochemical properties. The combination of using the bipyridine scaffold along with the wide-potential window of acetonitrile demonstrates the viability of using 9-borabicyclononane bipyridyl complexes as anolytes in redox flow batteries.

Experimental Section

Materials and Methods

Anhydrous toluene, 4,4'-di-tert-butyl-2,2'-dipyridyl, 4,4'-di-methoxy-2,2'-dipyridyl, B-bromo-9-borabicyclo[3.3.1]nonane solution (1.0 m in dichloromethane), [D6]DMSO and ammonium hexafluorophosphate were all purchased from Sigma Aldrich and used without further purification. Acetone, 1,10-phenanthroline, 3,4,7,8-tetramethyl-1,10-phenanthroline, and hexanes were all purchased from Fisher Scientific and used without further purification. All reaction mixtures were treated as air and water sensitive. All glassware was flame-dried and cooled under a flow of argon. Reactions were run under argon protection. Dry solvents were used.

NMR experiments were performed on a Bruker-500 Ascend Advanced III HD NMR spectrometer operating at 500.24 MHz (1H) and 160 MHz (11B). Chemical shifts (δ) were recorded in parts per million (ppm) relative to solvent residual peaks (1H) and BF3 (11B).

Absorption measurements were recorded using a Cary Eclipse UV/Vis Spectrophotometer. Solution phase emission and excitation spectra were collected using a Varian Eclipse fluorimeter. Samples were prepared at a stock concentration of 10–4– and diluted serially. The resulting solutions were degassed by three consecutive freeze-pump-thaw cycles prior to measurements.

A CH Instrument (CHI630C) potentiostat was connected to a glassy carbon disk electrode (working electrode), Ag/AgCl reference electrode, Pt wire counter electrode, and ferrocene as standard was used to gather cyclic voltammograms. A 0.1 m nBu4NPF6 acetonitrile solution was used as supporting electrolyte. Samples were prepared at 0.2 mm concentrations for analysis. CHI360 software was used for data collection and the resulting data processed using Origin 2018.
