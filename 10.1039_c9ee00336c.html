Asymmetric allyl-activation of organosulfides for high-energy reversible redox flow batteries†

Abstract

Organosulfides (R–Sn–R) exhibit a high theoretical capacity and low cost, but suffer from sluggish kinetics and poor reversibility. Here, we demonstrate an effective and universal strategy to improve their redox activity by replacing one organic functional group with an allyl (A) to form asymmetric allyl-substituted organosulfides (R–Sn–A). This promotes the cleavage of the A–S bond owing to facile generation of allyl radials, thereby improving the reduction kinetics and capacity. We verified the formation of asymmetric organosulfides by gas-chromatography–mass spectrometry and show that the allyl-activation strategy is universal to a series of functional groups. We successfully increased the discharge potential (up to 280 mV) and discharge capacity (up to 200%) of the organosulfide compared to its symmetric counterpart. A highly-concentrated liquid electrolyte (5 M) with a volumetric capacity of 224 A h L−1 was realized, demonstrating a substantial improvement in volumetric capacity compared to other reported liquid electrolytes to date. This strategy revitalizes organosulfides for high-energy and low-cost energy storage applications.

Introduction

Sulfur-containing materials have been widely applied for energy storage applications owing to its high energy capacity and low cost.1 Organosulfides, organic compounds containing sulfur atoms such as disulfide (R–S–S–R) and trisulfide (R–S–S–S–R) bonds, are promising redox active materials for energy storage due to their large abundance, high degree of environmental friendliness, high structural diversity, and high theoretical capacity.2–10 The reaction mechanisms of organodisulfide (R–S–S–R) and trisulfide (R–S–S–S–R) involve reversible redox reactions between organosulfides (R–Sn–R, 2 ≤ n ≤ 3) and thiosalts (R–S–) and sulfide ions (S2−) when n > 2:3,5,11–13

Visco et al. first reported tetraethylthiuram disulfide as a cathode material for sodium batteries.14 Subsequently, dimeric2,3 and polymeric organodisulfides5,15–17 have been reported. Liu et al. have systematically evaluated the redox behaviors of organodisulfides2,3 and found that the redox kinetics are sluggish as evidenced from the large peak separation of the cyclic voltammograms ranging from 1250 mV to 1630 mV for various organodisulfides.2 Organodisulfides exhibit intrinsic slow kinetics and low achievable capacities1 despite many efforts in developing electrocatalysts.6,9,15,18–20 This can be potentially attributed to the large bond cleavage energy required during electrochemical reduction, slow charge transfer rate and irreversible side reactions. Recently, Wu et al. reported that organotrisulfides (dimethyl trisulfide10 and diphenyl trisulfide13) exhibit a higher discharge capacity and faster redox kinetics than their organodisulfide counterparts owing to the extra sulfur atom (that is not bound to the alkyl group) and the reduced dissociation energy of the S–S bond.10

In this study, we introduce a new strategy to improve the reaction kinetics and achievable capacity of organodisulfides via asymmetric allyl-activation. We substitute one alkyl group (R) in the symmetric organodisulfides (R–S–S–R) with one allyl group (A = allyl) to form asymmetric organosulfides. Such substitution is expected to facilitate the cleavage of the A–S bond owing to the facile generation of the allyl radial (A˙).21 The formation of asymmetric organosulfides is confirmed by gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS). We show that the allyl-activation strategy is universal to a series of functional groups with different electron-withdrawing functional substituents and significantly improves the electrochemical activity (energy efficiency and achievable capacity) of all the organodisulfides investigated in this study. Using this strategy, we successfully increased the discharge potential (up to 280 mV) and discharge capacity (up to 200%) of organosulfides compared to their symmetric counterparts, providing an effective and universal strategy to revitalize organosulfides for high-energy and low-cost energy storage applications.

Results and discussion

The generation of an allyl radial (A˙) is known to be a facile process owing to its high resonance stability.21 As an everyday example, an allyl group may be easily released by carbon–sulfur bond cleavage upon heating diallyl disulfide (DDS), one of the principal organosulfides in distilled garlic oil.22 In fact, the name “allyl” itself is also derived from the Latin word for garlic, Allium sativum, demonstrating the origin of the recognition of this species. We aim to substitute one alkyl group (R) in the symmetric organodisulfides (R–S–S–R) with one allyl group (A) to form asymmetric organosulfides (R–S–S–A or R–S–S–S–A) (Fig. 1). The design consideration of this strategy is to utilize the S atom bonded to the allyl group for full electrochemical reduction to form lithium sulfide owing to facile A–S bond breakage to release the allyl radical:
R–S–S–A → R–S–S˙ + A˙
Fig. 1 Schematic of asymmetric organosulfide synthesis activated by an allyl group.

We select three organodisulfides (R–S–S–R, R = aminophenyl (MDS), phenyl (PDS), and fluorophenyl (FDS)) with functional groups having an increasing electron-withdrawing ability from aminophenyl (M) to phenyl (P) and fluorophenyl (F) (Fig. 1) and two allyl organosulfides (diallyl disulfide (A–S–S–A, DDS) and diallyl trisulfide (A–S–S–S–A, DTS)) as the source of allyl functional groups (termed “allyl activators”) (Fig. 1).

Identification of asymmetric organosulfides

To prepare asymmetric organosulfides with one allyl group, we take advantage of the well-known moiety interchange reaction23,24 by adding allyl-containing DDS (A–S–S–A) or DTS (A–S–S–S–A) into the three organodisulfides MDS, PDS and FDS. To confirm the formation of the asymmetric organosulfide, we conduct GC–MS to examine the electrolytes of MDS, PDS and FDS after mixing with DDS and DTS, respectively. Species were identified by matching the sample mass spectra with those of the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) MS spectral library and their corresponding mass spectra are shown in order in Fig. S1 (ESI†). Fig. 2 shows the GC–MS results of individual organodisulfides (MDS, PDS and FDS) and allyl activators (DDS and DTS) and their corresponding mixtures. In general, all the mixture contains signals from the individual phases plus one or two new phases (labeled by red numbers). All the new phases can be attributed to asymmetric organodi(tri)sulfides (R–S–S–(S)–A) bearing one alkyl group (M, P or F) and one allyl group (A). All the symmetric organodisulfides and allyl activators can be identified in the GC–MS except for MDS (very low intensity), which could be due to the too fast flow rate (6° min−1) for MDS vapor.
Fig. 2 GC–MS spectra of the initial mixed solution. (a) DDS + MDS. (b) DDS + PDS. (c) DDS + FDS. (d) DTS + MDS. (e) DTS + PDS. (f) DTS + FDS. Initial organosulfides are also included for comparison. The structures of the species associated with the peaks (peaks 1–10) observed in the GC spectrum are listed below. All samples are extracted from those initial electrolytes used in Fig. 3a–h (later section).

As shown in Fig. 2a, DDS shows two peaks (peak 1, m/z = 114; peak 2, m/z = 146) attributed to A–S–A and A–S–S–A, respectively. DTS has peak 1 and 2 plus an additional peak (peak 8, m/z = 178) attributed to A–S–S–S–A. PDS shows one peak (peak 5, m/z = 218) attributed to P–S–S–P (Fig. 2b) and FDS shows one peak (peak 7, m/z = 254) attributed to F–D–D–F (Fig. 2c). After mixing MDS with DDS/DTS activators, a new peak appeared and can be attributed to 4-aminophenyl allyl disulfide (peak 3, m/z = 197, M–S–S–A, MADS), as shown in Fig. 2a and d. After mixing PDS with DDS/DTS, new peaks appeared and can be attributed to phenyl allyl disulfide (peak 4, m/z = 182, P–S–S–A, PADS, Fig. 2b) and phenyl allyl trisulfide (peak 9, m/z = 214, P–S–S–S–A, PATS, Fig. 2e). After mixing FDS with DDS/DTS, new peaks appeared and can be attributed to 3-fluorophenyl allyl disulfide (peak 6, m/z = 200, F–S–S–A, FADS, Fig. 2c) and 3-fluorophenyl allyl disulfide (peak 10, m/z = 232, F–S–S–S–A, FATS, Fig. 2f). In addition, the integrated peak area of the asymmetric disulfide (e.g. MADS, PADS and FADS) in the DTS-mix is higher than that in the DDS-mix, indicating that more asymmetric disulfide is formed when using DTS as the activator. Since the same species in the GC spectra has same response factor, the quantity can be compared under the same test conditions.

Influence of asymmetric allyl-activation on the electrochemical activities of organosulfides

We first examine the electrochemical activity of symmetric organodisulfides with increasing functional group (from MDS to PDS and FDS) and compare them to their corresponding mixture after mixing with the allyl sources (DDS and DTS). We employ solid-state electrolyte Li1.5Al0.5Ge1.5(PO4)3 (LAGP)25 to separate the lithium anode and the positive electrode where liquid organosulfides are used as active materials and carbon paper (CP) is used as the current collector. The cell configuration is shown in Fig. S2 (ESI†). 1 M LiTFSI–0.4 M LiNO3 DOL:DME electrolyte is used as the electrolyte on the anode side. The concentration of all the organosulfides is designed to be 2.5 M and the mixture solutions contain 2.5 M of each component. Fig. 3 shows the galvanostatic voltage profiles (2nd cycle) of all the organosulfides at 0.1 mA cm−2. We use the 2nd cycle of all electrolytes for comparison since some mixture electrolytes show activation behavior in the 1st cycle (Fig. S3, ESI†).

The theoretical capacity of organosulfides depends on how R–S and S–S bonds break during electrochemical reduction. In general, organodisulfides are believed to take up 2 electrons per molecule where each sulfur atom takes one electron,2,3,5,9,11–13,26 yielding 134 A h Lcatholyte−1 for 2.5 M of organodisulfide.

R–S–S–R + 2e−+ 2Li+ → 2RSLi

Organotrisulfides, in principle, can take 4 electrons per molecule, yielding 268 A h Lcatholyte−1 for 2.5 M of organotrisulfide:

R–S–S–S–R + 4e−+ 4Li+ → 2RSLi + Li2S

If some RS bond is broken, a higher capacity could be achieved in both organodisulfides and organotrisulfides such as:

R–S–S–R → R–S–S˙ + R˙

R–S–S˙ + 3e− + 3Li+→ RSLi + Li2S

Therefore, the theoretical capacity of 2.5 M organodisulfides ranges from 134 A h Lcatholyte−1 (2 electrons per molecule) to 268 A h Lcatholyte−1 (4 electrons per molecule, all RS bonds are broken) and the theoretical capacity of 2.5 M organotrisulfides ranges from 268 A h Lcatholyte−1 (4 electrons per molecule) to 402 A h Lcatholyte−1 (6 electrons per molecule, if all RS bonds are broken), and so on.

The functional group plays a critical role in the redox potential and achievable capacity. As shown in Fig. 3a, pure PDS and FDS delivered a discharge capacity of ∼140 A h Lcatholyte−1 while the discharge capacity of MDS is negligible. First, FDS shows the highest discharge voltage of 2.1 V vs. Li/Li+ followed by PDS (1.9 V vs. Li/Li+) and MDS (1.65 V vs. Li/Li+), which is consistent with the electron-withdrawing ability of the functional group (F > P > M). That is, the higher the electron-withdrawing ability of the functional group, the higher the reduction potential of the molecule.26 Second, all three organodisulfides show large voltage hysteresis (>0.5 V), confirming the poor electrode kinetics of organodisulfides observed in the literature.2,3Fig. 3b shows the galvanostatic voltage profiles of the allyl-activators, DDS and DTS, which show a much lower discharge capacity (50 A h Lcatholyte−1) than FDS and PDS (140 A h Lcatholyte−1).

Allyl-activation improves the voltage and discharge capacity of all organosulfides we investigated here. Fig. 3c–h compare the galvanostatic voltage profiles of the DDS-mixed electrolytes (DDS + MDS, DDS + PDS and DDS + FDS) and the DTS-mixed electrolytes (DTS + MDS, DTS + PDS and DTS + FDS). The voltage profiles of the individual end members are included for direct comparison. Several critical observations are noted for the mixture electrolytes. First, all the discharge voltages of the mixture electrolytes are significantly higher than the individual electrolytes (up to 280 mV) and the charge voltages of the mixture electrolytes are significantly lower than the individual electrolytes (up to 250 mV). Consequently, the voltage hysteresis of the mixture electrolyte is much lower than the voltage hysteresis of the individual electrolytes. Considering the formation of asymmetric allyl-based organosulfides in the mixture electrolyte confirmed by GC–MS (Fig. 2), these observations indicate that the reaction kinetics of the asymmetric allyl-based organosulfides are substantially higher than the symmetric organosulfides. The underlying mechanism responsible for the improved reaction activity is studied by DFT, discussed in a later session. Second, the discharge capacity of the mixture electrolyte is larger than the sum of the individual electrolytes for some cases including DDS + MDS, DTS + MDS, and DTS + FDS. This suggests that the newly formed active species in the mixture (i.e. asymmetric allyl-based organosulfides) underwent a different reduction mechanism compared to the individual end members (higher sulfur utilization). Third, the effects of the electron-withdrawing group on the individual organodisulfides (M, P, F) on the voltage and capacity are consistent with the mixture electrolyte, where the discharge voltage and capacity of the mixture electrolyte are in the order of DDS + FDS > DDS + PDS > DDS + MDS and DTS + FDS > DTS + PDS > DTS + MDS, as directly compared in Fig. S4 (ESI†). We note that the mixed electrolytes exhibit an additional small plateau at a lower cell potential and a relatively low coulombic efficiency (CE) compared to the R–Sn–R members, which could be attributed to the contribution from the symmetric organosulfides (R–Sn–R and/or A–Sn–A) owing to an incomplete moiety interchange reaction. This can be supported by the comparison between the freshly prepared mixed electrolyte (i.e. the default method of this work) and a premixed electrolyte for over 4 months (allowing more time for moiety interchange). As shown in Fig. S5 (ESI†), the low-potential second plateau is significantly reduced and the CE is improved from 92.7% to 99.8% for the mixed electrolyte prepared with a longer mixing time. This suggests that a more complete moiety interchange reaction could improve the performance of the mixed electrolytes.

To understand the reversibility and reaction kinetics of the allyl-substituted asymmetric organosulfides, we conducted a cyclic voltammetry (CV) study of selected mixed electrolytes (DTS + FDS and DTS + PDS) along with their end members (pure DTS, pure FDS and pure PDS). Consistent with the galvanostatic voltage profiles (Fig. 3g and h), the mixed electrolytes exhibit higher electrochemical activity compared to their end members (Fig. S6, ESI†). Both DTS + PDS and DTS + FDS show higher reduction and oxidation current than their end members with stable cycling. These observations support that the allyl-activation strategy improves the kinetics, electrochemical activity and reversibility of the symmetric organosulfides. We further compare the effect of the mixing ratio of DTS-mixed FDS electrolytes on the achieved capacity, voltage hysteresis and the cycling stability by changing the amount of DTS while fixing the concentration of FDS from 2.5 M:2.5 M to 2.5 M:1.5 M and 2.5 M:0.5 M (Fig. S7, ESI†). As can be seen from the voltage profiles of the first three cycles (Fig. S7a, ESI†), the cell with the least DTS (0.5 M) shows the greatest differences between the 1st and 2nd cycle (i.e. slower activation) while the cell with highest DTS (2.5 M) quickly reached its optimum state. The achieved energy and energy efficiency of all ratios are compared in Fig. S7b (ESI†), showing that higher DTS gives higher achieved energy and energy efficiency. We compare the achieved capacity (2nd cycle at 0.1 mA cm−2), voltage hysteresis (2nd cycle at 0.1 mA cm−2) and the capacity retention (i.e. the ratio between the 100th discharge capacity and the highest discharge capacity of the cell at 1 mA cm−2) in Fig. S7c (ESI†). This shows that increasing the amount of DTS could improve the capacity and the allyl-activation effect is prominent when the DTS concentration is greater than 1.5 M. DDS + FDS shows the highest capacity of 202 A h Lcatholyte−1 and the highest average discharge voltage of 2.13 V among all the DDS-mixed electrolytes, and DTS + FDS shows the highest capacity of 224 A h Lcatholyte−1 and the highest average discharge voltage of 2.13 V among all DTS-mixed electrolytes. The achieved volumetric capacity demonstrates a substantial improvement compared to other reported liquid electrolytes to date.

DFT calculations of symmetric/asymmetric organosulfides

We perform density functional theory (DFT) calculations to understand the reduction mechanism of symmetric organosulfides and the allyl-substituted asymmetric organosulfides, aiming to identify the underlying mechanism responsible for the improved electrochemical activity observed in the experiments. Fig. 4a–f show the optimized structures of DDS, PDS, PADS, DTS, PATS and diphenyl trisulfide (PTS)13 for comparison. These systems are floppy and may exist in many conformations corresponding to neighboring local minima in the potential energy surface. Some of the molecules may be slightly stabilized by around 0.2 eV when the π moieties of the allyl or phenyl groups have spatial interactions.
Fig. 4 Optimized geometries of different organosulfides. (a) PDS. (b) DDS. (c) PADS. (d) PTS. (e) DTS. (f) PATS. The bond lengths between atoms are shown in the figure (in Å). The colors of elements C, S, and H are gray, yellow, and white, respectively.

The dissociative reduction of the symmetric/asymmetric organosulfides is an important topic in many biological and chemical processes. The cleavage of the disulfide bonds, through either a stepwise or concerted mechanism by electron transfer, has been extensively investigated in the literature:27

Stepwise: RSSR′ + e− ↔ [RSSR′]− ↔ [R–S]˙ + [R′–S]−

Concerted: RSSR′ + e− ↔ [R–S]˙ + [R′–S]−

The possible intermediate [RSSR′]˙− has been observed with a very short lifetime.28 The concerted mechanism cannot be easily ruled out as the [RSSR′]˙− species may be generated by either the stepwise reduction process or the recombination from [R–S]˙ and [R′–S]−. Theoretical characterization of the [RSSR′]˙− species with symmetric or asymmetric substituents confirms a universal unstable structure with a greatly elongated, to almost dissociated, S–S bond by around 2.8 Å, as shown in Table S1 (ESI†).

Symmetric organosulfides

The experimentally measured bond dissociation energy of S–S in PDS and DDS is 55 and 62 kcal mol−1, respectively, and the bond dissociation energy of C–S in DDS is 46 kcal mol−1.22,29 Self-cleavage of these bonds may be easily triggered by visible light or heat, leading to the homolytic dissociation reaction of organosulfides. The fragmented species may then undergo a one electron reduction process. We consider the fragmentation pathway as shown in Table S2 (ESI†). Our results confirm that the S–S bond breaking proceed through a homolytic pathway with a low reaction energy (1.35 eV for PDS), which is consistent with the work of Wu et al.13 For instance, during the reduction process, the PDS molecule may first decompose into two phenyl sulfide radicals (PS˙), then the [PS˙] could be reduced by the electron and finally couple with the Li+ cation to form LiPS,13 as shown by the following (eqn (1) and (2)):
 
P–S–S–P → 2PS˙ ΔG = +1.35 eV(1)
 
2PS˙ + 2Li+ + 2e− → 2LiPS(2)

When the system is extended to the trisulfide materials (PTS), the mechanism is very similar. It has been observed experimentally that the additional sulfur atom in the trisulfide ensures a higher capacity due to the formation of Li2S,13 as shown by eqn (3) and (4):

 
P–S–S–S–P → PS2˙ + PS˙ ΔG = +1.06 eV(3)
 
PS2˙ + PS˙ + 4Li+ + 4e− → 2LiPS + Li2S(4)

Asymmetric organosulfides

Interestingly, when different functional groups are involved in the organosulfides (asymmetric), the first decomposition step may proceed through a totally different pathway. The reaction energy of the homolytic cleavage of the C–S bond (1.33 eV) is found to be comparable to (or even lower than) the S–S cleavage energy (1.56 eV) in Table S2 (ESI†). This is due to the resonance-induced stability of the allyl radical (A˙) structure. In other words, a phenyl disulfide radical (PS2˙), whose existence is unlikely in the PDS system, will be formed in the first step of the reduction of the PADS. The [P–S–S˙] could then be reduced into the LiPS and the Li2S final products (eqn (5) and (6)):
 
P–S–S–A → A˙ + PS2˙ ΔG = +1.33 eV(5)
 
PS2˙ + 3Li+ + 3e− → LiPS + Li2S(6)

In other words, in the PDS + DDS mixture system, the DDS behaves like an activator that could make one of the sulfur atoms readily available for electrochemical reduction to Li2S, resulting in improved reduction kinetics and sulfur utilization as observed experimentally (Fig. 3d). A similar reaction mechanism can be extended to the PDS + DTS mixture system. Li2S is identified in the discharge process for DDS + PDS and DTS + PDS systems by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) as shown in Fig. S8 (ESI†).

One may expect that DDS alone will induce a release of two sulfur atoms by the formation of two allyl radicals and go through the 4-electron reduction reaction and give rise to the highest capacity in the disulfide-based systems. In fact, DDS alone has shown the worst performance (<35 A h Lcatholyte−1) as in Fig. 3b. For DTS, the capacity performance (50 A h Lcatholyte−1) is also far from 6-electron reduction (402 A h Lcatholyte−1, theoretically). Although facile C–S bond cleavage with a reaction energy of 1.33 or 1.48 eV has been theoretically predicted for the decomposition of DDS or DTS, it has been reported that several side reaction products could be produced by self-decomposition and recombination reactions of DDS itself.30–34 Fig. S9 (ESI†) summarizes some of the possible self-reaction products of the DDS molecules which could contribute to the poor electrochemical activity of the organosulfides that have two allyl functional groups such as DDS/DTS. Requirements for an effective activator should include: (1) high solubility; (2) organosulfides with three sulfur atoms and (3) non-destructive radical generator functional groups such as tBu–S–S–tBu. We note that while DFT calculations show that the additional sulfur atom leads to lower cleavage energy,13 too many sulfur atoms (e.g. tetrasulfide bonds) might not be stable owing to potential self-decomposition.35

Scalability and cycling stability of asymmetric electrolytes and their application in redox flow batteries

With the help of allyl trisulfide activation, the DTS-mixed PDS and FDS electrolytes demonstrated much higher discharge capacities and energy efficiencies than other mixed electrolytes (Fig. 3). In order to evaluate their potential toward practical energy storage application, we evaluate (1) the scalability and flow test of the catholyte and (2) the cycling stability. First, to demonstrate its scalability in flow batteries, we conducted galvanostatic measurements with 5 times the electrolyte volume compared to that of the previous tests (Fig. 3) using DTS + PDS as an example. As shown in Fig. 5a, the achieved discharge capacity at the 1st cycle using 5 times the electrolyte volume is comparable to the low electrolyte volume, confirming the scalability of the electrolyte volumetric capacity (Fig. 5a). Second, we evaluate the electrochemical activity of DTS + PDS in a continuous flow condition. Fig. 5b shows the voltage profile of the Li/DTS + PDS flow cell under a constant flow of 8 mL min−1. The flow cell showed a relatively stable cell performance over 40 hours in a continuous flow mode. To characterize the cycling stability of these high-capacity DTS-mixed electrolytes and evaluate their rate performance, we conducted cycling tests at a higher rate of 1 mA cm−2 and replace the highly resistive solid-state LAGP membrane with a Celgard separator coated with lithiated Nafion/polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF), i.e. PVDF-Nafion coated Celgard (PNC) (as shown in Fig. S10 and S11, ESI†). Fig. 5c–f show the energy retention and energy efficiency of the symmetric end members (pure DTS, pure DDS, pure PDS and pure FDS) and the mixed electrolytes (DTS + PDS, DTS + FDS, DDS + PDS, and DDS + FDS) at 1 mA cm−2 (the voltage profile, capacity retention and CE are shown in Fig. S12–S14, ESI†). First, all the pure symmetric organosulfides exhibit poor energy retention except for the pure FDS, which showed stable retention but a low energy efficiency (60%). Second, all the mixed electrolytes show a gradual increase of discharge capacity/energy upon cycling and subsequently decay and stabilize at ∼180 W h Lcatholyte−1 (100 A h Lcatholyte−1) (Fig. S12 and S13, ESI†), which is consistent with the activation cycle observed in the first cycle of the mixed electrolyte conducted at a lower current density (0.1 mA cm−2) shown in Fig. S3 (ESI†). The asymmetric organosulfide systems show high coulombic efficiencies (>98%) over 200 cycles with an average discharge capacity of 140 A h Lcatholyte−1 for DTS + PDS and 120 A h Lcatholyte−1 for DTS + FDS (Fig. S14a and b, ESI†). The discharge/charge profiles of DTS + PDS, DTS + FDS, DDS + PDS and DDS + FDS are shown in Fig. S15 (ESI†). We summarize the highest demonstrated capacities from the DTS-mixed electrolytes and compare them with other reported nonaqueous redox flow electrolytes (Fig. 6). The DTS-mixed organosulfides achieved ultrahigh volumetric capacities compared to those reported for all liquid redox flow batteries to date.

Conclusions

In summary, we demonstrated an effective and universal strategy to significantly improve the reaction kinetics and redox capacity of organodisulfides via asymmetric allyl-activation. We confirm the formation of asymmetric organosulfides by GC–MS and show that the allyl-activation strategy is universal to a series of functional groups with different electron withdrawing abilities. With this strategy, the discharge voltage was improved up to 280 mV and the discharge capacity was improved up to 200%. The (DTS + FDS) and (DTS + PDS) catholyte achieves 224 A h Lcatholyte−1 and 202 A h Lcatholyte−1, respectively, representing substantial improvement compared to other reported liquid-based redox flow electrolytes. At increasing cycling rates, the Li/(DTS + PDS) system delivers a high volumetric density (140 A h Lcatholyte−1) with high Coulombic efficiency (>98%) over 200 cycles at 1 mA cm−2. Future work to pre-synthesize the asymmetric organosulfides will be an effective method to directly obtain the active organosulfides. Our work offers a rational and universal strategy to revitalize organosulfides for high-energy and low-cost energy storage applications.
