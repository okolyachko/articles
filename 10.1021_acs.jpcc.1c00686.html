    Abstract

    When building energy dense redox flow batteries, the cell voltage of the flow cell is critical. However, the stability of the charged forms of redox-active species often suffers at extreme redox potentials due to either self-discharge in electrolyte solutions or molecular degradation, presenting a challenge for achieving high cell voltages. In this work, we explore the chemical and cycling stability of a series of organic posolytes with a 0.8 V vs Cp2Fe0/+ range in oxidation potentials in nonaqueous electrolyte systems. The relationship between the coulombic efficiency of posolytes during galvanostatic cycling and the chemical stability of their charged forms was compared by performing bulk electrolysis and UV–vis spectroscopy experiments. In general, these studies show that organic posolytes with high oxidation potentials exhibit either self-discharge and/or molecular degradation in their charged forms, lowering coulombic efficiency and/or cycle lifetime in galvanostatic cycling.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Redox-active organic molecules are of high interest as charge-storing materials for redox flow batteries (RFBs) as they could replace the less abundant (e.g., vanadium), corrosive (e.g., bromine), and toxic (e.g., chromium) active species that are used in commercially available RFBs. (1−3) To date, a large variety of electron-donating and electron-accepting organic molecular cores have been reported as posolytes and negolytes for RFB applications. (4−8) However, none of them have shown greater stability than the well-developed vanadium electrolytes, which offer up to 20 years of operational lifetime. (9) During the charging process of RFBs, posolyte molecules undergo oxidation and, if they are neutral diamagnetic species, generate radical cations. In general, radical cations are more reactive than their neutral forms, and they can participate in a variety of parasitic reactions, such as dimerization, reactions with nucleophiles, disproportionation, and self-discharge. Self-discharge is defined as discharge of a charged form to its ground state (unoxidized form) via an electron transfer process without accompanying chemical decomposition of the covalent framework of the molecule. All of these reactions limit the lifetime and coulombic efficiency (CE) of the battery. (6,10−16) While CE is important for battery operation, only a limited number of studies have evaluated the reasons behind efficiency losses. Low and/or fading CEs can result from the instability of the radical cations or from active species crossover through the membrane/separator. (11,14,17−20) Developing organic posolytes with stable charged forms is necessary for efficient and long-running organic RFBs.
    Among the reported posolytes, phenothiazine, (5,11,21,22) dialkoxybenzene, (18,23) ferrocene, (24,25) cyclopropenium, (15,26) nitroxyl radicals, (27,28) and phenazine (29,30) show particularly promising features as active materials for nonaqueous RFBs. Many of these molecules undergo chemically and electrochemically reversible oxidation reactions in cyclic voltammetry (CV) experiments. Despite this fact, their long-term stability in galvanostatic cycling remains moderate. As Kowalski et al. recently reported, it is difficult to compare the long-term stability and efficiency of active species in charge/discharge experiments—both within research groups and comparing the data from multiple groups—due to variable experimental conditions such as active species concentration, type of supporting salt and solvent, charge/discharge rate, and state of charge variations. (31) Thus, an easy and a robust standard technique is required to make meaningful comparisons.
    The cycling stability and efficiency of an active material can be predicted by measuring the chemical stability of the species’ less/least stable charged state. (12,32,33) Charged forms can be generated by a variety of techniques, including chemical oxidation, electrochemical oxidation via bulk electrolysis, or irradiation with gamma rays. (10,12,32,34) Using chemical oxidation, it is often possible to generate radical cation salts that, if they possess sufficient stability, can be isolated as crystalline solids that can be characterized via X-ray diffraction. By contrast, while radical cation generation with electrochemical techniques like bulk electrolysis (BE) is easy and fast, the products can be hard to isolate due to contamination from the working electrode solution, with trace side products crossing into the main chamber from the counter electrode. Furthermore, electrochemical reactions require conducting salts in solution, as nonaqueous solvents have low conductivities. Thus, electrochemically evaluating the stability of charged species in the absence of supporting salts is not possible unless the active materials are conductive and are used at high concentrations. Certainly electron paramagnetic resonance can be used to measure the concentration of radical species over time, but this technique does not allow for analysis of electron-paired species that form upon decomposition, and the polar nature of electrolytes makes tuning the instrument difficult. (35) Considering the complexity of electrochemical environments, we are interested in generating charged species using chemical oxidants, isolating the oxidized forms for stability measurements via spectroscopic methods, then using these results to predict cycling performance.
    To this end, we explored the variation of chemical and cycling stabilities of organic posolytes across a range of oxidation potentials. We selected redox-active organic molecules including phenothiazine, triphenylamine, carbazole, dialkoxybenzene, and cyclopropenium derivatives with different oxidation potentials for this study (Figure 1). The chemical stability of the charged species was analyzed using UV–vis spectroscopy in a common electrolyte solution. The variation of CE versus oxidation potential was examined using BE cycling at variable concentrations of selected active species and at variable charge/discharge rates. Here, we report the synthesis of charged and uncharged forms of these posolytes, the impact of trace impurities on the stability of the charged forms, and a correlation between self-discharge in UV–vis experiments and CE in BE experiments.

Tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate (TEABF4, >99%) was purchased from BASF. Anhydrous acetonitrile (ACN, ≥99.9%) and dichloromethane (DCM, ≥99.9%) were purchased from J.T. Mass spectra were obtained on an Agilent 5973 Network mass selective detector attached to an Agilent 6890 N Network GC system. Elemental analyses were performed by Atlantic Microlab, Inc. CV measurements and BE experiments were performed in a nitrogen-filled dry box.

    4,4,4-Trimethoxytriphenylamine Tetrafluoroborate ((MeOPh)3N-BF4)

    4,4,4-Trimethoxytriphenylamine (0.1 g, 0.3 mmol) and anhydrous dichloromethane (10 mL) were added to an oven-dried 50 mL round-bottom flask fitted with a rubber septum under a nitrogen atmosphere. Then, the reaction mixture was cooled in an ice bath for 10 min. Nitrosonium tetrafluoroborate (0.039 g, 0.33 mmol) was added to the prepared solution, and the resulting mixture was stirred for 30 min under a nitrogen atmosphere, after which anhydrous diethyl ether (20 mL) was added gradually with continued stirring. The resultant dark blue precipitate was filtered under nitrogen, then dissolved in anhydrous dichloromethane (5 mL), and re-precipitated with a second addition of anhydrous diethyl ether (10 mL). This process was repeated once more to ensure removal of any unreacted starting materials. The dark blue solid product (0.94 g, 75%) was dried under nitrogen and stored in an argon-filled glovebox.

    N-Ethyl-3,6-dimethoxycarbazole Tetrafluoroborate (DMeOECz-BF4)

    N-Ethyl-3,6-dimethoxycarbazole (0.50 g, 1.9 mmol) and anhydrous acetonitrile (20 mL) were mixed together in an oven-dried 250 mL round-bottom flask fitted with a rubber septum under a nitrogen atmosphere. Then, the reaction mixture was cooled in an ice bath for 10 min. Nitrosonium tetrafluoroborate (0.263 g, 2.25 mmol) was added to the reaction mixture and stirred for 30 min, after which anhydrous diethyl ether (100 mL) was added gradually with continued stirring. The resultant dark green precipitate was filtered under nitrogen, then dissolved in anhydrous dichloromethane (10 mL), and re-precipitated with a second addition of anhydrous diethyl ether (50 mL). This process was repeated once more to ensure removal of any unreacted starting materials. The dark green solid product (0.18 g, 45%) was dried under nitrogen and stored in an argon-filled glove box.

    1,4-Di-tert-butyl-2,5-dimethoxybenzene Tetrafluoroborate (DDB-BF4)

    1,4-Di-tert-butyl-2,5-dimethoxybenzene (0.51 g, 2.1 mmol) was dissolved in anhydrous dichloromethane (20 mL) in an oven-dried 100 mL round-bottom flask fitted with a rubber septum under a nitrogen atmosphere. Then, the round-bottom flask was placed in an ice water bath for 10 min, after which nitrosonium tetrafluroborate (0.297 g, 2.55 mmol) was added into the resultant reaction mixture and stirred for 30 min, after which anhydrous diethyl ether (40 mL) was added gradually with continued stirring, resulting in a green precipitate. The precipitate was filtered under nitrogen; then, it was further washed with anhydrous diethyl ether (60 mL). The light green solid product (0.45 g, 64%) was dried under nitrogen and stored in an argon-filled glove box.

    (N-Butylethylamino)cyclopropenium bis(tetrafluoroborate) (BECP-(BF4)2)

    (N-Butylethylamino)cyclopropenium tetrafluoroborate (0.20 g, 0.47 mmol) was dissolved in anhydrous dichloromethane (4 mL) in an oven-dried 20 mL glass vial fitted with a rubber septum under a nitrogen atmosphere. Then, the glass vial was placed in an ice water bath for 10 min, after which nitrosonium tetrafluroborate (67 mg, 0.57 mmol) was added into the resultant reaction mixture and stirred for 20 min. After 30 min, anhydrous diethyl ether (10 mL) was added gradually with continued stirring, resulting in a red/orange precipitate. The solvents were removed through a cannula under a positive pressure. The resulting red precipitate was dissolved in anhydrous dichloromethane (3 mL) and re-precipitated with a second addition of anhydrous diethyl ether (10 mL). The isolated red solid product (95 mg, 40%) was dried under nitrogen and stored in an argon-filled glove box.

    Cyclic Voltammetry

    CV experiments were performed using a 600D potentiostat (CH Instruments, Inc.) in a nitrogen-filled dry box. CVs were recorded in 0.1 TEABF4/ACN at 1 mM active species with glassy carbon (CH Instruments, Inc.) as the working electrode, freshly anodized un-fritted Ag/AgCl (CH Instruments, Inc.) as the reference electrode, and a Pt wire (CH Instruments, Inc.) as the counter electrode. Voltammograms were recorded at a scan rate of 100 mV s-1. Ferrocene was used as an internal reference and the oxidation potential was calibrated with respect to ferrocene/ferrocenium (Cp2Fe0/+) redox couple. 100% iR correction was applied to compensate the solution resistance before recording CVs. The solution resistance was determined using iR compensation technique (CH Instruments 600D potentiostat). The instrument calculates the amount of uncompensated resistance from the measured solution resistance between working and reference electrodes when no current passes through the electrochemical cell at a given compensation level (0-100%). (40) The resistance measured was ca. 60 Ω, which leads a to correction of less than 5 mV at the highest measured current.

The following scan rates were used for diffusion coefficient calculations: 25, 50, 75, 100, 200, 300, 400, and 500 mV s-1.

    UV–vis Spectroscopy

    UV–vis spectra were obtained using optical glass cuvettes (Starna) with 1 or 10 mm path length on an Agilent 8453 diode array spectrophotometer. All the radical cation solutions were prepared in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN and transferred into cuvettes inside an argon-filled glovebox. The capped cuvettes were taken out from the glove box for spectral analysis.

    UV–Vis Study at Different Concentrations

    MEEPT-BF4 was dissolved in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN to prepare 0.1, 0.5, 1, 5, and 10 mM solutions. Radical cation solutions (0.1 and 0.5 mM) were pipetted to 10 mm path length cuvettes, while 1 mm path length cuvettes were utilized for 1, 5, and 10 mM radical cation solution analysis. UV–vis spectra were collected at 5, 15, 30, 60, 120, 180, 240, and 300 min after preparing samples.

    UV–Vis Study at 10 mM

    All radical cation salts (DMeOEPT-BF4, DMeEPT-BF4, (MeOPh)3N-BF4, EPT-BF4, MEEPT-BF4, DMeOECz-BF4, BCF3EPT-BF4, DDB-BF4, and BECP-(BF4)2) were dissolved in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN at 10 mM and pipetted to 1 mm path length cuvettes. UV–vis spectra were collected at 5, 30, 60, 120, 180, 240, and 300 min and 24 h after preparing samples.

    Bulk Electrolysis

    BE experiments were performed in custom-made H cells as previously reported by Kowalski et al. (21) The H-cell has two electrolyte chambers separated by an ultra-fine porous glass frit (P5, Adams and Chittenden) which prevent the electrolyte mixing. Reticulated vitreous carbon (45 PPI, Duocell) served as the working and counter electrodes, which are placed in two chambers separately. Fritted Ag/Ag (cryptand)+, contained 10 mM AgBF4 in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN served as the reference electrode and it was placed next to the working electrode in the same chamber. In addition, a 3 mm-glassy carbon (CH Instruments, Inc.) electrode was attached to the working electrode chamber to record CVs, before and after BE experiments. 3.5 mL of electrolytes comprised of 10 mM neutral active species (MEEPT, DMeOECz, DDB, and BECP-BF4) in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN were added to both the working and counter electrode chambers for charge–discharge cycling. The voltage cut-offs were determined after recording CVs on the working electrode side to avoid accessing undesired redox couples or electrolyte decomposition. The voltage boundaries for each active species as follows; MEEPT: 0.65-0 V, DMeOECz: 0.76-0.16 V, DDB: 1.1-0.45, and BECP-BF4: 1.2-0.55 V vs. Ag/Ag+. Both the working and counter electrode chambers were continuously stirred during BE cycling to maintain a good mass transfer while charging and discharging. The experiment was conducted at variable C-rates, including 3C (2.814 mA), 2C (1.876 mA), and 1C (0.938 mA) by connecting to a BioLogic VSP potentiostat.

    Synthesis

    The discharged forms of the posolytes were either purchased or synthesized as previously reported (see the first subsection of the Methods section for details). The charged forms of all nine posolytes were synthesized by chemical oxidation with nitrosonium tetrafluoroborate (NOBF4), and the oxidized products were isolated as solids. In this reaction, the only byproduct is NO gas, and the charged (or oxidized) molecules are generated as tetrafluoroborate salts. In most cases, the oxidized forms are radical cations; however, in the case of the cyclopropenium derivative, the oxidized form is a radical dication. Synthetic procedures are provided in the Methods section (Synthesis).

    Cyclic Voltammetry

    UV–Vis Spectroscopy

    We next sought to evaluate the stability of the charged form of these posolytes over a longer timescale than that interrogated via CV. As such, we analyzed each of the charged species by UV–vis absorption spectroscopy, a technique we chose because all the charged species show absorption features in the UV and visible regions, allowing for analysis of species concentration over time based on changes in peak intensities. The radical cation samples were prepared in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN and transferred into cuvettes inside an argon-filled glovebox to exclude contacts with atmospheric water/air (see the third subsection (UV-vis) of the Methods section for details).
    Our previous studies showed that trace impurities present in both the solvent and supporting electrolyte can affect the stability of radical cations, which is evident based on analytes being more persistent when studied at higher concentrations. (10,42) Thus, it was first important to identify a lower limit for the concentration needed to analyze the long-term stability of the charged species in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN (selected due to its common use as a nonaqueous RFB electrolyte). For MEEPT-BF4, we evaluated persistence at concentrations ranging from 0.1 to 10 mM—the lower and upper limits of what we could use to confidently analyze peak intensities over time in UV–vis absorption spectroscopy. MEEPT-BF4 can be analyzed at the lowest concentrations by monitoring the peak at ca. 515 nm, and the upper limit of concentration is determined by the intensity of less-absorbing peaks at ca. 695, 770, and/or 860 nm to mitigate the UV–vis detector saturation. Furthermore, the cuvette pathlength was varied to prevent detector saturation in the long wavelength region of the absorption spectrum (ca. 600 to 950 nm), using a 10 mm pathlength cuvette for 0.1 and 0.5 mM solutions and 1 mm pathlength quartz cuvettes for 1, 5, and 10 mM solutions. Figure 3a–e shows the absorption spectra of MEEPT-BF4 in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN over a period of 3 h. To compare the decay percentage of each concentration after 3 h, the absorbance at 5 min is normalized to a value of 1 at 770 nm (Figure S3a−e). A comparison of normalized absorbance changes at 770 nm over time is shown for the different concentrations in Figure 3f. As expected, the stability of MEEPT-BF4 varies with concentration. At 0.1 mM, only 76% of the initial absorption intensity remained after 3 h. For 0.5, 1, 5, and 10 mM solutions, absorption intensity retention at 3 h was 96, 97, 98, and 99%, respectively. This result suggests that the trace impurities present in the electrolyte solution led to radical cation decay at lower concentrations. Based on these results, we studied the stability of all the charged posolytes at 10 mM.
    The chemical stability of each radical cation salt (or the radical dication salt for the BECP species) in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN was analyzed using UV–vis spectroscopy at 10 mM using 1 mm pathlength cuvettes. Changes in the absorption spectrum (loss of intensity and/or new peak formation) were monitored over a period of 24 h. The formation of new peaks in the absorption spectrum likely results from the formation or cleavage of covalent bonds, generating new chemical species. In contrast, losses of intensity could arise either from reduction of charged species to their neutral forms (or cation form for the BECP species) through an electron transfer (self-discharge) mechanism or from active species decomposition. (42)Figure 4 shows the UV–vis absorption spectra of the oxidized forms of the posolytes in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN over 24 h. Most of the phenothiazine radical cation salts (DMeEPT-BF4, EPT-BF4, MEEPT-BF4, and BCF3EPT-BF4) had peak absorbance values at ca. 1.4 absorption units in the long wavelength region. However, the UV–vis detector was saturated for the other samples. Thus, we are unable to obtain a quantitative decay analysis of these based on peak intensity but were able to follow less-absorbing regions of the absorption spectra to look for changes. From these results, it is clear that the oxidized forms of the low to moderate oxidation potential posolytes (DMeOEPT-BF4, DMeEPT-BF4, (MeOPh)3N-BF4, EPT-BF4, and MEEPT-BF4) are persistent. Even (MeOPh)3N-BF4 showed a stable absorption spectrum over time, demonstrating that its less reversible CV does not reflect inherent instability of the radical cation. By contrast, for the radical cations derived from higher oxidation potential posolytes [DMeOECz-BF4, BCF3EPT-BF4, DDB-BF4, and BECP-(BF4)], noticeable decay in absorption intensity was observed.
    We next sought to determine if the loss in absorption intensity for DMeOECz-BF4, BCF3EPT-BF4, DDB-BF4, and BECP-(BF4) was due to self-discharge, chemical decomposition, or both. To distinguish these possibilities, we diluted the 24 h 10 mM samples to a concentration of 1 mM and then recorded UV–vis spectra of the diluted solutions in 1 mm pathlength cuvettes. We compared the diluted absorption spectra to those of the uncharged species and found that in all cases, the neutral species was the major product in solution (Figure 5). While the spectra of DMeOECz-BF4 and DDB-BF4 show formation of new species in addition to their unoxidized forms, those of BCF3EPT-BF4 and BECP-(BF4)2 show only the unoxidized and oxidized versions of each compound. This result demonstrates that self-discharge occurs in the four cases in which absorption intensity was lost with additional reactivity in two cases. The source of electrons for the observed self-discharge reaction is unclear. We speculate that trace water or other possible trace impurities present in the electrolyte system such as metal ions, amine, amides, or acids could act as a reducing agent; or the self-discharge process could occur as a consequence of hydrogen transfer reaction with solvent/electrolyte as suggested by Zhang et al. considering dimethoxybenzene derivatives. (14)

    Bulk Electrolysis

    Next, we performed galvanostatic BE cycling to determine the effect of charged species self-discharge on CE. All BE experiments were performed in a custom-made H-cell, which has an ultra-fine porous glass frit (P5, Adams and Chittenden) to minimize electrolyte mixing. Experimental details are provided in the Methods section (see the fourth subsection (Bulk Electrolysis) for details). Before detailing the results, it is important to clarify the charging rates. At 3C, each charge/discharge takes 40 min. Similarly, at 2C, one charge/discharge cycle completes in 1 h, and at 1C, one charge/discharge cycle completes in 2 h.
    If trace impurities present in the electrolyte trigger the self-discharge of charged species, a relatively large portion of oxidized species should convert to its uncharged form at lower concentrations of active species, while at elevated concentrations, the amount of conversion should be proportionally smaller. Thus, at a high concentration of active species, we expect to observe a higher CE compared to the CE at a lower active species concentration under the same conditions. Furthermore, CE should change with the rate of charge/discharge. If a BE cell charges at a faster rate, there will be less time for self-discharge to occur compared to slower charging. Thus, a higher CE is expected at faster charging rates if self-discharge occurs. By contrast, the CE should not change with the charging rate if the charged form of the active species is both chemically stable and does not undergo self-discharge.
    With this in mind, we benchmarked our results by testing MEEPT, a moderate oxidation potential compound that did not undergo significant decay in the UV–vis experiment. BE experiments of a 10 mM solution of MEEPT in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN are shown in Figure 6. BE cycling was performed at three different C-rates (3C, 2C, and 1C) with five charge/discharge cycles at each C-rate. The accessed capacities increased at slower charge–discharge rates, consistent with greater cell polarization at higher charge–discharge rates. MEEPT showed high CE during charge/discharge cycling at ca. 98–99% per cycle. The first charge/discharge cycle exhibited relatively low CE as a higher charge capacity accessed in the first cycle. With this active species, the CE did not change with the C-rate, likely because the time per charge/discharge cycle is, at minimum, 24× faster than the time required for 10 M MEEPT-BF4 to lose 1% of its intensity in the same electrolyte. Post-cycling CV analysis showed no evidence of MEEPT decomposition (Figure S4a).
    Next, we analyzed the highest oxidation potential compound BECP-BF4, which showed significant self-discharge in the 24 h UV–vis experiment. The BE experiment was performed at two different concentrations (5 and 10 mM) in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN. The BE cycling was also done at three C-rates of 3C, 2C, and 1C with five charge/discharge cycles at each C-rate. Figure 7 shows the CE and capacity vs cycle number of BECP-BF4 at 5 and 10 mM in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN. As predicted, faster charging during BE resulted in a higher CE, consistent with faster charging allowing less time for self-discharge to occur. Also, a comparison of CE between 5 and 10 mM shows that BE cycling at 10 mM has a higher CE than at 5 mM at the same C-rate, which is likely due to the higher ratio of electrolyte trace impurities at 5 mM, leading to a higher degree of self-discharge. Furthermore, post-cycling CV analysis (Figure S4b) of 10 mM BECP-BF4 showed that a majority of the active material was present with about 20% loss, presumably due to crossover and slight active species decomposition. Thus, the relatively lower CE (90–95%) that was observed compared to MEEPT (Figure 6) is likely a result of a combination of crossover, self-discharge, and minute active species decomposition.
    Next, we analyzed the two additional high potential compounds that showed significant loss of intensity in UV–vis experiments: DMeOECz and DDB. Their charged species underwent a combination of self-discharge and possibly a chemical decomposition in the 24 h experiment. Both compounds were analyzed at 10 mM in 0.1 M TEABF4/ACN. Figure 8 shows CE and capacity change vs cycle number for DMeOECz and DDB. DMeOECz showed a noticeable drop in CE with variable rates. As evidenced by post-CV analysis, DMeOECz underwent significant molecular decomposition during the BE experiment (Figure S4c). By the time the variable charging rate experiment reached 1 C, the redox center of DMeOECz had completely decomposed: At this slowest charging rate of 1C, the charge capacity was higher than the theoretical value because DMeOECz kept charging without reaching the charge–voltage boundary until it met the charge–time boundary (65 min) as a result of the redox center having decomposed. Thus, we only completed three charge–discharge cycles at this C-rate. DDB showed C-rate-dependent CE variation (CE at 1C < CE at 2C < CE at 3C). Unlike in BECP-BF4, the discharge capacity decreased as a function of cycle, which is consistent with active species degradation. Thus, the observed CE variation is a result of the combination of radical cation self-discharge and molecular decomposition.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    This report describes the synthesis and isolation of the charged forms of nine different RFB posolyte candidates to determine their chemical stability in an electrolyte solution. A concentration-dependent MEEPT-BF4 stability study showed that the trace impurities present in the electrolyte system trigger radical cation decay at lower concentrations. UV–vis experiments indicate that the charged species derived from the low oxidation potential posolytes are persistent over time compared to the charged species derived from the higher oxidation potential posolytes. The charged forms of high oxidation potential posolytes exhibited either self-discharge or a combination of self-discharge and molecular degradation, limiting CE and/or cycle lifetime in the galvanostatic cycling, presenting a challenge of utilizing high potential compounds in nonaqueous RFBs. Variation of the supporting electrolyte and solvent may provide a viable pathway for minimizing self-discharge and other decomposition pathways in nonaqueous RFBs.

Here we synthesized the phenothiazine deriv., N-ethyl-1,9-dimethyl-3,7-bis(trifluoromethyl)phenothiazine to test this hypothesis. Indeed, oxidn. potentials reflect additive substituent effects, yielding a high-potential redox couple with a stable radical cation. Stability tests in soln. and the solid state show that the radical cation form of this phenothiazinium is stable and can be used to oxidize other org. compds. in soln.
