    Abstract

    Aqueous organic redox flow batteries (RFBs) have emerged as one the most promising technologies for grid-scale energy storage because of their sustainability, safety, and high power densities. Herein, by the molecular engineering of aqueous irreversible triphenylamine (TPA) to form 4,4′,4″-trihydroxytriphenylamine (P3), a reversible two-electron transfer system based on an enol/keto redox couple was obtained, with a high kinetic rate constant of 1.1 × 10–2 cm2 s–1. P3 was used as the catholyte and paired with a zinc anolyte in an aqueous RFB, which yields a high working voltage of ∼1.4 V with good cycling behavior. A capacity of 22.5 Ah L–1 can be obtained using a mixed solvent (DMF/H2O = 1:5, volume ratio) and the battery shows a minor self-discharge rate at full state of charge. This work demonstrates that irreversible or instable redox-active organic molecules can be converted into reversibly reactive analogues by molecular engineering, thereby broadening the range of redox-active materials for organic aqueous RFBs.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Although renewable energy can alleviate our reliance on fossil fuels, its supply from wind or solar sources can be intermittent due to seasonal and geographical fluctuations. Battery technologies provide one of the most realistic options for the storage of intermittent renewable energy. In particular, redox flow batteries (RFBs) have become increasingly popular for use in grid-scale energy storage because of their high energy-to-power ratios and low self-discharge, as well as their independent scaling of power (electrode area) and energy (volume). (1−3) In contrast to batteries based on solid-state electrode materials, RFBs utilize redox-active materials dissolved in solvents as electrolyte solutions. In RFBs, the anolyte and catholyte, selected according to their redox potentials, are contained in two tanks separated by an ion-exchange membrane and circulated by pumps. The tank volume defines the capacity and size of the cell, which in turn determines the power capability of the RFB. (2,3) The bulk of the RFB research efforts so far has focused on inorganic redox-active materials such as vanadium ions, bromine, and polysulfide ions. (4,5) However, these are disadvantaged by their small valence charges, limited electron-transfer capability, corrosiveness, and need to operate in corrosive electrolyte solutions such as concentrated sulfuric acid. (5−7) Although vanadium RFBs have been commercialized, vanadium’s rarity makes such batteries costly and its toxicity poses safety concerns. (8,9)
    In line with sustainable development, the use of organic redox-active molecules in RFBs is attractive since their chemical structures can be tailored according to requirements and their syntheses can often be based on inexpensive, bio-derived precursors. (10) However, the need to use large volumes of organic solvents in nonaqueous RFBs poses fire risks. (10) Aqueous RFBs based on organic compounds are preferable alternatives today, but there are limited numbers of water-soluble organic molecules that show stable redox performance. Various water-soluble organic derivatives of alloxazine, quinone, viologen, phenazine, and (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxidanyl (TEMPO) have been reported, among which most are related to anolytes. (11−15) Besides, TEMPO in the radical state turns to be unstable in an acidic ambience and undergoes side reactions during charging. (16,17) Consequently, the selection and paring of anolytes and catholytes can be refrained and the development of organic aqueous redox flow batteries would be deterred. Hence, there is clearly a need to develop stable organic redox couples for multi-electron transfer reactions in water. The molecular engineering of redox-active molecules can be performed to tune important properties such as solubility, redox potential, and stability. (10) Furthermore, most organic molecules sustain carbon radical formation during the redox pathway, resulting in radical coupling in an organic ambience and even radical quenching in an aqueous ambience. (18) Thus, a mechanistic understanding of the redox stability and multi-electron transfer capability of a redox-active organic molecule in an aqueous environment will contribute to developing molecular engineering strategies for aqueous organic RFBs.
    Triphenylamine (TPA) is an electron-rich aromatic molecule well-known for its use as a hole-transport material in solar cells, as well as its electrofluorochromic properties. However, its use in RFBs is relatively unknown due to its insolubility in the aqueous phase and poor chemical stability of its radical cation. (19) TPA has high redox potential and low ionization energy, and it readily undergoes anodic oxidation to form the TPA radical cation, (19) whose stability depends markedly on its para substituents. The redox instability of TPA stems from radical transfer from the N atom to the C atom, leading to dimerization of the radical cation to form tetraphenylbenzidine under nonaqueous conditions (20) and even radical quenching in an aqueous system. Herein, we report that the para-substitution of TPA with methoxy groups generates 4,4′,4″-trimethoxytriphenylamine (S3), which exhibits a reversible redox couple of 0.5/0.45 V (versus Ag/AgCl) with one-electron transfer in an aqueous ambience. Furthermore, an extremely rapid and reversible two-electron transfer derivative, 4,4′,4″-trihydroxytriphenylamine (P3), was also prepared from TPA, with redox potentials of 0.46/0.42 and 0.35/0.31 V (versus Ag/AgCl). P3 boasts a high kinetic rate constant of 1.1 × 10–2 cm s–1, which is much larger than those of previous redox couples used in flow batteries. (15,21,22) We have also evaluated the use of P3 as a catholyte and assembled a zinc–organic RFB. It delivers a working voltage of 1.4 V and presents good rate capability and stable cycling behavior. Furthermore, the zinc–organic RFB offered a high capacity of 22.5 Ah L–1 using a mixed solvent (DMF/H2O = 1:5, volume ratio). It delivered a long cycling life of 300 cycles with 80% capacity retention at a current density of 40 mA cm–2. The intermittent cycling test revealed minor battery capacity and voltage decay, indicating the good reactant stability of P3 as the catholyte. Our study suggests that engineering functionalities can tune the reversibility of the existing redox-active molecules, generating highly stable catholytes or anolytes for aqueous or organic RFBs.

    2.1. Organic Synthesis and Characterization

    The detailed synthesis of TPA derivatives in this work and molecular information are given in the Supporting Information. Deionized water and organic compounds after recrystallization were used to make the electrolytes, which were purged with nitrogen prior to use. 1H- and 13C-NMR spectra of all target molecules were obtained using a Bruker AV500 MHz spectrometer at 25 °C. XRD of single crystals of P3 was measured on a Bruker Smart APEX Single Crystal X-ray diffractometer.

    2.2. Full-Battery Tests

    Full-battery tests were conducted using a LAND cycler (Wuhan Kingnuo Electronics) and RFB cell. The cell consists of a smooth flat poly(vinyl chloride) (PVC) frame, graphite plates with flow fields, copper plates as the current collector, and graphite felt electrodes with an active area of 4 cm2. Galvanostatic charge–discharge performance was carried out at current density ranging from 10 to 80 mA cm–2 between 1 and 1.7 V. Nafion 212 was used as the ion-exchange membrane after ion-exchange treatment (exchanging H+ with Na+). This exchange procedure was carried out in 1 M NaCl at 60 °C for 5 h, then washed with deionized water, according to the previous literature. (23) Catholyte (10 mL) was circulated by a peristaltic pump at a flow rate of 20 mL min–1, while 10 mL of anolyte was not circulated. The compositions of the anolyte and catholyte were: 1.0 M ZnCl2 and 0.1 M NaCl aqueous anolyte and 0.05 M P3 in 0.1 M NaCl weak acidic catholyte (pH = 5) or 0.6 M P3 in a mixture solvent (DMF/H2O = 1:5, volume ratio), respectively. Coulombic efficiency and voltage efficiency were calculated by the ratios of the average discharge capacity to the average charge capacity and the average discharge voltage to the average charge voltage, respectively. Furthermore, energy efficiency equals the product of Coulombic efficiency and voltage efficiency. The specific capacity was calculated based on the volume of reactive P3. Energy density was calculated by multiplying the discharge capacity and the average discharge voltage of the initial cycle at a specific current density.

    2.3. Electrochemical Study

    CV and RDE voltammetry were conducted using an electrochemical workstation (Metrohm Autolab) with the Ag/AgCl aqueous reference electrode (CHI111, 3 M NaCl filling solution), platinum wire auxiliary counter electrode (CHI115, 0.5 mm diameter), and glassy carbon working electrode (CHI104, 3 mm diameter). CV curves in Figure 1 were measured at a scan rate of 10 mV s–1. For RDE experiments, linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) was carried out from 0 to 0.8 V versus Ag/AgCl at a sweep rate of 10 mV s–1, during which the disc electrode was rotated from 400 to 3600 rpm. The half-wave potential E1/2 = 0.4 V was calculated as the formal redox potential, which showed half of the limiting current at 400 rpm. The limiting currents measured at 0.75 V were obtained and plotted versus the rotation rate (ω) by linear fitting. The slope was deduced using the Levich equation: (24)i = 0.620 nFAcD2/3ν–1/6ω1/2, where i is the limiting current, n is the number of electron transfers in the reaction (n = 2 for P3), F is the Faraday constant (96 485 C mol–1), A is the surface area of the working electrode (0.0707 cm2), c is molar concentration in mol cm–3, D is the diffusion coefficient in cm2 s–1, ν is the kinetic viscosity in cm2 s–1 (9.0 × 10–3 cm2 s–1 for 2.5 mM P3 and 0.1 M NaCl aqueous solution, pH = 5), and ω is the rotating angular velocity in rad s–1.
    Koutecký–Levich plots, in which ik represents the current in the absence of any mass transport effects (as ω–1/2 → 0, ik = i), apply mainly to quasi-reversible and irreversible reactions. (25) The values of the vertical-axis intercepts at each overpotential, using the Koutecký–Levich equation: (25,26) 1/i = 1/ik + 1/0.620 nFAcD2/3ν–1/6ω1/2, were used to form the Tafel plot, which affords the values of the charge transfer coefficient (α) and kinetic rate constant (k0) based on the Tafel equation. log10(ik) was plotted versus overpotential η, the latter is the difference between the measured potential and the formal redox potential. In this work, the half-wave potential at 400 rpm was used as the formal redox potential E1/2 = E0 = 0.4 V. The linearly fitted plot was analyzed by the Tafel equation: log10(i) = log10(nFcAk0) + αnFη/2.303RT, where n is the number of electrons in the rate-limiting step (n = 1 for P3), (27) α is the charge transfer coefficient, R is the universal gas constant (8.314 J K–1 mol–1), T is temperature, k0 (cm s–1) is the kinetic rate constant, and c is the concentration of P3, which was assumed to be the same.

    2.4. In Situ Ultraviolet–Vis Spectroscopy and FT-IR Spectroscopy

    Ultraviolet–vis spectra were measured using a UV–vis spectrometer UV-1800 (Shimadzu Scientific Instruments). In situ UV–vis spectroelectrochemistry of methoxyl-substituted TPA derivatives S1–S3 and 4,4′,4″-trihydroxytriphenylamine (P3) was carried out with potentiostatic electro-oxidation at increasing potentials (Ag/AgCl micro reference electrode, integrated circuit with a Au working electrode and Au counter electrode). The spectra were measured in a wavelength range of 210–900 nm at a sampling interval of 0.5 nm. FT-IR spectroscopy was conducted using a Bruker VERTEX 80V and the spectra were obtained in the wavenumber range of 500–4000 cm–1. Pristine P3 was grown by recrystallization and electro-oxidized P3 was extracted using the DCM solvent from the parent system after the electro-oxidation process.

    2.5. Time-Dependent ESR Spectra

    Time-dependent ESR investigations were conducted using a JEOL FA200 ESR spectrometer (X-Band with LN2 VT accessories). A solution containing 2.5 mM P3 and 0.1 M NaCl was processed by electro-oxidation under slow stirring using a three-electrode system (Ag/AgCl reference electrode, platinum wire counter electrode, and platinum plate (1 × 1.5 cm2) working electrode). The electro-oxidized products were generated by the potentiostatic electro-oxidation process. The ESR patterns of samples after 0.5, 1, 2, 4, and 10 h treatment were acquired, which were within 320–360 μT field.

    3.1. Schematic of the TPA Redox Potential and Redox Instability Mechanism

    Scheme 1a presents some typical organic redox-active materials and their redox potentials within the water deposition electrochemical window, (8) wherein TPA has a high redox potential. However, the radical-based redox couples sustain redox instability. Myriad methods have been developed by chemists to stabilize short-lived organic radicals. (28) TPA undergoes an irreversible redox reaction due to radical quenching and minor intermolecular coupling in aqueous solution (Scheme 1b, and Figure S1a, Supporting Information). Based on the principle that the resonance delocalization of unpaired spins, coupled to the lone pair donation from oxygen groups, can stabilize N radical cations, we para-functionalized the phenyl groups in TPA with methoxy or carboxylic groups. Such a molecular engineering strategy managed to yield reversible and stable organic redox-active species. Figure 1a shows cyclic voltammetry curves of the parent TPA, and fully para-substituted derivatives of methoxylated and carboxylated TPA, in an aqueous ambience. This was designed to investigate the influence of para substituents on the redox reversibility of TPA. It is found that methoxylated TPA presents a reversible one-electron redox couple observed by cyclic voltammetry (CV), while carboxylic-substituted TPA becomes irreversible (Figure 1a). This is ascribed to the electron delocalization from the O of methoxy groups to form a conjugated system with the N radical cation, avoiding the formation of carbon radicals that are prone to quenching in an aqueous ambience (Figures 1c and S1b,c, Supporting Information). Further, the methoxyl-substituted TPA derivatives of S1–S3 are increasingly reversible, which all show one redox couple (one-electron transfer), such as that at 0.5/0.45 V (versus Ag/AgCl) for S3 (Figure 1b). The redox potential differences, in methoxyl-substituted and carboxylic-substituted TPA derivatives (Figure 1a,b), arise from the electron-donating and electron-withdrawing effects of the methoxy and carboxylic groups, which causes a shift to lower and higher redox potential, respectively. (12,29,30)
    ESR was used to follow the changes in the molecular structure during electro-oxidation. The one-electron transfer process to form the N radical cation was verified by the time-dependent ESR of S3 upon electro-oxidation in the water/methanol phase (volume ratio of 6:4) (Figure 1d). The radical derived from S3 is stable even after 10 h, which provides strong proof for N radical stabilization through resonance contribution of para-substituted methoxy groups in the aqueous phase. In contrast, the ESR signal from the N radical cation, derived from pristine TPA, is readily quenched (Figure S2, Supporting Information). In situ UV–vis spectroelectrochemistry was conducted to monitor the presence of radical cation species for S3 during redox reactions in the aqueous phase. Figure 1e shows a series of UV–vis absorption curves as a function of increasing potentiostatic electrode potentials. S3 is almost transparent in the visible range in a CH3OH/H2O solvent mixture and shows only an absorption peak at 300 nm due to the π–π* transition of the phenyl rings, which is typical of TPA-based molecules. (20,31) When the applied potential is increased, the relative intensity of the absorption peak at 300 nm decreases over time, and new peaks at around 260, 360, and 700 nm gradually increase in intensity, which indicates the formation of the N radical cation (Figure 1e). The evolution profile of these peaks matches with one-electron transfer in S3. The aqueous stability of the N radical cation is promoted by the para-substitution of methoxy-group from S1–S3 (Figures S3 and S4, Supporting Information). All the synthetic routes are provided in the Supporting Information.

    3.2. Investigation of Redox Pathways of a Two-Electron Transfer System

    To construct a two-electron transfer system, we functionalized TPA with para-hydroxyl groups to generate P1–P3 (Figure 2a). Two redox couples, located at 0.46/0.42 and 0.35/0.31 V (versus Ag/AgCl), respectively, are observed for P3, indicating two-electron transfer. Moreover, regardless of the number of para-hydroxyl groups, the CV curves of all the hydroxyl-functionalized TPA derivatives reveal two-electron redox couples, which indicates that only one phenolic hydroxyl group is needed for the two-electron transfer reaction. Figure 2b shows the two-electron transfer mechanism for P3. Due to the presence of the phenol moieties, the aqueous redox chemistry of P3 is enabled by keto–enol tautomerization, which supports the two-electron redox system on the aromatic TPA scaffold. To ensure the stability of TPA during electrochemical reactions; however, complete para-substitution is needed as any unsubstituted site will be open to radical transfer. For P1–P3, not only the reversibility is enhanced, but also the current density is enriched due to the solubility of hydrophilic phenolic hydroxyl groups (Figure 2a). Therefore, an aqueous irreversible TPA (one-electron transfer) has been turned into aqueous redox-active species by molecular engineering.
    Upon electro-oxidation of P3 for 0.5–10 h in weakly acidic NaCl solution, we can observe the characteristic N radical triplet ESR patterns located in the same magnetic field range as those for S3 (Figures 1d and 2c). However, the radical signal weakens and vanishes with prolonged electro-oxidation, which can be explained by the conversion of the N radical cations to bare N cations following the keto–enol tautomerization process. From the in situ UV–vis spectra (Figure 2d), we can see two stages in the spectral evolution profile of P3. When the potential increases to 0.4 V, absorption peaks at 375 and 700 nm increase in intensity, at the same time, the absorption peak at 300 nm gradually disappears, while a new peak at around 270 gradually increases in intensity. These peaks at 0.4 V correspond to characteristic absorption of the N radical cation, which is similar to that in S3. As the potential increases to 0.45 V, the absorption peak at 550 nm (O radical) (16) appears, but at 0.65 V, the peak at 700 nm indicating the N radical decreases, (20) suggesting the conversion of the radical state to the ionized state. New peaks at 250, 310, and 500 nm appear, suggesting the formation of the oxidized P3 in the fully charged state. All these results reflect structural changes based on a two-electron transfer process in P3. Furthermore, FT-IR was used to track the molecular reorganization due to keto–enol tautomerism in P3 before and after potentiostatic electro-oxidation at 0.55 V. The reduction in the intensity of the C–N bond stretch is accompanied by an increase in the intensities of the C═N and C═O stretches (32) after electro-oxidation, suggesting the occurrence of keto–enol tautomerization during the two-electron oxidation (Figure 2e). It is also appreciated that the fully charged P3 in the ionized keto form can be far more stable than the radical form, since it was extracted from the electrochemically oxidized electrolyte and verified by FT-IR below.

    3.3. Electrochemical Study of Two-Electron Transfer of Redox-Active P3

    The rate of electron transfer is an important performance metric of redox-active materials in any battery, and especially so for RFB. To evaluate the electron transfer and molecular diffusion kinetics, linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) study was carried out using a glassy carbon rotating disc electrode (RDE). Figure 3a illustrates the limiting diffusion current at various rotating angular velocities of the disc electrode, ranging from 400 to 3600 rpm at 10 mV s–1, using freshly prepared electrolyte (P3 after recrystallization, see single-crystal information in Figure S5 and Table S1, Supporting Information) for each data point. The half-wave potential (E1/2) of P3 is 0.4 V versus Ag/AgCl (0.61 V versus SHE) at pH = 5.0. Figure 3b presents a plot of the limiting current versus rotation velocity, which reveals that the redox reaction is diffusion-limited within the measurement range. (29) The diffusion coefficient of P3 is (1.1 ± 0.1) × 10–6 cm2 s–1, which is determined from the slope of 0.0153 mA rad–1/2 s1/2 using the Levich equation. (24)Figure 3c shows Koutecký–Levich plots, in which ik represents the current in the absence of any mass transport effects (as ω–1/2 → 0, ik = i). (25) These values were used to form the Tafel plot shown in Figure 3d, from which the charge transfer coefficient (α) and kinetic rate constant (k0) can be extracted. Figure 3d exhibits good linearization, with α = 0.32 for the oxidation of P3. The value of k0, kinetic rate constant for the oxidation of redox couple of P3, is 1.1 × 10–2 cm s–1, which is, few orders of magnitudes, greater than that of inorganic redox couples such as V2+/V3+ (5.3 × 10–4 cm s–1) and VO2+/VO2+ (2.8 × 10–6 cm s–1). (33) Detailed calculations are provided in the Supporting Information. The large diffusion coefficient and large kinetic rate constant of P3 contribute to offering a smaller polarization overpotential. (15) The good rate capability of P3 redox couple is also evidenced from its CV curves at high scan rates (Figure S6, Supporting Information). Therefore, the fast electrochemical kinetics of P3 reaffirms it as a suitable candidate for RFBs.

    3.4. Aqueous Redox Flow Battery Study of 4,4′,4″-Trihydroxytriphenylamine with Zinc

    An aqueous RFB based on 4,4′,4″-trihydroxytriphenylamine/Zn was tested at current densities of 10–30 mA cm–2, using 0.05 M P3 in 0.1 M NaCl (pH = 5.0) as the catholyte and 0.1 M + 1 M ZnCl2 as the anolyte, and Nafion 212 as the ion-exchange membrane. The CV curve of the Zn/Zn2+ redox couple is shown in Figure S7, Supporting Information. Zn/Zn2+ was used as the anolyte because of its low cost, high specific capacity based on the high concentration, and low negative redox potential. (5,34,35)Figure 4a shows a schematic of the cell, and the RFB configuration and electrode reactions are given in Figures S8 and S9, Supporting Information. Figure 4b presents the charge–discharge profiles at current densities of 10, 15, 20, and 30 mA cm–2. The initial discharge capacity at 10 mA cm–2 is 2.45 Ah L–1, which approaches the theoretical capacity of 2.68 Ah L–1 based on the volume of reactive P3. The average discharge plateaus are 1.41 V at a current density of 10 mA cm–2 and 1.38 V at a current density of 30 mA cm–2. The charging plateau is polarized from 1.4 to 1.5 V when the current density ranges from 10 to 30 mA cm–2, which reveals the good rate capability of the P3 redox couple. The practical working voltage of the aqueous organic RFB assembled is higher than that of the RFBs based on quinone, viologen, and TEMPO. (14,22,30) Most importantly, our zinc–organic RFB presents a good rate capability and high voltage efficiency at a high current density, which originates from the high kinetic rate constant of the P3 redox (Figure 4b,c). To improve the energy density of zinc–organic aqueous RFB, the addition of a small amount of water-miscible organic solvent improves the capacity without compromising the safety of aqueous RFB. A mixed solvent (DMF/H2O = 1:5, volume ratio) system improves the concentration of P3 up to 0.6 M, yielding a capacity >20 Ah L–1 at current densities ranging from 20 to 80 mA cm–2 (Figure 4d). The small amount of DMF has insignificant influence on the ion conductivity of the catholyte (Table S2, Supporting Information). Figure 4e exhibits the efficiencies of the battery at a current density of 10–80 mA cm–2, showing that high voltage and energy efficiency could be achieved due to the fast redox kinetics of P3. This presents an attractive advantage of aqueous organic RFBs. (13,29)

    3.5. Evaluation of Zinc–Organic RFB with a High Concentration Catholyte

    Figure 5a presents the polarization curve of voltage and power density as a function of discharge current density at a state of charge (SOC) of 50 and 100% (charge current density of 40 mA cm–2). The peak power density was determined to be 0.23 and 0.17 W cm–2 under a SOC of 100 and 50%, respectively, and at a current density of 0.25 A cm–2, which is better than that of all-vanadium RFB (0.12 W cm–2 at 0.15 A cm–2). (7,36) Furthermore, the current density is mainly limited by the zinc anode, since the kinetics of P3 is much faster than that of zinc plating/stripping during charge and discharge. (37) However, the current density our RFB can achieve is sufficiently high for RFB applications. (5,26,34)Figure 5b shows the stable cycling behavior of zinc–organic RFB based on 0.6 M P3 in a mixed solvent (DMF/H2O = 1:5, volume ratio) at 40 mA cm–2 (80% capacity retention after 300 cycles).
    Capacity fading in RFBs can be due to the decomposition of redox-active molecules, or/and their conversion into redox-inactive molecules, leading to irreversible capacity loss over time. (38) Herein, the anode (Zn2+/Zn) is the main limiting factor for the stability, which is revealed by the irreversible formation of anode residues (Figure S10, Supporting Information). Moreover, the intermittent cycling test of the RFB was carried out to investigate the self-discharge behavior of P3. When the battery was held for 12 and 24 h after charging at a SOC of 100%, corresponding to the stage when P3 exists in the oxidative ketone ionized form, the self-discharge rate is low, since the battery features high capacity retention and minor voltage decay (Figure 5c). This indicates that it is a promising catholyte. Figure 5d presents the bar comparison of peak power density and energy density of Zn//P3 RFB with reported representative aqueous organic RFBs. Our zinc–organic battery yields a high working voltage of 1.4 V and a high peak power density of 230 mW cm–2. (13,39,40) Moreover, the demonstrated energy density of 16 Wh L–1 is close to that of the commercially available vanadium redox flow battery. (7,36) In addition, the limitation of water solubility of P3 can be overcome by hydrophilic functionalization.

    4. Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we have identified a new class of organic catholyte based on para-substituted TPA that can be used in aqueous RFBs. One of our molecularly engineered products, 4,4′,4″-trihydroxytriphenylamine (P3), displayed fast two-electron transfer kinetics with a rate constant of 1.1 × 10–2 cm s–1. An aqueous RFB assembled using 0.05 M P3 as the catholyte and a zinc anolyte yields a high specific capacity of 2.45 Ah L–1, approaching a theoretical one of 2.68 Ah L–1 at a current density of 10 mA cm–2. Furthermore, the zinc–organic RFB delivers a high capacity of 22.5 Ah L–1 using a mixture solvent (DMF/H2O = 1:5, volume ratio).
