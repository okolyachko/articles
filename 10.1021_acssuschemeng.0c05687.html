    Abstract

    Nitroxide radicals are considered as ideal redox species in all-organic redox flow batteries due to their redox potential of ∼2 V. These radicals are predominantly used in their polymerized form as cathode materials to a high efficiency. Attempts to use poly(nitroxide)s as anode materials have been unsuccessful due to irreversibility of the reduction process as the reduced form of a nitroxide undergoes a fast, irreversible proton transfer with an electrolyte. In this study, reduction of the nitroxide radical, TEMPO, was shown to become reversible in an ionic liquid. A redox potential of 2.5 V was achieved, with the reduction reversibility being maintained after 200 cycles. A fabricated symmetric electrochemical cell demonstrated a high coulombic efficiency of 60% over an extended period of time. This is the first report demonstrating a high degree of reversibility of nitroxide reduction, thus leading to a paradigm shift in the future design of redox flow batteries.

    Synopsis

    This study demonstrates for the first time a reversible reduction process of the nitroxide radical TEMPO in selected ionic liquids.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Storage of energy on a large scale is crucial for the application of renewable energy sources such as solar and wind energy. (1,2) In response to this demand, there has been much research on redox flow batteries, specifically those with organic redox-active materials to act as a low-cost, renewable material with a long lifetime. (3,4) In recent years, the idea of using organic nitroxide radicals, both in the form of a polymeric, cathode-active material (5−7) and a standalone redox species in redox batteries, (8−10) has been the subject of several studies. However, while the most commonly used nitroxide radical, TEMPO, has been shown to be stable towards oxidation to form an oxoammonium cation, finding a suitable organic-based anode with a highly negative reduction potential has been the subject of much debate. (11) In all-organic redox flow cells, anodes regularly sacrifice their specific capacity, redox potential, or efficiency to ensure stable and reversible electrochemical reactions. This stems from the fact that the reduced state of TEMPO, a nitroxide aminoxy anion, produced at the anode is highly unstable due to fast proton transfer reactions with the electrolyte. (12,13) The reduction of TEMPO has thus been shown to be irreversible unless substituted with large sterically hindering groups. (14) For example, a nitroxide radical monomer (4,4′-[oxybis(2,1-ethanediyloxy-2,1-ethanediyloxy)]bisnitronylnitroxide) produced a decreased theoretical specific capacity of 81.7 mAh g–1, down from that of 171.5 mAh g–1 established for TEMPO. (15) Anode materials are often based on organic redox-active compounds such as 9-fluoronone and N-methylphthalimide, (16,17) with many additional alternatives also suggested. (18,19) However, the high voltage and high theoretical energy capacity predicted for a TEMPO symmetric battery that utilizes the TEMPO radical in both the catholyte and anolyte at the same time have not been achieved. This was attributed to the use of anolytes with a less negative reduction potential. In the recent report by Li et al., the 4,4′-dimethylbenzophenone-based anolyte and 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1,4-dimethoxybenzene-based catholyte achieved a 2.97 V cell potential accompanied with a relatively low discharge capacity of 51.2 mA h L–1. (20) While the nitroxide polymer catholyte paired with a bipyridinium-derived anolyte produces a higher capacity and efficiency cell, an operating voltage of only 1.25 V could be achieved. (21)
    Several attempts have been made in achieving a symmetric organic redox flow battery. Since its operation requires two stable redox pairs of the same species, these typically have a lower capacity compared to an asymmetric redox flow battery. (22,23) TEMPO has the advantage of having an oxygen center capable of both reduction and oxidation processes. Theoretically, this redox flow battery may have a very high capacity, especially when a high concentration of TEMPO can be achieved. In previous theoretical and experimental studies conducted in our group, ionic liquids (ILs) were shown to stabilize nitroxide radicals such as TEMPO, (24) and significantly increase its redox potential window approaching 5.5 V in some ILs compared to the maximum of 2.2 V observed in aqueous electrolytes calculated theoretically. (25) In the study by Maruyama et al., (26) TEMPO dissolved in tetrafluoroborate and bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)amide-based ionic liquids were shown to undergo a single cyclic voltammetry cycle, with a quasireversible reduction peak being found at around −1.3 V vs Ag/AgNO3. No repeated charging/discharging cycles were demonstrated due to the fast degradation of TEMPO in a full redox cell. However, our previous work indicated that by tuning the cation–anion combination it may be possible to ensure the reduction of TEMPO is reversible beyond a single cycle while increasing the overall redox potential, thus paving the way toward a high-voltage, high-capacity symmetric redox flow cell. (25)
    Here, we report a high-throughput strategy to tailor ionic liquids to enhance the thermodynamic and kinetic stability of the aminoxy anion, which has been previously suggested to be unstable in traditional electrolytes. (12) Through varying the cation–anion combination, we discovered ionic liquids that could significantly suppress proton transfer (PT) side reactions of the aminoxy anion. Gibbs free energies (GFE) of PT reactions fell into a wide range from highly exergonic for 1-methyl-3-methylimidazolium dicyanamide ([C1mim][N(CN)2]) (a GFE of −171.6 kJ mol–1) to highly endergonic for [C1mpyr][OTf] (a GFE of 193.7 kJ mol–1). The latter was confirmed experimentally with cyclic voltammetry (CV) achieving over 200 reversible cycles for TEMPO in neat 1-butyl-1-methylpyrrolidinium triflate ([C4mpyr][OTf]). A reduction peak of TEMPO was observed at around −1.7 V vs Ag/AgNO3 for each cycle validating the near reversibility of the TEMPO reduction reaction, with only a small degree of TEMPO degradation being observed. Although the quasireversibility of TEMPO reduction was previously reported in neat ionic liquids for a single CV cycle, (26) this is the first manifestation of how ionic liquids can be tuned to achieve long-term reversibility of both the reduction and oxidation reactions. A fully symmetric electrochemical cell was assembled to test the potential of TEMPO as an active species in redox flow batteries. Sustained cycling was achieved for 3 cycles over 10 hours with 61% of coulombic efficiency. Operating the cell at 2.6 V allowed us to successfully generate the aminoxy anion on the anode, which proved to be thermodynamically and kinetically stable for a week in a nitrogen atmosphere. The presence of the anion was confirmed with UV–vis spectroscopy. This unprecedented outcome was already achieved even with a comparatively low conductive ionic liquid. Theoretical research strategies presented in this work have showcased a significant potential of tuning nitroxide radicals and ionic liquids to design a stable and efficient all-organic redox flow battery.

    Results and Discussion

    Thermodynamics of Proton Transfer Side Reactions

    As discussed above, the biggest issue of the nitroxide-based redox battery is the instability of the aminoxy anion that was shown to undergo a spontaneous proton transfer (PT) with a traditional electrolyte, thus producing an electrochemically inactive species. In the case of the TEMPO radical, the formation of the TEMPOH species was identified (27) (see Figure 1). In this study, we predicted Gibbs free energies (GFEs) of the possible proton transfer side reactions of the aminoxy anion, TEMPO–, that result in the abstraction of labile protons from the ionic liquid cations to form TEMPOH (see Figure 1). Two different proton abstraction sites were considered for each cation, with each being Boltzmann-averaged to ensure that the lowest energy pathway was found. Three types of widely used ionic liquid cations, imidazolium, phosphonium, and pyrrolidinium, were chosen in the study. 1,3-Dimethylimidazolium (C1mim+), tetramethylphosphonium (P1,1,1,1+), and N,N′-dimethyl-pyrrolidinium (C1mpyr+) cations were set as molecular models for these distinct cation types. For the C1mim+ cation, the relatively high acidity proton of the C2–H bond adjacent to the two nitrogen centers and a proton of the methyl group were considered. For the P1,1,1,1+ cation, two symmetrically different protons of the methyl group were tested for PT, whereas for the C1mpyr+ cation protons on both methyl groups and on the carbon atoms within the pyrrolidinium ring were probed. Due to the absence of labile protons in the ionic liquid anions chosen (with the exception of mesylate), these were not considered in the study.
    Analysis of the predicted GFE values (see Figure 2) reveals a large variation between ionic liquids (ILs), with N,N-dimethylpyrrolidinium trifluoromethanesulfonate ([C1mpyr][OTf]) being the most stabilizing IL to discourage the proton transfer with the aminoxy anion producing a highly positive GFE of 193.7 kJ mol–1. Contrary to this observation, 1,3-dimethylimidazolium dicyanamide ([C1mim][N(CN)2]) was found to be most susceptible to undergo a proton transfer with the aminoxy anion with a GFE of −171.6 kJ mol–1. The largest determinant to the stability of the aminoxy anion was, as hypothesized, the nature of the ionic liquid cation. Pyrrolidinium-based ILs were found to be the least reactive towards proton transfer, whereas imidazolium-based ILs were found to be almost uniformly reactive and phosphonium-based ILs formed an intermediate group between these two extremes. However, interestingly, the anion was seen to strongly influence the reactivity of the aminoxy anion toward proton transfer, with dicyanamide leading to a spontaneous proton transfer when coupled with all three types of cations studied here. Triflate was the least reactive when coupled with the pyrrolidinium cation, whereas tetrafluoroborate was the most stable candidate in combination with imidazolium and phosphonium-based cations. Phosphonium ILs were shown to be an intermediate between imidazolium and pyrrolidinium, with their most reactive being [P1,1,1,1][N(CN)2] with a GFE of −88.6 kJ mol–1, with dicyanamide producing the most negative GFE for all the anions. Meanwhile, all other cation–anion combinations are slightly positive, with the most positive being [P1,1,1,1][BF4] with a GFE of 56.5 kJ mol–1. These findings indicate that the cation–anion combination plays a crucial role in determining the susceptibility of the aminoxy anion to undergo proton transfer.
    A stark difference among ILs susceptible to PT with the aminoxy anion was attributed to a large difference in interaction energy between an ion pair and TEMPO–/TEMPOH, in particular the electrostatic component of this interaction (see Figure 2). For example, the two extremes discussed above, [C1mim][N(CN)2] and [C1mpyr][OTf], were shown to have a huge variation in the electrostatic interaction for both TEMPOH and TEMPO–. For TEMPOH, [C1mim][N(CN)2] provided an electrostatic interaction energy of −27.0 kJ mol–1 compared to a repulsive contribution of 21.0 kJ mol–1 for [C1mpyr][OTf]. In contrast, the aminoxy anion has a high electrostatic interaction of −144.3 kJ mol–1 with [C1mpyr][OTf], which is reduced by half to −72.8 kJ mol–1 with [C1mim][N(CN)2]. The phosphonium-based ILs produced similar trends to those of pyrrolidinium-based ones with remarkably similar average total interaction energies with the TEMPO– anion, e.g., −195.4 kJ mol–1 for the phosphonium ILs on average vs −193.9 kJ mol–1 for pyrrolidinium ILs on average. However, the repulsive effects of the electrostatic interaction with the TEMPOH products were only seen in [P1,1,1,1][BF4], for which an electrostatic interaction of 28.3 kJ mol–1 was found. In the case of ILs with positive GFEs (e.g., [C1mpyr][OTf]), a strong electrostatic stabilization of TEMPO– coupled with the destabilization of TEMPOH were identified as crucial factors to potentially suppress the proton transfer process. Conversely, dispersion interaction is rather consistent in the systems studied exhibiting an average of 66.6 kJ mol–1 and a low standard deviation of 10.8 kJ mol–1 for TEMPO– and an average of 62.4 kJ mol–1 with a standard deviation of 9.4 kJ mol–1 for TEMPOH. This indicates that the electrostatic component is the key driver in stabilizing the aminoxy anion, and hence, facilitating the reversibility of the TEMPO reduction. Overall, N,N′-dimethylpyrrolidinium triflate was identified as the best candidate to promote this reversibility.

    Cyclic Voltammetry of TEMPO in Ionic Liquids

    To test the predicted stability of the aminoxy anion toward proton transfer side reactions, a series of cyclic voltammetry (CV) experiments (see Figure 3) were performed with the TEMPO radical dissolved in [C2mim][N(CN)2], [C2mim][BF4], and [C4mpyr][OTf] ILs, each of which exhibited varying GFEs of PT. For these CV experiments, a scan rate of 0.05 V s–1 using a 1.6 mm diameter glassy carbon electrode was used with 0.1 M concentration of TEMPO in neat IL and Ag/AgNO3 as a reference electrode. A solution of 4-hydroxy-TEMPO (TEMPOL) in water with 1 M NaCl was used as a control, with TEMPOL being used due to its high solubility in water unlike TEMPO, (21) with water being a necessary component due to its fast degradation of aminoxy anions; in these cases, Ag/AgCl was used as a reference. (18) TEMPOL has similar reduction and oxidation peaks to those of TEMPO, thus providing a straightforward comparison between the two. (28) It was shown that in the theoretically unstable [C2mim][N(CN)2] ionic liquid, the reduction of TEMPO was indeed irreversible due to the absence of an oxidation peak for the aminoxy anion to pair with the reduction peak observed at −1.0 V (see Figure 3). In contrast, in the best-identified candidate, [C4mpyr][OTf], the re-oxidation to the TEMPO radical from the aminoxy reaction was almost symmetrical with respect to the reduction reaction (see Figure 3). The reduction reversibility was maintained for 200 cycles. As an intermediate between the two, [C2mim][BF4], which was identified to have a slightly positive GFE of proton transfer (16.6 kJ mol–1), was found to have a partially reversible reduction in initial cycles. After 200 cycles, a complete degradation of TEMPO was observed, with the solution becoming colorless, which was attributed to the formation of TEMPOH. These experimental observations were in excellent agreement with our theoretical results.
    The reduction potential of TEMPO was found to vary between ILs, and especially, in comparison to the aqueous solution of NaCl. It was observed to shift from −0.9 V with TEMPOL in the aqueous electrolyte to −1.5 V in [C2mim][BF4] and −1.8 V in [C4mpyr][OTf], which is consistent with our previous theoretical studies, which indicated that the reduction should move to larger negative potentials due to increased stabilization of the TEMPO radical by the IL. (24,25) The oxidation potential appeared to be less dependent on the IL and was consistently observed in the region of 0.2 to 0.3 V for the three ILs studied. This observation is consistent with previous literature values of TEMPO oxidation in ILs. For example, the oxidation peak was reported between 0.21 V for N,N-diethyl-N-methyl-N-(2-methoxyethyl)ammonium tetrafluoroborate ([DEME][BF4]) and 0.28 V for [DEME][NTf2], (26) only ∼0.2 V lower than that of 0.43 V recorded in the aqueous electrolyte. (29) The maximum current observed for the reduction and oxidation peaks also varied between the ILs, with the conductivity altering it greatly. For the best candidate among ILs, [C4mpyr][OTf], a relatively low maximum current of ≈40 μA was recorded for oxidation and only ≈20 μA for reduction, which is around 10 times lower than that in the 1 M NaCl aqueous electrolyte. Given that conductivity of the ILs is lower than that of the 1 M NaCl aqueous electrolyte, this observation was not surprising as the current was found to be proportional to conductivity and viscosity of the ILs used, with [C4mpyr][OTf] having a conductivity of only 1.3 mS cm–1 at 20 °C. (30) A variation in the maximum currents was found for the oxidation and reduction processes, with the reduction having a flat peak. This difference is likely to arise due to the slightly slower reaction rate of TEMPO reduction in comparison to TEMPO oxidation, with future studies determining the rate constants of each being currently considered. The highest redox potential between redox pairs of 2.1 V was observed for [C4mpyr][OTf], while [C2mim][BF4] and [C2mim][N(CN)2] produced slightly reduced potentials of 1.7 and 1.5 V, respectively. This is a significant improvement compared to 1.3 V, a redox potential observed in 1 M NaCl aqueous electrolyte featuring irreversible reduction of TEMPO. To this end, we have confirmed that ionic liquids could be easily tuned using the established quantum chemical methodology to achieve the reversibility of the TEMPO reduction and increase the redox pair potential by 0.8–2.1 V.

    Stable and Reversible Symmetric Cell with TEMPO as Redox Species

    A symmetric electrochemical cell (see Figure 4) was used to study the cycling performance of a 0.1 M TEMPO solution in [C4mpyr][OTf]. Due to the high resistance of the electrolyte (and the glass frit separator), the charging current was limited to 0.1 mA cm–2 to avoid excess ohmic loss. The charging voltage of ∼2.6 V was achieved in good accordance with CV results. When the cell was charged further, the voltage increased immediately to ∼6.3 V, which is in the range of the electrochemical window for [C4mpyr][OTf] (5.4 V at 0.1 mA cm–2 and 20 °C) (30) coupled with additional resistance from the 20 μm pore frit separating the anolyte and the catholyte. This observation indicates that no TEMPO radical was present on either side of the cell after the charging was complete after 130 h. Figure 4 shows the resulting solutions on the cathode side and the anode side. It is obvious that the color changed from orange (the original TEMPO solution) to yellow on the cathode side and brown on the anode side, indicating that the TEMPO radical underwent an electrochemical transformation. The UV–vis spectra measured for the three solutions (see Figure 4) confirmed the formation of the oxoammonium cation (a peak at 455 nm) (31) in the catholyte and the aminoxy anion (a peak at 441 nm) in the anolyte, with the TEMPO radical absorbing at 452 nm with much higher intensity than the oxoammonium cation. (32) The absorption peaks for the TEMPO radical and oxoammonium cation are in agreement with previous literature values, both in their respective wavelengths and their relative intensities given by the difference in extinction coefficient between the radical and cationic forms. (31,32) However, since the aminoxy anion has not previously been stabilized, there are no prior literature values available for comparison. The produced aminoxy anion was found to be remarkably stable over a long period provided the atmosphere was kept inert and free of water and oxygen. Some degradation was still detected and was attributed to the presence of a small amount of water in the ionic liquid measured at 203 ppm, as quoted by the supplier. However, as expected, upon contact with air the brown solution became transparent over the course of 1 week due to the formation of the colorless TEMPOH species and evidenced by a decrease in intensity of the corresponding 441 nm peak (see Figure 5).
    To confirm that the TEMPO radical underwent an electrochemical transformation, control experiments were set up, with TEMPO being added to either the cathode or the anode side while keeping the opposite side free of TEMPO. The cell voltages were measured at 4.0 and 5.2 V for the catholyte and the anolyte, respectively (see Figure 5). In the symmetric cell setup (i.e., the TEMPO radical is present on both electrodes), a voltage of 2.6 V was obtained. Since TEMPO undergoes the reduction at −1.8 V and the oxidation at 0.3 V vs Ag/AgNO3, the resulting voltage of 2.6 V is in accordance with CV results (see Figure 3) when accounting for high resistance from the conductivity of the electrolyte [C4mpyr][OTf] (1.3 mS cm–1 at 20 °C) (30) and the 3.5 mm thick P4 glass frit separating the two compartments of the cell. The voltage of 4.1 V for the sample with TEMPO only in the catholyte was attributed to the oxidation of TEMPO at 0.2 V and a reduction of the ionic liquid cation at −3.4 V as previously determined with a current of 0.1 mA cm–2. (30) The voltage of 5.2 V for the cell with TEMPO as the anolyte comes from the reduction of TEMPO at −1.8 V and oxidation of the ionic liquid at 2.0 V (measured with the same current), (30) with an overpotential coming from the electrolyte resistance. It should be noted that all IL degradation values are taken from the literature are given at 20 °C, whereas the cell cycling in this work was performed at 50 °C, which not only influenced decomposition reaction kinetics but also increased the conductivity and shifted the redox potential. This means that the broader degrading IL reduction may be shifted to occur at around −2.9 V with a reduced current and lower temperature. (30,33) When the symmetric cell setup was operated with the neat ionic liquid, the voltage increased further to 6.3 V (see Figure 5), accounting for the electrochemical window of the ionic liquid of 5.4 V and an accumulated electrolyte resistance.
    To determine the reversibility of the aminoxy anion in a symmetric cell environment, the discharge limit was set to −1.0 V to compensate for the cell’s inner resistance. Cycling was performed with conditions similar to the control experiment detailed earlier, wherein 0.1 M of TEMPO in neat [C4mpyr][OTf] was cycled on both sides of the cell (shown in Figure 5) with a charge/discharge current of 0.1 mA cm–2, a charging/discharging time of 2 h, and a rest time of 10 min between charging and discharging to allow for equilibration and eliminate a rapid self-discharge. The current used is much lower than that reported in currently available all-organic electrochemical cells. (21,34,35) This is due to the low conductivity and high viscosity of the IL in comparison to aqueous NaCl used previously, with a 0.1 mA cm–2 current chosen due to its thorough characterization of voltage limits from previous studies. (30) From the cycling performed, the reduction and oxidation of TEMPO were shown to be reversible with up to 61% coulombic efficiency at a voltage of −0.6 V to counteract the 2.6 V charge voltage (shown in Figure 5). Several cycles were achieved without any sacrifice in performance. This result provides a proof of concept that a symmetrical redox flow battery with a nitroxide radical as a redox species may be possible.

    Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    For the first time, we have demonstrated the ability of ionic liquids to stabilize the aminoxy anion of the TEMPO radical, a product of a nitroxide radical reduction, whereby making the process reversible. This is by no means a consistent effect across ionic liquids. Using quantum chemical calculations, we analyzed proton transfer (PT) side reactions between the aminoxy anion and ionic liquid cations and determined that only several ionic liquids prevented the aminoxy anion from undergoing a proton transfer. In particular, [C1mpyr][OTf] predicted a Gibbs free energy (GFE) of proton transfer to be as high as 193.7 kJ mol–1, with some such as [C1mim][BF4] showing a slightly positive GFE and several ionic liquids such as [C1mim][N(CN)2] leading to a spontaneous PT. Positive GFE values were attributed to a stark increase in electrostatic stabilization of the aminoxy anion over the protonated species, TEMPOH.
    Theoretical trends were successfully confirmed experimentally using cyclic voltammetry. For ILs with a highly positive GFE of PT such as [C4mpyr][OTf], the reduction of TEMPO was found to be reversible, with 200 cycles being performed. In contrast, [C2mim][N(CN)2] was shown to cause an irreversible reduction of TEMPO within the first cycle. A significant increase in the redox potential of TEMPO was observed in the most stabilizing ionic liquid, [C4mpyr][OTf], at 2.1 V, up from 1.3 V measured in water. This result is in excellent agreement with our previous theoretical results. (25) The redox potential of 2.1 V indicates that a nitroxide-based redox flow battery could potentially compete with commercially available vanadium redox flow batteries. (36)
    A small symmetric electrochemical cell was fabricated to test the cycling ability of a 0.1 M TEMPO solution in neat [C4mpyr][OTf]. Several cycles were achieved with the coulombic efficiency of 61% following a two-hour charge/discharge pattern with a current of 0.1 mA cm–2. A voltage limit of −1.0 V had to be set upon discharging to compensate for cell resistance. This observation, therefore, provides a proof of concept for the viability of a redox flow battery based on nitroxide radicals and ionic liquids. Operating the asymmetric setup of the cell, whereby TEMPO was placed on the anode side, we produced the aminoxy anion of the TEMPO radical. The anion appeared brown in color and remained stable for over a week in an inert nitrogen atmosphere. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report of the electrochemical synthesis of the aminoxy anion as well as the manifestation of its stability in neat ionic liquids. The fabricated symmetric cell represents a first step towards a proof of concept in achieving a synergistic effect of nitroxides and ionic liquids in an all-organic redox flow battery. Future work will focus on matching nitroxide radicals with ionic liquids that are cheap to synthesize while also maintaining a high degree of stability and an increased cell potential.

    Methods

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    M06-2X/cc-pVDZ (37,38) was used to optimize complexes between the TEMPO radical and a single ion pair of ionic liquids. This was done using the Gaussian16 software package. (39) Two energetically preferred configurations were tested after optimization for each charged state of TEMPO with each IL based on the structures generated in the previous study, with their energies being Boltzmann-averaged. (25) Electronic energies were improved by performing single-point energy (SPE) calculations on the optimized structures at a higher level of theory. A Boltzmann average was applied to SPEs to predict Gibbs free energies of proton transfer. SPEs were performed using the Psi4 software package. (40) The ONIOM approach (41) was utilized, whereby the inner reaction center consisting of the cation and a TEMPO species (radical, cation, or anion) was calculated at CCSD(T)/CBS and the rest of the system was treated with SRS-MP2/cc-pVTZ. (42,43) HF energies of CCSD(T)/CBS and SRS-MP2/cc-pVTZ were further improved with the cc-pVQZ basis set. Temperature and entropic corrections were calculated using the standard physical chemistry formulae implemented in the in-house code.
