Abstract

Busy BEE: A biredox eutectic electrolyte (BEE) is derived from the intermolecular interactions between the redox-active molecules N-butylphthalimide and 1,1-dimethylferrocene. Given the high concentration of active materials, the flow cell based on the BEE delivers stable cycling performance, and high volumetric capacity and power density. The facile and effective strategy has potential for high-performance nonaqueous redox flow batteries for high-energy storage systems.

Abstract

One promising candidate for high-energy storage systems is the nonaqueous redox flow battery (NARFB). However, their application is limited by low solubility of redox-active materials and poor performance at high current density. Reported here is a new strategy, a biredox eutectic, as the sole electrolyte for NARFB to achieve a significantly higher concentration of redox-active materials and enhance the cell performance. Without other auxiliary solvents, the biredox eutectic electrolyte is formed directly by the molecular interactions between two different redox-active molecules. Such a unique electrolyte possesses high concentration with low viscosity (3.5 m, for N-butylphthalimide and 1,1-dimethylferrocene system) and a relatively high working voltage of 1.8 V, enabling high capacity and energy density of NARFB. The resulting high-performance NARFB demonstrates that the biredox eutectic based strategy is potentially promising for low-cost and high-energy storage systems.

Eutectic systems are low-temperature polynary ionic or metallic mixtures. Because of their unique chemical and physical properties, eutectic mixtures have been extensively employed in diverse energy storage systems such as latent heat thermal energy storage,1 rechargeable alkaline metal batteries,2 and redox flow batteries (RFBs).3 Inorganic and organic salt eutectic mixtures with low-cost and high latent heat such as NaCl-CaCl2-MgCl24 and fatty acid eutectic/polymethyl methacrylate composites,5 respectively, are attractive as phase-change materials for latent heat thermal energy storage. Benefiting from fluidity and high surface tension, eutectic alloys such as Na-K,2a,2b Ga-Sn,6 and Ga-In7 can be taken as flexible and dendrite-free electrode materials in liquid-metal batteries. Besides, because of the nonvolatilty, nonflammability, low toxicity, and thermal and electrochemical stability, deep eutectic solvents (DESs) obtained by mixing metal salts with hydrogen-bond donors (HDBs) have been used as electrolytes in lithium-ion batteries and electric double-layer capacitors.8 As an emerging class of electrolytes, an eutectic electrolyte, in which one of the components is redox-active, has been recently explored and shows the capability to enhance the energy densities of RFBs, as they offer advantageous features such as low cost, simple preparation, and high loading ratio of active materials.9

RFBs now represent a potent candidate for renewable energy storage.10 However, the drawbacks, most notably the relatively low working voltage and the low solubility of redox-active materials, limit their widespread applications. Enabled by the broad electrochemical stability window of nonaqueous solvent, nonaqueous RFBs provide a higher working voltage in comparison with the aqueous counterparts.11 However, the solubility of organic redox-active materials, especially in the presence of the supporting salt, is still low compared with the traditional RFBs using aqueous electrolytes and inorganic redox-active materials. Various techniques are used to improve the molarity of redox-active materials in nonaqueous solvents, including chemical modification,12 solid dispersion,13 redox-targeting,14 and so on. DES-based eutectic electrolytes obtained from metal chlorides such as FeCl3, AlCl3, and ZnCl2 with HDBs (urea or acetamide), can achieve high metal ion activity (1.7–5.4 m) and have been successfully employed in hybrid RFBs.15 However, the solvent molecules and at least one of the components in the DES-based eutectic electrolyte are electrochemically inactive, and would dilute the available concentration of the active component. Furthermore currently all concentrated electrolytes use ceramic separators to inhibit electrolyte contamination and maintain cycling stability. However, the low ionic conductivity of the ceramic separators limits the actual power density of the cells, and the operating current density is usually below 0.5 mA cm−2.14b, 15b,15d, 16 Porous separators are able to sustain higher current density,17 but their ion selectivity is poor because of the large pore size, leading to the crossover of redox materials.18 Several ways have been employed to mitigate crossover, such as employing a thicker separator, polymer-based redox species, mixed electrolytes, and applying higher operating current densities.17a–17c, 19 However, it is still challenging but highly desirable to develop concentrated and stable nonaqueous electrolytes which not only deliver high capacity but also retain good cycling stability, especially at high current density. In this study, we propose a novel biredox eutectic electrolyte (BEE), formed directly by the interaction between two different kinds of organic redox-active molecules without additional solvents (Figure 1). The BEE not only maximizes the concentration of redox molecules, but also simplifies cell design as both electrodes use the same electrolyte, thereby alleviating the capacity decay arising from electrolyte contamination.

The BEE can be simply prepared by mixing N-butylphthalimide (BuPh) and 1,1-dimethylferrocene (DMFc) together at ambient conditions without any further treatment. The resultant dark red liquid was formed quickly at various molar ratios of the two molecules (Figure 1 a; see Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). The lowest melting point of the BEE was approximately 9 °C, achieved at a mole ratio of 2:3 (BuPh/DMFc), which is much lower than each of the individual components (ca. 33 °C for BuPh and 38 °C for DMFc) as indicated in the phase diagram shown in Figure 1 b. Moreover, the eutectic electrolyte possesses highly concentrated redox-active materials, and the molarity of BuPh and DMFc reached 3.5 m at a mole ratio of 1:1 with the viscosity of only 4.5 MPa s at room temperature (see Table S1). The viscosity of the BEE is lower than the reported eutectic mixtures of DESs9 and “solvate ionic liquids”,20 and is beneficial for reducing the ohmic overpotential during the charge/discharge process. Although the ionic conductivity of BEE-based electrolytes decreased with increasing of concentration (see Table S1), mainly because of the limited solubility of the supporting salt (tetrabutylammonium tetrafluoroborate, denoted as TEABF4) in concentrated BEE, the value at high concentration (3.0 m) was still higher than other concentrated electrolytes.16, 17 Lithium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiTFSI) can be directly dissolved into BEE without any solvent (see Figure S2), however, the high viscosity resulted in low ionic conductivity of the electrolyte (see Table S1). In this work, ACN and TEABF4 are selected as the solvent and supporting salt, respectively, because of the relatively high ionic conductivity and good solubility among the reported non-aqueous electrolytes.21

For the BEE, BuPh and DMFc molecules take part in the redox reaction at the anode (BuPh/BuPh− redox couple) and cathode (DMFc/DMFc+ redox couple), respectively (Figure 1 c). The same BEE at both anode and cathode could reduce the concentration gradients and mitigate the electrolyte contamination. Taking advantage of the high concentration and high working voltage (1.8 V, Figure 1 d), the theoretical energy density of the proposed BEE reached 84.4 Wh L−1. It is worth noting that such a concept could also be expanded to other eutectic electrolytes, such as BuPh-TEMPO and BuPh-I2 (see Figure S3).

The formation mechanism of the BEE was studied by Raman and FTIR spectra. In the Raman analysis, no new vibration bands were observed in any of the eutectic electrolytes (Figure 2; see Figure S4), suggesting that BuPh and DMFc maintained their original structures. However, the C=O stretching vibration mode of BuPh showed a red-shift from 1757 cm−1 to 1765 cm−1 (Figure 2), while the C−CH3 vibration of DMFc exhibited a blue-shift from 339 cm−1 to 317 cm−1. The peak shifts for both C=O and C−CH3 groups were attributed to the interaction of BuPh and DMFc, through which electron density distribution and coordination environment change, hence lowering the melting point of BEE. Moreover, little spectroscopic variation was found when changing the mole ratios of BuPh and DMFc.

Density-functional theory (DFT) calculations were further employed to investigate the formation mechanism of the BEE. The electrostatic potential (ESP) map of BuPh (Figure 3 a) showed that the ring exhibited stronger electron-withdrawing ability than the alkyl chain. The alkyl chain, with the weaker electron-withdrawing ability, decreased the dipole moment of BuPh, thus decreasing the dipole–dipole interactions among BuPh molecules. For DMFc, the methyl groups served as a destabilizer for molecular crystal packing (Figure 3 b), and decreased the symmetry of the molecule and increased the distance among DMFc molecules, further decreasing the strength of the intermolecular interactions. In the BEE, the ring of BuPh showed strong interaction with DMFc because of the stronger electrostatic potential. The radial distribution functions (RDF) of Fe-H, showed that the peak around 3.8 Å originates from the π–π interaction (Figure 3 c). In addition, the interaction between DMFc and the line part of BuPh at 4.5–7.5 Å was a typical van der Waals interaction. The strong peak around 3.5 Å shown in Figure 3 d originates from the interaction between the methyl group of DMFc and oxygen atom of BuPh, and is consistent with Raman analysis. The intermolecular interaction strongly enhances the inter-solvation between DMFc and BuPh molecules to form a eutectic electrolyte.

The electrochemical behaviors of BuPh and DMFc were evaluated by cyclic voltammetry (CVs) in acetonitrile (ACN) with 0.1 m TBABF4 as the supporting salt (see Figures S5 and S6). Both BuPh/BuPh− and DMFc/DMFc+ redox couples were electrochemically reversible and the ratio of peak cathodic and anodic current was close to 1.0 at different scan rates. The calculated diffusion coefficients (D) of BuPh and DMFc molecules were 8.2–9.9×10−6 cm2 s−1 and 3.0–3.2×10−6 cm2 s−1, respectively, according to the anodic and cathodic peak currents at different scan rates (see Figures S7 and S8), which are on the same order of magnitude with other redox couples used in nonaqueous RFBs.22 BuPh and DMFc exhibited stable redox activity over 500 CV cycles without obvious decay, suggesting high chemical and electrochemical stabilities under CV conditions (see Figure S9).

The charge/dischage performance was evaluated using a full cell with BuPh-DMFc BEE serving as both anolyte and catholyte and Daramic 175 porous separator as membrane. As shown in Figure 4 a, the cell exhibited an open circuit potential of about 1.86 V, which is similar with the potential difference between BuPh/BuPh− and DMFc/DMFc+ redox couples. At 0.1 m and 1.0 m, the maximum power density of the BuPh-DMFc BEE reached 131 and 192 mW cm−2 with a corresponding current density of 110 and 190 mA cm−2, respectively (Figure 4 a). The performance was superior to the reported nonaqueous RFBs and comparable to some aqueous RFBs.12b, 15b, 23 At a current density of 20 mA cm−2, the cell using 0.1 m BuPh-DMFc BEE delivered an initial discharge capacity of 1.27 Ah L−1. The discharge capacities were maintained at 1.21, 1.16, 1.06, and 0.93 Ah L−1 at current densities of 40, 50, 60, and 80 mA cm−2 with Coulombic efficiencies (CE) of 89, 91, 92, and 94 %, respectively (Figure 4 b). The low CE could be attributed to the porous structure of the Daramic separator which has poor ion selectivity.17c, 19 The capacity maintained about 72 % of the initial value after 500 cycles at 60 mA cm−2 with corresponding Coulombic and energy efficiencies of about 93 and 51 %, respectively (Figure 4 c).

Electrochemical performance of the 1.0 m BuPh-DMFc BEE was evaluated under both flow and static modes (Figure 4 d; see Figures S10 and S11). In flow mode, the cell exhibited high material utilization (ca. 96 %), even at high current density (60 mA cm−2) with a charge and discharge capacity of 13.2 and 10.1 Ah L−1, respectively. The energy and voltage efficiencies of 50 and 66 % decreased to 45 and 54 % after 20 cycles (Figure 4 d). The CV curves of the electrolyte were also evaluated before and after cycling, and it can be found that the decay of peak current was observed at both sides and may be a result of the crossover of redox species during cycling. While in static mode, the cell had large overpotential and the discharge capacity maintained at 78 % after 50 cycles at 20 mA cm−2 (see Figure S12). Further increasing the concentration of BuPh-DMFc BEE to 2.0 m, the discharge capacities of the static cell reached 14 Ah L−1 and maintained 87 % of the initial capacity after 30 cycles (see Figure S13). Compared with the high utilization ratio at low concentration, the utilization of active materials in concentrated BuPh-DMFc BEE decreased, and is mainly due to the increased viscosity, low ionic conductivity of BEE, and limited solubility of the supporting salt. The increased viscosity decreases significantly the diffusivity of electroactive materials (see Table S1). Besides, the solubility of the supporting salt (TEABF4) was lower than 2 m in concentrated BEE, and would cause severe electromigration at high current density. Nevertheless, the achieved current density and capacity at 1.0 m and 2.0 m BuPh-DMFc BEE were still higher than the reported metal-free nonaqueous RFBs (see Figure S13 and Table S2).17b,17c, 24 To reduce overpotential, eliminate electromigration, and enhance cell stability of the BuPh-DMFc BEE, more effort should be made by means of boosting the liquidity of the BEE, and selecting more-soluble supporting salts, as well as advanced separators with high ion selectivity.

In conclusion, we have proposed a new class of active electrolytes for RFBs, the biredox eutectic electrolyte (BEE), which is capable of abating electrolyte contamination and achieving high concentration of redox-active materials through the intermolecular interactions between BuPh and DMFc. The cell based on the BuPh-DMFc BEE delivers a high volumetric capacity, good cycling stability, and high power density (192 mW cm−2 at 1.0 m), and is competitive among the demonstrated nonaqueous RFBs reported so far. More significantly, distinguished from the reported eutectic electrolyte in which the available concentration of the active component is diluted by the inactive component, all components in the BEE are electrochemically active, and is therefore highly desirable for the development of low-cost and high-performance next-generation RFBs.
