    Abstract

    Redox reservoirs (RRs) may be used to decouple the two half-reactions of water electrolysis, enabling the spatial and temporal separation of hydrogen and oxygen evolution. Organic RRs are appealing candidates for this application; however, their instability limits their utility. Here we show that a tetrathioether-substituted quinone, tetramercaptopropanesulfonate quinone (TMQ), exhibits significantly enhanced stability relative to anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonate, the most effective organic RR reported previously. The enhanced stability, confirmed by symmetric flow-battery experiments under relevant conditions, enables the stable electrochemical production of H2 and O2 in a continuous-flow electrolysis cell. The reduced RR, tetramercaptopropanesulfonate hydroquinone, is not susceptible to decomposition, whereas the oxidized state, TMQ, undergoes slow decomposition, evident only after sustained operation (>60 h). The analysis of the byproducts provides the basis for a decomposition mechanism, establishing a foundation for the design of new organic RRs with even better performance.

    Hydrogen is a sustainable energy carrier and a commodity chemical for industrial synthesis. Because the vast majority of contemporary hydrogen production relies on the steam reforming of methane, (1) water electrolysis driven by renewable power sources represents an appealing alternative. (2â4) Conventional water electrolysis takes place in a two-electrode configuration, with the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) taking place at the cathode, and the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) taking place at the anode. A membrane separator minimizes gas mixing; however, nontrivial gas crossover, which can occur when the system is operated at low current densities or elevated pressures, can create safety hazards, require gas purification, or cause electrolyzer degradation. (5â8) Decoupled water splitting circumvents this issue by pairing the individual HER and OER half-reactions with electrochemical reactions of a âredox reservoirâ (RR), (9) a redox mediator, or an electron-coupled-proton buffer (Figure 1a). (10â13) Analogous to the water reservoir in pumped hydroelectric storage, the function of a RR is to store the electrons and ions temporarily so that they can be used for different electrochemical half-reactions. Both solid-state (9,14â17) and molecular (18â26) redox mediators have been used for this purpose. Increasing attention has been focused on redox-active organic molecules because they are composed of earth-abundant materials and have properties that may be tuned synthetically (e.g., redox potential, solubility, stability, electrochemical kinetics). (27,28) One configuration for decoupled water splitting, schematically depicted in Figure 1b, features two individual electrolytic flow cells in which protons are exchanged via the soluble RR. Specifically, hydrogen evolution in the HER cell is coupled to oxidation of the RR rather than water oxidation, and water oxidation in the OER cell is coupled to reduction of the RR rather than proton reduction. This approach enables the spatial and temporal separation of hydrogen and oxygen evolution and also has other advantageous features. For example, the voltage of each individual cell is typically much less than that of a cell performing direct water splitting, potentially facilitating the integration of the decoupled process with renewable power sources (e.g., solar and/or wind), (10,18) and the fast HER rate is not intrinsically constrained by the comparatively sluggish OER rate. (29)

    Decoupled redox processes are well known in the photosynthetic electron-transport chains of bacteria and green plants. Such processes generate O2 gas and H2 equivalents in the form of NADH (NAD = nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) at separate sites, using quinone derivatives, such as plastoquinone and coenzyme Q10, and other mediators to shuttle electrons and protons. (30â32) In an abiotic analog of this process, Cronin and coworkers demonstrated hydroquinone monosulfonate (HQMS, Figure 1c) as a molecular RR for decoupled water splitting in 2013 (Figure 1c). (19) The oxidized form of this RR, quinone monosulfonate (QMS), is susceptible to nucleophilic attack by water, (33) decreasing its redox capacity and limiting its practical utility. The same group recently used anthraquinone-2,7-disulfonate (AQDS, Figure 1c) as a RR for decoupled water splitting in flow electrolysis cells. (24) AQDS is substantially more stable than QMS; however, its reduced form, anthrahydroquinone-2,7-disulfonate (AHQDS), is prone to disproportionation under acidic conditions to produce redox-inactive byproducts. (34â36) We recently reported the synthesis of a series of densely functionalized water-soluble quinones with redox potentials of 440â750 mV versus SHE. (37) The tetramercaptopropanesulfonate hydroquinone/quinone (TMHQ/TMQ) pair (Figure 1c) is appealing because stability tests performed in the context of fuel-cell (38) and flow-battery (39) applications indicated that the TMHQ/TMQ pair is much more stable than other hydroquinone/quinone pairs with similar redox potentials. Herein we show the utility of the TMHQ/TMQ pair as a RR for decoupled water splitting and demonstrate their improved stability relative to the previously reported AHQDS/AQDS RR. (24)

    Ideal molecular RRs for decoupled water splitting should meet several criteria: (10,11) exhibit a redox potential between the catalytic HER and OER potentials; buffer the solution pH by accepting and releasing protons upon reduction and oxidation, respectively; be readily synthesized from low-cost materials; and have compositional stability and high solubility in aqueous solutions. (40,41) The basic attributes of the TMHQ/TMQ pair suggest that it might be a good RR candidate for decoupled water splitting. (37â39) Cyclic voltammetry (CV) data show that the 2 H+/2 eâ redox potential of TMHQ/TMQ (0.61 V vs SHE) is approximately halfway between the standard potentials of the HER and the OER (see Figures S1 and S2 in Section 3 of the Supporting Information for the full CV and rotating-disk electrochemical analysis). TMHQ is readily prepared in >100 g quantities in a single step from inexpensive commercially available reagents. (37) The tetrasodium salt is readily soluble in aqueous solution with a saturation concentration of â¼0.25 M at room temperature, and the facile ion exchange of Na+ to protons using a resin column further increases the solubility to >1.0 M. (38)

    Our initial efforts focused on comparing the stability of the TMHQ/TMQ and AHQDS/AQDS redox couples by using a symmetric redox flow-battery approach reported by Goulet and Aziz. (35) This method uses the same redox electrolytes and identical concentrations on both sides of the cell to eliminate effects arising from crossover. Different electrolyte volumes are used, defined as the capacity-limiting side (CLS) and the non-capacity-limiting side (NCLS), respectively (Figure 2a). Preliminary 1H NMR spectroscopic studies (see Figures S3âS8 in Section 4 of the Supporting Information for details) revealed that each hydroquinone/quinone pair features a redox state that is comparatively less stable. Specifically, the oxidized TMQ state undergoes decomposition more rapidly than the reduced TMHQ state (see below for the analysis of the decomposition mechanism). In contrast, the decomposition of AHQDS/AQDS primarily occurs from the reduced state (AHQDS), as previously noted. Because effective RRs should be able to be stored in either the oxidized or the reduced form, subsequent stability testing prioritized the analysis of the less stable form of these redox couples (i.e., TMQ and AHQDS, respectively).

    Symmetric redox flow-battery tests started with the electrolytes in both the CLS and the NCLS having equal amounts of oxidized and reduced quinones/hydroquinones (i.e., at 50% state of charge (SOC)). Redox cycling was paused periodically after every two charge/discharge cycles to hold the CLS electrolyte at the less stable redox state (i.e., when charged/discharged to 100% TMQ and AHQDS, respectively; see Section 5 of the Supporting Information for details). (35) The experiments employed a 0.25 M concentration of the RRs in 1 M H2SO4(aq) electrolyte solutions, and the charge/discharge cycles were conducted at a constant current density of 50 mA/cm2 (Figure S9 in Section 5 of the Supporting Information). An elevated temperature of 50 Â°C was used to accelerate the decomposition rate. The TMHQ/TMQ symmetric flow-battery data show that this RR retains its charge capacity, with a <1.5% decay after 9 days under such stringent conditions (Figure 2b, and red curves in Figure 2d). 1H NMR analysis of the CLS solution after testing showed negligible byproduct formation (see Figure S10 in Section 5 of the Supporting Information). An identical symmetric flow-battery test was conducted with AHQDS/AQDS, and the data show a â¼26% loss in charge capacity over the same time period (Figure 2c, and blue curves in Figure 2d). (42)1H NMR analysis of this CLS solution revealed significant unidentified byproducts derived from AHQDS decomposition (see Figure S11 in Section 5 of the Supporting Information). These results demonstrate the improved stability of the TMHQ/TMQ RR pair relative to AHQDS/AQDS.

    While the data in Figure 2 highlight the potential utility of TMHQ/TMQ for decoupled water splitting, the slow decomposition of TMQ evident at 50 Â°C merits further attention. To characterize the byproducts from TMQ decay and probe the decomposition mechanism, we stored a solution of TMQ (0.25 M in 1 M H2SO4) at 80 Â°C, an even higher temperature intended to expedite its decay (see Section 6 of the Supporting Information for details). After 2 weeks, the 1H NMR spectrum of the resulting reaction mixture revealed two byproducts after the full consumption of TMQ: the hydroquinone TMHQ and a symmetrical disulfide in a 2.2:1 molar ratio (Figure 3a). Although the precise pathway for the generation of these byproducts is not certain, a plausible mechanism that accounts for the byproduct stoichiometry involves the stepwise nucleophilic substitution of the sulfonated thioether groups by water followed by the redox reaction between TMQ and the displaced sulfonated thiols (Figure 3b). (43) These results indicate that the âfully decomposedâ TMQ will still retain â¼80% of its capacity, because the major decomposition product is the reduced form of the quinone (i.e., TMHQ).

    The TMHQ/TMQ mixture was then tested as the RR for decoupled water splitting. (24) The experimental setup includes two independent electrolyzer assemblies (one each for RR-coupled HER and OER), three electrolyte tanks, and peristaltic pumps to transport the RR solution between the two electrolyzers (see Figure 4a for the schematics and Figure S15a for a photograph of the system). The RR tank was filled with 0.25 M TMHQ/TMQ in 1 M H2SO4 solution at 50% SOC, whereas both the HER and the OER solutions consisted of only 1 M H2SO4 solution. Carbon paper was used as the working electrode for the oxidation and reduction of the RR, whereas commercial Pt/C and IrRuOx electrocatalysts were employed for the HER and OER, respectively. Carbon paper and platinized titanium screen were used as the gas diffusion layers for the HER and OER, respectively. The decoupled water-splitting experiments were conducted at 50 Â°C with an electrolyte flow rate of 200 mL/min (see Figures S15 and S16 and Section 8 of the Supporting Information for details). Control experiments investigating direct water-splitting experiments using a single electrolyzer assembly under the same conditions were also performed (see Figures S13 and S14 and Section 7 of the Supporting Information for details).

    Figure 4b shows the polarization curves obtained with the direct HER/OER water-splitting cell and the RR-coupled HER and OER cells. The onset cell voltage of the direct water-splitting cell (Figure 4b, green curve) is â¼1.50 V, whereas the onset potential with the RR-coupled HER (Figure 4b, blue curve) and OER (Figure 4b, red curve) cells is much lower, corresponding to approximately 0.60 and 0.85 V, respectively. The practical device performance of the direct and decoupled water-splitting configurations was compared by conducting chronopotentiometry experiments at a high current density of 250 mA/cm2 (Figure 4c). The direct water-splitting cell exhibited a slow increase in the cell voltage, rising from 1.77 to 1.88 V over 24 h (Figure 4c, green curve). The small increase in voltage was traced to the deactivation of the IrRuOx OER catalyst over the course of the experiment. In the decoupled water-splitting tests, the HER cell maintained a steady 0.97 V cell voltage throughout the experiment (Figure 4c, blue curve). The 62 h time period is significantly longer than the 24 h period used to assess the previous AHQDS/AQDS RR, thus providing a more stringent test of RR stability. (24) The stable HER performance during this experiment reflects the stability of both the TMHQ/TMQ RR and the Pt/C HER catalyst. Cell polarization curves recorded before and after the long-term chronopotentiometry experiment nearly overlap, providing further confirmation of the system stability (see Figure S15b, blue curves and Table S1 in Section 8 of the Supporting Information). The decoupled OER cell exhibits a slight increase in the steady-state cell voltage, increasing from 1.40 to 1.60 V over the 62 h chronopotentiometry experiment (Figure 4c, red curve). The stability of the RR electrolyte, evident in the HER cell, suggests that the instability arises from the slow deactivation of the IrRuOx OER catalyst during the experiment. This conclusion is supported by the similar deactivation observed in the direct water-splitting experiment (Figure 4, green curve; also see Figure S13b in Section 7 of the Supporting Information), and nonoverlapping polarization curves obtained before and after the long-term chronopotentiometry experiment provided further evidence of this deactivation (see Figure S15b, red curves in Section 8 of the Supporting Information). Furthermore, volumetric measurement of the H2 and O2 gases produced from the decoupled HER and OER cells indicates â¼100% Faradaic efficiency (FE) based on the total charge passed during the extended experiment (Figure 4d; also see Table S2 in Section 8 of the Supporting Information). Further calculations showed that the energy efficiency of the decoupled water-splitting process enabled by the TMHQ/TMQ RR was 62.4% when the decoupled HER and OER cells were operated at 250 mA/cm2 in 1 M H2SO4 at 50 Â°C, comparable to the reported value achieved using the AHQDS/AQDS RR in a similar two-electrode flow cell setup under similar operating conditions (24) and higher than those achieved using other molecular RRs in three-electrode stirred cells (18,19) (see Table S3 and Section 8 of the Supporting Information for details).

    The results presented above demonstrate that TMHQ/TMQ is a compelling molecular RR system for decoupled water splitting, demonstrating stable performance in the flow electrolysis cells for >60 h. The appealing features of the TMHQ/TMQ as a RR pair reflect its facile synthetic accessibility from inexpensive precursors, the positioning of its redox potential between the catalytic HER/OER potentials, its high aqueous solubility that is capable of supporting high current densities, and its enhanced stability. This quinone/hydroquinone pair is found to be substantially more stable than AHQDS/AQDS, which has been previously used for decoupled water-splitting and flow-battery applications. Characterization of decomposition byproducts suggests that TMQ decays via the nucleophilic cleavage of the thioether linkage, displacing thiols that undergo oxidative coupling to disulfides in the presence of TMQ. This insight into the mechanism of the TMQ decay provides an important starting point for the future studies focused on designing even more stable redox couples capable of achieving even more robust performance for RRs or flow batteries.
