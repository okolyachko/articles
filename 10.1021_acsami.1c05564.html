    Abstract

    Nonaqueous redox flow batteries (NARFBs) are a potential candidate for high-energy-density storage systems because of their wider electrochemical windows than that of the aqueous systems. However, their further development is hindered by the low solubility of organic redox-active materials and poor high-current operations. Herein, we report a liquid anolyte material, 3-nitrotoluene (3-NT), which demonstrates high chemical stability and mass- and charge-transfer kinetics. The NARFB based on 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1-methoxy-4-[2′-methoxyethoxy]benzene/3-NT exhibits an energy efficiency of 71.8% even at a relatively high current density of 60 mA cm–2. Benefiting from the high miscibility of the redox species, an ultra-high volumetric energy density of 37.8 W h L–1 can be achieved at 1.0 M. This work provides a viable method to build an NARFB with both high operational current density and energy density for next-generation, low-cost, and high-energy storage systems.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Considering the energy crisis and environmental issues, renewable energy resources have attracted great interest in securing a sustainable development of human society. (1) In order to solve the intermittent issue of the renewable sources, safe and cost-effective energy storage technologies are in urgent need for power grid applications. (2) Among the various candidates, redox flow batteries (RFBs) have received increasing attention because of their high scalability and flexibility in design over the decoupled energy and power units. (3,4) For conventional aqueous RFBs, the energy density is low because the open-circuit voltage (OCV) is limited by the electrolysis of water on the electrodes. (5) In contrast, enabled by the wider electrochemical windows, nonaqueous RFBs (NARFBs) exhibit higher OCV, thus making it possible to achieve a much higher energy density (e.g., 50 W h L–1) (6−8) than that of their aqueous counterparts (25 W h L–1). (9)
    Nevertheless, a number of crucial issues need to be addressed for further commercial applications. The major limitations of the current NARFBs are the low operational current densities (j) and low energy density due to the insufficient solubility of the active materials. A variety of measures have been recently proposed to enhance the high-current operation through increasing the electrolyte conductivity and reducing the membrane resistance. (10−12) However, the j of these modified NARFBs is still relatively low, usually less than 40 mA cm–2. Correspondingly, one strategy is to develop highly soluble redox-active materials with fast mass- and charge-transfer kinetics, which is possible to achieve high-current-density operation. In addition, the solubility of most organic redox-active materials is relatively low and decreases further in the supporting electrolyte mixture, which is not conducive to achieving high energy density. (8,13) It is demonstrated that NARFBs based on liquid electroactive materials can reach fairly high theoretical energy densities (>100 W h L–1). (14−16) Therefore, the exploitation of redox-active materials with fast electrode kinetics and high solubility is the key to achieve high-performance NARFBs.
    Herein, we select four types of anolyte materials based on nitrobenzene (NB), namely, NB, 2-nitrotoluene (2-NT), 3-nitrotoluene (3-NT), and 4-nitrotoluene (4-NT). NB is selected as the basic compound owing to its fast mass- and charge-transfer kinetics, high miscibility, and highly reversible electrochemical moiety. (17,18) When liquid 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1-methoxy-4-[2′-methoxyethoxy]benzene (DBMMB) is used in the cathode side, the battery can yield high energy density. However, the battery suffered from irreversible capacity loss. The major decayed species was identified to be azobenzene. (18) To study the substituent effect, the methyl group is added at the pro/meta/para positions to generate 2-NT/3-NT/4-NT, respectively. The three liquid anolyte materials (NB, 2-NT, and 3-NT) are miscible with acetonitrile (MeCN) at room temperature, whereas 4-NT still exhibits a high solubility (5.4 M in MeCN) even though it is solid at room temperature. (18) All of them exhibit fast mass- and charge-transfer kinetics. Coupled with DBMMB having high miscibility as the catholyte active material, the resultant NARFBs exhibit high rate capability, high volumetric energy density, and good cycling performance.

    2. Experimental Section

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To

    2.1. Materials

    MeCN (Sigma-Aldrich, 99.9%), NB (TCL, 99.0%), 2-NT (TCL, 99.0%), 3-NT (TCL, 99.0%), 4-NT (TCL, 99.0%), tetraethylammonium tetrafluoroborate (TEABF4, Jiuding, 99.0%, China), lithium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)amide (LiTFSI, Meryer, 99.5%, China), and AgNO3 (Aladdin, 99.8%) were used. DBMMB and tetraethylammonium bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide (TEATFSI) were synthesized according to the reported procedures. (19,20)

    2.2. Computation Methods

    The quantum chemical parameters were obtained using the density functional theory (DFT) with the Gaussian 09 package. (21) The implicit solvent model used the M062X functional and 6-31G(d,p) basis set for all calculations.

    2.3. Electrochemical Test

    Cyclic voltammetry (CV), linear sweep voltammetry (LSV), and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) were performed on a VersaSTAT 3 electrochemical workstation (Princeton Applied Research, USA) and a CHI604D electrochemical workstation (Shanghai Chenhua Instruments Co., China), respectively. The details can be found elsewhere. (22) The diffusion coefficients and electrochemical kinetics can be calculated based on the modified Randles–Sevcik equation (23,24) and the Koutecky–Levich equation, (10) respectively.

    2.4. Flow Cell Tests

    A sandwich-structure battery containing Daramic-250 and two graphite felt electrodes with an active area of 4 cm2 (Morgan WDF-5) was tested in an argon-filled glovebox. Before the battery test, the flow cells and graphite felts were ultrasonically cleaned and dried under vacuum at 70 °C for 24 h. The commercial separator Daramic-250 was soaked in MeCN under ultrasonication for 20 min and dried under vacuum at 70 °C for 24 h. The electrolytes of 5 mL volume on each side were circulated at a flow rate of 50 mL min–1 by two peristaltic pumps (BT100-1L, LONGER, China). The flow cell was tested by a LAND CT2001A cell test system (LAND, China). The same mixed electrolyte is employed at the anode and cathode. The polarization curves (current–voltage curves) were also collected on the VersaSTAT 3.

    2.5. Other Characterizations

    After a certain number of cycles, the electrolyte was pre-treated and used for carrying out CV, ultraviolet–visible (UV–vis) spectroscopy, hydrogen nuclear magnetic resonance (1H NMR, Varian Inova 500 MHz spectrometer), and gas chromatography–mass spectroscopy (GC–MS, 7890GC-5977MS) tests in time. For GC–MS analyses, after the flow cell test, 5 mL of the sample was taken out from the anode electrolyte, evaporated naturally, and diluted with n-hexane. The supporting electrolyte was removed by centrifugation. An HP-5MS column was selected with an Agilent Technologies model 7890 chromatograph equipped with a model 5977 mass detector. The solubilities of NB, 2-NT, and 3-NT in 1.0 M TEATFSI/MeCN were evaluated by a UV–vis spectrometer (Shimadzu UV-2450 spectrometer). (25)

    3. Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Suitable supporting electrolytes need to be identified since they determine not only the electrolyte conductivity but also the chemical stability of redox materials. Wei et al. reported that the electrolytes based on MeCN and TEA+-based salts delivered higher conductivities than most other electrolytes. (11) Furthermore, it has been revealed by the electron spin resonance test that TEA+ and TFSI– in TEATFSI exhibit less damage on the stability of radical ions than Li+ in LiTFSI and BF4– in TEABF4. (10,11) During the screening process, the flow cell using TEATFSI exhibits relatively better cycling stability and higher efficiency over 50 cycles than that with TEABF4 and LiTFSI (Figure S1). Accordingly, TEATFSI was selected as the supporting electrolyte for the battery system.
    Instead of ion exchange membranes, we choose the porous separator Daramic-250 (250 μm thick, pore size 150 nm) to reduce the resistance of the system in favor of quickly charging and discharging at high current densities. However, the large pore size of the porous membrane usually causes crossover and damages the cycling stability of the battery. To solve this problem, mixed electrolytes are employed to reduce the concentration gradient and thus slow down the crossover. CV was first performed to measure the half-wave potentials of redox-active materials. As shown in Figure 1a, the half-wave potential of DBMMB is 0.69 V versus Ag/Ag+, which remains constant. Different anolyte active materials show different potentials. The half-wave potentials of NB, 2-NT, 3-NT, and 4-NT are −1.54, −1.65, −1.56, and −1.59 V versus Ag/Ag+, respectively. Thus, the OCVs are 2.23, 2.34, 2.25, and 2.28 V for NARFBs based on DBMMB/NB, DBMMB/2-NT, DBMMB/3-NT, and DBMMB/4-NT, respectively. The DFT calculation indicates that the substituent changes the lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals (LUMO) energetics. As shown in Figure S2, there is a linear relationship between the reduction potentials of anolyte materials and the LUMO energy. The solubilities of anolyte materials in 1.0 M TEATFSI/MeCN measured by UV–vis spectroscopy are 6.1, 5.4, 5.3, and 3.8 M for NB, 2-NT, 3-NT, and 4-NT, respectively (Figure S3). Consequently, high theoretical energy densities of up to 182, 169, 160, and 116 W h L–1 can be provided by the batteries with NB, 2-NT, 3-NT, and 4-NT, respectively, which are superior to that of most NARFBs (rarely exceeding 50 W h L–1). (10,26,27)
    The diffusion coefficient (D) of the anolyte materials was evaluated by CV tests at different scan rates (Figure S4). The calculated D values of NB, 2-NT, 3-NT, and 4-NT molecules are 3.25–3.33 × 10–5, 2.41–2.57 × 10–5, 2.93 × 10–5, and 3.06–3.11 × 10–5 cm2 s–1, respectively. LSV was carried out using the rotating disk electrode technique to examine the electrochemical kinetics of the anolyte materials (Figure S5). Based on the Koutecky–Levich equation, (10) the electrochemical kinetic rate constants (k0) are determined to be 2.85 × 10–2, 3.28 × 10–2, 3.57 × 10–2, and 1.46 × 10–2 cm s–1 for NB, 2-NT, 3-NT, and 4-NT, respectively. Compared with the reported values for NB (D = 2.05 × 10–6 cm2 s–1 and k0 = 3.57 × 10–2 cm s–1), (18) the different values may be due to different supporting electrolytes (TEATFSI vs TEABF4). The fast mass- and charge-transfer kinetics are originated from the small molecular size of the anolyte materials. To the best of our knowledge, the kinetic parameters of the three anolyte materials are the highest among the redox-active organic materials for NARFBs (Figure 1b). (10,12,16,22,24,28,29)
    To assess the substitutional effect in a realistic condition, mixed anolyte and catholyte materials were subjected to cycling test in flow cells with Daramic-250 as the porous separator. As shown in Figure 2a, there is a rapid capacity decay when 2-NT is utilized as the active material for an anode electrolyte. In sharp contrast, the utilization of 3-NT leads to an obvious improvement in the cycling stability, 83.6% capacity retention after 50 cycles compared with 66.1% for NB and 44.0% for 4-NT. As for the capacity decay, one main reason is the instability of the redox-active material, especially in the charged state. (10,11) The charged state of NB is a radical anion, whose degradation via protonation is the most likely mechanism due to its tendency to be electrically neutralized by the solvent. (18,30,31) The sources of protons include solvents (MeCN), supporting electrolytes (TEATFSI), and charged cathode redox-active materials (DBMMB•+). (11,32,33)
    DFT calculation indicates that the derivatization has an influence on the singly occupied molecular orbitals (SOMO) energetics of the radical anions (Figure 2b). The SOMO energetics are in the order of 3-NT•– (0.46 eV) < NB•– (0.47 eV) < 4-NT•– (0.50 eV) < 2-NT•– (0.53 eV). The 2-NT•– radical anion shows a higher value than NB•– and thus is more prone to nucleophilic reactions, whereas 3-NT•– is the opposite. The repeated CV results further confirm the electrochemical stability of NB, 3-NT, and 4-NT (Figure S6) and the instability of 2-NT, especially in the presence of DBMMB (Figure S7), which is in fair accordance with the DFT results. The cycled electrolyte based on 2-NT was tested with 1H NMR after removing the supporting electrolyte, which reveals that a new compound forms during cycling (Figure 2c). The major byproduct can be separated and identified as an azo-dimer (18) by GC–MS analysis (Figure S8). As a result, 3-NT is the optimal material in terms of high cycling stability among the four anolyte materials.
    The rate performance was evaluated by flow cell tests with a mixed electrolyte of 0.1 M DBMMB/0.1 M 3-NT in 1.0 M TEATFSI/MeCN at different j values, from 50 to 80 mA cm–2, five cycles at each j. The capacity is almost constant at each j, indicating the high stability of the flow battery (Figure 3a). The average discharge capacities are maintained at 2.6, 2.1, 1.5, and 0.9 A h L–1 for j values of 50, 60, 70, and 80 mA cm–2, respectively. When j increases from 50 to 80 mA cm–2, the cycling efficiency (CE) slightly increases due to the reduced crossover effect. However, the voltage efficiency (VE) and energy efficiency (EE) decrease from 81.0 and 76.1 to 64.0 and 61.4%, respectively, due to the increased polarization resistance (Figure 3b). Overall, the NARFB based on DBMMB/3-NT exhibits an excellent rate performance even at such relatively high current densities.
    The flow battery with a mixed electrolyte of 0.1 M DBMMB/0.1 M 3-NT in 1.0 M TEATFSI/MeCN as both cathode and anode electrolytes was evaluated for charge–discharge cycling at 60 mA cm–2. Figure 3c displays the polarization curves of the battery at the state of charge (SOC) values of 20, 50, and 100%. The OCV of the cell with 100% SOC is ∼2.3 V, similar to the redox difference in Figure 1a. The peak power density achieves a value of 237 W h L–1 at 100% SOC, which is superior to that of almost all the reported NARFBs. (8,22,34)Figure 3d shows the detailed CE and capacity at 60 mA cm–2. The battery maintains relatively high efficiencies (CE 93.7%, VE 76.6%, and EE 71.8%) over 50 cycles. The average discharge capacity is 2.25 A h L–1, corresponding to a material utilization of 84.0%. The flow cell shows an impressive performance, especially an EE of >70% even at a high j of 60 mA cm–2. (8,10−12) As shown in the EIS result (Figure S9), the membrane resistance is 4.3 Ω cm2. Such a low resistance, combined with the high diffusion capability of the active materials, contributes to the excellent performance at a relatively high j.
    To make full use of the high solubility of DBMMB and 3-NT, a flow cell with 1.0 M DBMMB/1.0 M 3-NT in 1.2 M TEATFSI/MeCN was assembled and tested at 80 mA cm–2. The high-concentration electrolyte is a homogeneous liquid phase (Figure S10). As shown in Figure 4a, the average CE, VE, and EE over 50 cycles are 92.8, 52.0, and 48.2%, respectively. The initial charge and discharge capacities are 13.7 and 11.2 A h L–1, respectively, corresponding to a charge energy density of 37.8 W h L–1 and a discharge energy density of 18.7 W h L–1. This work provides a viable method of building a promising NARFB with high operational current density and energy density, which are significantly higher than those of the reported NARFBs (Figure 4b). (8,10−12,16,18,29,34)
    Given that the theoretical capacity of the battery with 1.0 M DBMMB/1.0 M 3-NT in 1.2 M TEATFSI/MeCN is 26.8 A h L–1, the achieved discharge capacity of 11.2 A h L–1 indicates that the material utilization is rather low. Since a high concentration (1.0 M) was used, the increased viscosity hinders the diffusion of redox-active materials. In addition, the insufficient ionic conductivity due to the increased ion pairing (35) severely impairs the battery performance. The high-concentration battery fails to maintain stable cycling; the discharge capacity gradually decreases from 11.2 to 4.4 A h L–1 over 50 cycles (Figure 4a). To explore that, UV–vis and CV tests were run on the fresh and cycled electrolytes (Figures S11 and S12). The decay in the anode electrolyte is found, as evidenced by the appearance of new redox peaks at −1.73 and −1.80 V. Further 1H NMR (Figure S13) and GC–MS analyses (Figure S14) reveal that 3-NT degrades to 3,3′-dimethylazobenzene during cycling, which is similar to the reported decomposition pathway, that is, NB-based materials form nitrosobenzene and phenylhydroxylamine and finally dimerize into azobenzene-based materials. (18) Such a decay of the anolyte active materials must be the dominating reason for the poor cycling stability. There are at least two strategies to avoid such byproducts. One is to explore derivatives through DFT calculation and experiments to screen more stable active materials. The other is to develop more suitable solvents and supporting electrolytes to minimize side reactions. It is noted that the azo byproduct has been used in some RFBs, (22,34) which may be an advantage for the NB-based flow cells.

    4. Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In summary, we demonstrate that the liquid NB-based redox species can be promising anolyte materials for all-organic NARFBs. Especially, the battery with 3-NT as an anolyte material exhibits the lowest capacity loss due to high chemical stability and a low crossover effect. Benefiting from fast mass- and charge-transfer kinetics, the battery based on DBMMB/3-NT exhibits a relatively high EE of 71.8% at a large j of 60 mA cm–2. In addition, a fairly high volumetric energy density (37.8 W h L–1 at 1.0 M) is achieved due to the high miscibility of the liquid redox-active materials. More efforts should be made on increasing the material utilization ratio and reducing the capacity loss for the high-concentration electrolyte to further improve the DBMMB/3-NT flow battery.
