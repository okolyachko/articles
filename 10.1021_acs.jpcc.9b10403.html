    Abstract

    A systematic study is reported on the interaction of two representative redox active organic molecules and two solvent molecules with pristine and defective graphene surfaces as a model of an electrode surface of a redox flow battery (RFB). The redox active molecules include a catholyte, 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1,4-dimethoxybenzene (DDB), and an anolyte molecule, benzothiadiazole (BTZ), and the solvent molecules include acetonitrile (MeCN) and ethylene carbonate (EC). The graphene defects investigated include a single vacancy, double vacancy, zigzag step edge, and armchair step edge. Computations suggest that the interactions of all molecules with a pristine graphene surface are relatively weak (0.2 to 0.8 eV) and dominated by van der Waals effects; therefore, these molecules are chemically stable upon interacting with pristine nondefective graphene. The BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules interact strongly (1.5 to 5.5 eV) with the single vacancy and zigzag step edges of graphene that leads to the possible decomposition of the molecules with strength of interaction in the order of MeCN > BTZ > DDB > EC and MeCN > EC > BTZ > DDB, respectively. Calculations show that the BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules interact less strongly (0.2 to 1.4 eV) to the double vacancy and armchair step edge than to the single vacancy and zigzag step edge. The binding energies of the molecules were significantly reduced when interacting with the passivated defects, suggesting that the passivation of the defects could help prevent unwanted chemical interactions between the neutral molecules and the electrode surface. In all organic RFBs, one of the crucial bottlenecks is the stability of the constituent molecules with the electrode material, and this study provides insights into the chemical interaction of selected candidate species with a model carbon electrode.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Redox flow batteries (RFBs) are a promising technology in which chemical energy provided by electroactive materials dissolved in liquids in storage tanks can be used for grid level battery storage. The energy capacity of RFBs is decided by the amount of electrolyte materials stored, indicating a high scalability. (1) RFBs store energy in soluble redox active molecules with a redox transformation (oxidation or reduction) and electrochemical or chemical reactions (redox triggered reactions or surface reactions) taking place near the electrode surfaces. (2) The advantages of the RFB include scalability, cost of materials, longer lasting electrodes, and ability to separate the catholyte and anolyte solutions into different tanks. (2−4) Currently, there are several types of RFB concepts including metal–inorganic and organic-based systems. (5) In terms of the solvents used in organic RFBs, there are aqueous versus nonaqueous concepts. RFBs with redox active organic molecules (ROMs) and nonaqueous solvents are one of the newest to be researched and offer a variety of potential advantages over inorganic aqueous systems. (2,6−8) Among the various RFBs, battery materials using organic molecules offer a large diversity of structures, which allow for tuneability of redox flow systems. (4,9) The use of a nonaqueous solvent also offers the benefit of a higher working voltage window.
    Currently, fundamental research studies related to the RFB are in their early stages, and their longer-term performance depends on overcoming various bottlenecks including synthesis of scalable cost-efficient energy storage materials with adequate properties. An important bottleneck to address is the lack of materials with long-term chemical stability and solubility in a suitable solvent under electrochemical conditions. (10) The degradation of the ROM and solvent molecules is an ongoing problem that has to be overcome. (3,11) Although understanding the stability of these molecules in their charged or radical states is important, the first step is to understand their chemical stability in their neutral state in the presence of electrode materials.
    We have used density functional theory (DFT) to investigate the chemical stability of some representative ROMs and solvent molecules on the carbon electrode surface. Interactions with carbon are considered because it is a common choice of electrode material in RFBs. (12) DFT calculations have already played an important role for understanding how ion pairing and different reaction pathways determine the stability of nonaqueous redox flow molecules in bulk solution at an atomistic level. (13,14) There has also been a DFT study of the binding of organic molecules, including the solvent molecule acetonitrile used in RFB, to a pristine graphene surface (15) as well as various other studies of the reactivity of graphene with and without defect sites. (16−23) In the current work, a DFT-based study is reported for the interaction of representative organic ROMs and nonaqueous solvent molecules relevant to flow batteries with model carbon surfaces with and without defects, as illustrated in Scheme 1. The goal of this investigation is to provide insights into possible degradation mechanisms of both ROMs and solvent molecules that can be used to help develop strategies to limit the reaction of the molecules with the electrode.
    In this work, the carbon electrode is modeled as graphene including consideration of possible defects. The defects studied in this work include a single vacancy, double vacancy, “zigzag” step edges, “armchair” step edges, passivated single vacancies, and passivated zigzag step edges. In flow battery electrodes, electrochemical operating conditions and majority/minority species at the electrode surface during charge and discharge may result in unpassivated defect sites as we are considering in this study. In this work, only neutral molecule interactions are considered, and the surface is modeled with no electric field. The molecules studied in this work are shown in Scheme 1 and include the 2,5-di-tert-butyl-1,4-dimethoxybenzene (DDB) and 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BTZ) redox active molecules and the acetonitrile (MeCN) and ethylene carbonate (EC) solvent molecules. Optimized coordinates in the XYZ format for the DDB and BTZ molecules can be found in the Supporting Information. The organic catholyte DDB is promising because of its high oxidation potential (∼4 V Li/Li+) and electrochemical reversibility (24) and ability to cycle under battery conditions. (25,26) The organic anolyte BTZ is promising because of its low reduction potential (2.25 V Li/Li+) and its relative stability in nonaqueous RFBs. (10,14) The MeCN and EC molecules are typical nonaqueous solvents that offer the benefit of a large electrochemical window of operation. (9)
    In Section 2 the computational details are presented as well as the structures of the molecules and the carbon surfaces with and without defect sites. In Section 3 the results of the interactions of the molecules with the carbon sites and their energies are presented. Comparisons between results for pristine sites, defect sites, and passivated sites are made in this section. Conclusions are drawn in Section 4.

    2. Computational Details

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In this work, we use spin-polarized DFT calculations with the projector augmented wave method (27−29) and the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) generalized gradient approximation. (30) All calculations were performed with the VASP code. (31,32) The plane wave cutoff of 450 eV was used for all calculations, and the total energy difference criterion for the self-consistent cycles was 10–5 eV. Structural relaxation used the conjugate gradient algorithm and was completed when the total energy difference between ionic relaxation steps was less than 10–4 eV. A Gaussian smearing width of 0.05 eV was used for occupations. Corrections for van der Waals effects were included using the Grimme D3 method. (33)
    Calculations of molecular interactions with pristine, single vacancy, double vacancy, and a passivated single vacancy were performed with a single graphene layer. Lattice vectors for the a and b axes were obtained from the optimization of a unit cell of graphite with the given convergence criteria and 16 × 16 × 8 Monkhorst–Pack k-point sampling. For DDB, 9 × 9 supercells with a 25 Å vacuum was used. For all other molecules, 8 × 8 supercells were used with a 22.4 Å vacuum. We used 2 × 2 × 1 Monkhorst–Pack k-point sampling for these calculations. The slightly larger supercell for DDB is due to its larger size.
    For calculation of step edges, a 16 × 8 cell with a 22.4 Å vacuum was used with two layers of graphene, with half of the top layer removed to model the step edges. The vacuum is measured from the exposed bottom layer to its repeat image. For these step edge calculations, a 1 × 2 × 1 Monkhorst–Pack k-point sampling was used. Lattice parameters were held fixed during the simulations. To calculate the structure and energy of the individual molecules, a 40 Å box sampled at the Gamma point was used.
    The computational methods described in this work are similar to those of other studies; (15,16,18,19,21,34,35) while compared to some authors, reduced k-point meshes were employed. This was done to optimize the computational efficiency of the larger supercells used in this study while maintaining sufficient numerical precision for the purposes of this study. Convergence tests of the computational methods showed that the results for calculated binding energies are numerically converged to approximately <0.04 eV. These tests were based on increasing the plane wave cutoff from 450 to 550 eV, doubling vacuum sizes, and doubling k-point meshes for the a and b axes.

    3.1. Graphene Defects

    The graphene defects studied in this paper include a single vacancy, double vacancy, and two step edges. These defects are thought to be representative of common defects in graphene. (37) The computed formation energy of the single vacancy (Figure 1a) for the 8 × 8 supercell is 7.54 eV, and the computed magnetic moment is 1.02 μB. This formation energy agrees well with 7.5 eV reported by El-Barbary et al., (38) and 1.02 μB agrees well with 1.04 μB calculated previously with an equivalent sized supercell by Lehtinen et al. (34) These consistencies give confidence in the computational methods used in this work. For double vacancies, there are several possible types: an arrangement of one octagon and two pentagons, an arrangement of three pentagons and three heptagons, and an arrangement of four pentagons, four heptagons, and one hexagon. (37) The one used in modeling of binding energies in this work is the arrangement of three pentagons and three heptagons. This double vacancy, shown in Figure 1b, has the lowest formation energy and is also reported to be commonly found in graphene. (37) The double vacancy is found to have zero magnetic moment. For step edges, the zigzag and armchair configurations were chosen because they are representative of common edge structures in graphene. (39) The zigzag and armchair step edges are shown in Figure 1c,d, respectively.

    3.2. Molecular Interaction with Pristine and Defective Graphene

    The computed binding energies of BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC with pristine and defective (single vacancy, double vacancy, zigzag, or armchair) graphene models are shown in Table 1. The computed binding energies with the pristine graphene model are −0.56, −0.78, −0.23, and −0.38 eV, respectively, for the BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules. These binding energies scale with the size of the molecule, which is indicative of van der Waals type binding. In the previous work of Lazar et al., (15) the binding energy of MeCN was calculated for a coronene model and a 4 × 4 periodic graphene model in the range of −0.23 to −0.34 eV using several different density functionals and wave function-based methods, and our results for MeCN are consistent with their results. The work of Lazar et al. (15) also experimentally measured the binding enthalpy of MeCN to a graphene surface, which was −0.33 eV. The optimized structures of each molecule on pristine graphene with key bond distances are shown in Figure 2. In Figure 2, it can be seen that all the molecules prefer to bind in a flat/lengthwise-type manner. In Figure 2a, the BTZ molecule is shown to bind in a planar fashion; in Figure 2b, DDB is shown to bind lengthwise along the surface with the central aromatic ring planar with the surface; in Figure 2c, the MeCN molecule is seen to bind lengthwise with the nitrogen end downward, and in Figure 2d, the EC molecule is seen to bind with its central pentagon ring planar to the surface. Calculations excluding the van der Waals D3-Grimme correction and starting from the configurations shown in Figure 2 reveal a weak binding of −0.03 eV for BTZ, no binding for DDB, a weak binding of −0.03 eV for MeCN, and a weak binding of −0.04 eV for EC. These small values indicate that binding to the pristine graphene is dominated by van der Waals effects for all of the molecules.

    The results in Table 1 indicate that for all molecules, there is stronger interaction with the single vacancy defect with binding energies of −1.54 to −3.92 eV. These interactions are characterized by bond stretching/breaking and quenching of the magnetic moment of the defects. In Figure 3, optimized structures of the BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules adsorbed on a single vacancy graphene site are shown. In the optimized structure for the BTZ molecule shown in Figure 3a, the sulfur from BTZ has filled the graphene single vacancy, and there is significant elongation of the nitrogen–sulfur (N–S) bond along with an opening of the aromatic ring. The nitrogen–sulfur bond distance is 2.96 Å for this complex compared to 1.62 Å for the isolated molecule, which is due to the highly exothermic adsorption of BTZ (−2.29 eV) to the graphene with the single vacancy. To check the convergence with the number of layers of graphene, the BTZ molecule was calculated to have a −2.16 eV binding energy when using two layers of graphene, quantitatively similar to the −2.29 eV binding energy from a single layer calculation. Figure 3b shows the structure of the DDB molecule binding to the single vacancy; it is characterized by a hydrogen from a CH3 group being bound to the single vacancy, while the C from the remaining CH2 group is also bound to the vacancy. The binding energy of the structure in Figure 3b is −2.1 eV. Figure 3c shows the structure of the MeCN molecule binding to a single vacancy graphene. The nitrogen of MeCN is seen to fill the single vacancy with breaking of the nitrogen–carbon (N–C) bond, and the interaction is stronger than that of BTZ with a binding energy of −3.92 eV. In Figure 3c, the nitrogen–carbon distance with a broken bond is 2.52 Å compared to 1.17 Å for the isolated molecule. Figure 3d shows the structure of the EC solvent molecule binding to a single vacancy. The adsorbed EC molecule has oxygen and carbon bound to surface carbons, surrounding the single vacancy with a binding energy of −1.54 eV. In Figure 3d, the bond between carbon and oxygen interacting with the vacancy is 1.43 Å compared to 1.20 Å for the isolated molecule. The BTZ interaction with the single vacancy has another structure shown in Figure S1a with a weaker binding energy of −1.52 eV than −2.29 eV of the structure shown in Figure 3a. The MeCN interaction with the single vacancy also has another structure shown in Figure S1b with a slightly weaker binding energy of −3.69 eV than −3.92 eV of the structure shown in Figure 3c.
    The results in Table 1 also show that there is strong interaction of the BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules with the graphene zigzag step edge. Images of the binding of these molecules to these step edges are shown in Figure 4. Figure 4a shows the BTZ molecule with sulfur and nitrogen bound to two of the zigzag terminations with a binding energy of −1.36 eV. In Figure 4a, for BTZ, it is seen that the bond between nitrogen and sulfur bound to the zigzag terminations is broken with a measured distance of 2.57 Å compared to the distance in the isolated molecule of 1.62 Å. Figure 4b shows the DDB molecule binding to the zigzag step edge; it is characterized by a hydrogen atom from a tertbutyl group absorbing to a zigzag termination, while the carbon atom from the remaining CH2 group binds to the same zigzag termination. The binding energy of the configuration in Figure 4b is −2.86 eV. Figure 4c shows the MeCN molecule binding to the zigzag step edge, which is characterized by a nitrogen and carbon bonding to two of the zigzag terminations with a binding energy of −5.53 eV. In Figure 4c, the nitrogen–carbon bond of MeCN is seen to be stretched to 1.35 Å compared to 1.17 Å for the isolated molecule. Figure 4d shows the EC molecule binding to a zigzag step edge, which is characterized by oxygen and carbon interacting with two of the zigzag terminations and a binding energy of −4.47 eV. In Figure 4d, the oxygen–carbon bond of EC is seen to be stretched to 1.58 Å compared to 1.2 Å for the isolated molecule.
    The results in Table 1 show a weak interaction of almost all molecules with the double vacancy and the armchair step edge. The exception is the BTZ molecule binding to the armchair step edge, which is shown in Figure 5. The structure in Figure 5 shows that carbon and nitrogen of BTZ interact with one of the armchair terminations and have a binding energy of −1.36 eV. The BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules binding to the double vacancy are shown in Figure S2. Their binding energies are −0.54, −0.7, −0.27, and −0.4 eV, respectively. The DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules binding to the armchair step edge are shown in Figure S3. Their binding energies are −0.68, −0.26, and −0.26 eV, respectively.

    3.3. Passivated Graphene Defects

    The defects were passivated with O, H, and OH to assess the effects of passivation on the structures and binding energies. The BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules that exhibited the strongest binding with the unpassivated defects were investigated in this study.
    The first step of the passivation study involved finding the structure and binding energies of O, H, and OH to the single vacancy. The most favorable binding site is identical for all three passivation groups, and the structures are shown in Figure 6. The carbon atoms surrounding the vacancy form an isosceles triangle, and O, H, and OH bind to carbon opposite of the unique side of the triangle. The results for binding energies of H, O, and OH to the single vacancy and zigzag step edge are shown in Table 2 and show a similar binding energy of −4 to −4.5 eV for all three groups to the single vacancy. The −4.46 eV binding energy for H to the single vacancy is in reasonable agreement with −4.26 eV reported by Densis and Iribarne (16) and −4.36 eV by Lehtinen et al. (34)

    4. Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A systematic study of the interaction of the ROMs BTZ (anolyte), DDB (catholyte), and popular solvent molecules such as acetonitrile (MeCN) and ethylene carbonate (EC) with a pristine and defective graphene surface as a model for an electrode surface was performed in this investigation. The defects investigated are single vacancy, double vacancy, zigzag step edges, and armchair step edges. While there could be other defects to be considered such as dislocations and/or Stone–Wales, they were beyond the scope of this work. This study was performed to understand potential molecular level interactions of these species with a carbon electrode.
    1.

    For interaction of all molecules with a pristine graphene surface, it was found that the interaction is relatively weak (0.2 to 0.8 eV) and dominated by van der Waals effects. This leads to the conclusion that these molecules are stable upon interacting with pristine nondefective graphene.

    2.

    The BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules interact strongly with the single vacancy and zigzag step edges of graphene. In the case of BTZ, DDB, and MeCN, this could lead to decomposition of the molecules. The interactions are in the range of 1.5–5.5 eV for all of these molecules with these two types of defects.

    3.

    The BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules are found to interact less strongly to the double vacancy and armchair step edge than to the single vacancy and zigzag step edge.

    4.

    Passivation of the single vacancy and zigzag step edge with O, H, or OH is found to decrease the strong adsorption energies found for unpassivated defect sites. This leads to the conclusion that passivation of the defects could help prevent unwanted chemical interactions between the neutral molecules and the surface.

    Thus, the BTZ, DDB, MeCN, and EC molecules have the potential for strong binding to a graphene surface and possible decomposition, with single vacancies or zigzag step edges, and that this binding is suppressed by H, O, or OH passivation of the defects. The findings of this work suggest that a design principle in engineering a RFB should include a directed strategy toward controlled passivation of all reactive defects on the electrode surfaces. Another reason for controlled passivation of defects is that the passivation type may affect charge transfer; studies of defect effects on charge transfer at the electrified interface are the current area of future research.
