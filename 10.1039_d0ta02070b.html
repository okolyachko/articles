An organic-based aqueous hybrid flow battery with high power and long cycle life: a tetrapyridophenazine/ferrocyanide system†

Abstract

Aqueous hybrid flow batteries (AHFBs) have emerged as promising systems for large-scale electrical energy storage. We report a stable conjugated organic compound, tetrapyridophenazine (TPPHZ), as an active anode material for AHFBs, which exhibits highly negative redox potential, fast electrochemical reaction kinetics and extreme insolubility in aqueous alkaline electrolytes. When operated in a practical hybrid flow cell against a positive electrolyte comprising ferrocyanide salt, the cell displayed an open-circuit voltage of 1.37 V, specific power of 28.4 W g−1 at 100% state of charge (SOC) and energy efficiency of 87.2% at a current density of 10 A g−1 (10.2 mA cm−2). The capacity retention rate is among the highest of AHFBs reported to date, at 99.9980% per cycle over 1200 consecutive cycles.

Introduction

The electrical power produced from clean renewable sources (e.g., solar, wind, geothermal, etc.) is growing rapidly worldwide.1,2 Therefore, highly efficient and economical energy storage solutions have to be developed. Among various electrical energy storage technologies, aqueous rechargeable batteries that include aqueous metal-ion batteries and aqueous redox flow batteries (ARFBs) have attracted great attention, owing to the fast ion transfer, low cost, environmental friendliness and incombustibility of aqueous electrolytes.3,4 As compared to aqueous metal-ion batteries, ARFBs not only allow flexible operation, but also their power and energy capacity can be independently designed, which make them more suitable for large-scale energy storage requirements.5–7 The electro-active species for an ARFB is dissolved in electrolyte solutions,8 different from conventional batteries, leading to low energy density because of solubility limitations.9 To make energy density compete with that of traditional metal-ion batteries, aqueous hybrid-flow batteries (AHFBs) combining a solid anode with a flow cathode have been developed and attracted much attention, e.g., zinc//bromine, zinc//TEMPO and all copper AHFBs.10–14

The active materials in ARFBs and AHFBs can be either inorganic or organic compounds. The organic molecules involved in reversible redox reactions such as conjugated carbonyl compounds, aromatic heterocycles and radicals15–20 have emerged as promising active materials due to their high specific capacity (energy density), abundant sources, potential low-cost and environmental sustainability. In contrast to their inorganic counterparts in which the electrochemical reaction relies on the valence state change of metal or non-metallic centers, the redox reaction of organic compounds is generally governed by the charge state of electroactive groups (or units). The reversible redox reaction takes place at the conjugated organic groups/moieties and lone-pair electron donor atoms, such as N, O, and S, because the conjugated structure facilitates electron transfer and charge delocalization whereas a lone pair of electrons generally possesses high reactivity. Although zinc is widely used as the anode of AHFBs due to negative redox potential, large volumetric capacity and convenience of replacement, it also suffers from some drawbacks, including morphology shape change, dendrite growth, passivation and parasitic side reaction, which result in poor cycle life.21 In contrast, the structural designability of redox-active organic molecules makes it possible to circumvent these disadvantages of the zinc electrode.

The exploitation of redox-active organic compounds for electrochemical energy storage has gained increasing attention in recent years.8,16,17,19,20,22–26 For instance, quinone and phenazine derivatives were reported as active electrodes of Li(Na)-ion batteries23 and supercapacitors,24 and electrolytes of ARFBs.25 Soluble nitroxyl radicals or polymers containing these radical units were employed as high potential catholytes of aqueous and non-aqueous redox flow batteries.8,19,22 Polymers with anthraquinone pendants were utilized as solid-state anodes for aqueous rechargeable batteries.26 However, redox-active organic molecules also suffered from some drawbacks.27 For example, the insufficient solubility and high viscosity of organic compounds/polymers greatly limit the energy density and durability of redox flow batteries. The sharp performance decay for various metal-ion batteries and supercapacitors is observed due to dissolution of organic molecules into the electrolyte and/or unexpected side reactions. Besides, low electrical conductivity is the common cause of poor performance of organic-based electrodes or electrolytes. Obviously, the rational design and optimization of molecular structures are required, considering that the advantages and drawbacks of redox-active organic compounds are closely dependent on their molecular structures.

Herein, we report a redox-active organic molecule, tetrapyridophenazine (TPPHZ), with high activity and stability for AHFB application. As organic molecules with delocalized π-conjugated fused rings, TPPHZ and its analogues were often employed as bridging ligands for metal complexes because they have multiple coordination sites for the binding of metal ions.28 Owing to its large planar π-conjugated molecular system, the TPPHZ features a quasi-reversible two-electron transfer reaction at extremely negative potential (−0.94 V versus the standard hydrogen electrode, SHE) and extreme insolubility in aqueous KOH electrolyte. When paired with a ferricyanide/ferrocyanide couple, the TPPHZ//ferrocyanide system displays an open-circuit voltage (OCV) of about 1.37 V. Promoted by the fast redox kinetics, simple chemical structure and extreme insolubility of TPPHZ, the TPPHZ//K4[Fe(CN)6] AHFB can be stably operated at a current density of 10 A g−1 for long-term cycling with a discharge capacity retention of 97.6% after 1200 cycles (99.9980% per cycle). The TPPHZ//K4[Fe(CN)6] system presented here is among the most stable AHFBs to date, also comparable with existing highly stable organic-based ARFBs (Table 1).

Results and discussion

Cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of the TPPHZ electrode were first measured in 1 M KOH aqueous solution (Fig. 1a), in which two pairs of well-defined redox waves (peaks I/IV and II/III), with equilibrium potentials (E0) of −0.62 and −0.94 V (versus SHE), respectively, were observed clearly. An extremely small peak separation (ΔEp ∼ 6 mV at 25 mV s−1) is observed for the I/IV redox couple (ET1, Fig. 1a), combined with the fact that the redox peak currents increase linearly with the increase of the scan rates (ν) (Fig. 1b), suggesting that ET1 is a reversible surface-controlled reaction. According to Laviron theory,37 the electron transfer number (n) is related to the peak current (ip) (eqn (S1), ESI†).38 Therefore, the n value for ET1 is calculated to be about 0.93 from the slope of the ip–ν dependence (Fig. S1†), indicating that the ET1 is one-electron reaction. The electron transfer coefficient (α) and heterogeneous electron transfer rate constant (kET) of ET1 were determined using Laviron's method39 from eqn (S2) (ESI†). The plot of ΔEpversus log(ν) produces a straight line along with an intercept (Fig. S2†). The α value is calculated to be about 0.57 from the slope of the ΔEp–log(ν) dependence. The kET value is calculated to be ca. 17.3 s−1 from the intercept value.

The peak current of the II/III redox couple (ET2) is linearly increased with the increase of the square root of the scan rates (Fig. 1c), revealing a typical diffusion-controlled process. The ΔEp for ET2 is observed to be about 141 mV at 25 mV s−1, suggesting that ET2 is a quasi-reversible electron transfer reaction. The Cottrell equation with semi-infinite diffusion (eqn (S3), ESI†) was used to determine the diffusion coefficients (D) for ET2 (ref. 40) and shows good linear dependence of j versus t−1/2 (Fig. S3†). The average D value is calculated to be 2.32 × 10−10 cm2 s−1. Next, the Nicholson method was utilized to determine the kET of ET2.41 The kET value is estimated to be 3.81 × 10−4 cm s−1 from the slope of the Ψ ∼ ν−1/2 dependence (Fig. S4†), indicating again that the ET2 is quasi-reversible. The CV curves of the TPPHZ electrode at pH buffers are shown in Fig. 2a, from which a Pourbaix diagram (E0vs. pH, Fig. 2b) is plotted. The slope of the Pourbaix diagram for ET1 is 0 mV pH−1, corresponding to a 0-proton process. The ET2 reaction appears at a pH above 12 and the E02 shows a linear decrease with increasing pH. The slope of the Pourbaix diagram for ET2 is determined to be −59.2 mV pH−1, demonstrating a 2-proton/2-electron process.

Based on the above-mentioned observations, a possible electrochemical reaction mechanism is proposed (Fig. 3), which suggests that TPPHZ has two parallel electron-transfer processes (ET1 and ET2). According to the previously postulated mechanism of redox-active compounds containing the pyrazine ring (e.g. quinoxaline,42 phenazine,25 brimonidine43etc.), it is assumed that a similar redox reaction happens at the pyrazine moiety of TPPHZ, forming a dihydro-derivative.25 That is to say, the ET2 mechanism is plausible. To further verify the possibility of the ET1 mechanism, TPPHZ was dispersed onto the surface of porous graphene nanosheets (BET area of ca. 750 m2 g−1) to form a graphene-supported TPPHZ composite (TPPHZ/G). Significantly different from TPPHZ, in the CV curve of the TPPHZ/G composite (Fig. S5†), only a pair of redox waves appears with an equilibrium potential of about −0.956 V (versus SHE), generally consistent with the redox waves of ET2. No redox peaks corresponding to the ET1 reaction are observed for the TPPHZ/G composite. The discrepancy is probably attributed to TPPHZ on graphene which can sufficiently come into contact with water molecules and hydroxide ions because of its much smaller particle size (detected by PXRD patterns shown in Fig. S6†) compared to the unsupported one. Therefore, we infer that the ET1 reaction is an electron transfer reaction involving the participation of TPPHZ molecules within the inner layer of the crystal and the formation of free radicals or anions, similar to those previously reported quinones in neutral aqueous electrolytes.44

The effects of alkaline electrolytes on the electrochemistry of the TPPHZ electrode were further investigated. Fig. S7† shows the CVs of the TPPHZ electrode in a 0.1 M KOH (pH = 13) solution containing different concentrations of K+, from which a relationship between E0 and log[K+] is plotted. The plot of the redox potential of ET1 (E01) versus log[K+] is fitted well by a linear relationship E01 = −625 to 33.5log[K+], indicating that potassium ions are involved in the ET1 reaction. The peak current of ET1 is almost constant with the increasing potassium ion concentration, demonstrating that the concentration of redox-active sites involved in ET1 is not affected by the concentration of K+. In contrast, the redox potential of ET2 (E02) is observed to be −889 mV, hardly changed with the increase of the concentration of K+. Although the E02 value is independent of [K+], the reaction current increases obviously with the increasing K+ concentration, revealing an increased utilization of redox-active sites.

The CVs of TPPHZ in the KOH electrolytes of different concentrations are shown in Fig. S8,† from which the relationship between E0 and log[K+] (or log[OH−]) is plotted. The plot of E01versus log[K+] is fitted well by a linear relationship E01 = −633 to 33.0log[K+]. The slope value of 33.0 mV dec−1 is very close to that obtained from Fig. S7† (33.5 mV dec−1), revealing again that potassium ions instead of hydroxides are involved in the ET1 reaction. In addition, the plot of E02versus log[OH−] is fitted well by a linear relationship E02 = −944 to 63.3log[OH−]. The slope of −63.3 mV pH−1 corresponds to a 2-proton/2-electron reaction. The ΔEp value for ET2 gradually decreases with the increasing OH− concentration, combined with the fact that the current density increases sharply, demonstrating that a high concentration of OH− can improve the reversibility of ET2 and the utilization of redox-active sites.

The CVs of the TPPHZ electrode in different alkaline electrolytes are shown in Fig. S9a,† from which electrochemical parameters are extracted (Table S3†). The equilibrium potentials for ET1 in 1 M KOH, NaOH and LiOH are −633, −588 and −606 mV, while those for ET2 are −944, −932 and −913 mV, respectively. The TPPHZ electrode in KOH solution displays more negative redox potentials for both ET1 and ET2 in comparison to those in NaOH or LiOH solutions. The different potentials of ET1 in different alkaline electrolytes are due to the fact that the hydrated cations (e.g. K+, Na+ and Li+) which balance TPPHZ−˙ radical anions possess different hydration numbers and hydration radii, which lead to different ion-pair interactions between TPPHZ−˙ radical anions and hydrated cations. However, the subtle difference in ET2 redox potentials is likely to originate from different electrostatic interactions between different hydrated cations and hydroxide ions. Although the E02 value in 1 M NaOH solution is slightly higher than that in 1 M KOH, the TPPHZ electrode in 1 M NaOH solution shows a smaller ΔE value and a jpa/jpc ratio of nearly 1, suggesting better redox reversibility.

The electrochemical stability of TPPHZ in different alkaline electrolytes is also evaluated (Fig. S9b, c† and Fig. 1d). The peak currents are slightly decreased after repeating 100–200 CV cycles, demonstrating excellent electrochemical stability of TPPHZ in these strongly alkaline electrolytes.

To serve as the solid electrode materials in an aqueous battery, besides high electrochemical stability, redox-active organic molecules must also be chemically stable and highly insoluble in aqueous electrolytes. The TPPHZ treated in 1 M KOH for three days at room temperature shows almost the same 1H NMR signals as untreated TPPHZ (Fig. S10†), indicating that TPPHZ is not degraded chemically. The solubility of TPPHZ in 1 M KOH has been determined to be only 0.047 mg L−1 at room temperature by using the quantitative analysis of UV-vis absorption spectra (Fig. S11†), very close to its theoretical value (0.054 mg L−1 at 25 °C, calculated using ACD Labs software version 11.02). In addition, a theoretical calculation reveals that the reduced state of TPPHZ also possesses an extremely low solubility (only 0.007 mg L−1 at 25 °C, calculated using ACD Labs software version 11.02) in neutral and alkaline aqueous solutions.

Fig. 4a shows CVs for TPPHZ and K4Fe(CN)6 in 1 M KOH solution. When used as the anode and catholyte in an AHFB, respectively, it can be seen that the expected cell voltages are ca. 1.11 V (ΔE1) and 1.42 V (ΔE2). A TPPHZ//K4Fe(CN)6 cell (Fig. 4b) was assembled using 2.7 mg TPPHZ (0.54 mg cm−2) as the anode-active material, 1 M KOH + 0.2 M KCl solution as the anodic supporting electrolyte and 50 mM K4[Fe(CN)6] (1.0 mmol) in 1 M KOH solution as the catholyte. Excess quantities of K4Fe(CN)6 were used here to ensure that the anode was the capacity-limiting side for the purpose of evaluating the stability of TPPHZ during cycling. Potassium chloride (KCl) was added in the anolyte to ensure equal K+ concentrations on both sides of the ion exchange membrane. The open circuit voltage (OCV) versus state of charge (SOC) shows a monotonous increase (Fig. 4c). The OCV at 50% SOC can be observed to be ca. 1.37 V, located between two predicted equilibrium cell voltages (1.11 and 1.42 V), since TPPHZ has two parallel redox reactions of ET1 and ET2. Polarization studies for the TPPHZ//K4[Fe(CN)6] cell conducted at room temperature (Fig. 4d) show peak specific powers of 28.4 W g−1 at 100% SOC and 20.3 W g−1 at 50% SOC, respectively, significantly higher than those of previously reported ARFBs15–19,29–32 and polymer AHFBs.36

The discharge capacity of the TPPHZ//K4[Fe(CN)6] cell at a current density of 1 A g−1 was observed to be 79.6 mA h g−1, which is ca. 61.8% of its theoretical capacity (128.8 mA h g−1) (Fig. 5a and Table S4†). The charge capacity at 1 A g−1 is 110.2 mA h g−1, ca. 85.6% of the theoretical capacity. Varying the current density does not have a significant effect on the discharge capacity (inset of Fig. 5a). The discharge capacity remains basically stable for current densities between 1 and 5 A g−1. The coulombic efficiency (CE) and energy efficiency (EE) were about 72.1% and 68.3% at 1 A g−1, respectively. At 5 A g−1, they increased to 93.8% and 84.7%, respectively. The CE and EE increase with an increase in current density, which may be attributed to the shortening of discharge time at higher current densities. The TPPHZ//K4[Fe(CN)6] cell was cycled at a constant current density of 5 A g−1 (Fig. 5b), demonstrating excellent cycling stability and reproducibility. After 1200 consecutive charge/discharge cycles, 97.0% of the initial discharge capacity (ca. 66.3 mA h g−1) was retained, indicating a highly stable redox behaviour of TPPHZ (Fig. 5c). This hybrid flow cell displays a capacity retention rate of 99.9975% per cycle. The charge capacity remains at 71.1 mA h g−1, corresponding to a retention ratio of 97.7%. During the whole 1200 cycles, the average CE exceeded 91.8%, with a stable round-trip EE of 81.4%. Considering that the standard potential (−0.94 V vs. SHE) of ET2 is lower than the potential (−0.83 V vs. SHE) of the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) at 1 M KOH (pH 14); relatively low CE might be due to minor hydrogen evolution occurring during charging–discharge cycling.

To verify the stability of TPPHZ during the cycling process, 1H NMR post-analysis was conducted on the TPPHZ anode after 1200 charge–discharge cycles (Fig. S10†). According to the 1H NMR spectrum of TPPHZ in CDCl3, three hydrogen signals are observed, which are at 7.82, 9.35 and 9.55 ppm, respectively. After 1200 cell cycles, no new peak is observed, indicating that TPPHZ is chemically stable. The change of the area-specific resistance (ASR) is widely used to evaluate the performance and cycling stability of flow batteries. The ASR mainly comes from the high frequency ASR (Rhf) measured by EIS analysis.16 Therefore, we obtained the Rhf values by directly determining the impedance value at the intersection of the Nyquist plots with the real axis.29 As shown in Fig. 5d, the Rhf values before and after 1200 charge–discharge cycles are observed to be 1.215 and 1.210 Ω cm2, respectively. The high frequency area specific resistance hardly changed before and after the cycling test, indicating an outstanding cycling stability of the TPPHZ//K4[Fe(CN)6] cell.

During the cycling stability test, the applied current density is 5 A g−1, equivalent to 2.7 mA cm−2 since the TPPHZ loading is 0.54 mg cm−2, which is too small for the practical application and also obviously lower than those of previously reported Zn-based AHFBs (3–30 mA cm−2, Table 1).11–13,33–35 Considering the excellent rate performance of the TPPHZ//K4Fe(CN)6 system, we anticipate that the increase of the TPPHZ loading will be conducive to enhancing current density. Therefore, we assembled another TPPHZ//K4Fe(CN)6 cell by using 5.1 mg TPPHZ as the anode material, in which the loading of TPPHZ is ca. 1.02 mg cm−2, nearly 2 times higher than that of the previously fabricated cell (0.54 mg cm−2). Fig. 6a and b show galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles of this cell at different current densities and the rate performance. The discharge capacity at 1 A g−1 was observed to be 92.4 mA h g−1, which is ca. 71.7% of its theoretical capacity. The discharge capacity at 20 A g−1 retains 57.0 mA h g−1, ca. 44.3% of its theoretical capacity, demonstrating its outstanding rate performance. Then, a constant current density of 10 A g−1 (equivalent to 10.2 mA cm−2) was used to evaluate the cycling stability (Fig. 6c and d). After 1200 cycles, 97.6% of the initial discharge capacity was retained. The capacity retention rate is ca. 99.9980% per cycle, higher than that of the cell with a TPPHZ loading of 0.54 mg cm−2 at 5 A g−1 and TPPHZ/graphene composites recently reported by our group (∼99.9938% per cycle at 5 A g−1).45 In addition, during 1200 cycles, the CE and EE are 98.6% and 87.2%, respectively. The significant increase in efficiency and capacity retention for the cell with a TPPHZ loading of 1.02 mg cm−2 may be mainly attributed to the optimized loading of active materials and the increase in the current density.

Although this current density (10.2 mA cm−2) applied for the cycling stability test is about 2/3 of that of zinc-based AHFBs (or ∼1/10 of that of traditional ARFBs), as a new HFB prototype, it still has great room for improvement. Optimization of engineering and operating parameters such as the loading of TPPHZ, carbon additives, electrode design, membrane separator, temperature etc. should lead to significant performance improvements in the future, as it has for traditional and hybrid flow batteries.

All the long-term cycling results suggest that the TPPHZ//K4[Fe(CN)6] cell is exceptionally stable. This stability might be due to several possible causes, namely chemical stability, resonance structures, and remarkable insolubility in aqueous electrolytes. Firstly, TPPHZ molecules are chemically inert in alkaline aqueous solutions due to lack of chemically active groups/sites. Secondly, a variety of resonance structures of oxidized and reduced states of TPPHZ (Fig. S12†) lead to electron delocalization and decline in potential energy, which make them less prone to decomposition.18 Finally, extremely low solubility of TPPHZ in aqueous solution avoids any losses of redox-active species during the long-term cycling.

Conclusions

In summary, a stable conjugated organic molecule, TPPHZ, has been found to be a high performance redox-active negative material for AHFBs and has an extremely negative redox potential, fast electrode kinetics and complete insolubility in aqueous alkaline electrolytes. The AHFB based on TPPHZ and ferrocyanide salt not only exhibits an OCV of 1.37 V, high specific power and high energy efficiency, but also displays exceptionally long lifetime. It features a capacity retention rate of 99.9980% per cycle over 1200 consecutive cycles.

Experimental

Materials

Tetrapyridophenazine (TPPHZ, 98 wt%, CAS no. 168646-52-4) was purchased from Jinan Henghua Technology. All the inorganic reagents and organic solvents that were obtained from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent were used as received without further purification. Deionized water (Milli-Q, 18.2 MΩ) was used for all aqueous solutions.

Electrochemical measurements

The TPPHZ (2 mg) and carbon black (2 mg) were premixed and dispersed into a mixture containing 5 wt% Nafion solution (75 μL, Aldrich), deionized water (0.5 mL) and isopropanol (0.5 mL) to form a homogeneous ink. The ink (3 μL) was transferred onto a pre-polished and cleaned glassy carbon electrode (diameter, 3 mm) and left to dry at 60 °C for 8 h, and it served as the working electrode. The electrochemical measurements were performed with a three-electrode cell by using a CHI 660B potentiostat (CH Instruments) at room temperature. The platinum foil was used as the counter electrode. The saturated calomel electrode (SCE, 0.242 V versus SHE) was employed as the reference electrode in neutral and weak alkaline solutions whereas a mercury/mercuric oxide reference electrode (MMO, 0.098 V versus SHE) was used in strong alkaline solutions. All potentials are reported versus the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE). The three-electrode cell was filled with ca. 40 mL of electrolyte solution. Cyclic voltammetry techniques were used to investigate the electrochemical behaviours of TPPHZ. Prior to experiments, the electrolyte solution was bubbled with nitrogen for at least 10 min. Then, the nitrogen gas bubbling stream was switched to a nitrogen gas blanket above the surface of the solution.

The Pourbaix diagram (E0versus pH) of the TPPHZ electrode was determined in pH buffers using the following reagents: NaH2PO4/Na2HPO4 (0.1 M, pH 8.5–9.0), Na2HPO4/Na3PO4 (0.1 M, pH 9.5–11.5), and NaOH (pH > 12). The pH was adjusted with NaOH solutions (0.001–1 M) and measured with a precision pH meter (PHS-3C, Shanghai Instrument).

Flow-cell testing

Flow-cell hardware equipped with two pieces of graphite serpentine flow field plates for both sides was purchased from Fuel Cell Technologies. The anode was made by mixing TPPHZ, carbon black and Nafion ionomers at a weight ratio of 6:3:1 and then coating on Toray carbon paper (TGP-H-060). Two TPPHZ anodes were prepared and the TPPHZ loadings are ca. 0.54 and 1.02 mg cm−2, respectively. Toray carbon paper was directly utilized as the cathode. The geometric area of both the electrodes is 5 cm2. A pretreated Nafion membrane (N112, DuPont) was sandwiched between the anode and cathode.31 The space between two graphite flow plates was sealed with silicone rubber gaskets to avoid leakage of liquid. The catholytes were pumped through PFA tubing into and out from the flow cell using peristaltic pumps (BT-600EA, Chongqing Jieheng Peristaltic Pumps Co., Ltd.) at a rate of 50 mL min−1. The flow-cell system was purged with high purity nitrogen for at least 20 min prior to any experiments. A steady nitrogen flow was maintained during flow-cell cycling. Galvanostatic cell cycling was carried out on an Interface 1000 potentiostat (Gamry) at 22.5 mA (5 A g−1), with voltage cut-offs of 0.8 and 1.6 V. Polarization curves were acquired by charging the cell to certain states of charge (SOC) and then polarized by linear sweep voltammetry at a scan rate of 10 mA s−1 to avoid the effect of concentration polarization.

Solubility measurement

The solubility of TPPHZ was measured by equilibrating the excess sample in 1 M KOH aqueous solution at 25 ± 1 °C for 48 h. After filtering the mixture through a 0.5 μm syringe-tip filter (Millipore, type FH), a saturated solution of TPPHZ in KOH (200 mL) was obtained. Then, TPPHZ was extracted by using chloroform (50 mL). The chloroform solution of TPPHZ was concentrated by a known amount (5 mL) and the concentration was evaluated with a UV-vis (Shimadzu UV mini 1240) spectrophotometer at 276 nm. The concentration was calculated according to a pre-calibrated absorbance–concentration curve of known concentrations of TPPHZ in chloroform.
