Abstract

Highly water-soluble azobenzene-based molecules with an asymmetric configuration and multifunctional hydrophilicity are presented as organic redox-active molecules for aqueous redox flow batteries. A battery comprising the azobenzene anolyte achieved stable cycling with high capacity and energy efficiency as a consequence of the reversible redox activity of the azo group. Key: 4-amino-1,1′-azobenzene-3,4′-disulfonic acid monosodium salt (AADA).

Abstract

Aqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs) are promising alternatives for large-scale energy storage. However, new organic redox-active molecules with good chemical stability and high solubility are still desired for high-performance aqueous RFBs due to their low crossover capability and high abundance. We report azobenzene-based molecules with hydrophilic groups as new active materials for aqueous RFBs by utilizing the reversible redox activity of azo groups. By rationally tailoring the molecular structure of azobenzene, the solubility is favorably improved from near zero to 2 M due to the highly charged asymmetric structure formed in alkaline environment. DFT simulations suggest that the concentrated solution stability can be enhanced by adding hydrotropic agent to form intermolecular hydrogen bonds. The demonstrated RFB exhibits long cycling stability with a capacity retention of 99.95 % per cycle over 500 cycles. It presents a viable chemical design route towards advanced aqueous RFBs.

Introduction

The demand of renewable energy sources such as solar or wind power has increased continuously because of the world energy crisis and carbon footprint. And it is being realized that the paradigm shift in energy utilization from conventional fossil fuels to sustainable alternatives becomes necessary.1 However, designing promising energy storage technologies with high energy, high power and low cost features to efficiently store electricity from these renewable sources with inherent intermittency has become a contemporary challenge.2 Compared to the widely implemented lithium ion batteries, redox flow battery (RFB) is considered as one of the most promising technology for grid-scale energy storage due to its configuration flexibility, decoupled energy and power, long cycle life and safety.3

Currently, as the representative of aqueous RFBs using redox species dissolved in aqueous solutions, vanadium-based RFBs have been successfully commercialized.4 In this context, aqueous RFBs have shown clear advantages compared with nonaqueous candidates, such as low cost, noninflammability, high power, and many available ion-selective membranes.5 However, in conventional aqueous RFBs, the choice of redox species is limited to metal-based active materials, such as vanadium-based ions. The relatively high cost of transition metals and limited energy density have impeded their extensive application.4, 6 Tremendous research efforts have been made on exploring next-generation flow battery technologies as potential alternatives, such as zinc-based flow batteries or aqueous organic RFBs.7 Zinc-based flow batteries have high working voltage, but they are commonly suffering a short cycle life because of zinc dendrite issues, especially under high current density.8 Recently, aqueous organic RFBs utilizing organic molecules as electroactive materials have attracted great attention, as they offer considerable promise as redox species such as chemical diversity, earth-abundant elements and low crossover effect.1 To date, a variety of molecules have been reported as potential redox species for aqueous RFB applications, such as quinone,9 viologen,10 phenazine,11 phenothiazine,5a and TEMPOs.12 Among these organic redox-active molecules, several candidates have achieved great advance in the enhanced cycling stability, improved solubility and tailored redox potential. However, in the view of optimization of electrolyte properties, it still has significant potential to further improve the aqueous organic systems, correspondingly, in all aspects of performance parameters. Though it is proved that the solubility, stability, and redox potential of organic molecules can be tuned via molecular modification, new organic compounds with promising features are still highly desired in order to accelerate the development of next-generation RFBs.1, 13 Moreover, as for now, highly tailored improvements are limited to a few organic compounds, such as quinone or phenazine systems. Many organic redox-active molecules are still struggling with the problems of serious capacity fading or limited solubility even with the help of molecular engineering.2b, 13, 14

Herein, we report a new type of organic redox-active molecules for high-capacity aqueous RFBs based on the azo group (-N=N-) as redox-active center, which could potentially provide a two-electron transfer reaction (Scheme 1). Given the available derivatives, cost effectiveness and demonstrated stability in alkali-ion batteries,15 azobenzene-based molecules are considered as promising candidate anolytes for aqueous RFBs. Since azobenzene as the parent molecule is hardly soluble in the aqueous medium because of high hydrophobicity of the symmetric molecular structure. We thus focus on studying the influence of hydrophilic functional groups and hydrotropic agents on the dissolution behavior of azobenzene-based molecules in order to realize a stable electrolyte under high concentrations. Through introducing the amino and sulfonic groups as functional moieties, 4-amino-1,1′-azobenzene-3,4′-disulfonic acid monosodium salt (AADA) with the asymmetrical configuration and multifunctional hydrophilicity is able to achieve a high solubility (>1 M) in the alkaline environment with a deprotonation effect. Moreover, urea as the hydrotropic agent is found to play an important role in the stabilization of concentrated electrolytes through the possible anisotropic hydrogen bond interactions. When paired with the ferro/ferrocyanide catholyte, the electrochemical properties of AADA-based anolyte as the counter electrolyte are measured in such an aqueous RFB (Scheme 1), showing a long cycle stability with a capacity retention of 99.95 % per cycle over 500 cycles. The solvation behavior and redox chemistry of azobenzene-based molecules are investigated by spectroscopy analysis and density functional theory (DFT) calculations. This work provides an effective approach to design stable and high-capacity organic molecules for next-generation aqueous RFBs.

Results and Discussion

To design new-type organic molecules for RFBs, highly reversible redox-active cores should be first identified with a following structural functionalization of the core to tailor their primary properties, such as relatively high solubility, good chemical stability and proper redox potential. Azobenzene-based molecules are commonly used as low-cost industrial dyes and photo-responsive materials, offering a huge number of available derivatives with diverse functional groups.16 Thus, it has a lot of chance to optimize the electrolyte properties for potential RFB applications. For cost estimation, taking AADA material as an example, it has a lower cost in the market than many reported organic active materials for RFBs (Supporting Information, Figure S1). Figure 1 a shows the molecular structures of various azobenzene-based derivatives for the study of dissolution behavior in aqueous medium, and the corresponding solvation energies are determined by DFT simulations (Figure 1 b). It can be seen that the solvation energies of alizarin yellow R sodium salt (AYRS) and 4-aminoazobenzene-4′-sulfonic acid sodium salt (ASAS) are both much higher than azobenzene due to the para- or meta-substituted hydrophilic groups, while ASAS shows a slightly better solubility due to the strong polarity of amino and sulfonic groups.1, 11 When substituting the additional sulfonic group in the meta-position of ASAS structure adjacent to the amino group, AADA with an asymmetrical configuration shows a higher solvation energy, because the asymmetric charge distribution can provide strong preferential solvation sites.11, 17 In Figure 1 c, the solubility measurement shows consistent results. The solubility of AADA in water is 0.12 M, which is obviously higher than that of AYRS (<0.05 M) or ASAS (<0.07 M). Further, according to the simulated solvation results (Figures 1 a,b), we also find that strongly alkaline electrolytes (NaOH) can be utilized to deprotonate the sulfonic groups in the AADA structure to enhance its solubility by forming highly charged structures (AADA1 and AADA2; Figure S2). With the help of deprotonation effect, the solubility of AADA was significantly enhanced to 2 M in 2 M NaOH solution, and the solution is tested to be highly alkaline (Figures S3 and S4), suggesting the formation of the highly charged structure named as AADA2, which has a high solvation energy. In contrast, for AYRS or ASAS, the solubility behavior becomes even worse in the alkaline environment. Thus, considering the huge difference of solubility observed, AADA is selected as the example molecule for the next electrochemical characterization.

Besides, when the concentration of NaOH was further increased over 2 M, the maximum solubility of AADA began to decrease (Figure 1 c). The possible reason is that such high concentration of hydroxide ions surrounded by lots of water molecules would suppress the hydrolysis of amino groups in AADA structure, thus decreasing the preferential solvation sites. This phenomenon is identical to the simulated solvation energies, in which the AADA1 structure has a lower energy than AADA2. On the other hand, the long-time stability of highly concentrated electrolytes at ambient environment is also important for the electrochemical measurement. Although the concentrated solution of AADA can be obtained in the alkaline medium, some solids in the bottom are still observed when standing for a long time, indicating a possible precipitation. To identify the precipitates, the corresponding Raman and UV-vis spectra when redissolved into aqueous solutions were tested, which are identical to that of AADA in NaOH solutions, indicating it should contain AADA (Figure S5). Moreover, in order to obtain a stable AADA electrolyte under high concentrations, urea is used as the hydrotropic agent to induce the location-specific intensified solvation.7d As shown in Figure S6, some precipitation occurs in the 0.5 M AADA-based solution without urea when standing for 30 days. As a comparison, the 0.5 M AADA-based solution with adding certain amount of urea maintains the original sate, giving a long-term electrolyte stability (>30 days). The underlying mechanism is revealed by Raman spectra and DFT calculation. In the Raman analysis, the N-C-N symmetric stretching vibration of urea at 1000 cm−1 disappears, implying the interaction between urea and AADA. Both the -N=N- and C-N stretching vibrations between benzene ring and -N=N- show a significant blue shift when NaOH is added (Figure 1 d; Figure S7), implying the deprotonation of sulfonic acid groups by reacting with NaOH. Figure 1 e shows the electrostatic potential (ESP) maps of AADA2 and the protonated urea, suggesting that AADA2 has an asymmetric charge distribution and high polarizability, which can enhance intermolecular interaction and solubility. The -SO3− group of AADA2 shows high electron density and strong nucleophilicity, while the protonated urea exhibits strong electron-withdrawing ability. Thus, the strong intermolecular interaction should be formed between sulfonic groups (AADA2) and amino groups (protonated urea), which is confirmed by the DFT calculation (Figures 1 e,f). The intermolecular distance of the protonated urea and AADA2 (N−H⋅⋅⋅O−S) is located in the range from 1.80 Å to 1.88 Å, which is a typical hydrogen bond interaction (Figure 1 f). As displayed in Figure S8, one of amino groups could be hydrolyzed in aqueous solution to interact with the sulfonate groups of AADA. And as another amino group can interact with water molecules, the adding of urea not only reduces the intermolecular interaction between AADA molecules to prevent the formation of molecular cluster, but also reinforces the interaction between AADA and water molecules, thus enhancing the solvation effect. As a result, the solution stability of AADA-based electrolytes can be strongly improved by adding urea. Therefore, urea is used as the hydrotropic agent in highly concentrated AADA-based electrolytes (≥0.5 M) for the following battery test in order to maintain the solution stability.

The electrochemical kinetics of AADA was investigated using the rotating disk electrode (RDE) technique. Figure 2 a shows the linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curves of the AADA-based electrolyte containing 5 mM AADA and 2 M NaOH from 200 to 3600 rpm. These curves have well-defined plateaus, indicating the limiting currents (iL) are controlled by mass transport. Figure 2 b is the fitted linear relationship between the limiting current (iL) and the square root of rotation rate (ω1/2), from which the diffusion coefficient of AADA is calculated to be 3.21×10−6 cm2 s−1. Koutecky-Levich plots at different overpotentials are extrapolated to infinite rotation rate to obtain the kinetic current density, ik (Figure 2 c), which is then plotted as a function of the overpotential (Figure 2 d). The exchange current (i0) is determined to be 3.31×10−5 A according to the Y-axis intercepts, and then the kinetic rate constant of AADA is calculated to be 4.85×10−4 cm s−1. We also conducted the RDE test of AADA in 2 M/4 M urea and 2 M NaOH solutions to evaluate the influence of urea on the electrochemical properties (Figures S9 and S10). The determined diffusion coefficient and kinetic rate constant are 2.49/3.03×10−6 cm2 s−1 and 3.66/3.47×10−4 cm s−1 for 2 M/4 M urea and 2 M NaOH solutions, respectively. The values are slightly reduced when adding urea but they are still comparable. The details can be found in the supporting material.

To evaluate the electrochemical performance of AADA in the aqueous RFB, the AADA-based anolyte is paired with the ferro/ferricyanide catholyte, and the 2 M NaOH aqueous solution is applied as the supporting electrolyte. For the applied concentration of urea, the relatively optimal number is in the range of 2–4 M, which is necessarily needed to enable a good solution stability for a long time, and meanwhile, it can also maintain the good physical and electrochemical properties of AADA electrolytes. Figure 3 a shows the rate performance of the demonstrated RFB with the anolyte containing 0.5 M AADA + 2 M urea in 2 M NaOH from 10 to 40 mA cm−2. At the current density of 10 mA cm−2, it delivers a charge and discharge capacity of 26.0 and 25.7 Ah L−1, respectively, showing a high Coulombic efficiency (CE) of 99 % and high capacity utilization of 96 %. Based on the obtained capacity value, AADA should have the two-electron storage capability in the aqueous electrolyte. When the current density is increased to 40 mA cm−2, the high capacity utilization (92 %) is also achieved with a high CE (above 98 %). Since an obvious color change is observed during the electrochemical test, the UV-vis spectra of AADA anolyte after charging and discharging is conducted to probe the possible mechanism (Figure 3 b). The major peak in the UV-vis spectra of the initial AADA anolyte is located at around 383 nm, which should be related to the azo group in the trans-structure.16b When the AADA molecule is reduced in the charging process, the UV-vis peak intensity at around 256 nm increases and the shape becomes sharper, while the peak at around 383 nm disappears due to the change of electron density stemming from the opening of π bond of the azo group (-N=N-). This result is further proved by the UV-vis spectrum of hydro-azobenzene as shown in Figure S11. During the charging process, the anolyte color is switched from red into yellow, which is very close to the color of hydro-azobenzene in water. When discharging, the anolyte color is returned to the original state gradually, and the UV-vis spectrum identical to that of the original AADA anolyte is observed in the end of discharging, implying the reversible change of molecular structure.

As long cycle life is critical towards realizing advanced RFB technologies with the potential for practical implementation, cycling stability should be a key parameter for assessing the potential of new organic molecules as active materials for RFB applications. Figure 3 c presents the cycling capacity and CE of the AADA-based aqueous RFB with 0.1 M AADA at 30 mA cm−2 in a static mode. The battery exhibits a high capacity retention of 81 % after 700 cycles with a capacity fade of 0.03 % per cycle, and the derived CE is as high as 99.7 %. At a high concentration of 0.5 M AADA, the discharge capacity after 500 cycles can still retain about 76 % at a current density of 20 mA cm−2, and the average CE is over 98.5 % (Figure 3 d). Except for the intrinsic property of AADA, the slight air leaking occurred in the joint part of battery device may also play a role in the capacity decay. Besides, a small amount of electrolyte may gradually become inaccessible to current collectors, which also contributes to the capacity loss.

For aqueous organic RFBs, organic molecules with large molecular size should have a low crossover rate with the ionic-selective membranes, and thus the battery performance is mainly dependent on the intrinsic properties of electrolytes. In Figure 4 a, the rate performance of the AADA-based RFB containing 0.1 M AADA in the flow mode is investigated under various current densities from 10 to 60 mA cm−2 with the charge/discharge cutoff voltages of 1.4/0.25 V. The efficiency data can be found in Figure S12. It delivers a high discharge capacity of approximately 5.1 Ah L−1 at 10 mA cm−2, which is close to the theoretical capacity of 5.36 Ah L−1 for 0.1 M AADA electrolytes. With the increase of current density from 10 to 60 mA cm−2, the CE is improved from 96.7 % to 99.6 %. Since the reduced AADA is very sensitive to air, both the sealing of system and charge/discharge rate may play a role in the CE value. Benefited from high solubility and solution stability of AADA in NaOH supporting electrolytes with adding urea, the 0.8 M AADA-based anolyte can achieve a reversible capacity up to 41.5 Ah L−1 at 10 mA cm−2 with the CE of 98.8 %, showing a high material utilization of 96.7 % (Figure 4 b). In this case, the flow cell also delivers a high voltage efficiency (VE) of 88.7 % and energy efficiency (EE) of 87.6 % at a current density of 10 mA cm−2. Moreover, we observe a short plateau showing at about 0.43 V, and the proton coupled electron-transfer process of the redox-active molecule in the structure of AADA2 might be the reason, as displayed in Figure S13.18 The CV curve of AADA in the Na2SO4 electrolyte exhibits a strong reduction peak, but the oxidation peak becomes very weak in the reverse sweep process (Figure S13a), indicating the -N−-N−- redox center in the reduced AADA structure may be coupled by protons from the -SO3H group of AADA (proton donor). However, the -N−-NH- structure in the reduced AADA should have the reversible electroactivity according to the reported literature.18, 19 On the other hand, if the -N−-NH- structure is further transformed to the -NH-NH- structure by proton coupling, the reversibility of AADA will be damaged obviously.19 Thus, except for enhancing the solubility by the deprotonation effect, alkaline solutions as the preferred supporting electrolyte can simultaneously avoid the proton coupling influence on the reversibility of AADA. Figure S13b shows the CV curves of 10 mM AADA in 2 M NaOH solution and 10 mM K4Fe(CN)6/K3Fe(CN)6 in 1 M NaOH solution at a scan rate of 50 mV s−1. Two pairs of redox peaks appear at the average redox potentials of −0.79 V (vs. Ag/AgCl) and −0.29 V (vs. Ag/AgCl), which are assigned to the reversible -N=N-/-N−-N−- and -N=N-/-N−-NH- redox couples, respectively. Since the molecular structure of AADA in the NaOH solution can transform into AADA1 and AADA2 configurations according to the alkalescency of amino groups, the redox reactions involved in here may have two pathways as shown in Figure S13c. Therefore, the short plateau in the discharge curve may also originate from the proton transfer from -NH3+ to -N−-N−- group. The nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectra of the AADA-based anolyte before and after this short discharge plateau support our proposed assumption (Figure S14). The identical 13C NMR spectra of the anolyte before and after the short discharge plateau prove that the molecular skeleton of AADA should remain the same. Meanwhile, the observed difference in the 1H NMR spectra (two additional peaks at 6.93 ppm and 7.12 ppm before the short plateau) indicates the possible proton transfer existed.

To demonstrate the long-term cycle stability, the AADA-based aqueous RFB with 0.1 M or 0.5 M AADA anolytes is tested at the reasonable setup. The results show that the AADA-based flow cell achieves a stable cycling performance at both low (0.1 M) and high (0.5 M) concentrations with a capacity fade of 0.05 % per cycle and 0.16 % per cycle, respectively (Figures 4 c,d). Compared with the cycling test in the static mode, the battery test in the flow mode has a slightly faster capacity fade, which should be mainly attributed to the unstable state of reduced AADA without the complete protection of an inert atmosphere. As shown in Figure S15, even if the reduced AADA electrolyte was just exposed in air for a very short time (less than 30 s) during the sample preparation for NMR tests, the color slightly turns into red on the top, indicating it is very sensitive to air. More importantly, the slight difference in the UV-vis spectra after the air exposure of the reduced AADA electrolyte is observed, hinting the stability of reduced AADA may be affected by possible side reactions such as generating azoxy groups. And it should be mentioned that our flow test was conducted without the protection of professional glove box. Moreover, the relatively stable cycling performance of the AADA-based anolyte at a higher concentration of 0.8 M is also obtained (Figure S16). However, in the practical perspective, such aqueous organic systems still have a lot of space for further improvement, such as improving the electrochemical stability of AADA, increasing the demonstrated capacity in long-term cycling, or replacing the ferro/ferricyanide catholyte (limited solubility) with high-performance organic-molecule-based catholytes.

The detailed NMR analysis is conducted to reveal the molecular structure change during the redox reaction process in order to provide a comprehensive understanding of reaction mechanism. Figure 5 a displays the 13C NMR spectra of the anolyte containing 0.5 M AADA and 4 M urea in 2 M NaOH at different charge and discharge cycles (D2O as the NMR solvent). The NMR peak located at 162.8 ppm is coming from the structure of urea, which is consistent with the 13C NMR spectrum of pure urea (Figure S17). Compared with the initial 13C NMR spectrum of the AADA-based anolyte, most of carbon atoms in the reduced AADA structure (Charge 1) experience large chemical shifts to the high field (towards lower ppm), especially the C4 and C5 that are located at the ortho position of -N=N- in the benzene ring. It implies that the electron cloud densities of the AADA molecular structure increase due to the reduction of -N=N- in the charging process, involving the π bond opening of the azo group. This result is consistent with the UV-vis analysis. Subsequently, the 13C NMR spectrum of the anolyte is recovered to the original pattern after the fully discharging. And it is worth noted that the 13C NMR spectrum of the AADA-based anolyte after 5 cycles remains the same as that after the first cycle, confirming that AADA molecule should have good electrochemical stability and reversibility during the cycling. In addition, the 1H NMR spectra displayed in Figure 5 b also reflect the similar results, further demonstrating that the azo group should be the redox-active center. The more details can be found in supporting information (Figures S18–22).

Conclusion

In summary, azobenzene-based molecules represent a new family of organic compounds for RFB applications. With the decoration of strong hydrophilic groups and asymmetrical configuration, it is proved to be a promising anolyte alternative for high-performance aqueous RFBs based on the reversible activity of the azo group with two-electron transfer reactions. As one of the easily accessible derivatives in the market, the AADA molecule in the highly charged and asymmetric structure shows a high solubility of 2 M in alkaline electrolytes though the deprotonation effect. And the long-term solution stability of AADA-based electrolytes at high concentrations can be achieved by adding urea as the hydrotropic solubilizing additive to introduce the hydrogen bond interactions. In this way, it provides a generic approach to achieve stable and high-capacity organic-molecule-based electrolytes. The electrochemical analysis and NMR spectra reveal that the AADA molecule has good electrochemical reversibility and stability in aqueous electrolytes. When paired with the ferro/ferricyanide catholyte, the flow cell based on 0.1 M AADA anolyte displays a high CE of 99 % and long cycle stability with a capacity retention of 99.95 % per cycle over 500 cycles. The flow cell based on 0.8 M AADA anolyte delivers a high anolyte capacity of 41.5 Ah L−1 and high energy efficiency of ≈88 %. This study shows an important approach in designing highly soluble organic redox-active molecules for developing advanced aqueous RFBs.
