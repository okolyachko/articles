Abstract

Improving upon nature: Three nicotinamide-containing conjugates have been synthesized, characterized by X-ray crystallography, and biochemically evaluated as coenzymes for enoate reductase. The corresponding dihydropyridine of one compound was found to outperform the natural coenzyme, NADH, with comparable kinetic values for three substrates.

Abstract

Nicotinamide and pyridine-containing conjugates have attracted a lot of attention in research as they have found use in a wide range of applications including as redox flow batteries and calcium channel blockers, in biocatalysis, and in metabolism. The interesting redox character of the compounds’ pyridine/dihydropyridine system allows them to possess very similar characteristics to the natural chiral redox agents NAD+/NADH, even mimicking their functions. There has been considerable interest in designing and synthesizing NAD+/NADH mimetics with similar redox properties. In this research, three nicotinamide conjugates were designed, synthesized, and characterized. Molecular structures obtained through X-ray crystallography were obtained for two of the conjugates, thereby providing more detail on the bonding and structure of the compounds. The compounds were then further evaluated for biochemical properties, and it was found that one of the conjugates possessed similar functions and characteristics to the natural NADH. This compound was evaluated in the active enzyme, enoate reductase; like NADH, it was shown to help reduce the C=C double bond of three substrates and even outperformed the natural coenzyme. Kinetic data are reported.

Introduction

Nicotinamide- and pyridine-containing conjugates have attracted a lot of attention due to their variety of applications in different fields, from playing a key role in metabolism to being an electron carrier in many biological pathways, for their roles in medicinal scaffolds, and as redox flow batteries.1-6 The most common and biologically significant nicotinamide conjugate is NAD+/NADH, nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide, which is a cofactor involved in over 300 biochemical reactions and biological processes including redox reactions, cellular metabolism, and cellular respiration.7, 8 Nicotinamide conjugates containing the pyridine/dihydropyridine system, which NAD+ possesses, are the closest analogues of the natural cofactor. It has been reported that dihydropyridine compounds are unique in the critical sense that they are catalytic hydride donors, similar to NADH, rather than stoichiometric hydride reagents.9 This is due to the fact that the pyridines′ catalytic behavior lies in the chemistry of the 1,2-dihydropyridine/pyridine redox couple, which is driven by a dearomatization–aromatization process. The biologically important redox couple reversibly forms a C−H bond centered on the nicotinamide ring, thus representing an efficient system for numerous biological hydrogen-transfer reactions.9 Considerable work has gone into creating nicotinamide conjugates that are able to mimic the activity, that is the electrochemical behavior, of the NAD+/NADH redox couple.10, 11 Many groups have studied the electrochemical behavior and regeneration of NAD+/NADH.12-15 Sanford et al. designed and synthesized various pyridine-containing compounds and used their redox properties to create redox flow batteries.16 They reported the evolutionary design of a series of pyridine-based anolyte materials that exhibit up to two reversible redox couples at low potentials in the presence of Li-ion-supporting electrolytes. The Colbran group reported the synthesis of pyridine/dihydropyridine conjugates for bio-inspired, multielectron reduction,17 and demonstrated that an organic hydride pyridinium compound and a metal center can form a complex to give an efficient catalyst for transfer hydrogenation. In addition, Musgrave et al. reported the reduction of CO2 to methanol catalyzed by a biomimetic organo-hydride produced from pyridine, in which the 1,2-dihydropyridine acts as a recyclable organo-hydride that reduced CO2 to methanol in three hydride and proton-transfer steps.18 Dihydropyridine compounds have also been extensively reported to act as calcium channel blockers and have been explored for the development of pain therapeutics.4, 20 Given their potential application in biocatalysis, it is not surprising that the synthesis of these pyridine conjugates has attracted the attention of numerous researchers for years.21-27

Not only has there been considerable interest in creating model compounds that mimic the activity of the NAD+/NADH redox couple, but mimetics containing the nicotinamide moiety have been synthesized and tested in active enzymes as replacements for the natural cofactors. Lowe et al. developed an artificial coenzyme, CL4, that retained the nicotinamide portion and replaced the phosphate and adenine moieties with other functional groups. They showed its activity in active enzyme horse liver alcohol dehydrogenase (HLADH).28 A couple years later, Fish et al. demonstrated that a simpler nicotinamide coenzyme mimetic, which retained the nicotinamide portion but substituted the rest with a simple benzyl group, displayed activity in HLADH.29, 30 This group went on to report these mimetics as being able to catalyze the reduction of aldehydes to chiral alcohols, while also being useful in other hydrogenation applications.30-33 Through many studies, it was demonstrated that the nicotinamide moiety of NAD+ is all that is necessary for enzyme recognition and redox function.29, 34, 35 This concept was applied by Clark et al. in cytochrome P450 enzymes for which they engineered heme-containing enzymes for improved activity towards the same biomimetic cofactor.36 In addition, Paul and Hollman designed and evaluated a range of NADH biomimetics in enoate reductases (ERs).34, 37 Although biomimetics of NAD(H) have been developed and have found applications in chemoenzymatic synthesis, to date, only a few reports have been published that include kinetic data for reductases.34, 37, 38 In addition, many of the reported NADH mimetics mentioned above are similar in structure; other functional groups conjugated to the pyridine ring for further alterations have not been explored.

Herein, we report the synthesis and characterization of three nicotinamide-containing conjugates. These were characterized, two of them by single X-ray crystallography, to allow intra- and intermolecular interactions to be determined. Specifically, these NAD mimetics were designed bearing in mind future modifications that might allow further alteration and conjugation. From the X-ray structures, we observed intermolecular bonding between neighboring nicotinamide moieties through the carbonyl and amide nitrogen functional groups with hydrogen bond lengths in a comparable range to those of the natural cofactor. Through electrochemical evaluation of the compound, we found that the three conjugates also exhibit a characteristic NADH reduction peak at −1.1 to −1.6 V.12, 13 We then explored the reduction of the compounds into dihydropyridines. This was followed by enzymatic transformations using the dihydropyridine derivatives as hydride transfer agents involving ER, a yeast recombinant made in Escherichia coli (EC 1.3.1.16; Scheme 1). ERs are a class of redox enzymes that catalyze the asymmetric reduction of activated C=C bonds to form stereogenic centers.39-41 Their ability to catalyze reductions and their wide acceptance of different substrates are driving the exploitation of ERs for novel applications in redox biocatalysis and their implementation in industrial processes.42a The ER catalytic reaction resembles an asymmetric Michael-type addition of a chiral hydride to an enone. The catalytic cycle can be divided into two half reactions. In the first, a hydride is transferred from NADH to the enzyme-bound flavin.34 After release of the oxidized NAD+, the hydride is then transferred from the bound flavin to the activated alkene substrate (Scheme 1).

Compound 4 displayed biological activity with ER, thus allowing us to drive the enzymatic transformation of three enoate substrates. Herein, we report a class 2 ene reductase (see the Supporting Information) that accepts a nicotinamide coenzyme biomimetic with a side-by-side comparison of kinetics to the natural coenzyme, NADH. We provide details including the design and synthesis of the compounds, their biochemical evaluation, and detailed enzyme kinetic analysis.

Results and Discussion

Three pyridine-containing conjugates were synthesized from nicotinamide and nicotinic acid. Conjugate 1, a phenylalanine derivative, was synthesized by peptide coupling, followed by pyridine modification with benzyl bromide. Conjugates 2 and 3 had nicotinamide as the starting material and were synthesized by a simple one-step reflux reaction with 5-bromovaleric acid and (4-bromomethyl)benzoic acid, respectively. It has been reported that the nicotinamide moiety is all that is necessary for enzyme recognition and for involvement in the oxidation/reduction process, whereas the adenine moiety allows catabolic reactions to be differentiated from anabolic, which is irrelevant in catalytic applications. All three compounds retained the nicotinamide moiety, and modifications at the pyridine nitrogen and amide nitrogen were explored. These compounds were chosen with possible further modifications in mind, including modifications for surface studies, specifically, for compounds 2 and 3 by adding the carboxylic acid functional group to the reported benzyl group. Reduction of 1–3 was explored by using sodium dithionite as the reducing agent to form 1,2-dihydropyridines. We were able to reduce conjugates 1 and 3 to the corresponding products 4 and 5, although no discernable reduction product was obtained from 2 (Table 1). This is surprising, as all three conjugates can be reduced electrochemically (vide infra). At this point, we can only speculate that the aliphatic substituent on the pyridine nitrogen interferes with the chemical reduction using dithionite. Potential differences in the electronic interactions between benzoic acid and valeric acid might play a role, as reductions with dithionite can be dependent on electron-withdrawing groups. 42b–42d Both compounds show a strong absorption, λmax, 360 nm, which is characteristic of dihydropyridines and which is also present for NADH at 340 nm.43 The UV spectra of compounds 1, 3, 4 and 5, can be seen in Figure S22 in the Supporting Information.

Electrochemical reduction of the nicotinamide conjugates 1–3 was also explored by cyclic voltammetry (CV).12, 13 All CV experiments were carried out on a 1 mm solution of the compounds in 100 mm phosphate buffer (pH 7.05). Glassy-carbon working and counter electrodes were used along with a Ag/AgCl reference electrode. At a scan rate of 100 mV s−1, the cyclic voltammograms of the compounds exhibited a reduction signal at −1.1 to −1.6 V (Figure S21), which is characteristic of reduction of the nicotinamide moiety.12, 13, 44, 45a

The stability of the mimetics was also assessed (Figures S44 and 45), as it had been reported the stability of dihydropyridines varies under different conditions.45b Compound 4 was assessed at three different temperatures, 20, 30, and 40 °C, each at five different pH values. From this study, we concluded that 4, like other dihydropyridine compounds, is stable at all temperatures under slightly basic to basic conditions. Under acidic, weakly acidic, and neutral conditions, the compound had a fast rate of dissociation. Therefore, the studies were performed at pH≥7.5 in order to ensure that the compound was stable through all the tests, while maintaining a physiological pH for the enzyme to functionally operate at.

Crystallographic studies

Single crystals suitable for X-ray crystallography analysis were obtained for 1 and 2. Compound 1 crystallized from dimethyl sulfoxide in the trigonal space group P31, whereas crystals of 2 were obtained by slow evaporation from chloroform and diethyl ether and were found to be in the triclinic space group P-1. Details of the crystallographic analyses are provided in Tables S1–S4. The molecular structures of 1 and 2 are shown in Figure 1 A, B. Compound 1 has a single hydrogen bond between the amide nitrogen and the bromide anion (d[N1-Br1]=3.524(5) Å). Hydrogen bonding involving amide NH and Br anions was also reported for a similar nicotinamide conjugate, N-(3-(aden-9-yl)propyl)-3-carbamoylpyridinium bromide, with a length of 3.397(7) Å.46 In contrast, compound 2 engages in three intermolecular hydrogen bonds, thereby forming a supramolecular assembly involving the amide and carbonyl groups of adjacent molecules, and a linkage through a bromide anion to neighboring molecules with bond lengths of d[N1−O1]=2.870(3) Å, d[N2−Br1]=3.356(2) Å, and d[O4−Br1]=3.1811(19) Å. Similar to in nicotinamide,47 N-benzylnicotinamide (a very commonly reported NAD+ mimetic),48 and the natural NAD+ itself,49-51 the nicotinamide moiety of 2 engages in intermolecular bonding with neighboring nicotinamide moieties through the carbonyl and amide nitrogen functional groups (Figure 1 C). The lengths of the hydrogen bonds between the amide and carbonyl groups for the nicotinamide compounds stated above are reported to be in the range of 2.87–2.99 Å, which compound 2 (d=2.870(3) Å) also falls into. In addition, compound 2 crystalized in the triclinic space group, similar to NAD+, and, as expected, the pyridine group remains planar.48 A more detailed look of the intermolecular patterns between many molecules of compound 2 can be found in Figure S44. Next, the ability of the conjugates to be used as NADH mimetics that enable biochemical hydride transfer reactions was probed.

Biochemical evaluation of nicotinamide derivatives

The nicotinamide conjugates were biochemically evaluated in ER, (EC: 1.3.1.16) to explore the ability of the compounds to act as a coenzyme mimetic of NADH. ER-catalyzed reactions in the absence of NADH analogues served as negative controls, and thus it was decided to investigate these reactions further by employing our synthetic derivatives as NADH analogues. Research into NADH-dependent alkene reduction catalyzed by these enzymes has been described in respect to their applications in industrial processes.52 ER also exhibits cofactor promiscuity and has been reported to accept a wide range of substrates; this emphasizes its versatility and the expansion of its use.52 Therefore, our synthesized NADH analogues were evaluated in an ER for which clear controls had previously been run prior and precise results had been drawn. The three substrates in this study were chosen for their wide use, but the lack of kinetic data reported for their use in ER. Due to solubility issues with 5, it was not possible to accurately measure enzyme activity in this case. In addition, given the bulkiness of the amino acid substituent on the amide nitrogen, a proper fit in the active site might not be possible, similar to the Hantzsch ester previously tested. However, 4 acts as a coenzyme mimetic in the active enzyme, and enzyme kinetics were measured for the enzymatic ene reductions (Table 2). These reductions were conveniently followed by GC-MS, which allowed the reduced product to be identified. These data are presented in Figures S26–28 (4) and S32–34 (NADH), and the retention times for the substrates and products are reported in Table S6. In addition, the enzymatic transformations were followed spectroscopically by monitoring the characteristic absorbance of 4 at λmax=360 nm; which decreases when the substrate is converted. Scheme 2 shows the reduction of the C=C bond in 2-methylpent-2-enal (6) by ER with 4 as the coenzyme. Firstly, we were able to calculate the degree of conversion of 4 into 3 by using the calculated molar extinction coefficient (Table S5) and the absorbance values at beginning of the reaction and after 6 h. The highest conversion (93 %) of 4 to 3 was found with substrate 6. The conversion with the other two substrates was not nearly as high: 31 % for carvone (8) and 25 % with the ketoisophorone (10).

Secondly, we were able to confirm that 4 was capable of functionally substituting the natural coenzyme NADH in this reaction. Like NAD+/NADH, 3 and 4 exhibit absorbance peaks at 280 nm for the oxidized compound and at 340–360 nm for the reduced. We observed a decrease in absorbance at 360 nm, thus further proving that 4 can substitute NADH in the reductase. Figure 2 A and B shows the decrease in absorbance at 360 nm for compound 4 and NADH, respectively, over a 6-hour time period after 1 min, 75 min, 2.5 h, 4 h, and 6 h. Figure 2 C and D shows the profiles of the reaction with 4 and NADH over time; the decrease in absorbance from the start of the reaction to the end of the 6-hour time course is clear. From both GC-MS and UV studies, we concluded that all three products from the enzymatic reaction were formed and that the decrease in absorbance corresponded to substrate conversion. Next the kinetics of the enzymatic transformation were explored with 4 and the three substrates.

Kinetic evaluation of 4 as a NADH mimetic

Michaelis–Menten kinetics were evaluated such that the enzyme and coenzyme concentrations remained constant while the concentrations of the three substrates 6, 8, and 10 were varied at 0.16, 0.33, 0.67, 1.67, and 3.33 mm. Each reaction was monitored over 6 hours, and readings were taken at 5-minute intervals. The reaction was repeated with NADH as a control. All raw kinetic data are provided in Figures S23–34, concentrations are reported from blue (highest) via red, purple, and green to black (lowest) for (a) in each figure. From these, Michaelis–Menten curves and Lineweaver–Burk plots were constructed for each substrate (Figure S37–43) in order to determine the Km and Vmax values (Table 2). In addition, the catalytic efficiencies (kcat/Km) were also calculated (Table 2) by using the Vmax values.

From the side-by-side comparison of 4 and NADH, we can now provide a second example of a coenzyme mimetic outperforming the natural coenzyme. In addition, we provide kinetic data for a class 2 ER and show its acceptance of a nicotinamide mimetic with a range of substrates. As shown in Figure 2, over 6 hours, the absorbance peak at 360 nm for 4 decreases to almost 0. Although NADH also shows a decrease, it is not nearly as fast as the turnover of 4 over the 6-hour period. Comparing the catalytic constants further proves that 4 outperformed NADH, as a higher Vmax and catalytic efficiency were reported for each substrate (Table 2). For both 4 and NADH, a preference for substrate 6 over substrates 8 and 10 is found, as a lower Km value and a higher conversion were reported. The Km for substrate 6 was 0.077 mm and the Vmax was 0.0147 μmol min−1, whereas for substrates 8 and 10—which has industrial relevance due to the importance of its product, levodione (11), in carotenoid synthesis40—the Km values were 0.223 and 0.264 mm with Vmax values of 0.0039 and 0.0028 μmol min−1, respectively. These values are in a similar range in comparison to other reported substrates used in ER transformations.53, 54 From these data, it can be concluded that 4 is an effective NADH mimetic in the three reactions examined here, allowing the transformation of substrates 6, 8, and 10 into their products.

Conclusions

Due to the expanding interest in exploring nicotinamide derivatives as mimetics for biochemical transformations involving NADH and NAD+, three new conjugates were synthesized as potential mimetics of the natural redox agents, and their chemical properties, including their redox behavior, were examined. It was possible to reduce compounds 1 and 3 to their dihydropyridines, 4 and 5. The reduced compounds were shown to exhibit the properties of typical dihydropyridine systems, including the characteristic absorbance at 360 nm. Compound 4 was used in biochemical transformations by ene reductase; it allowed the enzymatic reduction of three unsaturated substrates, and was proven to act as a NADH mimetic. In addition, 4 outperformed the natural coenzyme, having a higher catalytic efficiency and Vmax value, thus providing the second example of a nicotinamide biomimetic that outperforms the natural NADH in ER. Kinetic data are reported for biotransformations with three substrates: 6, 8, and 10; in each case, the catalytic values were shown to be comparable to, if not better than, those of the natural NADH. Both NADH and 4 performed best with substrate 6, which has the lowest Km value of the three substrates and a low value compared to other similar substrates for ER reported in the literature.34, 53, 54 Herein, we have provided kinetic data for substrates that have been used in ER but for which the kinetics are little reported; in general the kinetics of class 2 ERs are not as heavily reported as those for classes 1 and 3. Our results suggest that there is potential for compound 4 to be useful in other enzymatic reactions that use NADH as the coenzyme.

Experimental Section

General considerations: Nicotinamide, nicotinic acid, n-hydroxysuccinamide, pyridine, sodium dithionite, 2-methylpent-2-enal, carvone, ketoisophorone, 1,3-dicyclohexylcarbodiimide (DCC), 1,2-dimethoxyethane, (4-bromomethyl)benzoic acid, 5-bromovaleric acid, and ene reductase were all purchased from Sigma–Aldrich. Boc-Phe-OMe was purchased through Advanced ChemTech. All reagents were used without further modifications unless specified. Milli-Q water was used throughout this study for all purposes including electrochemistry, sample solutions, and rinsing.

Synthesis of 1: Compound 1 was prepared by synthesizing a nicotinic acid ester. N-hydroxysuccinamide (35 mmol) and DCC (35 mmol) were added to a solution of nicotinic acid (35 mmol) dissolved in pyridine (70 mL). The solution was stirred for 20 h then filtered, re-dissolved in chloroform and washed 3× with water. The crude product was recrystallized with ethanol to give white needles.

This ester was dissolved in 1,2-dimethoxyethane cooled in an ice bath. A sodium hydrogen carbonate solution containing phenylalanine (16 mmol) was added dropwise to the solution over 30 min. The solution was left to stir for 2 h at 0 °C, then for 14 h at room temperature. The solution was filtered and acidified to pH 4 with 10 % citric acid, then washed with ethyl acetate. The organic layer was dried over sodium sulfate and evaporated under reduced pressure. The oil product was purified by silica column chromatography with CH2Cl2 and MeOH as eluents.

The nicotinoyl-l-phenylalanine product (7 mmol) was dissolved in 1,4-doixane (50 mL)/methanol (12.5 mL). Benzyl bromide (15 mmol) was added, and the solution was refluxed for 5 h in an oil bath. The product was filtered and washed with hexanes to obtain the final pure product. Yield: 78 %; 1H NMR (500 MHz, [D6]DMSO): δ=13.17 (s, 1 H; COOH), 9.62 (s, 1 H; pyr-1), 9.28 (d, 1 H; pyr-5), 8.98 (d, 1 H; pyr-3), 8.59 (s, 1 H; NH2), 8.31 (dd, 2 H; pyr-4), 8.19 (s, 1 H; NH2), 8.00 (d, 2 H; arom.), 7.63 (d, 2 H; arom.), 6.00 ppm (s, 2 H; CH2); 13C NMR ([D6]DMSO): δ=167.20 (1 C, COOH), 163.16 (1 C, CONH2), 147.05 (1 C, pyr-5), 138.93 (1 C, arom.), 134.68 (2 C, pyr-2), 132.08 (1 C, pyr-4), 130.47 (2 C, arom.), 129.48 (2 C, arom.), 128.83 (1 C, arom.), 63.51 ppm (1 C, CH2); MS: m/z calcd for C14H13N2O+: 257.0921 [M]+; found: 257.0918.

Synthesis of 4: Sodium hydrogen carbonate (6 mmol) was added to Milli-Q water (20 mL). Once this had dissolved, 3 (1 mmol) was dissolved in the solution. Under N2 and in the dark, sodium dithionite (4 mmol) was added slowly to the mixture. The reaction mixture was stirred vigorously for 3 h at room temperature. The pure compound was precipitated by using concentrated HCl. Yield: 57 %; 1H NMR (500 MHz, [D6]DMSO): δ=13.16 (s, 1 H; COOH), 7.92 (d, 2 H; arom.), 7.36 (d, 2 H; arom.), 6.99 (s, 1 H; pyr-1), 6.58 (s, 2 H; NH2), 5.94 (d, 1 H; pyr-5), 4.63 (dt, 1 H; pyr-4), 4.39 (s, 2 H; CH2), 2.97 ppm (d, 2 H; pyr-3); 13C NMR ([D6]DMSO): δ=169.44 (1 C, COOH), 167.69 (1 C, CONH2), 143.72 (3 C, pyr-1), 138.26 (1 C, arom.), 130.07 (2 C, arom.), 128.77 (1 C, pyr-5), 127.83 (1 C, arom.) 127.60 (1 C, arom.), 102.44 (1 C, pyr-2), 101.14 (2 C, pyr-4), 55.97 (2 C, CH2) 22.76 ppm (1 C, pyr-3); MS: m/z calcd for C14H14N2O3: 259.1004 [M+H]+; found: 259.1069.

Synthesis of 5: Sodium hydrogen carbonate (6 mmol) was added to Milli-Q water (20 mL). Once this had dissolved, 1 (1 mmol) was added along with CH2Cl2 (15 mL) until fully dissolved. Under N2 and in the dark, sodium dithionite (4 mmol) was added slowly to the mixture. The reaction mixture was stirred vigorously for 3 h at room temperature. The compound was collected in the organic layer and purified by silica column chromatography. Yield: 37 %; 1H NMR (500 MHz, CDCl3): δ=8.68 (s, 1 H; amide NH), 7.14–7.48 (m, 10 H; arom.), 5.73 (dd, 1 H; pyr-1), 4.99 (dq, 1 H; pyr-5), 4.49 (d, 1 H; CHα), 4.41 (dd, 1 H; pyr-4), 4.30 (q, 2 H; CH2), 3.73 (s, 3 H; CH3), 3.34 (d, 2 H; pyr-2), 3.15 ppm (m, 2 H; CH2β); 13C NMR ([D]chloroform): δ=172.77 (1 C of COOMe), 167.47 (1 C, CONH), 141.39 (1 C, pyr-1), 137.48 (1 C, arom.), 136.24 (1 C, pyr-5), 128.24 (10 C, arom.), 99.77 (1 C, pyr-2), 98.66 (1 C, pyr4), 84.52 (1 C, CH2), 57.81 (1 C, CHα), 54.95 (1 C, CH3), 52.83 (1 C, CH2β), 38.27 ppm (1 C, pyr-2); MS: m/z calcd for C23H24N2O3: 377.1787 [M+H]+; found: 377.1864.

UV/Vis studies of the enzymatic transformations with 4: In a quartz cuvette, ene reductase (100 μg mL−1), 4 (0.1 mm), and MOPS buffer (3 mL) were added to various concentrations (0.16–3.33 mm) of each substrate. The cell was kept at 30 °C. The decrease in the absorbance at 360 nm, which is characteristic of the dihydropyridine, was monitored with an 8453 W Peltier UV/Vis spectrophotometer.

GC-MS studies: GC-MS studies were carried out on an Agilent DC-624 (30 m×0.32 (1.0 μm). The enzymatic reactions were set up in a temperature-controlled water bath. The buffer (MOPS, 50 mm, pH 7.5 with 5 mm CaCl2), substrate (2-methylpent-2-enal, carvone, or ketoisophorone), coenzyme mimetic (4) and enzyme (ER) were added in this order. Aliquots (250 μL) were taken at different times over 6 h, extracted with ethyl acetate (600 μL) and centrifuged at 6000 rpm for 2 min. The ethyl acetate layer was then analyzed by GC-MS.

NMR studies: Compounds 1–3 were characterized by NMR spectroscopy (Bruker 500 MHz spectrometer). Compounds 1 and 5 were dissolved in CDCl3; compounds 2–4 were dissolved in [D6]DMSO, all at 2 mm. Spectra are shown in the Supporting Information.

Electrochemical studies: Electrochemical experiments were carried out at room temperature by using a CHI660B electrochemical workstation (CH Instruments Inc. TX, USA). All electrochemical experiments used a glassy carbon working electrode, glassy carbon as a counter electrode, and Ag/AgCl (saturated with 3 m KCl) as reference electrode. The glassy carbon electrodes were polished with Al2O3 (0.05 μm), then sonicated in Milli-Q water, ethanol, and Milli-Q water again for 10 min each to fully remove any absorbed Al2O3. The electrodes were thoroughly rinsed with Milli-Q water after each sonication. Both electrodes were electrochemically cleaned in aqueous KOH (1 mm). Solutions of compounds 1–3 (1 mm) were prepared in phosphate buffer (1 mm, pH 7.5) and were used for all electrochemical experiments. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) was performed at a 100 mV s−1 scan rate.

X-ray crystallography studies: Crystallographic data were collected on a Bruker Kappa APEX-DUO diffractometer by using a Copper ImuS tube with multilayer optics. The data were processed by using APEX2 and SAINT. Absorption corrections were carried out with SADABS. The structure was solved with SHELXT and refined by using SHELXL-2014 for full-matrix least-squares refinement that was based on F2. All H atoms were included in calculated positions and allowed to refine in riding-motion approximation with U∼iso∼ tied to the carrier atom.
