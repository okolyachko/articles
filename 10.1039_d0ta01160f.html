Naphthalene diimide as a two-electron anolyte for aqueous and neutral pH redox flow batteries†

Abstract

Developing organic materials that show stable and highly reversible redox-activity is a key challenge for achieving cost-effective and sustainable aqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs) for practical energy storage applications. We report a water-soluble naphthalene diimide (NDI) derivative using carboxylate groups, which was tested and established as a new class of versatile anolytes capable of storing two electrons in a single molecule in aqueous and neutral pH RFBs. The potassium salt of N,N′-bis(glycinyl)naphthalene diimide [K2-BNDI] showed reversible and stable two-electron reductions accompanied by ion- pairing. A prototype aqueous RFB consisting of [K2-BNDI] and 4-OH-TEMPO was constructed and showed excellent cyclability, high energy and voltage efficiencies. This study suggests a versatile way of designing new anolytes inspired by organic semiconductors that operate in a multi-electron redox mode.

Introduction

To more effectively utilize renewable, but intrinsically intermittent energy sources such as solar, wind and hydroelectric power, significant advances in energy storage technology are urgently needed.1–4 Redox flow batteries (RFBs)5 are one of the promising solutions because the energy and power density characteristics are decoupled from each other, and they are relatively cheap making them economically viable for practical applications.6–8 Currently available commercial solutions utilize vanadium-based redox couples for both positive and negative charge carriers, denoted catholyte and anolyte, respectively. Redox-active organic molecules are exciting alternatives that may allow for reducing the cost and increasing the versatility, as their chemical properties can be tuned over a broad range by decorating a redox-active core with various functional groups. Thus, organic charge carriers have the potential to be cost-effective and powerful platforms for future aqueous energy storage systems.9,10

In an ideal organic RFB, molecules designated as anolytes are reduced at highly negative potentials, and the catholytes are oxidized at highly positive potentials to maximize the working voltage while displaying high levels of reversibility and stability. An organic anolyte that meets these demands at practically useful levels remains elusive, however. An effective strategy for increasing the energy density is to use molecules capable of two-electron redox reactions, potentially doubling the energy density. Anthraquinones and methyl viologens are among the most widely used water-soluble anolytes. The anthraquinone derivatives displayed good RFB performance as a two-electron storage material in alkaline solutions.11–13 Methyl viologen is another example of a two-electron redox system,9 while some charge carriers that have been studied thus far are limited to a single-electron transfer in aqueous solutions.14–16 Two-electron redox chemistry was recently reported for viologen-thiazoles in neutral pH solution,17 but the redox potentials of viologen-derived anolyte are not sufficiently negative, typically being notably more positive than −0.7 V vs. SHE.16 Alloxazine is another example of a two-electron storage anolyte,18 but an undesired ring-opening reaction during cycling was observed. Therefore, a new class of organic anolytes that can stably store two electrons at neutral pH is highly desirable.

We developed a new class of organic anolytes that can store two electrons in a single molecule. Inspired by n-type organic semiconducting materials that typically display high electron affinities and excellent thermal and oxidative stability, we selected naphthalene diimide (NDI) because the extended π-conjugation established over polycyclic aromatic rings is expected to stabilize the molecular structure with minimal change during the reduction process.19–24 NDI derivatives were previously employed for non-aqueous RFBs.25 The main reason why the NDI core has not been considered widely for aqueous RFB applications to date despite its attractive electrochemical properties is that it is not soluble in water.20 While this publication was being prepared, one example of a water-soluble NDI tethered to a propyl ammonium functionality was reported employing an asymmetric aqueous cell design in static mode. However, experiments in 25 mM concentration showed poor coulombic efficiency in that case.26 In this work, we expand on the strategy of preparing stable NDI anolytes by simply decorating the redox-active core with carboxylate groups. The utility of these new anolytes was tested in a prototype RFB application, and the fundamental redox behavior of the two-electron redox events was studied, revealing that ion-pairing plays a critical role in promoting electron transfer.

Results and discussion

Preparation of the anolytes and their two-electron redox behaviors

Due to the strong π–π interaction caused by the polycyclic aromatic rings, NDI readily precipitates, and it is used only for solid electrodes for lithium and sodium-ion batteries.19–24 We hypothesized that the solubility in neutral aqueous solution might be increased by adding two carboxylate groups that are ion paired with K+ or Na+ ions, as shown in Scheme 1, which is denoted as [K2-BNDI] (potassium salt of N,N′-bis(glycinyl) NDI) or [Na2-BNDI], respectively. They were prepared easily and in high yields of around 90% from cheap precursors.
Scheme 1 Synthesis of [M2-BNDI] (M = K or Na).

The electrochemical behaviors of [K2-BNDI] and [Na2-BNDI] were investigated using experimental and computational techniques. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) studies showed two redox events in 1 M KCl aqueous solution (pH ∼7). As illustrated in Fig. 1a, [K2-BNDI] exhibits two stepwise transfers of single electrons with an Eo (formal potential) of −0.20 (c1/a1) and −0.47 V (c2/a2) vs. NHE, associated with the redox couples [K2-BNDI]/[K2-BNDI]1− and [K2-BNDI]1−/[K2-BNDI]2−, respectively. Remarkably, the two redox processes are very stable and reproducible upon repeating the CV scans for 500 times. The current density (J) for both redox events are increased as the scan rates are increased (Fig. 1b). The Randles–Sevcik's plots reveal that J and the square root of scan rate (ν1/2) are linearly dependent on each other (Fig. S2†), indicating that the redox process is diffusion controlled. Notably, the second reduction peak (c2) broadens towards the negative potential direction with increasing scan rate suggesting a sluggish quasi-reversible reaction for the [K2-BNDI]1−/[K2-BNDI]2− couple (Fig. 1b). The diffusion coefficients (D) were estimated to be 3.95 × 10−6 and 1.95 × 10−6 cm2 s−1 for the first and second reduction, respectively, using the Levich equation (Fig. S3†). The electron-transfer rate (k0) for the first reduction was calculated to be 0.27 cm s−1 from the Nicholson method (Fig. S4†),27 while the second reduction could not be defined due to the peak broadening and shifting with increasing scan rate. This unique behavior of the 2nd reduction of the [K2-BNDI] may be attributed to the ion-pair formation, as will be discussed below. These electroanalytical results establish a slower rate of the second reduction than the first reduction of [K2-BNDI] (Table S1†).


Fig. 1 Cyclic voltammograms of 5 mM [K2-BNDI] in 1 M KCl aqueous solution (a) for 500 cycles at 20 mV s−1 and (b) at scan rates from 5 to 640 mV s−1. (c) Computed redox potentials with and without ion pairing.

To better understand the redox-properties, DFT calculations were carried out.28 The two carboxylates and one of the carbonyl oxygens of BNDI were found to interact strongly with the two K+ ions, establishing metal–O distances in the range of 2.80–2.60 Å (Fig. 1c). The NDI-fragment remains planar and the π-conjugation is unperturbed during the two redox events. A careful inspection of redox active molecular orbital (RAMO) of the neutral [K2-BNDI] reveals that the first and second electrons add to the π* orbital that is delocalized across the NDI fragment.

Our calculations place the first reduction of [K2-BNDI] at −0.20 V vs. NHE, in excellent agreement with the measured value of −0.20 V vs. NHE. The second redox potential is computed to be −0.79 V vs. NHE, which is 300 mV more negative than the experimental value of −0.49 V vs. NHE. As seen previously,29 the addition of another equivalent of K+ ion to form a tight contact ion-pair [K3-BNDI]1− resolves this discrepancy. The addition of a K+ ion is associated with a free energy changes of −8.0 kcal mol−1. When this energy is included, the computed redox potential becomes −0.44 V vs. NHE (Table S2†), which is in excellent agreement with the experimental value. Interestingly, the K+ ion binding to the singly reduced [K2-BNDI]1− is unfavorable by 17.3 kcal mol−1 (Fig. 1c), indicating that the ion-pairing does not occur within the time-scale of the first reduction, but is critical for the second.

This process is of course diffusion limited and is associated with a notable barrier, as the hydrated K+ ion must shed some water solvents to form the ion-pair. Given the large solvation energy of −70.6 kcal mol−1 of K+ ion, this desolvation likely causes the slow kinetics for the second reduction, leading to the scan-rate dependent broadening, as is characteristic for an EC-mechanism, where the electrochemical process is followed by a chemical event. This prediction is supported by a similar reversibility and two redox potentials of [Na2-BNDI], which shows approximately two-fold lower D and smaller k0 values likely due to the higher solvation energy of Na+ (Fig. S5 and S6†). It is interesting that our calculations reproduced the first potential without considering a contact ion-pair. These findings suggest that water prefers to keep the monoanionic species in solution such as [K2-BNDI]1− and [K3-BNDI]1− after the first and second reduction, respectively, whereas the dianionic species of [K2-BNDI]2− is avoided by adding a counter-cation. While intuitively understandable, this finding has some consequences for the battery charging rates and is a feature that has not been considered prominently in the field thus far.

Chemical and electrochemical stability of [K2-BNDI]

The chemical/electrochemical stability of [K2-BNDI] over an extended period of time was assessed in a static H-cell where the reduced [K2-BNDI] was used as the counterpart of the redox couple (Fig. 2a). In the galvanostatic examination, two potential plateaus are exhibited for the first cycle at −0.24 and −0.49 V vs. NHE (Fig. 2b), which agrees well with the Eo values seen in the CV experiments. Interestingly, the first reduction potential gradually diminished upon cycling and merged with the second reduction plateau, as marked by an arrow in Fig. 2b, which leads to a negative potential shift of the corresponding oxidation. This diminishing of the first reduction potential (c1) and extension of the second potential (c2) were not observed in the CV over 500 cycles (Fig. 1a), indicating a time-dependent chemical reaction that only occurs under the galvanostatic condition. Such a decay of reduction potential was not displayed when the applied potential is limited to the first redox process (Fig. S7a†). In addition, we found that resting for one day at the open circuit voltage alleviates this unusual behavior in RFB system (Fig. S7b and c†). Taking these experimental probes together, we suggest that this phenomenon is associated with the contact ion-pairing with K+ ion during the second reduction. Although the ion-pairing is nullified after the second oxidation, the K+ ions may be kinetically trapped and remain in close proximity to [K2-BNDI] upon rapid discharge, which may hamper the first reduction and lead to this unusual polarization of the potentials during the recharging process. More importantly, this unique redox behavior does not deteriorate the overall capacity retention for two-electron transfer process, and the H-cell stably performed over 225 cycles over 14 days. The capacity-fading rate was found to be remarkably small at 0.042% per day (Fig. 2b), demonstrating that [K2-BNDI] is exceedingly redox-stable.

WE, CE and RE: working, counter, and reference electrodes (b) galvanostatic cycling test for 225 cycles (14 days) at a current density of 70 mA g−1 based on the [K2-BNDI] weight. The inset arrow marks the potential shift of during the first reduction.

RFB performance

Next, a RFB system was constructed using [K2-BNDI] as the anolyte and 4-OH-TEMPO (4-hydroxy-2,2,6,6-tetramethyl piperidine-1-oxyl) as the catholyte (Fig. 3a). As shown in Fig. 3b, 4-OH-TEMPO is capable of single electron transfer at a potential of +0.80 V vs. NHE, yielding the voltage gaps of 1.0 and 1.27 V, which is successfully demonstrated in the galvanostatic profiles shown in Fig. 3c. The average cell voltage is higher than what is typically reported for most aqueous organic RFBs.17,30–36 The ideal molar concentration ratio of 4-OH-TEMPO to [K2-BNDI] is 2:1 based on the number of electrons they can donate and accept. At this ratio, a significant capacity fading was observed, however. The capacity of the flow cell consisting of 25 mM [K2-BNDI] and 50 mM 4-OH-TEMPO decreased from 1.13 to 0.12 A h L−1 over 100 cycles (Fig. S8†), which may be attributed to instabilities of the catholyte 4-OH-TEMPO that is known to decompose via disproportionation.32,36,37 Employing an excess quantity of the catholyte should therefore support the much more stable [K2-BNDI] anolyte. Thus, we have repeated the flow cell tests with a molar ratio of 1:4. The maximum solubility of K2-BNDI in DI water was found to be 167 mM, and 30 mM in the presence of 1 M KCl solution (Fig. S9†). Fig. 3d highlights the excellent cycling performance of a flow cell using 25 mM [K2-BNDI] and 100 mM 4-OH-TEMPO in 1 M KCl solution. The initial capacity of 1.25 A h L−1 was retained at 83.2% after 100 cycles along with 96% coulombic efficiency (CE) at a current density of 5 mA cm−2. The shortened capacity of the first charging potential plateaus while maintaining the total charging capacity (Fig. 3c) is ascribed to residual ion-pairing, as described above. The initial 20 cycles underwent moderate capacity fluctuation, possibly due to electrochemical stabilization processes.
Fig. 3 Aqueous RFB consisting of [K2-BNDI] and 4-OH-TEMPO in 1 M KCl. (a) Schematic and redox processes. (b) Combined CVs of 10 mM [K2-BNDI] and 20 mM 4-OH-TEMPO at a scan rate 40 mV s−1. (c) First 20 cycles of galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles with 25 mM [K2-BNDI]‖100 mM 4-OH-TEMPO in a flow cell (flow rate: 15 mL min−1, current density: 5 mA cm−2) (d) capacity retention (Q) and coulombic efficiency (CE) for 100 cycles.

The polarization curves at a state of 100% charge showed a peak power density of ∼37 mW cm−2 (Fig. S10†). The rate capability from 5 to 20 mA cm−2 gave 1.0 A h L−1 at 10 mA cm−2 and 0.5 A h L−1 at 20 mA cm−2, as shown in Fig. 4a. The voltage efficiency (VE) was evaluated to 91, 83, 72 and 60% at 5, 10, 15 and 20 mA cm−2, respectively (Fig. 4b), showing an analogous linear trend in energy efficiencies (EE). Moreover, regardless of the large current change, the capacity was stably maintained at each current density in line with ∼98% CE for overall 100 cycles, as illustrated in Fig. 4c, demonstrating again the high stability of the [K2-BNDI] anolyte in the electrochemical condition applied. A post-mortem analysis after 120 cycles showed that [K2-BNDI] is intact with the aromatic signals in 1H NMR being unchanged (Fig. S11†), while some extra signals in the aliphatic region suggested crossover contamination of 4-OH-TEMPO and its decomposition products. We therefore attributed the capacity decrease emerging from 80th cycle to decomposition of 4-OH-TEMPO catholyte and its crossover. To further confirm the crossover through the anion-exchange membrane (AEM), we carried out electrospray ionization-mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) studies after exposing the electrolytes to an AEM in a static cell for 3 days at the open circuit voltage. As shown in Fig. S12,† 4-OH-TEMPO could be detected on the anolyte side, whereas no trace of the [K2-BNDI] was found on the catholyte side. This finding may be attributed to the π–π interaction between the planar BNDI cores,38 which may significantly increase the effective molecular size of the anolyte. Even after the reduction of BNDI, this π–π interaction is prominent as there is no geometrical change in the molecule, as confirmed by DFT calculations. In addition, the ion-pair formation that we found to be critically important for the second reduction leads to a partial compensation of the negative charge during the charging process. Thus, we conclude that crossover contamination of [K2-BNDI] across the membrane is insignificant.

The flow rate was 15 mL min−1 for the current densities of 5 and 10 mA cm−2, and 20 mL min−1 for 15 and 20 mA cm−2. (a) Voltage profiles at current densities from 5 to 20 mA cm−2. (b) The relationship of CE, voltage efficiency (VE) and energy efficiency (EE) with current densities. (c) Capacity retention and CE profiles for 100 cycles.

We also assessed the anolyte performance of the sodium analog [Na2-BNDI], which displays a high solubility of ∼210 mM in DI water and ∼40 mM in the presence of 1 M NaCl (Fig. S9†). The capacity was more elongated with [Na2-BNDI] at a high current rate of 20 mA cm−2, compared to [K2-BNDI] (Fig. 4a). By upshifting the cut-off voltage by 1.7 V, however, a new charging plateau appeared, caused by the hydrogen evolution reaction at the anolyte side (Fig. S13†). This voltage window caused the capacity fading as shown in Fig. 5b. Nonetheless, a flow cell with 40 mM [Na2-BNDI] in 1 M NaCl shows an improved capacity retention and CE up to 200 cycle (Fig. 5b). In addition, the ESI-MS and 1H NMR analyses revealed no evidence of anolyte decomposition (Fig. 5c and S14†). The new signals appearing in the range of 150–250 m/z correspond to 4-OH-TEMPO moved from the catholyte. The resulting cyclic stability highlights that excellent chemical stability can be achieved with NDI-based anolytes. It is also clear that the anion in the supporting electrolyte has a profound influence on the cell polarization. Replacing the Cl− electrolyte with NO3− increased the voltage polarization incrementally, and showed a notable decrease in the capacity retention (Fig. S15†). Most likely, this is caused by lower ionic molar conductivity (71.44 cm2 Ω−1 mol−1) of NO3− compared to Cl− (76.34 cm2 Ω−1 mol−1),39 reflecting on the fact that the NO3− ion moves slowly across the AEM.

(c) ESI-MS analysis with the positive ionization mode of [Na2-BNDI] anolyte after 200 cycle (top) and as-prepared [Na2-BNDI] (middle) and 4-OH-TEMPO (bottom).

Conclusions

In conclusion, we prepared a water-soluble naphthalene diimide derivative, [K2-BNDI] and [Na2-BNDI], as a new category of a two-electron anolyte material for aqueous, neutral RFB applications displaying excellent stability. Two-electron storage of NDI derivatives is stabilized by pairing with contact cations after charging. A prototype flow cell containing [K2-BNDI] anolyte and 4-OH-TEMPO catholyte displayed good cyclability with 86% and 91% of EE and VE, respectively. In addition, a symmetric cell test showed an outstanding cycling performance with only 0.042% capacity fade per day. These results demonstrate that multi-electron redox behaviour coupled to redox-stability can be designed rationally to yield a water-soluble anolyte. To make this new class of organic charge carriers more valuable for practical applications, future work must focus on increasing the solubility, which may be achieved by introducing polar functionalities with which the NDI core can be decorated. This work is currently ongoing in our laboratories. The principles demonstrated in this work is applicable to a broad class of diimides and paves the way to designing new anolyte materials for aqueous RFBs.
