    Abstract

    An electrochemical analysis strategy based on the Marcus–Hush approximation is presented to analyze the kinetic component of organic redox flow battery (RFB) electrolytes. The procedure was applied to aqueous solutions of methyl viologen (MV) and 2,2′-bipyridyl (diquat, DQ) derivatives as model redox-active electrolytes; although these systems are promising negolyte candidates in organic RFBs, their electrode kinetics continues to be unclear. For compound MV, the voltammetric analysis revealed an adsorption process of electrogenerated species to the glassy carbon electrode surface, so its electron transfer rate constant ks should not be estimated by applying outer sphere electron transfer formulations. For the remaining compounds studied, experimental ks values were obtained and they range from 0.22 to 0.62 cm s–1. Quantum chemical modeling was applied not only to decipher properties of the adsorption process of the MV structure but also to rationalize the kinetic differences in compounds studied through their total and inner reorganization energies. This experimental and theoretical approach allowed elucidation of the kinetic component of compounds studied, revealing that ks values for MV and DQ compound derivatives should not exhibit the reported differences of at least one order of magnitude. Finally, the experimental ks value (0.62 cm s–1) obtained for compound 5,5′-DMDQ is the largest value reported to date in the literature of aqueous organic RFBs, which makes it a strong anolyte candidate.

    Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Electron transfer reactions of organic redox-active materials are key processes for controlling the energy conversion and storage in natural and artificial systems. (1−3) One very important application of this phenomenon is the recent development of systems known as organic redox flow batteries (RFBs), which are a promising technology to store wind and solar electricity, where reversible oxidation and reduction reactions occurring at electrodes are fundamental reactions to charge and discharge the redox-active electrolytes of the device. (2,3) Compared to vanadium systems, (4) the electrolytes of organic RFBs can be composed of a wide range of synthetically tunable, potentially inexpensive, and environmentally friendly organic redox-active compounds; (3,5) such organic compounds often exhibit significantly higher electron transfer rate constants (ks) than inorganic compounds. (2,3) The latter parameter in combination with the diffusion coefficient determines the lifetime of the reactant at the electrode surface before the electron transfer reaction is completed during the charge and discharge processes of RFBs, where a high rate constant indicates a fast electrode transformation and allows for high current densities that can be drawn from the cell in the absence of major voltage losses. Therefore, determining the electrochemical parameters of the employed redox-active electrolytes is essential for designing more efficient systems. (6) In the case of diffusion coefficients, these values have been successfully estimated by rotating disc electrode (RDE) tests. (2,3,6)
    The electrode kinetics of redox flow batteries is typically examined by linear sweep voltammetry and cyclic voltammetry experiments, (7−9) where ks data are calculated from Koutecký–Levich (10) and Nicholson equations, (11) respectively. However, both approaches have limitations that are generally not considered and consequently, numerous inconsistencies in the literature of organic RFBs have been generated; there are many reported ks values of different orders of magnitude for same molecular systems. Two very clear examples of these differences are the methyl viologen and the bis(3-trimethylammonio)propyl viologen tetrachloride (BTMAP2+-Vi2+). The ks of the first reduction reaction of MV was calculated to be 0.022 cm s–1 using the Koutecký–Levich equation; however, it was determined to be 0.35 cm s–1 by Nicholson’s method. (3) For BTMAP2+-Vi2+, this rate constant was calculated to be 0.022 cm s–1 with the Koutecký–Levich and Tafel equations (8) but measured to be 0.309 cm s–1 estimated by Nicholson’s method. (12)
    It should be kept in mind that applying Koutecký–Levich and Tafel equations to determine the rate constant of a fast-redox reaction may result in a significant underestimation of ks. (3) On the other hand, the use of Nicholson’s equation requires examining differences between the cathodic and anodic peak potential (ΔEp = Epc – Epa) values of voltammograms as a function of the scan rate (11) but some restrictions apply; (11,13) this method is based on the assumption that electron transfer kinetics are described by the Butler–Volmer formalism; the electron transfer coefficient α = 0.5; the switching potential is 112.5/n mV greater than the peak potential; and the temperature is 298 K. There is also a key condition widely ignored from kinetic studies of RFBs with fast-redox reactions that should be strictly fulfilled to apply the Nicholson approach, it is necessary to move the system from a reversible condition toward a quasireversible behavior, by increasing the scan rate. (11)
    The rate constant of RFB electrolytes are typically estimated by considering the limiting Nicholson bound values of the kinetic parameter Ψ (e.g., 20) (11) but this implies that systems studied remained as reversible in all studied conditions. For example, Luo et al. (14) applied the Nicholson method for analyzing the one-electron reduction of a methyl viologen structure, obtaining a ks value of 0.28 cm s–1; their voltammetric responses exhibited ΔEp differences less than 61 mV and for that reason, they were unable to plot different values of Ψ vs v1/2; so they considered Ψ = 20 for ks calculations. With the above considerations, such a reported ks value is inconsistent according to Nicholson’s definition. (11,15)
    To face this problem, Wang et al., (6) recently published an electrochemical methodology to address the kinetic component of RFB electrolytes, where it is proposed to carry out ultrahigh-speed cyclic voltammetry experiments for achieving conditions where the Nicholson equation is valid in estimating ks values >0.1 cm s–1. Performing these experiments often represents a challenge due to equipment limitations and/or due to the lack in experience for compensating the IR drop of the cell, which increases up to kΩ values due to the indisputable use of ultramicroelectrodes.
    A very important discussion in the latter work is that voltammetric simulations using commercial software (like DigiElch or DIgisim) could facilitate the acquisition of experimental kinetic information of RFB electrolytes. This tool has also allowed the examination of experimental results of organic compounds in aprotic solvents, within the framework of the Marcus–Hush theory. (16) As the latter approach is not limited to specific ΔEp values of voltammograms, (17−19) it could provide relevant information in elucidating the kinetic component of widely used organic RFB electrolytes. The present work aims in complementing the experimental analysis strategy proposed by Wang et al., (6) by encouraging readers to also examine the kinetic component of their RFB electrolytes within the framework of the Marcus–Hush theory. Unlike the work presented by Wang, in this case the procedure is presented with real experiments. Solutions of diquat and methyl viologen compound derivatives were tested as examples because these compounds are strong candidates for developing efficient RFB electrolytes and their electrode kinetics continues to be unclear. The Marcus–Hush electrode kinetic model offers the advantage of providing specific properties of electrogenerated species; (17−19) so the experimental information obtained was reinforced with electronic structure calculations.
    The chemical structure of each studied compound is presented below: methyl viologen, bis(3-trimethylammonio)propyl viologen (BTMAP2+-Vi2+), 1,1′-bis(3-sulfonatopropyl)-4,4′bipyridinium ((SPr)22–-Vi2+), and four 2,2′-bipyridyl derivatives; diquat (DQ), 4,4′-DMDQ, 5,5′-DMDQ, and 6,6′-DMDQ are shown in scheme 1.

    Electrochemistry

    To analyze the electron transfer process of compounds studied, cyclic voltammetry experiments using a glassy carbon electrode in an aqueous solvent electrolyte were performed. The electrochemical responses (Figure 1) obtained for all studied compounds at 0.1 V s–1 exhibited a typical first reversible one-electron reduction reaction of MV and DQ compound derivatives to form a radical cation; (20,21) the electrochemical process occurring at the electrode surface is represented in eq 1.(1)Such voltammograms (Figure 1) are located at different redox potentials, where methyl derivatives presented signals at the lowest values. These thermodynamic effects have been extensively discussed in the literature and the variations detected (less than 0.15 V) do not represent important quantities to modulate the voltage in a flow battery. (2,3,20,21) However, more information on the kinetic component of studied compounds in protic conditions is required.
    In outer sphere electron transfer reactions, the electrode kinetics can be experimentally examined with the analysis of voltammetric responses as a function of the scan rate. (22) For compound MV, the increase in the scan rate (10 V s–1) produced a voltammetric signal of higher currents (Figure 2A), the reverse oxidation peak current grew much faster than the reduction peak current and the voltammetric signal obtained at 10 V s–1 probably exhibits a reduction prewave, at less negative potential values than the main diffusive peak. These changes in the electrochemical behavior can be easily detected upon normalizing the voltammetric signals vs v1/2. Nevertheless, additional information about the reaction taking place at the electrode surface is required. For this purpose, analyte concentration (C*) effects were also examined by cyclic voltammetry. The results obtained are compared (Figure 2) to the ones acquired from the base structure DQ, since there is evidence that its electrogenerated radical cations DQ+• are stable in solution at high concentrations of C* (0.2 mol L–1). (9) For the latter compound, reversible and diffusion controlled one-electron voltammetric waves (Figure 2B) were detected for all concentrations studied, the signals exhibited marginal changes in potential values and in the normalized peak current intensities vsC*; this result disclosed the expected high stability of electrogenerated DQ+• species in solution. However, for compound MV, things were different.
    An increase in the concentration of C* caused strong effects on both the wave shape and peak potentials (Figure 2C): the signal shifted toward less negative potential values; the oxidation peak height rapidly dropped to zero; and some overcrossings on the cathodic branches were noticed. As the latter phenomenon is typically related to the existence of a nucleation process at the electrode surface, (23) the overall observations are consistent with an inner sphere electron transfer process that forms MV+• radical cations, which attach to the glassy carbon electrode. (22−24) Under these conditions, the typical formulations (e.g., the Nicholson equation (11)) used to obtain ks are not valid. (22)
    In RFBs operating with MV electrolytes, the electrogenerated MV+• species can experiment bimolecular interactions to form dimers; (3,21,25,26) these dimers are also believed to further undergo a disproportionation mechanism to give MV2+ and MV0 species. The poorly soluble MV0 structures can then precipitate out of solution or react with protons to produce H2 even at pH 7; both possibilities, dimerization and dismutation mechanisms, are accepted as main routes leading to the fading of these batteries. In this sense, these mechanisms could also be related to the loss in reversibility detected when increasing MV concentrations in our cyclic voltammetry experiments (Figure 2C). Nevertheless, it is unlikely this disproportionation will occur because the comproportionation constant of MV0 and MV2+ is very large (Kcomp = 1 × 106). (27)
    Regarding the other option, Alden et al. (28) also examined concentration dependent effects of MV electrolytes/aqueous media by cyclic voltammetry. Using a platinum working electrode, the authors prevented the adsorption process of species and also demonstrated that dimerization interactions in MV+• structures occur as a fast and reversible process, (28,29) reporting an equilibrium constant of Keq = 720 mol–1 L; (28) this parameter was also reported to be Keq = 330 mol–1 L (30) but these authors used mercury as a working electrode; so, our studies using a glassy carbon electrode, in combination with the latter results, suggest that chemical properties of the electrode materials significantly impact the reduction mechanism of MV electrolytes, and, in fact, the low equilibrium constant (Keq = 330 mol–1 L) reported by Rueda et al. (30) was discussed on the basis of a weakly adsorption process of MV electrogenerated species at the mercury surface. Finally, both authors agree that the kinetic dimerization rate constant for this system approaches 1.0 × 104 mol–1 L s–1, so this mechanism cannot cause the pronounced effect in reversibility detected upon increasing MV concentrations (Figure 2C). Therefore, we believe that such losses in reversibility are mainly due to the above commented adsorptive abilities of MV+• electrogenerated species at the glassy carbon electrode.
    For the remaining compounds, the increase in the scan rate produced changes in potential values and in the peak current intensities (Figure 3); following the trends established by the Randles–Sevcik equation. (22) The voltammetric behavior detected is exemplified in Figure 3, with the responses obtained for compounds (A) BTMAP2+-Vi2+ and (B) DQ. In these experiments, scan rates exceeding 1.0 V s–1 were required to produce significant increases in peak to peak separation and this suggests the occurrence of fast-redox reactions. (15,16) From Figure 3, the transition of voltammetric waves toward quasireversibility is clearly seen, upon increasing the scan rate. An important advantage of using high concentrations of the supporting electrolyte (such as in our case: 0.5 mol L–1 acetate buffer) is that uncompensated solution resistance decreases significantly (in this case IR ∼ 35 Ω) and can be easily compensated without representing a challenge, until experiments at much higher scan rates will be performed. (15)

    An important advantage of using working curves based on a Marcus–Hush approximation is that it can be visualized if ks differences obtained for the family of compounds are consistent based on scan rates selected for experimentation. For instance, and regardless of the method used for the voltammetric analysis, DeBruler et al. (12) studied a family of methyl viologen derivatives by gradually increasing the scan rate up to 2 V s–1, reporting ks values ranging from 0.30 cm s–1 to 0.35 cm s–1. If the maximum scan rate used by the authors is located at the log v (0.3 V s–1) axis of a working curve (Figure 4B), the graph reveals that estimations were carried out about very small potential differences and consequently, they must be improved by performing experiments at higher scan rates. The working curves can also be constructed by analyzing variations at the oxidation site or even using the peak to peak separations.
    The improved results reported in the present article indicate that ks values of diquat and methyl viologen compound derivatives/aqueous media are in the same order of magnitude, where the 5,5′-DMDQ derivative exhibited the largest value. In particular, diquat compound derivatives exhibited ks values sufficiently larger than those reported by Huang et al. (ks = 0.0064 cm s–1) (20) and Lv et al. (ks = 0.029 cm s–1), (9) which employed Koutecky–Levich and Nicholson equations, respectively, even with their previously commented advantages. To understand how the chemical structure modifies the electron transfer kinetics of these compounds, an analysis based on the use of electronic structure calculations is presented below.

    Theoretical Description

    As commented above, the compounds studied do not exhibit important quantities to modulate the voltage in a battery. Theoretical exploration of MV and DQ cores with different functional groups can help modulate the redox potential and solubility of these families of compounds to predict better anolyte candidates for aqueous organic redox flow batteries. For that reason and to explain the effects of including functional groups in diquat and methyl viologen cores, reduction redox potentials (E0) were calculated with the density functional theory (DFT) level of theory (Table 1). Redox potential values obtained with DFT are consistent with experimental E1/2 data. Comparing against the base structure DQ, E0 values for 4,4′-DMDQ, 5,5′-DMDQ, and 6,6′-DMDQ derivatives decrease around 0.12 V, this variation is in agreement with that observed upon introducing methyl substituent groups onto the 2,2′-bipyridine core. (33) For (SPr)22–-Vi2+ and BTMAP+2-Vi2+ the opposite behavior is detected; that means, the electron-withdrawing effect of amino and sulfonate groups (having Hammett parameters of 0.86 and 0.30, respectively) (34) cancel the electron-donating abilities of the propyl chain (having a Hammett parameter of −0.06). (34)
    Then, we examined the MV adsorption process in terms of two theoretical models to shed light on some aspects. The glassy carbon electrode surface was modeled with a considerable number of carbon atoms as in refs (35,36) (Figure 5); the interaction energy between this surface and MV and DQ species was calculated. The results obtained are compiled in Table 2.     Now, we turn our attention to an examination of the localization of the charge densities for the study of DQ derivatives. In Table S2, we show sN for the oxidized dimethyl diquat (DMDQ) species, which are isoelectronic. In 5,5′-DMDQ the electron density is more delocalized than in the other species. The three derivatives have the same electron-donating methyl substituents and they can activate the ring for accepting an electron during the reduction process. Charge density delocalization could be related to the ring activation. We also found that ks for 5,5′-DMDQ is more than twice the ks of the other species, suggesting that molecular charge density delocalization, along with other factors, such as geometry or thermodynamics, could play a role in the kinetics of the reduction reaction. Finally, and to the best of our knowledge, the experimental ks value (0.62 cm s–1) of the 5,5′-DMDQ compound resulted in the largest value reported to date within the literature of organic redox flow battery electrolytes, which makes it an excellent candidate to store negative charge in a device.

    Chemicals

For each selected Λ0 data, a ks value is calculated from eq 2, which, in turn this value is used to extract its corresponding λt data from eq 4. Then, each resulted combination of λt and ks data is introduced into the DigiElch program to simulate voltammograms at different scan rates (from 0.1 to 10 V s–1). For each combination, a plot of simulated Epc – E0 variations vs log v is obtained to get working curves, where Epc data is dependent on each scan rate tested and E0 is approximated to the value detected at the lowest scan rate (in this case 0.1 V s–1) used. Now, experimental Epc – E0 (as they are obtained or in their dimensionless forms) variations for each studied compound vs log v are measured and plotted within simulated working curves (Figure 4). Each set of experimental data will fit to one of the curves or branches. The corresponding λt and ks values of each fitted curve are finally assigned to each experimentally studied compound.

    Conclusions

    An electrochemical analysis strategy based on the Marcus–Hush approximation is presented to analyze the kinetic component of organic redox flow battery (ORFB) electrolytes. The procedure was applied to a series of MV and DQ compound derivatives/aqueous media, which are promising negolyte candidates in ORFBs. For most of the studied compounds, consistent ks values were acquired and it is remarkable that the measure ks for 5,5′-DMDQ (0.62 cm s–1) is very high, which makes this molecule suitable for proofs into a RFB.
    Theoretically computed E0s are roughly consistent with the tendency of the experiments. Structural similarities between MV and DQ along with the noteworthy contrast in their adsorption in the electrode highlights the importance of the subtle differences they have in their electronic structure, as demonstrated by the interaction energies and delocalization of the charge density.
    On the other hand, delocalization of the charge density is consistent with the differences among ks values of 4,4′-DMDQ, 5,5′-DMDQ, and 6,6′-DMDQ species. Similarities between computed inner reorganization energies are in agreement with the closeness of the measured ks for MV and DQ derivatives, providing evidence on the reliability of the Marcus theory in elucidating the kinetic component of ORFB electrolytes.
