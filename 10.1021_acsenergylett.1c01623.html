    Abstract

    Many organic redox materials are chemically unstable and sparingly soluble in nonaqueous media. Additionally, the crossover of redox materials and the availability of limited membranes have restricted the examination of the long-term cyclability of these materials in nonaqueous redox flow batteries (RFBs). To overcome these limitations, we developed a new class of pyridinium-based negolytes. The π-conjugation structure of the pyridinium molecules was extended by introducing benzothiazole into the C4-position of pyridinium, which improved the stability of these molecules. Cationic ammonium functional groups at the N-substituent suppressed the crossover of the pyridinium negolytes through an anion exchange membrane. Furthermore, the solubility of the negolyte was increased up to ∼1 M in acetonitrile and 0.3–0.5 M with tetrabutylammonium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (TBATFSI) and acetonitrile. A 0.1 M solution of the dicationic benzothiazolylpyridinium exhibited 0.0083% capacity-fading rate per cycle in symmetric RFBs for 250 cycles and 0.08% in full RFBs comprising the ammonium-substituted ferrocene as a posolyte for 500 cycles.

    Redox flow batteries (RFBs) have emerged to store intermittent energy in an economical and safe manner. (1−3) Aqueous vanadium RFBs have been developed for grid-scale and widely used energy storage systems (ESSs). (4) However, to meet the increasing demands of high-energy-density and low-cost systems, evolutionary development is needed through the design of tunable redox substances, such as organic molecules and coordination complexes. (5−10) Chemical tailoring based on fundamental reactivity can systematically modulate the redox potential, solubility, and chemical stability of redox substances. In addition, various synthetic methods facilitate the reduction of the material and synthesis costs without relying on the limited elemental resources. However, optimization of molecular design is still challenging as highly stable redox materials are required for the long-term performance of RFBs.

    Nonaqueous RFBs can demonstrate high energy densities at high operating voltages. As nonaqueous media are stable at highly negative potentials as compared to aqueous solutions, (11) extendable cell voltage is responsible for the negative-side redox electrolyte, called a negolyte (or anolyte), and its redox potential. However, to date, only a few viable negolytes have been reported. Although phenanthrene-fused heteroarenes, diquat derivatives, N-methylphthalimide, and 9-fluorenone showed promising redox potentials at −1.5 to −2.0 V vs the ferrocene/ferrocenium couple (Fc/Fc+), (12−15) the performances of the corresponding RFBs were not stable (Table S1). Viologen entities have been widely used to realize multielectron transfer and reasonable stability of the RFB performance (Chart S1). (16−19) However, the first redox potential of these entities is approximately −0.69 V vs Fc/Fc+, which is relatively low as a negolyte. In addition, the second redox event is unstable and requires 400 mV of the total overpotential. Thus, an alternative approach is to use a single pyridinium core into which a stabilizing substituent is incorporated. Previously, pyridinium derivatives containing ester and acyl groups have been used to obtain the more negative redox potential between −0.8 and −1.3 V vs Fc/Fc+ (Chart S1). (20−22) However, these intensive studies revealed that the protonation of the carbonyl groups at negative potentials led to decomposition of the redox molecules and capacity fading in RFBs, highlighting the optimum design of substituents. The other technical difficulty is incomplete installation of the full RFBs comprising a negolyte and a posolyte (positive-side electrolyte or catholyte) because of their severe crossover through a porous membrane. Galvanostatic evaluations are often conducted using symmetric cells containing mixed electrolytes. (13,16,21−23) However, these approaches are unfeasible because of the double cost of the redox materials, limited solubility, and chemical instability of redox-induced molecules. Recently, the installation of long alkyl or polyethylene glycol chains to redox molecules enlarged the molecular sizes and suppressed the crossover in RFBs. (17−19) In addition, dimerized redox molecules were designed to increase the size while minimizing the net increase of the molecular weight. (24) Although these approaches addressed one or two of the critical issues, a combined approach to improve redox potential, solubility, and crossover has been still underexplored. The winning combination that can be simultaneously implemented for nonaqueous RFBs is highly desirable to promote both further modifications of established redox cores and streamlined molecular engineering of new redox-active compounds.

    We herein show a fundamental design principle for pyridinium-based negolytes. Introduction of a five-membered heteroaromatic ring as a neutral electron-withdrawing group into pyridinium imparted chemical stability to pyridinium (Scheme 1a), and the resulting pyridinium derivative exhibited a redox potential of −1.2 V vs Fc/Fc+ (Scheme 1b). (25) The solubility of pyridinium derivatives in acetonitrile (MeCN) was significantly increased up to ∼1.0 M by changing the counterion from tetrafluoroborate (BF4–) or hexafluorophosphate (PF6–) to bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (TFSI–). Moreover, the crossover issue was suppressed by incorporating the cationic 3-(trimethylammonio)propyl (TMAP) group into the pyridine ring via N-alkylation and employing an anion-exchange membrane (AEM) in the RFBs. The resulting pyridinium negolyte was assembled with the bisTMAP(BTMAP)-substituted ferrocene as a posolyte (BTMAP-Fc, Scheme 1c), and a capacity fading rate of 0.08% per cycle and a Coulombic efficiency (CE) of 99.80% were achieved during 500 cycles for the corresponding full RFBs.

    To increase the stability of the redox-active pyridinium core, we initially introduced a heterocycle substituent into the pyridinium core based on the bioisosteres strategy used in medicinal chemistry for successfully increasing the metabolic stability of drug candidates by replacing esters with heterocycles. (26) The incorporation of 1,3-azole heterocycle through its C2 position into the pyridinium core can extend the π-conjugation of the core and provide an electron-withdrawing effect owing to the imine-like C═N bond, thereby stabilizing the radical species generated by one-electron reduction (Scheme 1a). (27) Pd-catalyzed direct C–H arylation allowed rapid access to a variety of heteroarene-substituted pyridines in a systematic manner. (28,29) In addition, the N-substituent was readily introduced by N-alkylation, (30) and anion exchange was further performed when an alternative counterion was required. As a result, a series of heteroarylpyridiniums were prepared, and their preliminary cycling and solubility tests suggested that both 4-(benzo[d]thiazol-2-yl)-1-methylpyridin-1-ium (BTP+) and 4-(benzo[d]thiazole-2-yl)-1-(3-trimethylammonio)propyl)pyridin-1-ium (TMAP-BTP2+) are promising negolyte candidates (see details in Figures S1–S3 and Tables S2 and S3). In addition, the type of anion is a critical factor for the solubility of pyridinium derivatives. The as-synthesized pyridinium ions with the Br– counteranion did not dissolve in MeCN. When anion exchange was performed to afford the corresponding BF4–, PF6–, and TFSI– salts, the solubility of the pyridinium derivatives considerably increased (Figure S4). The replaced PF6– increased the solubility of the TMAP-BTP2+ to 0.25 M in MeCN and to 0.026 M in a 0.5 M tetrabutylammonium(TBA)PF6/MeCN electrolyte solution (Figure S5). Furthermore, TFSI– enhanced the dissolution of TMAP-BTP2+ up to 0.89 M in MeCN and 0.34 M in 0.5 M TBATFSI/MeCN (Figure S6). Similarly, the solubility of BTP+ was improved to 1.00 and 0.47 M using TFSI– (Figures S4, S7, and S8). The anion-dependent solubility may be attributed to the ion association strength and the size and hydrophobicity of anions. (2,31,32) However, considering the interactions of the anion with the solvent and cation, the principle of solubility should be more complex than the above-mentioned simple physicochemical property. (33,34) Via this anion-exchange method, the solubility of the pyridinium derivatives can be substantially increased without any structural alteration of the redox-active cores.

    Electrochemical characterizations of TMAP-BTP2+ were performed using cyclic voltammetry (CV). The CV curve of TMAP-BTP2+ demonstrated two redox waves at the formal potentials (Eo′) of −1.19 V and −1.87 V vs Fc/Fc+ and indicated diffusion-limited processes (Figure 1a–b and Table S3). The presence of the benzothiazole ring stabilized the redox-active pyridinium core during the first electron-transfer process. Although the second electron-transfer process possibly occurred via the delocalization of electron density to the benzothiazole ring (Figure S9), the CV curve revealed unstable profiles for 100 cycles (Figure 1a). Therefore, we focused only on the stable first process, and the cathodic/anodic event was designated as c1/a1, respectively. The diffusion coefficient (D) and electron-transfer constant (k°) of TMAP-BTP2+ were 1.91 × 10–6 cm2 s–1 and ∼0.1 cm s–1, respectively (Figures 1b,d and S10). The anodic-to-cathodic peak current ratio was close to unity for 100 cycles, and the peak-to-peak potential difference was maintained at 105 mV (Figure 1c). Furthermore, the reduced form of TMAP-BTP2+ (i.e., TMAP-BTP•+ as the distonic radical cation) remained stable for 4 days in the electrolyte solution (0.22% concentration decay per day, Figure S11). Density functional theory (DFT) calculations were conducted employing the B3LYP-D3/6-31G**/cc-pVTZ(-f) level of theory, (35) as implemented in the Jaguar software package, (36−40) to better understand the redox-properties of TMAP-BTP2+. The calculated redox potential for the first TMAP-BTP2+/•+ redox couple was −1.18 V vs Fc/Fc+ (Figure 1e and Table S4), in excellent agreement with the experimental results. The redox-active molecular orbitals (RAMO) of TMAP-BTP2+ and its reduced form illustrate that the resonance-based delocalization of excess electrons established through the π-accepting benzothiazole moiety lowers the singly occupied molecular orbital (SOMO) and the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energies during the reduction process. This electron delocalization enforces the planar molecular structure of the redox couple and inhibits any structural distortion from planarity, which significantly contributes to stabilizing the different redox states.

    Stability of TMAP-BTP2+/•+ was further analyzed using the galvanostatic mode in unbalanced compositionally symmetric RFBs. An AEM (FAPQ-275-PET, Fumasep) was applied to the RFB, which ideally interferes with the crossovers of TMAP-BTP2+ and TMAP-BTP•+ while enabling the entry and exit of anion and neutral molecules. The permeabilities of TMAP-BTP2+ and TMAP-BTP•+ through the AEM were slow (3.64 × 10–9 and 1.44 × 10–8 cm2 s–1, respectively, Figure S12). For symmetric RFB tests, we used a 1:1 TMAP-BTP2+ and TMAP-BTP•+ mixture, where the former was reduced on the negolyte side and the latter was oxidized on the posolyte side during the charging process (Figure 2a). The first charging capacity approached 2.314 Ah L–1, reflecting ∼86% utilization of the redox couple (Figure 2b). Galvanostatic cyclability of TMAP-BTP2+/•+ was excellent, demonstrating ∼90% capacity retention for over 250 cycles (total of 106.4 h of operation) and ∼99.85% CE at a current density of 18 mA cm–2 (equal to 4C rate) (Figure 2b–c). The estimated capacity-fading rate per cycle was ∼0.0083% for over 250 cycles, demonstrating insignificant chemical disproportionation and minimal undesired reactions, as confirmed by post-mortem 1H-nuclear magnetic resonance (1H NMR) spectroscopy (Figure S13). In addition to the stabilization of the pyridinyl radical by benzothiazole, the cationic TMAP groups repelled the redox molecules and prevented chemical reactions. (41)

    Encouraged by the considerable chemical stability of TMAP-BTP2+/•+, we evaluated full RFB performances. BTMAP-Fc2+ with 2TFSI–, designated as BTMAP-Fc (Scheme 1c, details in Figures S14–16 and Table S3), was employed as the posolyte, and TMAP-BTP2+ was used as the negolyte. Note that although the redox potential of BTMAP-Fc (−0.03 V vs Fc/Fc+) is not sufficiently high (Figure S15), BTMAP-Fc is one of a few posolytes possessing sufficient solubility and stability along with the crossover-suppression ability of the TMAP groups. The assembled full RFBs consisted of 0.1 M TMAP-BTP2+ negolyte and 0.15 M BTMAP-Fc posolyte in a 0.5 M TBATFSI/MeCN solution, which were separated through the AEM (Figures 3a and S17). TMAP-BTP2+ played a decisive role in the determination of the RFB performance with an excess of BTMAP-Fc. A working voltage of ∼1.16 V was employed, estimated from the CV profiles (Figure 3b). The AEMs were stable, as demonstrated by the constant area-specific resistance (ASR) of 16.5–17.0 Ω cm2 at open-circuit voltage (OCV), throughout the growing state of charge (SOC) (Figure 3d). The installed full RFBs delivered the first charging capacity of 2.50 Ah L–1, which was 93.28% of the theoretical capacity at a current density of 4.5 mA cm–2, and demonstrated a CE of >98.96% and energy efficiency (EE) of ∼86% (Figure 3c). Rate capability verified the stable RFB performance (Figure 3e). Notably, when the current rate was increased to 27 mA cm–2, the capacity decreased by only 14% as compared to that at 4.5 mA cm–2. Polarization curves indicated a maximum power density of 17.8 mW cm–2 at 90% SOC (Figure 3f). An ASR was 17.5–18.5 Ω cm2 in the polarization curves, where the resistance of the AEM accounted for ∼94% (Figure 3d). We note that although the ASR of the AEM in the nonaqueous medium is higher than that in the aqueous medium (<2.5 Ω cm2 in 0.5 M NaCl), (42) this resistance is comparable to that of a commercial porous membrane (Daramic, 5–23 Ω cm2) (14,15,22) and cross-linked microporous polymers (∼10 Ω cm2) (43) and substantially lower than that of the nanoporous aramid membrane (150–360 Ω cm2). (18)

    Long-term cyclability of the full RFBs was evaluated with 0.1 M TMAP-BTP2+ at a current density of 18 mA cm–2. After 100 and 500 cycles, 89.8 and 65.7% of the first charging capacity (2.57 Ah L–1) was maintained (Figure 4a) with the average CE exceeding 99.80% for 500 cycles (Figure S18). The capacity-fading rate was 0.08% per cycle for 500 cycles and 0.22% per hour from a total of 189 h (Figure 4b), offering an excellent result among previously reported negolytes in terms of long-term cyclability and high concentration level (Table S1). In addition, we did not observe any precipitation with the naked eye up to 0.3 M of TMAP-BTP2+, which was close to the maximum concentration (0.34 M, Figure S4) in the given electrolyte solutions (Figure S19). It demonstrated high solubility of the negolyte during galvanostatic cycles. Post-mortem analyses were conducted on both sides of the electrolyte solutions after 500 cycles with 0.1 M TMAP-BTP2+. 1H NMR spectrum of the negolyte-side solution exhibited a negligible side product related to TMAP-BTP2+ (Figure 4c), indicating the excellent chemical stability of TMAP-BTP2+. However, monotonous capacity fading over 500 cycles implied that a gradual crossover occurred throughout the long-term cycles. We found the signals of both TMAP-BTP2+ and BTMAP-Fc for both the negolyte- and posolyte-side solutions (Figure 4d). Consistently, CV analysis showed a c1 wave of TMAP-BTP2+ for both solutions; however, a ∼2.75 times higher current density was noticed on the negolyte side (Figure 4e–f). This suggests that although the TMAP entity suppresses the crossover, more optimizing design is needed to reduce the loss of TMAP-BTP2+ through the crossover (approximately 26.7% after 500 cycles) that is mainly attributed to the total capacity decay (∼34.3%).

    Nonetheless, the results with TMAP-BTP2+ represent significant prevention of the crossover as compared to the case of the neutral methyl group-loaded pyridinium ion BTP+ (Figures S11, S20, and 21 and Table S3). The permeabilities of BTP+ and the neutral pyridinyl radical BTP• through the AEM were 2.85 × 10–8 and 2.07 × 10–7 cm2 s–1, respectively (Figure S12), which are higher than those of TMAP-BTP2+ and TMAP-BTP•+ species. As expected, the full RFBs comprising the BTP+ negolyte and the BTMAP-Fc posolyte demonstrated rapid decay of capacity, approaching ∼60% of the initial capacity after 100 cycles. However, in the subsequent cycles, the capacity fading retarded, and ∼50% capacity was maintained after 220 and 335 cycles (Figure S22). Although the 1H NMR spectra indicated no side product formation after 335 cycles, we found a pronounced BTMAP-Fc signal in the spectrum of the negolyte-side solution and BTP+ peaks in the spectrum of the posolyte-side solution (Figure S23). CV curves exhibited a similar c1 intensity of BTP+ for both the negolyte- and posolyte-side solutions (Figure S22b), demonstrating capacity loss occurring until an equal concentration of BTP+ was present in the negolyte- and posolyte-side tanks. In contrast, the anodic CV wave of BTMAP-Fc was 2.3 times more intense for the posolyte-side solution than that for negolyte-side solution, confirming the advantages of the TMAP entity in conjunction with the AEM system. Accordingly, we expect further crossover mitigation achieved by the optimum design of charged functionalities and the development of membranes for nonaqueous media.

    We also demonstrated that the chemical stability of the pyridinium redox core was enhanced by benzothiazole as compared to that substituted by thiazole. The thiazolylpyridinium (TP+) rendering weaker π-conjugation showed an unstable CV profile (Figure S24). The 1:1 TP+/BTMAP-Fc mixture used for symmetric RFBs with a porous membrane resulted in a low capacity (<50%), poor CE (∼40%), and increased charging and discharging voltages for 50 cycles (Figure S25a,b). Undesired reactions produced electrochemically active species that were responsible for the low RFB performances. The symmetric RFBs containing BTP+/BTMAP-Fc delivered a constant discharging voltage and a higher CE (∼80%) under harsh conditions, demonstrating better chemical stability of BTP+ than TP+ (Figure S25c). However, capacity was only 50% of the theoretical one at the initial cycle and reduced to ∼42% at the 50th cycle. In stark contrast, mixed TMAP-BTP2+/BTMAP-Fc cells delivered 75–80% of the theoretical capacity and 85–90% CE, both of which were stable during 200 cycles (Figure S25d,e). These studies validated the chemical stability of TMAP-BTP2+ is superior to that of others by fulfilling π-conjugated structure and cationic functionality.

    In summary, we demonstrated three strategies for the development of stable organic redox molecules and evaluated their viability in nonaqueous full RFBs. When benzothiazole was introduced into the C4-position of pyridinium, it extended the π-conjugated structure and stabilized the pyridinyl radical states. To increase the solubility of organic redox molecules, we used TFSI– in MeCN and realized the solubility of benzothiazolylpyridinium up to 0.3–0.5 M with 0.5 M TBATFSI/MeCN, which was 5–13 times higher than the employment of PF6– and BF4– in MeCN. Incorporation of a positively charged alkyl group into the pyridinium core along with an AEM led to a capacity fading rate of ∼0.0083% per cycle for over 250 cycles in unbalanced compositionally symmetric RFBs. Integrated with ferrocene derivatives as the posolyte, the nonaqueous RFBs with 0.1 M dicationic benzothiazolylpyridinium negolyte delivered 89.8 and 65.7% capacity retention after 100 and 500 cycles, respectively. The cell cycling tests with almost the maximum concentration (0.3 M) of the negolyte also supported its high solubility during galvanostatic cycles. The heteroarylpyridinium scaffold and the combined strategies to address stability, solubility, and crossover issues will promote further development of nonaqueous organic RFBs.
