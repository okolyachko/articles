    Abstract

    Redox-enhanced electrochemical capacitors (Redox-ECs) in which electrons are stored and released by redox reactions of organic molecules either dissolved in an electrolyte or adsorbed on an electrode surface represent a promising energy storage system with electrochemical characteristics of both rechargeable batteries and electrical double-layer capacitors. However, the choices for redox-active molecules in Redox-ECs are often limited due to an irreversible nature induced by chemical processes, such as hydrolysis, coupled with e–-transfer reactions. Here, we describe the effects of nanoconfinement on enhanced reversibility in the redox reaction of an electroactive organic molecule undergoing irreversible hydrolysis after e–-transfer in a nanoporous carbon electrode. The redox reaction between hydrated rhodizonic acid (RDZ·2H2O) and hexahydroxybenzene (HHB) via tetrahydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone served as a model in which RDZ is irreversibly hydrolyzed to RDZ·2H2O. This phenomenon results from electrolysis acceleration within confined nanoregimes in a porous carbon matrix, which is analyzed by finite-element analysis. We built asymmetric ECs composed of nanoporous carbon electrodes, one of which was coated with RDZ·2H2O. Due to the enhanced reversibility of the RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction in a nanoporous carbon electrode, Coulombic efficiency of the cell remained near 90% despite the irreversible nature of RDZ via hydrolysis. This research provides fundamental insights into the use of organic molecules in energy storage using redox electrolytes such as Redox-ECs and organic redox flow batteries.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Electrochemical capacitors (ECs) offer higher power densities and longer charge/discharge cycles compared with various secondary batteries. (1−3) Carbon-based electrical double-layer capacitors (EDLCs) are the most common type of EC because of their relatively low cost and the relatively high electrical conductivity associated with their large surface areas. (4−6) However, EDLCs using activated carbon exhibit significantly lower energy densities compared with various types of rechargeable batteries because their charge storage is limited to the nonfaradaic process, which depends only on the surface area of the activated carbon. (7−10)
    The introduction of electroactive species to EDLCs can enhance their energy densities due to the partial contribution of the faradaic process through reversible redox reactions from the electroactive species. Transition metal oxides, (11,12) nitrides, (13,14) and conducting polymers (15,16) have been explored in the past, although they suffer from low cycling stability and conductivity, degradation of the composites, and high costs. (17−19)
    Recently, redox-active organic molecules, including 1,4-hydroquinone, anthraquinone, and its derivatives, have been considered promising candidates because of their low environmental impact, low costs, large gravimetric energy densities, and chemical tunabilities. (20−22) Despite these advantages, only a few redox molecules can be utilized in ECs because many of them undergo irreversible chemical processes during e–-transfers, which leads to poor Coulombic efficiency during charge–discharge cycles. (23−26) Improving the reversibility of redox molecules with multi-e– transfers, which have irreversible chemical reactions, could lead to development of ECs with high energy densities and low Coulombic losses.
    Nanoconfinement effects in porous electrodes have been studied extensively to understand electrochemical behaviors observed only in confined nanoregimes, which can be distinguished from those in open macroscale domains; (27−32) Li et al. reported a built-in catalytic process to accelerate lithium polysulfide conversion in confined nanoreactors, which are laterally stacked ordered pores encompassed by MoS2-coated carbon thin layers, (27) Liu et al. reported encapsulated stable iodine formation in microporous carbon by controlling solid–liquid conversion reactions, (28) Presser et al. reported facilitated electrochemical kinetics of I–/I2 (or I3–) redox reaction in a zinc iodide energy storage system by strong adsorption of iodide within nanoporous carbon, (29) and Cheng et al. reported the improvement of CO2 electrochemical reduction to CO using space confinement between gold or silver nanoparticles. (30)
    Chung et al. (33,34) suggested increasing the collision frequency of electroactive reactants onto the electrode surface within confined nano-pores to enhance the electrokinetics of nanoporous electrodes. If we consider a single nanopore in a porous electrode matrix a “nanosized electrochemical reactor (NER)”, the facilitated electrochemical reaction would eventually lead to electrolysis acceleration of electroactive species in NERs.
    Based on electrolysis acceleration in NERs, we expanded the nanoconfinement effect on the reversibility of an electrochemical reaction (E) coupled with an irreversible chemical reaction (Cirrev), abbreviated as ECirrev as follows: Ox + ne– ⇌ Red (E); Red ⇌ Product (Cirrev).
    Although most of Red from electroreduction of Ox would be converted to Product based on ECirrev, the electrolysis rate of Red back to Ox would be fast in NERs because of the increased electrokinetics, which would kinetically drive all Product to Red in Cirrev, eventually leading to enhanced reversibility of ECirrev.
    We confirmed this type of nanoconfinement effect through the electrochemical analyses of redox reactions among hydrated rhodizonic acid (RDZ·2H2O), tetrahydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone (THQ), and hexahydroxybenzene (HHB) within a microporous carbon (micro-C) electrode with an average pore radius of approximately 2 nm where THQ is electrochemically oxidized to RDZ, and RDZ is irreversibly hydrolyzed to RDZ·2H2O; (25,35) it was reported that the equilibrium constant for hydration of RDZ to RDZ·2H2O is ca. 1.3 × 104 in an aqueous solution. (36) We also describe an asymmetric EC composed of micro-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|micro-C with high Coulombic efficiency due to the nanoconfinement effect on enhanced reversibility of ECirrev. We believe this proof of concept can be applied to other redox molecules to develop ECs with high energy densities without a Coulombic loss.

    2.1. Preparation of Carbon Electrodes

    PG (pyrolytic graphite) sheets were purchased from PASONIC. A microporous carbon (AC8K30, Asahi Organic Chemicals Industry Co. Ltd., Japan) and ordered mesoporous carbon (UniAM Korea) were used to prepare porous carbon electrodes. Each porous carbon electrode was prepared by mixing porous carbon powder with a conductive additive (Super P, TIMCAL, Switzerland) and binder at a mass ratio of 8:1:1 in deionized water as a dispersion solvent. The binder was prepared by mixing sodium carboxymethyl cellulose (Sigma-Aldrich, C5678) and styrene-butadiene rubber (MTIKorea) at a mass ratio of 1:1 in deionized water. The slurry was stirred at room temperature for 24 h, casted on a stainless steel foil (20 μm thickness, Nilaco Corp.), which also served as a current collector, and then dried in a vacuum oven at 120 °C for 10 h. After drying, the coated foil was rolled-pressed and punched into a disk with its diameter of 14mm for use in a synchronous 2-3-electrode cells. Each electrode was manufactured with a 0.5 cm × 2 cm geometric electroactive surface area for the studies employing three-electrode configurations.

    2.2. Preparation of HHB- or RDZ-Adsorbed Carbon Electrodes

    HHB- or RDZ-adsorbed carbon electrodes were fabricated by drop-casting. Solutions of HHB (PH016112, Sigma-Aldrich) and RDZ·2H2O (rhodizonic acid dihydrate, Alfa Aesar, L12460, 98%) were prepared by dissolving organic molecules in deionized water. The solution of organic molecules was drop-cast on PG, mesoporous carbon, and microporous carbon electrodes, which were dried at 60 °C for 1 h.

    2.3. Electrochemical Measurements

    Electrochemical experiments were performed on either a VSP potentiostat/galvanostat (BioLogic Science Instruments, France) or a CHI-600e potentiostat (CH Instruments, Austin, TX). For three-electrode electrochemical cell experiments, a platinum wire and Ag/AgCl (1 M KCl) were used as counter and reference electrodes, respectively, as purchased from CH Instruments. For a working electrode, a glassy carbon macrodisk electrode with a radius of 1.5 mm (CH Instruments, USA), PG sheet, mesoporous carbon, and microporous carbon electrodes with/without adsorbed HHB were used. For two-electrode and synchronous 2–3–electrode electrochemical cell experiments, a positive electrode and negative electrode were assembled face-to-face at a polypropylene membrane with a pore size of ∼64 nm (Celgard 3501, polypore) as the separator in a SB9-S cell (BioLogic Science Instruments, France). In a synchronous 2–3-electrode electrochemical cell, Ag/AgCl (saturated KCl, BioLogic Science Instruments, France) was used as the reference electrode. In all experiments, 1 M H2SO4 was used as a supporting electrolyte. Finite-element analyses for the explanation of electrochemical results were performed using either DigiElch-professional v6. F (ElchSoft.com) or COMSOL Multiphysics v.5.3a (COMSOL, Inc., Burlington, MA).

    2.4. Physical Characterization of Electrode Materials

    The morphologies of the carbon powders and electrode were recorded by field emission-scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM, Hitachi S-4800, Verios G4 UC). The microstructures of the microporous and mesoporous carbon powders were observed by transmission electron microscopy with 300 kV (TEM, Tecnai G2 F30 S-Twin, FEI Company and JEM-2100F, JEOL Ltd.). Nitrogen adsorption/desorption isotherms were measured at 78 K using a BELSORP-max instrument (BEL JAPAN Inc.) XPS was performed with a K-alpha (Thermo Scientific) device with a monochromatized Al Kα X-ray source (1486.6 eV).

    3.3. Experimental Approach: Electrolysis Acceleration of RDZ·2H2O in Micro- and Mesoporous Carbon Electrodes

    To experimentally verify the enhanced reversibility of both RDZ·2H2O/HHB and CHH·8H2O/HHB in a nanoporous electrode, we chose two types of nanoporous carbons as electrode materials. For comparison purposes, we also investigated the reversibility of those redox reactions on a nonporous carbon electrode material, in this case a pore-free pyrolytic graphite (PG) sheet.
    The morphologies of the two types of nanoporous carbon materials are shown in Figure S4a,b. The microporous carbon (micro-C) in our experiment is spherical with an average particle radius of 5 ± 3 μm (Figure S4a), while a mesoporous carbon (meso-C) exhibits a worm-like structure (Figure S4b); a scanning electron microscope image of PG shown in Figure S4c appears to have a flat surface. The microstructures of micro-C and meso-C were also observed by TEM and are shown in Figure 4a,b for micro-C and Figure 4c,d for meso-C, respectively. The high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) image of micro-C in Figure 4b clearly shows randomly distributed micropores. The typical stripe-like and hexagonally arranged TEM images are observed in meso-C and are shown in Figure 4c. From the higher magnification image in Figure 4d, the array of mesopores with the hexagonal structures marked as yellow circles is clearly shown with their average diameter is approximately estimated as 4 nm, which is in good agreement with the pore size obtained using the N2 sorption measurement discussed below.
    The N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms for micro- and meso-C are shown in Figure 5a,b, and the amount of nitrogen adsorbed onto them was used to calculate the specific surface area by the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller method. The amounts were estimated as 3634 and 970 m2 g–1 for micro- and meso-C, respectively. The pore-size distributions of micro- and meso-C are shown in Figure 5c,d, and the average pore diameters of the micro-C and meso-C were 2.1 and 3.8 nm, respectively.
    We also investigated the wettability of micro- and meso-C by an aqueous solution with 1 M H2SO4 and is shown in Figure S5, which is one of important properties as an electrode material for an electrode in an energy storage system. (40,41) The measured contact angle on micro-C was 78.0°, while that on meso-C was 99.6°, which would indicate that the aqueous electrolyte would be more permeable to micro-C than meso-C.
    Figure 6a,b shows CVs measured in a 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution on either a micro-C (Figure 6a) or a meso-C (Figure 6b) electrode; preparation techniques for micro- and meso-C electrodes are described in the Experimental Section. The CVs exhibited typical rectangular shapes, which are associated with a primarily capacitive characteristic from the corresponding electrodes. Small, broad, and paired peaks in the forward and reverse scans of the CVs were evident at 0.2–0.4 V, which can be attributed to a redox reaction of possible quinoids on the surface of a micro- and meso-C. (42−45) The evidence was found through X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy of C1s, as in Figure S6, which shows that both the micro- and meso-C were functionalized with C–O (46) and C═O. (47)
    Next, we generated CV scans using a micro-C electrode as the working electrode in a three-electrode cell containing a 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution with 5 mM HHB; corresponding CVs are shown in Figure 6c. The shape of the voltammetric peaks was close to that associated with an electro-oxidation/reduction of a surface-bound electroactive species, although HHB was not adsorbed on a micro-C but only dissolved in the solution, which is typical in a thin-layer electrochemical cell in which electroactive species are completely and rapidly electrolyzed due to the small volume of an electrolyte solution. (48−50) This indicates that HHB permeated into a micro-C network and was electrolyzed in each pore with a small volume, resulting in thin-layer voltammetric characteristics.
    The first redox-paired peaks attributed to the THQ/HHB redox reaction displayed completely reversible behavior (indicated by the red line in Figure 6c), as expected from the CV on a GC macrodisk electrode in Figure 1a. Because the peak was only attributed to electrolysis of HHB to THQ and vice versa in pores of micro-C, we estimated the reversibility of the THQ/HHB redox reaction quantitatively by measuring the charge for electroreduction of THQ, Qred over that for electro-oxidation of HHB, Qox, Qred/Qox for THQ/HHB, which is estimated at ∼1; the integrated area for the estimation of Qred and Qox from the CV is shown in Figure S7a.
    As dissolved HHB was further oxidized to RDZ·2H2O via THQ on a GC macrodisk electrode, the reduction peak from electroreduction of RDZ·2H2O to THQ was almost absent because of the irreversible hydrolysis of RDZ to RDZ·2H2O coupled with electro-oxidation of THQ. This irreversible voltammetric behavior was also clearly observed on a nonporous PG electrode, as shown in Figure S8a. However, from a micro-C electrode, the reduction peak from RDZ·2H2O to THQ was partially evident (green line in Figure 6c), indicating that the dehydrolysis of RDZ·2H2O to RDZ during its electroreduction in micropores of micro-C would be kinetically accelerated. When the potential was even more negatively swept to −0.2 V, at which point THQ was eventually electrolyzed to HHB, Qred/Qox for the overall RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction remained at ∼1, implying complete reversibility in a micro-C electrode at the given voltammetric time scale. The integrated area for the Qred and Qox estimates from the CV is shown in Figure S7b.
    When HHB was adsorbed on a micro-C, Qred/Qox for the overall RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction was estimated at ∼1 (see Figure S7c for the integrated area), which also indicates almost complete reversibility (Figure 6e). In addition, the reduction peak associated with RDZ·2H2O to THQ was more clearly observed than that measured in the solution containing dissolved HHB likely because of the decreased over-potential for RDZ·2H2O reduction to THQ; we discuss the reason below. The observed reversibility enhancement of RDZ·2H2O/HHB on a micro-C electrode is not attributable primarily to an interaction between a micro-C and HHB but originated with the accelerated electrolysis rate in pores of a micro-C electrode, supporting the theoretical expectations set out in the previous session. For verification, HHB was also adsorbed on a nonporous PG electrode. When adsorbed HHB was oxidized to RDZ·2H2O at the forward scan of the corresponding CV in Figure S8b, the paired reduction peak at the reverse scan was absent.
    We also investigated the further oxidation of RDZ·2H2O to CHH·8H2O and the reversibility of the overall CHH·8H2O/HHB redox reaction in a 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution in which 5 mM of HHB was dissolved, as shown in the CV (blue line) of Figure 6c. The sequential reduction peaks in the reverse scan of the CV for the CHH·8H2O/RDZ·2H2O/THQ redox reactions were absent, and the magnitude of the peak associated with electroreduction of THQ to HHB was smaller than those seen when HHB was electro-oxidized to either THQ or RDZ·2H2O. Based on the theoretical analyses shown in Figure 2, the reversibility would not be enhanced dramatically likely because of a larger forward kinetic constant for the hydrolysis of CHH·2H2O than that of RDZ.
    The CV behaviors attributed to the CHH·8H2O/RDZ·2H2O/THQ/HHB redox reactions on a meso-C electrode in a sulfuric acid solution containing dissolved HHB were similar to those on a micro-C, as shown in Figure 6d. However, the voltammetric behavior of the RDZ·2H2O/THQ/HHB redox reactions on a HHB-adsorbed meso-C electrode (Figure 6f) was somewhat different from that on a HHB-adsorbed micro-C (Figure 6e). At first, the current density associated with the faradaic processes on micro-C is larger than those on meso-C, which would be mainly due to the large surface area per mass of micro-C than that of meso-C. Second, the potential difference between the anodic and cathodic peaks attributed to the THQ/HHB redox reaction adsorbed on micro-C is 0.20 V, which is 0.05 V larger than that on meso-C. We consider that this would be mainly because of a higher internal resistance for ion transfers into/from a microporous network than that into/from a mesoporous one, (51) resulting in more voltammetric distortion driven by an iR drop.
    Third, the paired peak associated with electroreduction of RDZ·2H2O to THQ at the reverse scan was not observed on the meso-C electrode, which was shown at approximately 0.2 V on the micro-C. Nonetheless, Qred/Qox for the overall RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction was close to 1. These observations indicate that the kinetics of RDZ·2H2O reduction to THQ, which is adsorbed on meso-C, is more sluggish than that adsorbed on micro-C. To distinguish the kinetics for the electrolysis of RDZ·2H2O to HHB adsorbed on micro-C from those on meso-C, finite-element analysis was performed based on the introduced simulation model with reactions 12 and 13. Figure S9a shows the normalized current versus t curves as rpore values are 1.072 and 1.9 nm, which are the average radii of micro-C and meso-C at kf = 1 and 5000 s–1, and a constant kb of 1 s–1 in reaction 13. All the results were identical, indicating that neither rpore nor kf affects telectrolysis. However, we found kb to be the main factor responsible for changes in telectrolysis in the pores of micro- and meso-C, as shown in Figure S9b, and this theoretical data indicate that the kb value of reaction 13 was smaller for meso-C than for micro-C, which resulted in longer telectrolysis. Based on the theoretical analyses, the varying reversibility of the RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction for micro- and meso-C originated from the faster dehydrolysis rate from RDZ·2H2O to RDZ on micro-C than on meso-C. Grepioni et al. (52) reported a crystalline RDZ·2H2O structure, indicating that RDZ·2H2O should form an ordered packing structure on an electrode surface. We assumed that an ordered orientation of RDZ·2H2O would become more difficult in a micro-C pore, driving the instability of RDZ·2H2O compared with that of meso-C.
    Lastly, we also investigate possible desorption of HHB from micro-C during its redox-cycling process. At first, cyclic voltammetry was successively performed on a HHB adsorbed micro-C electrode in a conventional three-electrode cell containing ca. 10 mL of an aqueous solution with 1 M H2SO4, which is the same condition as for the experiment in Figure 6e. The successive CV in Figure S10a shows the dramatic decrease of the peak currents as potential cycling was proceeded, which would be indicative of a continuous loss of adsorbed HHB from an electrode surface during its redox cycles. As the control experiment, a successive cyclic voltammetry was also performed in a compact electrochemical cell composed of micro-C coated with HHB|1 M H2SO4|micro-C, which contain only less than 300 μL of an aqueous electrolyte. The corresponding CV in Figure S10b shows almost steady-state voltammograms over the potential cycling, which would be distinctively different from that in the electrochemical cell with a large volume of the electrolyte. This observation indicates that adsorbed HHB on a micro-C would be significantly more stabilized during its redox cycling process in the compact cell, which would be eventually advantageous to its potential application to a redox-active electrochemical capacitor described in the next section.

    3.4. Electrochemical Asymmetric Capacitors Composed of Micro-C Coated with RDZ·2H2O|micro-C

    A micro-C electrode can greatly increase the electrolysis rate of RDZ·2H2O to HHB, leading to enhanced reversibility for the RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction. We then extended our fundamental understanding demonstrating an asymmetric electrochemical capacitor composed of micro-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|micro-C.
    Figure 7a shows the potential profiles measured in synchronous 2–3-electrode micro-C|1 M H2SO4|micro-C. While the cell potential was polarized from 0 to 1.0 V and vice versa, the two potential profiles from each half-cells were symmetric because electrochemical behavior on both the positive and negative electrodes was characterized primarily by nonfaradaic charge accumulation. However, in the potential profiles of the half-cells from micro-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|micro-C, as shown in Figure 7b, the potential of the positive electrode linearly increased or decreased as a function of time while that of the negative electrode displayed potential plateaus, indicating significant faradaic processes associated with electroreduction of RDZ·2H2O to HHB via THQ and vice versa.
    Based on the obtained potential profiles from the two types of electrochemical cells, the estimated specific capacitances of the corresponding negative (Cspec,–) and positive (Cspec,+) electrodes and the full cell (Cspec,cell) are shown in Figure 7e; detailed procedures for the calculation of Cspec are described in the Supporting Information. In the symmetric electrochemical cell (micro-C|1 M H2SO4|micro-C), Cspec,+ was almost the same as Cspec,– during the charging and discharging processes: Cspec,+ = 238.9 and Cspec,– = 251.7 F g–1 in the charge and Cspec,+ = 230.1 and Cspec,– = 242.4 F g–1 in the discharge at 0.5 A g–1, respectively. The Coulombic efficiency of the cell (Qeff,cell = Cspec,cell in discharge/Cspec,cell in charge × 100) was high (96.3%) because Cspec from both the positive and negative electrodes was mainly associated with electrical charge accumulation in a double-layer region.
    In the asymmetric electrochemical cell (micro-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|micro-C), Cspec,+ (290.6 F/g in the charge and 261.2 F/g in the discharge) exhibited specific capacitance similar to that of a symmetric electrochemical cell. However, Cspec,– (606.4 F/g in the charge and 545.0 F/g in the discharge) was almost 2 times higher than Cspec,– of a symmetric electrochemical cell during charge/discharge processes due to the faradaic process by the overall RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction via THQ. Slight losses in Coulombic efficiency on both negative and positive electrodes can be seen in Figure 7e. We anticipated that the main cause was the self-discharge due to the cross-over of O2 through a porous separator, as generated on the positive electrode during the charging process. In Figure S11, which shows the CVs on the micro- and meso-C electrodes measured in 1 M H2SO4 solution, an irreversible oxygen evolution reaction (OER) begins at approximately 0.7 V (vs Ag/AgCl) on both the micro- and meso-C electrodes. In the micro-C electrode coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|micro-C, the electrochemical potential for the positive electrode was polarized to 0.8 V (vs Ag/AgCl) and is shown in Figure 7b, which was enough to drive OER. (53,54) Nonetheless, the Coulombic efficiency of the cell was limited to 90%. Lastly, Cspec,cell values from the two types of electrochemical cells were compared. Cspec,cell from the asymmetric micro-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|micro-C was 83.4 F/g in the discharge, which was 1.4 times higher than that from the symmetric micro-C|1 M H2SO4|micro-C cell (59.0 F g–1 in the discharge), which is largely the result of significant contributions of the faradaic RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reactions to Cspec,–.
    For comparison purposes, we also assessed the electrochemical cell composed of meso-C|1 M H2SO4|meso-C and meso-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|meso-C. The E–t curve behavior at 0.5 A g–1 from the symmetric electrochemical cell composed of meso-C (Figure 7c) was almost analogous to that from micro-C (Figure 7a). However, the E–t characteristics from meso-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|meso-C shown in Figure 7d are clearly different compared to those from the asymmetric cell composed of micro-C. During the charge process, the electrochemical potential on the negative electrode decreased rapidly over a shorter period to maintain the potential plateau region, resulting in a larger overpotential for the electrolysis of RDZ·2H2O to HHB in a pore of meso-C (Figure 6f). From the E–t curve on the positive electrode, two potential plateaus from faradaic oxidation reactions were observed at approximately 0.35 and 0.5 V (vs Ag/AgCl). Based on the CVs in Figure 6b,d, the first and second plateaus would be originated from electro-oxidation of quinoids on a meso-C and RDZ·2H2O to CHH·8H2O, respectively. This result strongly indicates that RDZ·2H2O would not be easily confined in a pore of meso-C, leading to cross-over from the negative to the positive electrode side. Due to the observed self-discharge effect by RDZ·2H2O in addition to O2 in the asymmetric electrochemical cell composed of meso-C, the cell potential drops rapidly during discharge, and Figure 7f shows the severe Qeff loss of this cell: Qeff,cell = 48.8% (Qeff,cell of meso-C|1 M H2SO4|meso-C = 97.8%).
    The self-discharge occurring in the asymmetric electrochemical cells composed of micro- and meso-C was more investigated because of its importance in the energy storage devices. (55)Figure S12 shows the cell voltage versus time for an open circuit, toc curves measured from the two different asymmetric electrochemical cells after a constant 0.5 A/g was applied to render the cell voltage of 1 V in each of the electrochemical cells for the charging process. During the open-circuit mode after the charging in micro-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|micro-C, the cell voltage was significantly decreased from 1 to 0.38 V as toc became 1 h due to the self-discharge process, which would be mainly associated with cross-over of O2 generated in the matrix of the positive electrode during the charging process. The initial self-discharge rate in this electrochemical cell could be expressed as −2 mV/s. The self-discharge occurring in the asymmetric cell composed of meso-C was more severe than that in micro-C. The cell voltage dropped to 0.06 V at toc = 1 h, which reaches almost equilibrium of the net redox reaction in the electrochemical cell. In addition, the initial self-discharge rate is −6 mV/s, which is 3 times higher than that shown in the asymmetric cell composed of micro-C. This observation could strongly support that the self-discharge in meso-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|meso-C is associated with cross-over of not only O2 but also RDZ·2H2O, which is desorbed from meso-C of the negative electrode.
    Figure 8a shows Cspec,cell during both charge and discharge in both the symmetric and asymmetric electrochemical cells composed of micro-C as a function of an applied current density. As the current density increased from 0.5 to 4 A g–1, Cspec,cell during both the charge and discharge processes decreased because of the larger ohmic drop induced by the faradaic process in the cell. Still, approximately 74 and 81% of Cspec,cell in the charge and discharge were retained, which was 1.34 times higher than that of Cspec,cell from the symmetric cell composed of micro-C at 4 A g–1. Also, Qeff from the asymmetric cell achieved more than 98% of the given range of the current density, which was comparable to the symmetric cell. However, Cspec,cell from the asymmetric cell composed of meso-C during the charge process showed large differences from those during the discharge when the applied current density was less than 2 A g–1 (Figure 8b), resulting primarily from severe self-discharge due to the cross-over of RDZ·2H2O and O2. The corresponding Qeff was therefore low compared with the values obtained from the symmetric cell composed of meso-C.
    The cycle stability of micro-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|micro-C was confirmed, as shown in Figure S13. Over 2000 cycles, Cspec,cell decreased only from 66.3 to 56.5 F g–1 at 0.1 V s–1, which show approximately 87% Cspec,cell retention. This result suggests that only small decomposition or side reactions will occur in an asymmetric cell composed of micro-C during charge/discharge cycles.

    4. Conclusions

    We addressed the nanoconfinement effect for enhancing reversibility of the HHB/RDZ·2H2O redox reaction using a nanoporous carbon electrode. On either a flat GC macrodisk or nonporous PG electrode, the THQ/HHB redox reaction exhibited reversible voltammetric behavior, while further RDZ·2H2O/THQ and CHH·8H2O/RDZ·2H2O redox reactions produced irreversible characteristics mainly due to the coupled proceeding irreversible hydrolysis processes after the electro-oxidation of THQ to RDZ and RDZ·2H2O to CHH·2H2O.
    However, on a micro-C electrode with an average rpore of 1.072 nm, the RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction became fully reversible, and we found this effect could be attributed to the enhanced electrolysis rate of RDZ·2H2O to HHB, which is supported by finite-element analyses. Meso-C with an average rpore = 1.9 nm also showed enhanced reversibility of the RDZ·2H2O/HHB redox reaction compared with a nonporous PG electrode. However, it exhibited the slower reduction kinetics of RDZ·2H2O to HHB rather than those of micro-C, which originated from instability of oriented RDZ·2H2O in a pore of micro-C.
    Next, we built asymmetric ECs composed of micro-C using HHB/RDZ·2H2O as the half-redox reaction, which had been considered an inappropriate choice for a rechargeable energy storage device due to irreversibility induced by the hydrolysis of RDZ to RDZ·2H2O during electro-oxidation of HHB. However, asymmetric micro-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|, micro-C exhibited a relatively high Cspec,cell of 83.4 F g–1and a Qeff of 89.9% because of electrolysis acceleration of RDZ·2H2O to HHB within the nanopores of micro-C. Meso-C coated with RDZ·2H2O|1 M H2SO4|meso-C achieved only 48.8% of Qeff, as a result of severe self-discharge due to the cross-over of RDZ·2H2O.
    We believe our findings for electrolysis acceleration in a nanoporous electrode can open new possibilities for the access to the redox-active electrolytes in either redox flow batteries and Redox-ECs, which have been limited due to their poor reversibility resulting from coupled irreversible chemical processes (such as hydrolysis) despite their multi-e– transfer characteristics.
