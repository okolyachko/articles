    Abstract

    Non-aqueous all organic redox flow batteries (NORFBs) are one of the promising options for large-scale renewable energy storage systems owing to their scalability with energy and power along with the affordability. The discovery of new redox-active organic molecules (ROMs) for the anolyte/catholyte would bring them one step closer to the practical application, thus it is highly demanded. Here, we report a new class of ROMs based on cationic triazenyl systems supported by N-heterocyclic carbenes (NHCs) and demonstrate, for the first time, that the triazenyl can serve as a new redox motif for ROMs and could be significantly stabilized for the use in NORFBs by the coupling with NHCs even at radical states. A series of NHC–triazenyl ROM families were successfully synthesized via the reaction of a synthon, N-heterocyclic carbene azido cation, with various Lewis bases including NHCs. Remarkably, it is revealed that NHCs substituted on the triazenyl fragments can serve as a versatile platform for tailoring the electrochemical activity and stability of triazenyl-based compounds, introducing various ROMs exploiting triazenyl redox motif, as demonstrated in the full cell of NORFBs for an anolyte.

    1. Introduction

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    Zero-carbon energy sources including solar and wind have emerged as privilege energy sources in recent years with the increase in environmental issues. (1−5) Due to the fluctuation of the energy production from these renewable energy sources, paring with energy storage systems (ESSs) is inevitable. (3,6−8) Among the several ESSs, redox flow batteries (RFBs) have been addressed as one of the most suitable ESSs because they offer benefits of scalability, energy efficiency, design flexibility, and decoupled energy and power. (9−15) As the conventional RFBs, which employ redox reactions of metal-based ions/molecules, cause environmental concerns and cost, trends in RFBs are moving forward to the sustainable and environmentally benign organic RFBs (ORFBs) utilizing redox-active organic molecules (ROMs). (2,16−22) In particular, non-aqueous all organic RFBs (NORFBs) would enable a higher working voltage than the conventional aqueous RFBs, which are limited by water electrolysis. (2,18,23−25) Since ROMs play a crucial role in the performance of NORFBs, the development of ROMs possessing properties of low (anolytes) or high (catholytes) redox potentials along with high solubility and persistent redox cycling in organic solvents is highly desirable. (3,19) During the past decade, a growing number of reports demonstrating new types of ROMs has been published, which could aid in the substantial improvement of the performance and reliability of NORFBs (Scheme 1). (14,26−42)
    Many of the reported ROMs are based on a few commonly known redox motifs such as pyridinium salt and quinone as illustrated in Scheme 1, and their derivatives modified by substitutions or functionalization have been also subsequently studied as new ROMs. (28−30,43−45) Discovery of a new redox-active motif, in this respect, can significantly expand the existing candidacy of ROMs by bringing a new redox-active family with potentially various properties in NORFBs. Herein, we introduce a redox-active triazenyl motif as a new potential redox center in designing novel ROMs. Triazenyl compounds have been often utilized in the pharmaceutical field based on their outstanding pharmacokinetic, antitumor, and mutagenic properties, taking advantage of unique reactivities of the triazenyl motif. (46) DNA alkylating agents such as dacarbazine (DTIC), temozolomide (TMZ), exploiting the triazenyl group as an active site, were actively employed in the treatment of neoplastic diseases. (46) Furthermore, a triazenyl moiety is an efficient directing group enabling various transformations in organic synthesis including halogen exchange, direct C–H cross coupling, and C–H annulation. (47) Although triazenyl types have been widely used in organic and clinical studies, the application of triazenyl compounds in electrochemical systems has been rarely investigated so far because the radical form of triazenyl compounds is critically unstable itself and could result in uncontrollable side reactions. Recently, our group reported stable and redox active triazenyl cations supported by two identical N-heterocyclic carbenes (NHCs). (48) These bis(NHC) triazenyl cations ([NHC2N3]+) were one-electron reduced with potassium metal, producing the first NHC-stabilized triazenyl radicals (NHC2N3). This previous work motivated us to explore the potential applicability of cationic NHC–triazenyls as an active component in the electrochemical system particularly for NORFBs because of not only redox-active properties but also high stability of corresponding radicals. In the following, we present a novel series of redox-active triazenyl cations supported by various NHCs, and their electrochemical properties are demonstrated as active ROMs in an anolyte in a full cell of NORFBs with 10-[2-(2-methoxyethoxy)ethyl]-10H-phenothiazine (MEEPT) as a catholyte.

    2. Results and Discussion

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    A unique mono(NHC)-stabilized triatomic nitrogen molecule, N-heterocyclic carbene azido cation, (1,3-bis(2,6-diisopropylphenyl)-1,3-dihydro-2H-imidazol-2-ylidene)triaz-1-yn-2-ium chloride (IPrN3Cl, 1) was used as a versatile synthon for the preparation of redox-active NHC–triazenyl systems. Compound 1 was prepared in a 25% yield from 1,3-bis(2,6-diisopropylphenyl)- 2-chloroimidazolium chloride (IPrCl2) with 1.0 equiv. of sodium azide (NaN3) in dry acetonitrile (MeCN) at room temperature (Figure 1a). The molecular structure of 1 was analyzed by X-ray crystallography (Figure 1b). The angle of N3–N4–N5 was measured to be 168.2(1)°, which was slightly different from the expected 180°. The bond lengths of N3–N4 and N4–N5 were 1.253(2) and 1.121(2) Å, respectively, which were in good agreement with the optimized structure at the B3PW91/6-31G(d,p) level using density functional theory (DFT) (Table S7). As the computed LUMO of 1 is a π*-type orbital located on the azido moiety (N3–N4–N5), terminal nitrogen N5 was anticipated to act as an electron acceptor site when reacted with sterically hindered electron donors. (Figure 1c). In this reason, various NHCs are expected to coordinate to 1 through N5, which can easily generate diverse structures of unsymmetrical NHC–triazenyl cations ([IPr-N3-NHC]+). This synthetic route will allow us to overcome the bottleneck of the previous method, which was only accessible to the symmetrical triazenyl compounds (NHC2N3). Consequently, 1 was treated with several NHCs including 1,3- bis(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)imidazol-2-ylidene (IMes), 1,3-bis(2,6-diisopropylphenyl)imidazol-2-ylidene (IPr), 1,3-(dimethyl)imidazol-2-ylidene), and 1-[(2,6-diisopropyl)phenyl]-3,3,5,5-tetramethyl-2-pyrrolidinylidene (CAAC). In addition, a non-NHC electron donor, triphenylphosphine (PPh3), was used in order to investigate the effect of NHCs on the electrochemical properties of triazenyl systems.
    When 1 was reacted with 1.3 equiv. of IMes and 1.1 equiv. of IPr in tetrahydrofuran (THF) at room temperature, IMes and IPr were indeed coordinated to the terminal nitrogen (N5) of the azido moiety (N3–N4–N5) affording [IPrN3IMes]Cl (2) in a 48% yield and [IPr2N3]Cl (3) in a 36% yield, respectively (Scheme 2). The molecular structure of 2 was confirmed by X-ray single crystallography and showed a zigzag-shaped C1–N3–N4–N5–C2 moiety with all bond lengths indicating a bond order of 1.5 (Figure 2a). Compound 3 was previously synthesized from the different reactions of IPrCl2 with trimethylsilyl azide and fully characterized by our group. Proton NMR spectroscopy of 3 was well matched with the reported data (Figure S1). In the case of IMe, which is the least sterically hindered NHC, 1,3-dimethylimidazolium-2-carboxylate (IMeCO2) was reacted with 1 as an IMe carbene fragment transfer reagent instead of isolated IMe due to the instability of IMe itself. (49,50) The reaction of 1 with 1.1 equiv. of IMeCO2 in MeCN produced desired [IPrN3IMe]Cl (4) with a 34% isolated yield. The structure of 4 was determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction and displayed the similar W-shaped structure with 2 and 3 (Figure 2b). Moreover, three compounds presented the almost identical bond lengths of the C1–N3–N4–N5–C2 moiety (Table S13). The reaction of 1 with in situ-generated CAAC from its precursor, 1-(2,6-diisopropylphenyl)-2,2,4,4-tetramethyl-3,4-dihydro-2H-pyrrolium triflate, in THF at room temperature yielded a predictable CAAC-coordinated triazenyl cation, [IPrN3CAAC]OTf (5) along with unexpected 6, which was formed from further reaction of 5 with CAAC (Scheme 2). Due to the small singlet–triplet gap and the strong electrophilicity of CAACs, CAACs are well known to activate C–H bonds. (51) The isolated yield of 5 and 6 were 7 and 24%, respectively. At a lower reaction temperature, the formation of 6 was slightly inhibited as the yield of 5 was improved to 12%, and the yield of 6 was reduced to 19% (Figure S2). Molecular structures of 5 and 6 were confirmed by single crystal X-ray analysis (Figure 2c,d). Unlike other NHC-bonded triazenyl cations, single crystal structures of 5 and 6 showed the exceptional structure that the imidazole ring of IPr, pyrrole ring of CAAC, and the azido moieties are in the same plane. The bond length of C4–C5 in 6 was 1.520(3) Å, which is consistent with the typical C–C single bond. The reaction between 1 and 1.0 equiv. of PPh3 in dichloromethane (DCM) at room temperature also furnished a PPh3-bound triazenyl cation, [IPrN3PPh3]Cl (7) in a 67% yield, and the structure was confirmed by single crystal X-ray diffraction (Scheme 2 and Figure 2e). Compound 7 showed a singlet in the 31P{1H} (52) NMR resonance at 24.7 ppm, which was downfield shifted from that of PPh3. This deshielding indicates σ donation of lone-paired electrons on phosphine to electron acceptor 1. The molecular structures of all newly prepared triazenyl cations were reproduced well by the DFT calculations at the B3PW91/6-31G(d,p) level (Tables S8–S12).
    The electrochemical properties of as-prepared triazenyl species were initially characterized by cyclic voltammetry in THF solution containing 0.1 M NBu4PF6 as the electrolyte. Figure 3 presents that all triazenyl cations supported by two NHCs could exhibit reversible one-electron reductions. On the contrary, 7 containing non-NHC, PPh3, showed an irreversible reduction cycle with a cathodic peak at −1.29 V versus sat. Ag/AgCl. It indicates that the introduction of NHCs to the organic molecules could aid in obtaining a stable redox cycle, which is attributed to the π accepting ability of NHCs that delocalizes the radical’s spin density onto the carbene moiety and leads to the lifetime elongation of unstable organic radicals formed during redox cycling. (53−68) The remarkably strong π-accepting ability of CAAC in comparison with other NHCs resulted in the higher redox potential of 5 and 6 (at E1/2 = −0.85 V and E1/2 = −0.84 V versus sat. Ag/AgCl, respectively) than that of 2 (at E1/2 = −1.24 V versus sat. Ag/AgCl), 3 (at E1/2 = −1.16 V versus sat. Ag/AgCl), and 4 (at E1/2 = −1.26 V versus sat. Ag/AgCl). (69−71) The almost identical redox potential of 5 and 6 along with the similar UV–vis absorption peak of 5 and 6 suggests that the substitution at the NHC backbone does not affect the electrochemical activity significantly (Figure S6). These results emphasize the distinct influence of the substituent’s electronic properties on the electrochemistry of triazenyl species. Additionally, NHC-stabilized triazenyl cations (2, 4, 5, and 6) that exhibit reversible electron transfer behavior were chemically reduced by potassium metal, and the formation of the corresponding triazenyl radicals were confirmed by X-band electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy (for details, see the Supporting Information).
    To demonstrate the potential applicability of the new redox-active NHC–triazenyl platforms in NORFBs, 4 was chosen as the model anolyte because of the lowest redox potential among the NHC–triazenyl compounds investigated here. In addition, 4 has the most polar structure among 2, 3, and 4, leading to a high solubility of 1.19 M in MeCN, which is expected to deliver a high energy density. In the NORFB configuration, the anolyte was coupled with the MEEPT catholyte, which is commercially available and well known as the stable p-type molecule (Figure 4a). (26) As shown in Figure 4b, 4 in the supporting electrolyte of 0.5 M LiTFSI in MeCN undergoes a reversible redox reaction at −1.51 V versus Ag/AgNO3. This is lower than those of most anolyte materials reported, presenting its merit as the anolyte. (36,37,72) Taking MEEPT as the catholyte with a redox potential of 0.39 V versus Ag/AgNO3, the flow cell could achieve a cell voltage of 1.90 V. The intrinsic kinetic properties of 4 were investigated by rotating disk electrode (RDE) measurement, concerning the mass-diffusion and charge-transfer processes. As shown in Figure S8a, the linear relationship between iL and the square root of ω was well fitted by the Levich equation and the linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curves; the diffusion coefficient (D) of 4 was calculated to be 7.20 × 10–6 cm2 s–1 (see the Supporting Information for the detailed description). (73) The kinetic-controlled currents (ik) with respect to the overpotentials (η) were obtained by extrapolation in the Koutecký–Levich plots (Figure S8b). (73) The logarithm of the exchange current (log i0) was determined to be −4.11 for 4 (Figure S8c), yielding a kinetic rate constant (k0) of 4.10 × 10–3 cm s–1. Notably, the kinetic capability was on a par with those of previously reported redox-active molecules even though 4 has a relatively bulky molecular structure, implying that 4 would be favorable for a low-polarization and high-efficiency RFB. (35,74)
    The electrochemical performance of the flow cell employing a redox couple of MEEPT/4 was further investigated by using a custom flow cell, which was described in the previous work. (37)Figure 5a depicts a representative charge–discharge curve of the flow cell. Well-defined plateaus were observed during the charge and discharge process with an average cell voltage of 1.85 V, which is consistent with the CV experiment. It should be noted that a discharge capacity of 0.254 Ah L–1 corresponds to ∼95% of the theoretical value (0.268 Ah L–1), indicating the remarkable stability of the redox couple. Figure 5b shows the cycling efficiencies and capacity retention of the RFB at a current density of 20 mA/cm2. After the initial cycle, the flow cell maintained a high coulombic efficiency (CE) of ∼99% and an energy efficiency (EE) of ∼71% over 30 cycles. More importantly, the MEEPT/4 flow cell demonstrated remarkable capacity retention without any noticeable capacity fading for 30 cycles, which highlights the highly stable radical species of the redox couple. DFT calculations could theoretically support the stabilization mechanism of the triazenyl radical, the reduced form of 4, with respect to the spin density distribution, as presented in Figure 4c. It illustrates the strong delocalization of the unpaired electron that occurs over the conjugated π system of the IPr–N–N–N–IMe moiety, which reconfirms the stabilization effect of NHCs on triazenyl radicals.

    3. Conclusions

    ARTICLE SECTIONS
    Jump To
    In conclusion, novel redox-active triazenyl cations supported by NHCs were prepared by the reaction of the N-heterocyclic carbene azido cation 1 with various NHCs. Notably, the electrochemical properties of the new family of triazenyl species could be stably demonstrated and, furthermore, could be tunable by the modification of NHCs. The introduction of NHCs to ROMs was crucial due to the stability enhancement of organic radicals formed during the redox reactions, which could enable their application to NORFBs. When applied as an anolyte in NORFBs, pairing with MEEPT as a catholyte, the highly stable radical species of the redox couple contributed to the outstanding performance of the NORFB, where discharge capacity corresponding to ∼95% of the theoretical value and high capacity retention over 30 cycles could be achieved. The generation of new libraries of triazenyl species with varying NHC groups can pave the unexplored way for the discovery of high-performance ROMs for NORFB technologies.
